# Defense Technology: Reform Roadmap

## Overview

Implementing these solutions requires a phased approach, moving from immediate fixes that can be executed under existing authorities to structural reforms that require legislation, and finally to transformational changes that reshape the force and industrial base over a decade. The roadmap is organized around a central strategic imperative: the window of maximum danger in the Western Pacific (widely assessed as the late 2020s) sets the pace. The DoD does not have the luxury of decades-long reform timelines; it must deliver meaningful capability improvements within 3-5 years while simultaneously building the institutional foundations for sustained innovation.

## Phase 1: Immediate Actions (Years 1-2)

These actions can be taken under existing authorities or with minor legislative adjustments. They focus on demonstrating that the system can move fast and on preventing the loss of innovative companies in the Valley of Death.

| Priority | Action | Lead Organization | Key Performance Indicator (KPI) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Technology Transition Fund** | Congress appropriates $1-2 billion for a pilot "Technology Transition Fund" managed by OUSD(R&E). | OUSD(R&E), DIU, Congress | Fund established; first 20-30 projects bridged from prototype to initial production. |
| **2. Replicator Execution and Expansion** | DoD delivers on Replicator I and II, fielding thousands of autonomous systems and validating rapid scaling of commercial technology. | DepSecDef, DIU, Services | Autonomous systems delivered to INDOPACOM; operational concepts validated in exercises; production contracts awarded to non-traditional firms. |
| **3. Software Acquisition Acceleration** | Expand the Software Acquisition Pathway; implement BA-8 pilot programs for 10+ major software-intensive systems. | OUSD(A&S), CDAO | 25% increase in programs using agile software authorities; average software update cycle reduced from months to weeks. |
| **4. Data Rights and Open Architecture** | DoD issues binding policy mandating open APIs, modular open systems architecture (MOSA), and government-purpose data rights in all new contracts above $50 million. | OUSD(A&S), CIO | All new major RFPs include mandatory interoperability and data rights clauses; vendor lock-in provisions identified and eliminated in 50+ existing contracts. |
| **5. Clearance Acceleration** | Fully implement Continuous Vetting and automate background investigation processing. Target 90-day average processing time for new Secret clearances. | DCSA (Defense Counterintelligence and Security Agency) | Average Secret clearance processing time reduced from 8-12 months to 90 days; backlog reduced by 50%. |
| **6. Responsible AI Implementation** | CDAO publishes updated RAI implementation guidance; all AI systems in lethal decision-support roles undergo independent ethical review. | CDAO, DOT&E | RAI Toolkit applied to 100% of new AI programs; first independent ethical reviews completed for Project Maven successors and CCA autonomy software. |

**Phase 1 Goal**: Prove that the DoD can move fast on specific priorities, stop the bleeding of startups dying in the Valley of Death, and deliver initial autonomous capabilities to the Indo-Pacific theater.

### Phase 1 Key Metrics Dashboard

| Metric | Baseline (2025) | Year 1 Target | Year 2 Target |
|---|---|---|---|
| Startups with active DoD production contracts | ~50 | 75 | 120 |
| Average time from DIU prototype to production contract | 36 months | 24 months | 18 months |
| Autonomous systems fielded to INDOPACOM | <100 | 500+ | 2,000+ |
| Programs using Software Acquisition Pathway | ~30 | 45 | 60 |
| Average Secret clearance processing time | 8-12 months | 6 months | 90 days |

### Phase 1 Political Strategy

- Build a bipartisan "Innovation Caucus" in both chambers, anchored by members whose districts host non-traditional defense companies and commercial tech firms.
- Use Replicator success stories to demonstrate that speed and accountability are not mutually exclusive.
- Engage the VC community to publicly advocate for the Transition Fund, putting private sector credibility behind the request.

**Risk**: Phase 1 actions will face resistance from the acquisition bureaucracy, which will argue that rapid authorities are being "abused" and that oversight is insufficient. Sustained senior leadership engagement (Secretary and Deputy Secretary level) is essential to overcome this resistance.

## Phase 2: Structural Reform (Years 3-5)

These actions require legislation (typically through the NDAA) and represent fundamental changes to the rules governing defense acquisition, budgeting, and workforce management.

| Priority | Action | Lead Organization | Key Performance Indicator (KPI) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. PPBE Reform Legislation** | Congress passes legislation implementing the PPBE Reform Commission's key recommendations: portfolio budgeting, delegated reprogramming authority, and a unified software appropriation. | Congress (SASC, HASC), OUSD(Comptroller) | 50% of acquisition budget managed via portfolio authorities; reprogramming within portfolios delegated to PEOs below a $100M threshold. |
| **2. Workforce Overhaul** | Implement lateral entry across all services for technical specialties (cyber, AI, software, space). Establish competitive pay scales for acquisition workforce technical roles. Create a dedicated "Technology Acquisition" career field. | OSD Personnel, Service Secretaries | 1,000+ mid-career tech experts hired or commissioned per year; acquisition workforce attrition to industry reduced by 30%. |
| **3. DIU Production Authority** | Grant DIU statutory authority to award production contracts (not just prototype OTAs) for commercial technologies that have demonstrated capability, up to $500M per contract. | Congress, DIU | DIU awards 10+ production contracts per year; average time from DIU prototype to production reduced from 36 months to 12 months. |
| **4. Joint Simulation Environment** | Complete the Joint Simulation Environment (JSE) for all domains. Require all major programs to undergo JSE testing before Milestone B (production) decisions. | OUSD(R&E), DOT&E | All major acquisition programs conduct JSE-based operational assessments; JSE availability for classified and unclassified testing across all service environments. |
| **5. Allied Technology Integration** | Implement AUKUS Pillar II technology sharing: create ITAR exemptions for Australia and the UK for specified technology areas. Extend similar frameworks to Japan and potentially South Korea. | State Department (DDTC), OSD Policy | Seamless classified and unclassified technology collaboration with Australia and UK on AI, quantum, hypersonics, and undersea capabilities; first co-development programs initiated with Japan. |
| **6. Microelectronics Resilience** | Achieve initial operating capability for CHIPS Act-funded fabs (TSMC Arizona, Intel Ohio, Samsung Texas). Establish a strategic semiconductor reserve for defense-critical chips. | Commerce Department, OUSD(R&E) | Domestic fabrication of defense-relevant chips at 5nm node or below; strategic reserve of 12-month supply for critical military components. |
| **7. Non-Traditional Market Share** | Mandate and track the percentage of procurement dollars awarded to non-traditional defense companies. Set a target of 10% within 5 years. | OUSD(A&S), DIU, Congress | Non-traditional firms capture 10% of major procurement contract dollars (up from estimated 3-5% today). |

**Phase 2 Goal**: Change the underlying rules of the game -- budgeting, personnel, and acquisition authorities -- to make innovation the institutional norm rather than the exception. Build the domestic microelectronics and industrial capacity needed for sustained technological competition.

### Phase 2 Key Metrics Dashboard

| Metric | Baseline (2025) | Year 3 Target | Year 5 Target |
|---|---|---|---|
| % of acquisition budget under portfolio authorities | 0% | 25% | 50% |
| Mid-career tech experts commissioned/hired per year | <100 | 500 | 1,000+ |
| Non-traditional firm share of procurement dollars | ~3-5% | 7% | 10% |
| DIU production contracts per year | <5 | 10 | 20+ |
| Domestic advanced chip fabrication capacity (% of global) | ~12% | 15% | 20% |

### Phase 2 Legislative Strategy

- **Vehicle**: The annual National Defense Authorization Act (NDAA) is the primary legislative vehicle.
- **PPBE Reform**: Introduce a standalone bill or NDAA amendment implementing the PPBE Commission's top 10 recommendations. Secure bipartisan co-sponsors on both Armed Services Committees.
- **Workforce**: Authorize lateral entry through a "21st Century Defense Workforce Act" modeled on successful pilot programs.
- **Allied tech sharing**: Attach ITAR reform provisions to the State Department authorization bill or as NDAA amendments, framing them as essential to AUKUS implementation.
- **Coalition**: Mobilize the defense tech startup community, VCs (Andreessen Horowitz, Founders Fund, Shield Capital), and reform-oriented think tanks (CNAS, CSET, SCSP) to provide testimony and public advocacy.

**Risk**: Structural reform requires sustained congressional attention and bipartisan commitment. The risk of "reform fatigue" is high: past reform efforts (Packard Commission, Goldwater-Nichols acquisition reforms, Weapon Systems Acquisition Reform Act of 2009) produced initial momentum that was eventually absorbed by the bureaucracy. Sustained pressure from the executive branch, industry, and external advocates is needed to prevent this.

## Phase 3: Transformation (Years 6-10)

These are aspirational goals that represent a fundamentally different force structure, industrial base, and acquisition culture. Achievement depends on the success of Phases 1 and 2.

| Priority | Action | Lead Organization | Key Performance Indicator (KPI) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. AI-Native Force** | AI and autonomy are fully integrated into doctrine, force structure, and decision-making processes. Every major platform has an autonomous variant or an autonomous complement. | Services, CDAO, Joint Staff | Majority of new air, sea, and ground assets entering the force are unmanned or optionally manned. AI-enabled C2 systems operational across all combatant commands. |
| **2. Diversified Industrial Base** | The "Big Five" primes are joined by 3-5 new major defense technology firms that compete for prime contracts on major programs. | OUSD(A&S), DIU | Non-traditional companies capture 20%+ of prime contract dollars. At least 2 non-traditional firms have achieved annual defense revenue exceeding $5 billion. |
| **3. Dynamic Acquisition** | Acquisition cycle times for software-intensive and autonomous systems match commercial technology cycles (12-24 months from concept to fielding). | OUSD(A&S), OUSD(R&E) | Average time to field a new software-defined capability: 18 months (down from 7-10 years). Continuous delivery of software updates to deployed systems. |
| **4. Resilient Supply Chains** | The U.S. and its allies have end-to-end domestic or allied-nation supply chains for all critical defense technologies: semiconductors, rare earths, energetics, solid rocket motors, precision munitions. | Commerce, DoD, allied governments | Zero single-foreign-source dependencies for Tier 1 defense supply chain components. Munitions production capacity sufficient to sustain 6+ months of high-intensity conflict. |
| **5. Global Technology Alliance** | The AUKUS technology-sharing model has been extended to a broader coalition of democratic allies (Japan, South Korea, EU nations, Canada), creating a shared innovation ecosystem that outpaces authoritarian competitors. | State Department, OSD Policy | Multilateral co-development programs in AI, quantum, hypersonics, and space with 5+ allied nations. Common technology standards and interoperable systems across the alliance. |
| **6. Ethical AI Leadership** | The U.S. has established itself as the global leader in responsible military AI, with international norms and confidence-building measures that reduce escalation risks. | OSD Policy, State Department | International framework for AI in warfare endorsed by 75+ nations. Bilateral U.S.-China mechanisms to prevent AI-enabled accidental escalation. |

**Phase 3 Goal**: A transformed defense industrial base, force structure, and acquisition system capable of sustaining technological overmatch against any peer competitor through superior innovation, adaptability, and allied integration -- while maintaining ethical leadership in the development and deployment of AI and autonomous systems.

### Phase 3 Success Indicators

- The U.S. military can field a new software-defined capability from concept to operational deployment in under 18 months.
- At least 50% of combat aircraft entering the inventory are unmanned or optionally manned.
- The defense industrial base includes 3-5 non-traditional firms with annual defense revenue exceeding $5 billion.
- Allied co-development programs produce interoperable autonomous systems, AI platforms, and quantum-secured communications.
- No critical defense supply chain has a single-foreign-source dependency.
- The U.S. is recognized internationally as the leader in responsible military AI, with norms and frameworks adopted by a majority of democratic nations.

## Historical Comparison: Reform Timelines

Understanding the timeline of past defense reforms provides context for the ambition of this roadmap:

| Reform | Initiated | Key Legislation | Time to Impact | Outcome |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Packard Commission | 1985 | Goldwater-Nichols (1986) | 5-10 years | Improved joint operations; acquisition reforms partially absorbed by bureaucracy. |
| Acquisition Reform (Perry/Kaminski) | 1993 | FASA (1994), Clinger-Cohen (1996) | 3-5 years | Temporary gains in commercial buying; many reforms eroded by 2000s. |
| Third Offset Strategy | 2014 | Various NDAA provisions | 5+ years | Conceptual success; execution hampered by counter-terrorism focus and acquisition inertia. |
| PPBE Reform Commission | 2022 | Pending | TBD | Recommendations published March 2024; implementation incomplete. |

The historical pattern is clear: reform initiatives produce initial momentum that dissipates within 5-10 years as the bureaucracy adapts to absorb changes without fundamentally altering its behavior. Breaking this pattern requires embedding reforms in statute, creating institutional champions (DIU, CDAO) with permanent authority, and maintaining sustained external pressure.

## International Benchmarks

Reform timelines should be assessed against what competitors are achieving:

- **China**: The PLA has reorganized its entire force structure (2015-2016), established a Strategic Support Force for space, cyber, and electronic warfare, and fielded operational hypersonic weapons -- all within approximately 8 years. China's Military-Civil Fusion strategy enables technology integration at a pace the U.S. acquisition system cannot match.
- **Israel**: The Israel Defense Forces routinely field new technology within 6-18 months of identifying a need. The Iron Dome system was developed in approximately 3 years from concept to operational deployment. Israeli defense tech companies (Rafael, Elbit, IAI) operate under acquisition frameworks that emphasize speed and iterative improvement.
- **Ukraine**: Under wartime pressure, Ukraine has demonstrated that commercial technology can be adapted for military use in weeks to months. Ukraine's drone production ecosystem scaled from near-zero to producing thousands of FPV drones per month within approximately 12 months.

These benchmarks demonstrate that the U.S. acquisition timeline of 7-10 years for major systems is not an inherent law of technology development -- it is a choice embedded in institutional structures that can be changed.

## Sequencing Logic

The three phases are not independent; they build on each other:

```text
Phase 1 (Years 1-2): Prove it works
    - Bridge Fund prevents startup deaths
    - Replicator proves autonomous systems can be fielded at speed
    - Software pathway demonstrates agile development in DoD
        |
Phase 2 (Years 3-5): Change the rules
    - PPBE reform makes portfolio budgeting permanent
    - Workforce reform brings in technical talent
    - DIU production authority eliminates the Valley of Death structurally
    - Allied integration expands the innovation base
        |
Phase 3 (Years 6-10): Transform the force
    - AI-native force design leverages reformed acquisition
    - Diversified industrial base competes on merit
    - Resilient supply chains insure against crisis
    - Ethical AI leadership shapes global norms
```

Each phase creates the conditions for the next. Phase 1 demonstrates feasibility and builds political support. Phase 2 institutionalizes the reforms in law and policy. Phase 3 harvests the results in transformed capability.

## Key Dependencies and Risks

| Risk | Probability | Impact | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Congressional failure to pass PPBE reform | High | Very High | Build bipartisan coalition; tie reform to China competition narrative. |
| Primes lobbying to gut non-traditional set-asides | High | High | Mobilize VC community and startup CEOs as counterweight lobby. |
| Taiwan contingency disrupting chip supply before CHIPS Act fabs operational | Medium | Catastrophic | Strategic semiconductor reserve; accelerate allied-nation fab timelines. |
| AI safety incident (autonomous system civilian casualties) | Medium | Very High | Robust TEVV regime; human-on-the-loop for all lethal systems; rapid incident response protocol. |
| Clearance reform stalls due to security concerns | High | Medium | Pilot programs at DIU and DARPA; demonstrate that faster clearances do not increase security incidents. |
| Innovation fatigue and bureaucratic reversion | High | High | Institutional anchoring (DIU elevation, CDAO permanence); congressional reporting requirements; public metrics dashboards. |
