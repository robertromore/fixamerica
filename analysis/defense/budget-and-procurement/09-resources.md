# Budget and Procurement: Resources

This section provides a curated list of key government sources, reports, expert analysis, and legal authorities on the U.S. defense budget and acquisition system.

---

## Primary Government Sources

### Department of Defense

- **DoD Comptroller**
    - The official source for all DoD budget documents, including the annual President's Budget request, R-1 (RDT&E) and P-1 (Procurement) exhibits, Future Years Defense Program (FYDP) data, and historical budget tables.
    - <https://comptroller.defense.gov/>

- **Cost Assessment and Program Evaluation (CAPE)**
    - Provides independent cost estimates and program evaluations directly to the Secretary of Defense. Publishes Selected Acquisition Reports (SARs) for major programs.
    - <https://www.cape.osd.mil/>

- **Director, Operational Test & Evaluation (DOT&E)**
    - The DoD's independent testing office. Its annual report provides blunt assessments of whether new weapon systems actually work in realistic combat conditions. One of the few consistently candid public documents in the acquisition system.
    - <https://www.dote.osd.mil/>

- **Defense Acquisition University (DAU)**
    - The DoD's internal university for training the acquisition workforce. Its website is a vast repository of information on acquisition policy, the Adaptive Acquisition Framework, and the DoD 5000 series regulations.
    - <https://www.dau.edu/>

- **DoD Inspector General**
    - Conducts audits, investigations, and evaluations of DoD programs and operations. Publishes reports on acquisition fraud, waste, and systemic management failures.
    - <https://www.dodig.mil/>

### Congressional Oversight Bodies

- **Government Accountability Office (GAO)**
    - The primary congressional watchdog for defense acquisition. The GAO produces hundreds of reports annually on the performance of DoD programs, cost overruns, industrial base health, and systemic acquisition issues. Its annual *Weapon Systems Annual Assessment* is the single most important public document on acquisition performance.
    - Key reports:
        - *Defense Acquisitions Annual Assessment* (annual, e.g., GAO-24-106556)
        - *Defense Contracting: Actions Needed to Increase Competition* (GAO-24-106890)
        - *F-35 Joint Strike Fighter* reports (multiple annually)
        - *Sentinel ICBM* cost assessment reports
    - <https://www.gao.gov/>

- **Congressional Budget Office (CBO)**
    - Provides non-partisan analysis of the defense budget, long-term cost projections for the nuclear triad, shipbuilding plans, and the fiscal implications of policy options. CBO's independent cost estimates are frequently more accurate than the DoD's own projections.
    - Key publications:
        - *Long-Term Costs of the Administration's Future Years Defense Program* (annual)
        - *The U.S. Military's Force Structure: A Primer*
        - Individual program cost analyses (Columbia-class submarine, nuclear forces, etc.)
    - <https://www.cbo.gov/>

- **Congressional Research Service (CRS)**
    - Provides non-partisan research and analysis for members of Congress. CRS reports on defense acquisition are widely used by staff and the public.
    - Key reports:
        - *Defense Acquisitions: How DOD Acquires Weapon Systems and Recent Efforts to Reform the Process* (RL34026)
        - *Defense Primer: The Defense Industrial Base* (IF10548)
        - Individual program reports (F-35, Columbia-class, Sentinel, Ford-class, etc.)
        - *Continuing Resolutions: Overview of Components and Practices* (R46595)
    - <https://crsreports.congress.gov/>

### Reform Commission Reports

- **PPBE Reform Commission**
    - *Final Report: Defense Resourcing for the Future*. Washington, DC: PPBE Reform Commission, 2024.
    - Congressionally mandated examination of the DoD's budget and resource allocation process. Concluded the system was "designed for a different era" and recommended fundamental overhaul.

- **Section 809 Panel**
    - *Report of the Advisory Panel on Streamlining and Codifying Acquisition Regulations*, Volumes 1-3. Washington, DC: Section 809 Panel, 2018-2019.
    - The most comprehensive acquisition reform blueprint since the Packard Commission, with 98 recommendations across three volumes.

- **National Security Commission on Artificial Intelligence**
    - *Final Report*. Washington, DC: NSCAI, 2021.
    - Recommended significant investment in government technical talent, acquisition reform for AI and software, and new pathways for commercial technology firms.

## Books

- Fox, J. Ronald. *Defense Acquisition Reform, 1960-2009: An Elusive Goal*. Washington, DC: Center for Military History, 2011.
    - Comprehensive academic history of acquisition reform efforts over five decades, documenting why each wave of reform failed to produce lasting change.
- Fallows, James. *National Defense*. New York: Random House, 1981.
    - Classic analysis of Pentagon procurement dysfunction and the military reform movement of the 1980s. Still relevant for its description of the cultural and bureaucratic roots of acquisition failure.
- Gansler, Jacques S. *Democracy's Arsenal: Creating a Twenty-First-Century Defense Industry*. Cambridge, MA: MIT Press, 2011.
    - Former Under Secretary of Defense for Acquisition argues for fundamental reform of the defense industrial base and procurement system.
- Augustine, Norman R. *Augustine's Laws*. Reston, VA: AIAA, 1997.
    - Former Lockheed Martin CEO's satirical analysis of defense procurement dysfunction, including the famous "Law" predicting that at current cost trends, the entire defense budget would eventually buy a single aircraft.
- Hartung, William D. *Prophets of War: Lockheed Martin and the Making of the Military-Industrial Complex*. New York: Bold Type Books, 2011.
    - Detailed history of the largest defense contractor and its political influence on procurement decisions.
- Wheeler, Winslow T., and Lawrence J. Korb. *Military Reform: A Reference Handbook*. Westport, CT: Praeger Security International, 2007.
    - Reference guide to defense reform efforts and proposals across multiple decades.
- Adams, Gordon. *The Iron Triangle: The Politics of Defense Contracting*. New York: Council on Economic Priorities, 1981.
    - Foundational study of the triangular relationship among the Pentagon, Congress, and defense industry.

## Academic Research

- Alexander, Raquel, Stephen W. Mazza, and Susan Scholz. "Measuring Rates of Return for Lobbying Expenditures: An Empirical Case Study of Tax Breaks for Multinational Corporations." *Journal of Law and Economics* 52, no. 2 (2009): 401-426.
    - Demonstrates the high return on investment of corporate lobbying ($6-$20 per dollar spent).
- Thorpe, Rebecca U. *The American Warfare State: The Domestic Politics of Military Spending*. Chicago: University of Chicago Press, 2014.
    - Academic analysis of how geographic distribution of defense spending shapes congressional voting on defense programs.
- Arena, Mark V., et al. *Historical Cost Growth of Completed Weapon System Programs*. RAND Corporation, 2006.
    - Quantitative analysis of cost growth patterns across decades of major defense programs.

## Key Legal Authorities

- **10 U.S.C. Chapter 137** -- Procurement Generally
- **10 U.S.C. 4374** -- Nunn-McCurdy Amendment (unit cost breach notification and certification requirements)
- **10 U.S.C. 3453-3458** -- Major Defense Acquisition Program milestone decisions
- **Federal Acquisition Regulation (FAR)** -- Governs all federal procurement, including defense
- **Defense Federal Acquisition Regulation Supplement (DFARS)** -- Defense-specific procurement rules
- **10 U.S.C. 4401-4403** -- Other Transaction Authority (OTA) for prototyping and production
- **Section 804 of the FY2016 NDAA** -- Middle Tier of Acquisition authority for rapid prototyping and rapid fielding
- **41 U.S.C. Chapter 21** -- Procurement Integrity Act (restrictions on obtaining and disclosing procurement information)
