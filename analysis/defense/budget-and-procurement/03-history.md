# Budget and Procurement: History

## Historical Development

The history of U.S. defense budgeting and procurement is a story of continuous attempts to impose rational management on an inherently complex and political process, with each generation of reformers grappling with the same persistent problems of cost overruns and inefficiency. The cyclical pattern -- a spending surge or scandal triggers reform, the reforms are partially implemented but absorbed by the bureaucracy, the underlying incentive structures remain unchanged, and the problems re-emerge -- has repeated itself every decade for over sixty years.

---

## World War II and the Birth of the Permanent Defense Industry (1940s-1950s)

Before World War II, the United States did not maintain a permanent defense industrial base. The "Arsenal of Democracy" was created through emergency mobilization: automobile factories converted to tank and aircraft production, shipyards were built from scratch, and the entire economy was redirected toward war production. At its peak, defense spending consumed approximately 40% of GDP.

The Cold War shattered the demobilization pattern that had followed every previous conflict. The perceived Soviet threat, the nuclear arms race, and the Korean War (1950-1953) convinced policymakers that the United States needed to maintain permanent military readiness. Defense spending surged from $13.7 billion in FY1950 to $52.8 billion in FY1953 (approximately $600 billion in 2024 dollars). Companies that had planned to return to civilian production -- Boeing, Lockheed, General Dynamics, and others -- found that government defense contracts were more lucrative and more reliable than commercial markets. The permanent defense industry was born.

### Early Procurement Challenges

Even in this formative period, the patterns that would define defense procurement were already visible:

- **The B-36 Peacemaker controversy (1949)**: The Navy accused the Air Force of pushing the Convair B-36 bomber through political connections rather than military merit, in the so-called "Revolt of the Admirals." This foreshadowed decades of inter-service acquisition battles, where the choice of weapon systems was driven as much by bureaucratic politics as by strategic need.
- **Cost-plus contracting**: The default procurement mechanism, inherited from wartime urgency, reimbursed contractors for all costs plus a guaranteed profit margin, creating minimal incentive for cost control. This contract type became entrenched because both contractors (who preferred guaranteed profits) and the government (which lacked the expertise to write good fixed-price contracts) found it convenient.
- **The "follow-on imperative"**: Defense firms learned that the end of one contract had to be followed immediately by the start of another to maintain workforce and facilities, creating a structural drive for continuous program initiation regardless of strategic need.
- **The National Security Act of 1947**: Created the institutional architecture that would house the acquisition system -- the Department of Defense (unifying the War and Navy Departments), the National Security Council, the CIA, and the Joint Chiefs of Staff. This infrastructure created permanent demand for weapons systems and a permanent set of contractors to supply them.

## The McNamara Revolution: PPBS (1960s)

- **Context**: Before the 1960s, the defense budget was largely a bottom-up process where each military service submitted its own "wish list" with little central strategic direction. The services competed fiercely for budget share, with each proposing weapon systems that maximized its institutional role rather than national strategic needs.
- **The Reform**: As Secretary of Defense, Robert McNamara, a former Ford Motor Company executive, introduced the **Planning, Programming, and Budgeting System (PPBS)** in 1961. This was a revolutionary attempt to bring corporate-style, data-driven management to the Pentagon.
- **Key Concepts**:
    - **Planning**: Started with national strategy and objectives, not service wish lists.
    - **Programming**: Linked strategy to specific, multi-year programs and forces, organized by mission area rather than by service.
    - **Budgeting**: Translated the programs into an annual budget request.
    - **Systems Analysis**: Used quantitative analysis (operations research, cost-effectiveness studies) to evaluate weapon systems. McNamara's "whiz kids" -- young analysts from RAND and academia -- applied these techniques to challenge service parochialism.
- **The TFX/F-111 Controversy**: McNamara's insistence on a single "commonality" fighter for both the Air Force and Navy -- the Tactical Fighter Experimental (TFX), which became the F-111 -- was an early case study in how top-down requirements mandates can go wrong. The attempt to force a common airframe on two services with fundamentally different needs produced a compromised design that satisfied neither. The Navy version was eventually canceled. General Dynamics won the contract over Boeing in a decision widely criticized as politically motivated.
- **Legacy**: PPBS (now PPBE, with "Execution" added in 2003) remains the foundational intellectual framework for the entire DoD budget process. It was a genuine improvement over the pre-McNamara era, but its implementation became increasingly bureaucratic over time. The PPBE Reform Commission, established by Congress in the FY2022 NDAA and reporting in 2024, concluded that the system had become "rigid, slow, and disconnected from strategy" and recommended fundamental overhaul.

## The Post-Vietnam Era and the Rise of Reform (1970s-1980s)

### Procurement Scandals and the Reform Movement

- **Context**: The Vietnam War and a series of procurement scandals in the 1970s led to public outcry and congressional investigation. The C-5A Galaxy transport aircraft, built by Lockheed, became a symbol of defense procurement dysfunction.
- **The C-5A Scandal**: Pentagon cost analyst A. Ernest Fitzgerald testified to Congress in 1968 that the C-5A program was $2 billion over its $3.4 billion contract -- and was fired for his candor. His subsequent reinstatement, after years of legal battles, established the principle (though not the reliable practice) of whistleblower protection in defense procurement.
- **Senator William Proxmire** conducted years of investigations into procurement waste, documenting "buy-in" pricing, gold-plating, and the revolving door between the Pentagon and industry.
- **The Lockheed Bailout (1971)**: Lockheed's simultaneous cost overruns on the C-5A and the L-1011 commercial aircraft brought the company to near-bankruptcy. Congress passed the Emergency Loan Guarantee Act of 1971 -- a $250 million federal bailout that established the precedent that major defense contractors were "too big to fail."
- **The "Revolt of the Colonels"**: A group of reform-minded military officers and civilian analysts -- including Pierre Sprey, John Boyd, Chuck Spinney, and others known as the "Fighter Mafia" or "Military Reform Movement" -- argued that the Pentagon was buying overly complex, expensive, and unreliable weapons at the expense of simpler, more effective alternatives. Their advocacy influenced the design of the F-16 and A-10, both of which proved more effective and affordable than the gold-plated alternatives the services had preferred.

### The Packard Commission and Goldwater-Nichols (1986)

- **Context**: The Reagan-era defense buildup (FY1981-FY1985) increased defense spending by 40% in real terms but was accompanied by a series of embarrassing procurement scandals -- **$640 toilet seats**, **$7,600 coffee makers**, and the **Operation Ill Wind** bribery investigation (1986-1990) that produced 90 convictions.
- **Packard Commission (1986)**: In response, President Reagan chartered the Blue Ribbon Commission on Defense Management, chaired by David Packard (co-founder of Hewlett-Packard and former Deputy Secretary of Defense). Its recommendations included strengthening the Under Secretary of Defense for Acquisition, using competitive prototyping, reducing oversight layers, and increasing use of commercial products.
- **Goldwater-Nichols Act of 1986**: While primarily a reform of the military operational chain of command (establishing the Chairman of the Joint Chiefs as the principal military adviser), Goldwater-Nichols also created the Under Secretary of Defense for Acquisition as the Pentagon's chief weapons buyer and strengthened the Chairman's role in the requirements process.
- **Defense Acquisition Workforce Improvement Act (DAWIA) (1990)**: Created a professional, trained, and certified acquisition corps within the DoD, establishing education and certification requirements for the acquisition workforce.
- **Legacy**: Many Packard Commission recommendations were enacted into law, but the reforms were largely absorbed by the bureaucracy without fundamentally changing the incentive structures that produced the problems. The competitive prototyping mandate was honored mostly in the breach. The newly created Under Secretary of Defense for Acquisition was intended to centralize control, but the services retained significant influence over requirements and program management, diluting the reform's impact.

### Key Budget Milestones (1970s-1980s)

| Year | Event | Impact on Budget/Procurement |
|---|---|---|
| 1970 | Congressional Budget and Impoundment Control Act | Created CBO; established modern congressional budget process |
| 1971 | Emergency Loan Guarantee Act | Bailed out Lockheed; established "too big to fail" precedent |

## The Post-Cold War "Procurement Holiday" (1990s)

- **Context**: With the collapse of the Soviet Union, defense budgets were cut by approximately 35% in real terms between FY1989 and FY1998. The "peace dividend" led to dramatic changes in both the budget and the industrial base.
- **"Procurement Holiday"**: The DoD dramatically cut funding for buying new weapon systems, from a peak of $120 billion in FY1985 to just $44 billion in FY1998 (constant dollars). Instead of starting new programs, it focused on life-extending Cold War-era systems -- a decision that created the aging fleet and deferred modernization challenges the DoD faces today.
- **The "Last Supper" (1993)**: Deputy Secretary of Defense William Perry and Under Secretary John Deutch hosted a now-famous dinner with defense industry CEOs, telling them that the post-Cold War drawdown would dramatically reduce defense spending and that the industry needed to consolidate. The government went further: under the "Paygo" policy, the DoD agreed to reimburse companies for the costs of mergers and acquisitions, effectively subsidizing its own loss of competition. Between 1993 and 1997, the Pentagon paid approximately $1 billion in merger subsidies.
- **The Merger Wave**: The number of prime defense contractors collapsed from over 50 to fewer than 10 in just four years (1993-1997). Lockheed merged with Martin Marietta; Boeing acquired McDonnell Douglas and Rockwell Defense; Raytheon acquired Hughes Aircraft and Texas Instruments' defense division; Northrop merged with Grumman. The 1999 blocking of the Lockheed Martin-Northrop Grumman merger was the last major antitrust action in the defense sector.
- **"Acquisition Reform" of the 1990s**: Led by Secretary of Defense William Perry, this wave of reform focused on adopting commercial practices. The Federal Acquisition Streamlining Act of 1994 and the Clinger-Cohen Act of 1996 encouraged the use of commercial off-the-shelf (COTS) products and sought to simplify regulations. While these reforms had some positive effects, they also weakened government oversight capacity and contributed to the erosion of the defense acquisition workforce.

### The Consolidation Timeline

| Year | Event | Result |
|---|---|---|
| 1993 | "Last Supper" hosted by Deputy Secretary Perry | DoD encouraged defense industry consolidation |
| 1993 | General Dynamics sells fighter jet division to Lockheed | GD exits tactical aircraft |
| 1994 | Lockheed merges with Martin Marietta | Created world's largest defense firm |
| 1994 | Northrop merges with Grumman | Created Northrop Grumman |
| 1995 | Lockheed Martin acquires Loral Defense | Expanded electronics/space capabilities |
| 1996 | Boeing acquires Rockwell Defense & Space | Boeing expands into defense |
| 1997 | Boeing merges with McDonnell Douglas | Reduced fighter jet makers from 3 to 2 |
| 1997 | Raytheon acquires Hughes Aircraft + Texas Instruments Defense | Created missile/radar near-monopoly |
| 1999 | DoD/FTC blocks Lockheed Martin-Northrop Grumman merger | Last major antitrust action in defense sector |

The result: the number of prime defense contractors fell from over 50 to fewer than 10 in just six years. The government had funded its own loss of competition -- paying approximately $1 billion in merger subsidies while eliminating the competitive pressure that had historically constrained contractor pricing.

## The Post-9/11 Spending Surge and Its Aftermath (2001-2014)

- **Context**: The wars in Iraq and Afghanistan triggered the largest sustained increase in defense spending since the Korean War. The base defense budget (excluding war costs) grew from $316 billion in FY2001 to $530 billion by FY2010. Including Overseas Contingency Operations (OCO) funding -- the supplemental war budget that was exempt from spending caps -- total defense spending reached $691 billion in FY2010.
- **Urgent Needs, Bypassed Processes**: The urgent need for equipment like Mine-Resistant Ambush Protected (MRAP) vehicles led the DoD to bypass the traditional acquisition system entirely, using rapid procurement authorities to field thousands of vehicles in months rather than the years the normal process would have required. The MRAP success demonstrated that the DoD *could* move fast when it had to -- and raised the question of why the normal process was so slow.
- **The "Lost Decade"**: The focus on counterinsurgency and counterterrorism led to underinvestment in capabilities for great power competition -- advanced fighter aircraft, submarines, long-range strike, hypersonic weapons, and electronic warfare.
- **Wartime Contracting Waste**: The Commission on Wartime Contracting in Iraq and Afghanistan (2008-2011) estimated that between **$31 billion and $60 billion** in wartime contracting was lost to waste and fraud, including phantom meals charged by KBR, defective contractor-built infrastructure, and insufficient oversight of a massive contracting enterprise.
- **Budget Control Act (BCA) of 2011**: Imposed strict caps on defense spending and led to a period of fiscal austerity known as "sequestration." In FY2013, the DoD absorbed approximately $37 billion in cuts through furloughs of 680,000 civilian employees, cancellation of training exercises (including an Army Combat Training Center rotation and Air Force flying hours), and deferred depot maintenance, severely harming military readiness.

## A New Era of Reform (2015-Present)

- **Better Buying Power (2010-2015)**: A series of internal DoD initiatives under Under Secretary Frank Kendall focused on achieving affordability, controlling cost growth, and improving competition. The initiatives had modest positive effects but did not change the fundamental dynamics of the system.
- **The "Third Offset Strategy" (2014-2016)**: Secretary of Defense Chuck Hagel and Deputy Secretary Bob Work launched an initiative to maintain U.S. military superiority through technology -- artificial intelligence, autonomous systems, human-machine teaming -- rather than simply outspending adversaries. This led to the creation of the Defense Innovation Unit (DIU) in Silicon Valley and the Strategic Capabilities Office.
- **Section 809 Panel (2018)**: A congressionally mandated Advisory Panel on Streamlining and Codifying Acquisition Regulations produced 98 recommendations across three volumes, constituting the most comprehensive acquisition reform blueprint since the Packard Commission. Key recommendations included simplifying the contracting process, expanding use of commercial solutions, and professionalizing the acquisition workforce. Implementation has been slow and uneven.
- **Adaptive Acquisition Framework (AAF)**: The DoD's current reform model, which creates six different acquisition "pathways" tailored to different types of programs, replacing the rigid, one-size-fits-all DoD 5000 process.
- **PPBE Reform Commission (2024)**: Established by the FY2022 NDAA, this commission examined the fundamental budget process and concluded that PPBE was "designed for a different era" and recommended a shift toward more agile, portfolio-based resource allocation. Its recommendations are being debated but have not yet been implemented.

### The Recurring Pattern

The history of defense acquisition reform is cyclical: a period of high spending and scandals leads to a wave of reform, which is followed by a period of budget cuts and bureaucratic absorption, and then a new crisis or scandal reveals that the fundamental problems were never solved. The underlying incentive structure of the Iron Triangle -- contractors seeking profits, members of Congress seeking jobs and contributions, and military services seeking advanced weapons -- has survived every reform wave intact.
