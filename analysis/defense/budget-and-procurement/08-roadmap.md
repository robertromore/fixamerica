# Budget and Procurement: Reform Roadmap

## Overview

Reforming the defense budget and procurement system requires a phased approach that starts with achievable process changes and builds momentum for deeper, structural reforms. The sequencing reflects political reality: comprehensive reform cannot be achieved in a single legislative action, and each phase must build the evidence base and institutional capacity needed for the next. The roadmap prioritizes reforms that create transparency and accountability first, because these are politically defensible and generate the public support needed for more ambitious structural changes.

---

## Phase 1: Laying the Groundwork for Accountability (Years 1-2)

This phase focuses on immediate legislative and administrative actions to increase transparency, establish data infrastructure, and create the conditions for more significant reform. These actions can be accomplished primarily through executive authority, DoD directives, and provisions in the annual NDAA.

| Priority | Action | Mechanism | Key Performance Indicator (KPI) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Empower Program Managers** | The Secretary of Defense issues a new directive empowering Program Managers (PMs) with greater authority and linking their tenure and promotion to program performance. Minimum 5-year tenure requirement. | DoD directive | At least 50% of major defense program PMs are on 5-year tenures. The first PM of a critically breaching program is removed. |
| **2. Create Public Performance Dashboard** | Develop and publish a real-time public dashboard showing cost, schedule, and performance data for all 83+ Major Defense Acquisition Programs, including comparison to original baselines. | DoD IT initiative (CAPE/CIO) | Dashboard operational within 12 months; updated quarterly. |
| **3. Pilot Fixed-Price Development Contracts** | Congress authorizes a pilot program requiring at least five new major programs entering Milestone B to use fixed-price incentive fee contracts. | NDAA provision | Five programs initiated using FPIF contracts; cost performance tracked against historical cost-plus baselines. |
| **4. Expand "Should Cost" Reviews** | Mandate independent "should cost" reviews for all contracts above $100 million, conducted by CAPE or DCAA with full access to contractor cost data. | DoD policy memo / NDAA | At least 10 "should cost" reviews completed in Year 1, documenting savings opportunities of $500M+. |
| **5. Charter "BRAC for Weapons" Commission** | Congress passes legislation chartering an independent "Defense Programs Review Commission" to begin its analysis of legacy weapon systems for potential termination or restructuring. | Federal legislation | Commission fully staffed with conflict-of-interest screening; holds first public hearings; begins analysis of at least 15 candidate programs. |
| **6. Establish "Valley of Death" Fund** | Congress establishes and provides initial funding ($5 billion) for a "Transition and Scaling Fund" to bridge the gap between prototype success and program of record. | Appropriations | The fund awards its first bridge contracts to at least 10 companies within 18 months. DIU and AFWERX transition rates improve by 50%. |
| **7. Strengthen CAPE Authority** | Require CAPE independent cost estimates to serve as the official baseline for Nunn-McCurdy reporting on all new programs entering Milestone B. | NDAA provision | All new Milestone B decisions use CAPE estimates as baseline (not program office estimates). |
| **8. End New-Start Prohibition in CRs** | Pass legislation allowing new program starts during continuing resolutions at 80% of requested funding levels, eliminating the modernization freeze. | Federal legislation | No program start delays attributable to CR restrictions in Year 2. |

**Goal of Phase 1**: Introduce real accountability and transparency into the system. Build the evidence base for Phase 2 reforms by demonstrating the scale of cost growth, the feasibility of fixed-price contracts, and the viability of new entrant support.

### Phase 1 Political Strategy

Phase 1 reforms are designed to be achievable with existing authority or bipartisan consensus:

- **Transparency measures** (the public dashboard, "should cost" reviews) are difficult for opponents to argue against publicly -- no member of Congress wants to be seen opposing transparency in defense spending.
- **The PM empowerment directive** can be implemented unilaterally by the Secretary of Defense without congressional action, making it the fastest reform to implement.
- **The "valley of death" fund** appeals to the technology sector and venture capital community, creating new political allies for reform outside the traditional defense establishment. Silicon Valley firms, which have been frustrated by DoD procurement barriers, would become active supporters.
- **The fixed-price pilot** is framed as an experiment, not a mandate -- reducing industry opposition by limiting its initial scope to five programs.
- **The "BRAC for Weapons" commission** charter merely begins the analysis process; the politically difficult up-or-down vote does not occur until Phase 2, allowing time to build support.

### Phase 1 Dependencies and Sequencing

The Phase 1 actions are largely independent and can be pursued in parallel:

- Actions 1 (PM empowerment), 2 (dashboard), and 4 ("should cost" reviews) can be implemented through DoD directives without legislation.
- Actions 3 (fixed-price pilot), 5 (commission charter), 6 (valley of death fund), 7 (CAPE authority), and 8 (CR reform) require NDAA or appropriations provisions.
- The dashboard (Action 2) creates the data infrastructure needed to support the "BRAC for Weapons" commission analysis (Action 5).
- The "should cost" reviews (Action 4) generate the evidence base for the fixed-price contract mandate in Phase 2.

## Phase 2: Implementing Structural Reforms (Years 3-5)

This phase focuses on implementing the major structural changes to the budget and acquisition processes, building on the evidence and political momentum generated in Phase 1.

| Priority | Action | Mechanism | Key Performance Indicator (KPI) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Pass "BRAC for Weapons" Package** | The Defense Programs Review Commission submits its first report to Congress recommending specific program terminations and restructurings. Congress holds an up-or-down vote on the full package. | Federal legislation | Legislative package passed; DoD begins implementing program terminations; savings of at least $15 billion identified for reinvestment. |
| **2. Shift to Biennial Budgeting** | Congress passes a "Biennial Budgeting Act for National Defense," shifting the DoD to a two-year budget cycle with off-year adjustment and oversight. | Federal legislation | The DoD submits its first biennial budget request. Oversight hearings in the off year increase by 50%. |
| **3. Mandate Fixed-Price Contracts** | Based on the results of the Phase 1 pilot, Congress passes legislation requiring fixed-price incentive fee contracts for all new programs entering Milestone B, with cost-plus requiring a written waiver from the MDA. | Federal legislation | All new Milestone B decisions default to FPIF contracts. Cost-plus waiver requests are publicly reported. |
| **4. Scale Software Acquisition Pathway** | The DoD fully implements and scales the software acquisition pathway across all services, with dedicated DevSecOps teams, continuous ATO, and government-owned architectures. | DoD policy + NDAA | 50% of all DoD software development uses the DevSecOps model. Average software delivery cycle reduced from 18+ months to under 6 months. |
| **5. Mandate Competitive Prototyping** | For all new programs above $5 billion in total acquisition cost, require at least two competitive prototypes through TMRR before Milestone B downselect. | NDAA provision | At least 3 new major programs initiated with competitive prototyping. |
| **6. Mandate Government Data Rights** | Require government-purpose data rights for all technical data produced under contracts above $10 million. Mandate modular open systems architecture (MOSA) for all new major programs. | Federal legislation | All new Milestone B programs include MOSA requirements. First competitive sustainment contracts awarded using government-owned data. |
| **7. Elevate CAPE to Senate-Confirmed Position** | Elevate the CAPE director to a Senate-confirmed position with statutory authority to reject program budgets that rely on estimates more than 15% below the independent cost estimate. | Federal legislation | CAPE director confirmed; first budget rejection exercised. |
| **8. Create Defense Acquisition Career Track** | Establish a competitive pay scale for acquisition professionals, with salaries benchmarked to the 75th percentile of comparable industry positions. | Federal legislation + DoD request | Acquisition workforce retention improves by 25%. Hiring targets for software engineers and cost analysts met. |

**Goal of Phase 2**: Make the big, structural changes to the budget and acquisition systems that have been debated for decades but never implemented. The "BRAC for Weapons" vote is the centerpiece -- if it succeeds, it demonstrates that the Iron Triangle can be broken.

### Phase 2 Political Strategy

Building a legislative coalition requires assembling an unusual, cross-partisan alliance:

- **Fiscal conservatives**: Attracted by cost controls, audit requirements, fixed-price contracts, and the prospect of reducing the $700+ billion in cumulative cost growth across the major program portfolio.
- **Progressives**: Attracted by anti-corruption measures (revolving door restrictions, lobbying transparency), reduced industry influence over procurement decisions, and the potential to free up resources for domestic priorities.
- **Technology-sector allies**: Companies like SpaceX, Anduril, Palantir, and Shield AI -- and the venture capital firms that back them -- benefit from increased competition, lower barriers to entry, and the "valley of death" fund. These firms bring significant lobbying resources of their own.
- **Centrist good-government advocates**: Attracted by evidence-based reforms, competition, efficiency improvements, and the recommendation of nonpartisan bodies like the GAO and the PPBE Reform Commission.

The messaging frame is "strong defense through smart spending" -- reforms are presented as strengthening national security by ensuring defense dollars are spent effectively, not as cutting defense. The "BRAC for Weapons" commission should include credible national security leaders from both parties to give its recommendations bipartisan legitimacy.

### Phase 2 Dependencies

- The "BRAC for Weapons" vote (Action 1) depends on the commission charter and analysis from Phase 1 (Action 5).
- The fixed-price mandate (Action 3) builds on the pilot results from Phase 1 (Action 3).
- The software pathway scaling (Action 4) depends on early successes in Kessel Run, Army Software Factory, and similar pathways demonstrated during Phase 1.
- The CAPE elevation (Action 7) strengthens the independent cost estimation capacity that underpins the fixed-price mandate and the "BRAC for Weapons" analysis.
- The acquisition career track (Action 8) addresses the workforce capacity needed to implement all other reforms -- without competent, well-compensated government acquisition professionals, no structural reform can succeed.

## Phase 3: Achieving a Modern Acquisition System (Years 6-10)

This phase focuses on institutionalizing the reforms and realizing the vision of a faster, more competitive, and more accountable system.

| Priority | Action | Mechanism | Key Performance Indicator (KPI) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. A More Dynamic Industrial Base** | The "Valley of Death" fund, competitive prototyping mandates, and data rights reforms lead to a measurable increase in the number of non-traditional companies winning DoD contracts. | Procurement actions | The share of DoD contract dollars going to companies outside the "Big Five" increases by 25%. At least 5 new mid-tier defense firms competing for major program work. |
| **2. Improved Program Performance** | The combination of empowered PMs, fixed-price contracts, independent cost estimates, and biennial budgeting reduces cost and schedule overruns. | System-wide outcome | Average cost growth on major programs reduced from ~30-40% to under 15%. Average schedule delay reduced from 2.5 years to under 1 year. Nunn-McCurdy breaches decline by 50%. |
| **3. A Strategy-Driven Budget** | The biennial budget process and the "BRAC for Weapons" mechanism allow the DoD to more effectively align its budget with the National Defense Strategy. | System-wide outcome | Savings from terminated legacy programs are successfully reinvested into higher-priority modernization efforts (AI, autonomous systems, hypersonics, cyber). |
| **4. Restored Competition** | Competitive procurement rates increase significantly from the current ~50% baseline. | Acquisition policy | Competitive contract rate reaches 65%+. Sole-source share reduced from ~37% to ~25%. Average number of qualified bidders per major competition increases from 1.5 to 2.5+. |
| **5. Professionalized Workforce** | The acquisition workforce is adequately staffed, competitively compensated, and capable of matching contractor expertise. | Workforce metrics | Acquisition workforce expanded by 20%. Retention rates for senior acquisition professionals improve by 30%. |

**Goal of Phase 3**: The U.S. has a budget and procurement system that is a strategic asset, not a liability -- enabling the nation to out-innovate and out-produce its adversaries while spending defense dollars efficiently and accountably.

### Phase 3 Sustainability

The key to Phase 3 sustainability is building institutional capacity that becomes self-reinforcing and resistant to erosion by future lobbying pressure:

- **A professionalized, well-compensated acquisition workforce** creates an institutional constituency for good procurement practices that can push back against contractor pressure.
- **Independent cost estimation authority** (CAPE as a Senate-confirmed position) is difficult for future administrations to quietly weaken.
- **Government-owned data rights and MOSA mandates** reduce contractor lock-in structurally, making competition the default rather than requiring affirmative action for each program.
- **The public performance dashboard** creates sustained transparency that enables media and watchdog scrutiny, even without active political attention.
- **Competitive prototyping mandates** create a structural expectation of competition that becomes the norm rather than the exception.

These reforms are designed to be "sticky" -- once established, reversing them requires visible action that would appear to favor waste and corruption, making rollback politically costly.

## Risk Assessment

| Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Industry mobilizes effective opposition to Phase 2 legislation | High | High | Build broad coalition; frame as "strong defense" not "cut defense"; mobilize technology-sector allies |
| New administration reverses executive actions from Phase 1 | Medium | Medium | Prioritize codifying key reforms in legislation (NDAA) rather than relying solely on directives |
| "BRAC for Weapons" commission is captured by industry | Medium | High | Strict conflict-of-interest screening; require public disclosure of all commission member financial ties |
| Fixed-price pilot programs experience contractor failures | Medium | Low | Accept as evidence the reform is working (risk transfer to contractors); ensure bridge plans for critical programs |
| Congress blocks biennial budgeting to preserve annual control | High | Medium | Offer enhanced oversight authority in off years as compensation; build support from Armed Services chairs |
| Acquisition workforce reforms undermined by pay caps | Medium | High | Seek direct hire authority outside normal GS scale; use Title 10 special pay authorities |
| International crisis shifts attention away from reform | Medium | Medium | Frame reform as essential to crisis readiness; use wartime procurement urgency to advance streamlining |

## Sequencing Rationale

The three-phase approach reflects the political reality that comprehensive reform requires building incrementally:

1. **Phase 1 (transparency and quick wins)** creates the evidence base and public awareness needed to support Phase 2 legislation. Transparency measures are difficult for opponents to resist publicly.
2. **Phase 2 (structural legislation)** creates the legal framework and institutional authority needed for Phase 3 systemic changes. The "BRAC for Weapons" vote is the critical test -- if it passes, it demonstrates that the Iron Triangle can be broken.
3. **Phase 3 (systemic transformation)** implements deep structural changes that require the institutional capacity and legal authorities built in Phases 1 and 2.

Each phase is designed to be partially irreversible -- transparency measures, independent cost estimation authority, and competitive prototyping mandates, once established, are difficult for opponents to dismantle without appearing to favor waste and corruption.
