# Budget and Procurement: What You Can Do

While the defense budget and acquisition system are notoriously complex, informed citizens can play a crucial role in demanding accountability and supporting reform. Because the military-industrial complex operates through bipartisan institutional capture, effective advocacy must be nonpartisan and focused on transparency, accountability, and good governance rather than on partisan spending debates.

---

## 1. Educate Yourself: Follow the Money

### Understand the Basics

- Learn the difference between budget "authorization" (which sets policy through the annual NDAA) and "appropriation" (which provides the actual money through the defense spending bill). Both must pass for a program to be funded.
- Understand the major spending categories: Operations & Maintenance (O&M), Military Personnel (MILPERS), Procurement, and Research, Development, Test & Evaluation (RDT&E). The allocation among these categories reflects the readiness vs. modernization tradeoff.
- Familiarize yourself with the PPBE process (Planning, Programming, Budgeting, and Execution) -- the mechanism by which the DoD translates strategy into budget requests.
- Learn the acquisition milestone process: Milestone A (technology maturation), Milestone B (formal development start), Milestone C (production). These are the key decision points where programs either proceed or should be stopped.

### Track Major Programs

- Read the GAO's annual *Weapon Systems Annual Assessment* -- it is the most authoritative public accounting of how defense programs are performing on cost and schedule. It is available free at gao.gov.
- Track specific high-profile programs and their performance: the F-35 Joint Strike Fighter ($1.7 trillion lifecycle), the Sentinel ICBM (critical Nunn-McCurdy breach), the Ford-class carriers (chronic technology problems), and the Columbia-class submarines (rising costs).
- Use USAspending.gov to look up specific defense contracts -- who got the money, how much, and whether the contract was competitive or sole-source.
- Read the DOT&E annual report for blunt assessments of whether new weapons actually work.

### Know Your District's Interests

- Use FPDS.gov (Federal Procurement Data System) to find out which defense contractors are major employers in your state or congressional district.
- Check OpenSecrets.org for your representatives' defense industry campaign contributions -- how much they receive and from which contractor PACs.
- Identify whether your member of Congress sits on the Armed Services or Appropriations committees, which control defense spending decisions.
- Research whether your district benefits from "congressional adds" -- unrequested funding that Congress adds to the defense budget for programs in specific districts.

## 2. Demand Accountability from Congress

Congress holds the "power of the purse" and is the most important lever for change. Members of Congress respond to constituent pressure, especially when it is specific, informed, and sustained.

### Ask Tough Questions

- **About cost overruns**: "The F-35 program is now $200+ billion over its original budget and 12 years behind schedule on full-rate production. The Sentinel ICBM triggered a critical Nunn-McCurdy breach. What are you doing to hold the contractor and the DoD accountable for this performance?"
- **About sole-source contracting**: "Approximately 37% of DoD contract dollars are awarded without competition. What are you doing to increase competition and reduce sole-source contracting that drives up costs?"
- **About congressional adds**: "Congress added $24 billion in unrequested spending to the FY2024 defense budget. How much of that was directed to your district, and which contractors benefited?"
- **About continuing resolutions**: "The DoD has operated under continuing resolutions for most of the past 15 years, wasting billions in inefficiency. What are you doing to pass appropriations on time?"

### Challenge the "Jobs" Argument

When a politician defends a failing or unnecessary program by saying it "creates jobs," push back:

- "Is this the most cost-effective way to create jobs? Studies show that spending on infrastructure, education, or clean energy creates more jobs per dollar than defense spending."
- "The Army has said it does not need additional Abrams tanks, yet Congress keeps funding them. Is this about national security or about protecting jobs in a specific district?"
- "Could the savings from cutting an underperforming program be reinvested in a more effective defense program that also creates jobs?"

### Support Specific Reforms

Voice your support for specific reform proposals and tell your representatives you support them making tough, strategic choices:

- Support a "BRAC for Weapons" commission that would allow the DoD to cut wasteful programs through an up-or-down congressional vote.
- Support biennial budgeting for defense to provide more stable funding and reduce the impact of continuing resolutions.
- Support fixed-price contracts for development programs to shift cost risk to contractors.
- Support competitive prototyping requirements for major programs.
- Support strengthening the independence of CAPE, DOT&E, and the GAO.

## 3. Support Oversight and Watchdog Organizations

The defense industry spends ~$140 million annually on lobbying and ~$50 million per election cycle on campaign contributions. Reform-oriented watchdog organizations operate on a fraction of these resources. Citizen support is essential to sustaining their work.

### Key Organizations to Support

| Organization | Focus | How to Get Involved |
|---|---|---|
| **Project on Government Oversight (POGO)** | Defense oversight, revolving door, procurement waste, contractor accountability | Donate; share their research; sign up for email alerts; use their revolving door database |
| **Taxpayers for Common Sense** | Federal spending accountability including defense earmarks | Join taxpayer advocacy campaigns; monitor your state's defense earmarks via their annual reports |
| **Citizens Against Government Waste** | Wasteful government spending | Monitor their annual "Pig Book" identifying defense pork barrel spending |
| **National Priorities Project** | Federal budget transparency and analysis | Use their budget visualization tools to educate your community about spending tradeoffs |
| **Quincy Institute for Responsible Statecraft** | Responsible defense policy and spending reform | Attend events; amplify restraint-oriented analysis; support independent research |
| **Government Accountability Office (GAO)** | Congressional watchdog | Advocate for full GAO funding; share and amplify GAO findings in communications with elected officials |

### Amplify Independent Analysis

- Share GAO reports, CBO analyses, and POGO investigations on social media and in letters to the editor. These organizations produce the objective, data-driven analysis needed to counter the defense industry's lobbying narrative.
- When a defense program is in the news, look up the GAO's assessment and share the actual cost and schedule performance data -- not just the contractor's press release.

## 4. Engage in the Budget Process

The annual defense budget process offers specific opportunities for citizen engagement:

- **February-March**: The President releases the budget request. Review the DoD Comptroller's overview and CBO's analysis of the request.
- **April-June**: Armed Services Committees mark up the NDAA. Watch hearings (available via webcast) and monitor how your representatives vote on amendments related to program oversight, competition, and reform.
- **June-September**: Appropriations Committees mark up the defense spending bill. Track congressional adds that benefit specific districts.
- **October-December**: Conference negotiations between House and Senate produce the final NDAA and appropriations bill. Monitor the conference report for last-minute additions.

Submit public comments during the budget process. Attend Armed Services Committee hearings during recess periods. Contact your representatives' offices with specific questions about defense spending in your district.

## 5. Build Coalitions

Defense procurement reform is one of the rare policy areas where left-right coalitions are possible and effective.

### Potential Coalition Partners

- **Fiscal conservatives** concerned about government waste and cost overruns
- **Progressives** concerned about corporate influence, the revolving door, and budget priorities
- **Good-government centrists** focused on transparency, accountability, and evidence-based policy
- **Technology-sector advocates** who want lower barriers for innovative companies to compete for defense contracts
- **Veterans' organizations** whose members have personal experience with procurement dysfunction -- equipment that arrived late, was unreliable, or was ill-suited to their actual needs
- **Small business organizations** representing mid-tier defense suppliers frustrated by prime contractor dominance and the complexity of the procurement process
- **Local economic development groups** interested in diversifying defense-dependent communities

### Effective Messaging

- **Be specific**: General complaints about "defense spending" are less effective than specific concerns about named programs, contracts, or cost overruns. Reference GAO report numbers, specific dollar figures, and specific programs.
- **Be nonpartisan**: Frame reform as patriotic and pro-national-security, not as anti-military. The goal is a stronger, more effective military -- not a weaker one.
- **Use the warfighter frame**: "Our troops deserve equipment that works, is delivered on time, and does not waste their fellow citizens' tax dollars."
- **Be persistent**: The defense lobby operates continuously, year-round, with hundreds of millions of dollars. Citizen advocacy must be sustained, not episodic.

## 6. Electoral Actions

### Evaluate Candidates

When evaluating candidates for Congress, consider:

- Do they accept defense industry PAC contributions? If so, how much, and from which contractors?
- Do they sit on the Armed Services or Appropriations committees? If so, have they challenged specific programs or simply voted for higher spending?
- Have they supported procurement reform measures such as competitive prototyping, fixed-price contracts, or revolving door restrictions?
- Do they distinguish between "strong defense" and "more defense spending"?
- Are they willing to challenge specific weapon programs that are over budget or underperforming, even if those programs have a presence in their district?

### Support Campaign Finance Reform

The defense industry's campaign contributions are a key mechanism for maintaining the Iron Triangle. Support:

- Public financing of congressional elections to reduce dependence on defense industry contributions
- Stronger disclosure requirements for PAC contributions and bundled donations
- Restrictions on fundraising by members of committees with jurisdiction over defense spending during NDAA and appropriations markup periods
