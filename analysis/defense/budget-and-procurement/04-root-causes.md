# Budget and Procurement: Root Causes

## Systemic Analysis

The chronic problems of the defense budget and procurement system -- particularly the persistent cost overruns, schedule delays, and resistance to reform -- are not the result of isolated failures or incompetent individuals. They are the product of deep, systemic flaws in the structure and incentives of the process itself. Each actor within the system -- contractors, Pentagon officials, members of Congress, and the acquisition bureaucracy -- behaves rationally within the incentive structure they face, yet the collective outcome is a system that systematically produces waste, delay, and misallocation. Understanding these root causes is essential to designing reforms that can actually change outcomes rather than merely rearranging the bureaucracy.

## Root Cause 1: The "Iron Triangle"

The single most powerful driver of dysfunction is the "Iron Triangle": a mutually beneficial, self-reinforcing relationship between the three key players in the system.

1. **The Defense Industry (Contractors)**: Wants to win large, stable, profitable contracts. They achieve this by lobbying Congress, making campaign contributions, hiring former Pentagon officials, and strategically distributing subcontracts across congressional districts to create political constituencies for their programs.
2. **Congress**: Wants to bring jobs and federal money to their districts and states to help get re-elected. Members of the Armed Services and Appropriations committees receive 3-5 times more defense industry campaign contributions than non-committee members. They fight to fund weapon systems built in their districts, regardless of strategic necessity.
3. **The Department of Defense (Military Services & Bureaucracy)**: Wants larger budgets and new, high-tech weapon systems to fulfill their mission, maintain their service's relevance in inter-service competition, and advance the careers of program managers and senior leaders. They achieve this by advocating for new programs, often with overly optimistic cost and schedule estimates.

**The Dynamic**: The military asks for a new weapon. A contractor promises to build it and create jobs in key congressional districts -- a practice known as "political engineering." Lockheed Martin's distribution of F-35 subcontracts to over 1,700 suppliers in 45+ states is the canonical example. Members of Congress then fight to fund the program to secure those jobs. All three sides get what they want. The taxpayer, who is not part of this triangle, bears the cost. This dynamic makes it almost impossible to cancel a major weapon system once it has started, regardless of its performance. The M1 Abrams tank production line in Lima, Ohio has been kept running by congressional action even when the Army explicitly said it did not need additional tanks.

## Root Cause 2: The Flawed Requirements Process

Programs often fail because they are set up for failure from the very beginning through unrealistic requirements.

- **"Requirements Creep"**: The military services often begin with a reasonable set of requirements for a new weapon, but over the long development process, they continuously add new "must-have" capabilities. Each addition increases complexity, cost, and schedule risk. The Joint Requirements Oversight Council (JROC) is supposed to enforce discipline, but in practice it tends to validate service requests rather than challenge them. The F-35's attempt to satisfy Air Force, Navy, and Marine Corps requirements in a single airframe is a textbook case of requirements overreach.
- **"Technological Optimism"**: Planners consistently overestimate the maturity of new technologies and underestimate the difficulty of integrating them into complex weapon systems. They plan for the best-case scenario, which almost never occurs. The Ford-class carrier's simultaneous adoption of four new technologies (EMALS catapult, Advanced Arresting Gear, dual-band radar, and new nuclear reactor) -- all unproven at scale -- drove years of delays and billions in overruns.
- **The Disconnect from Cost Reality**: The requirements process (JCIDS) operates largely independently from the budgeting process (PPBE). Requirements are validated based on operational need, with limited consideration of whether the resulting system is affordable or producible. The "Will to Cost" initiative attempted to inject cost discipline into requirements, but cultural resistance within the services has limited its effectiveness.
- **Concurrency**: To accelerate fielding, programs often begin low-rate initial production (LRIP) before development and testing are complete. When testing reveals problems -- as it invariably does -- hundreds of already-produced units must be retrofitted at enormous cost. The F-35 program produced over 800 aircraft during its "low-rate" production phase, requiring expensive retrofit programs for deficiencies discovered during testing.

## Root Cause 3: The "Conspiracy of Optimism" in Cost Estimation

The budgeting process creates systematic incentives to underestimate costs and overestimate performance.

- **The Problem**: To get a new program approved and funded, both the military service and the contractor have a powerful incentive to be overly optimistic in their initial cost and schedule estimates. A realistic, higher estimate might lead Congress or the Office of the Secretary of Defense to cancel the program before it starts, or to choose a competing program instead.
- **"Buy-In" Pricing**: Contractors submit artificially low bids to win development contracts, knowing that once they are the sole source for production, they can recover costs through contract modifications, engineering change proposals, and the government's inability to credibly threaten cancellation. The government's cost estimation capabilities are consistently inferior to industry's, enabling this strategy.
- **The Impact**: Programs are "born broken," with unrealistic baselines that they are guaranteed to exceed. The initial low-ball estimate gets the program started, and once billions are spent, the "sunk cost fallacy" makes it politically impossible to cancel. The Sentinel ICBM's 53% cost growth from its original estimate to its Nunn-McCurdy breach is a recent example of this pattern.
- **Independent Cost Estimation**: The Cost Assessment and Program Evaluation (CAPE) office provides independent cost estimates, and the CBO provides its own projections. Both consistently produce higher, more accurate estimates than the DoD's own figures. But the independent estimates are often overridden or ignored in favor of the more optimistic program office numbers when budget decisions are made.

## Root Cause 4: The Broken Budgeting Model

The fundamental processes for funding programs are mismatched with the realities of weapon system development.

### Annual Budgeting vs. Multi-Decade Programs

- **The Problem**: The DoD budgets in one-year increments (the annual appropriations process), but it acquires weapon systems over multi-decade timelines. A Columbia-class submarine takes over 10 years to build; the F-35 program has been in development and production for over 20 years.
- **The Impact**: Program managers have no guarantee what their budget will be next year. This uncertainty forces contractors to maintain excess capacity "just in case," makes long-term workforce planning impossible, and prevents the kind of bulk purchasing that would reduce unit costs. Multi-year procurement authority exists but is used sparingly and requires specific congressional approval.

### Continuing Resolutions

- **The Problem**: Congress has failed to pass defense appropriations on time in all but one year since FY2010, forcing the DoD to operate under continuing resolutions that freeze spending at prior-year levels and prohibit new program starts.
- **The Impact**: CRs are estimated to cost the DoD $5-10 billion annually in inefficiency. Programs that are ready to enter a new phase cannot proceed. Contracts that should be signed in October are delayed until February or March, compressing the execution timeline and wasting months of productive work.

### The Color of Money

- **The Problem**: Congressional appropriations law requires that defense funds be spent within specific categories (RDT&E, Procurement, O&M, MILPERS, MILCON), each with its own rules and restrictions. Transferring money between categories requires congressional approval, even within the same program.
- **The Impact**: A program manager who needs to shift $10 million from R&D to procurement within the same weapon system faces months of paperwork and committee approval. This rigidity is particularly harmful for software-intensive programs where the distinction between "development" and "procurement" is artificial.

## Root Cause 5: Risk Aversion in the Bureaucracy

- **The Problem**: For a government program manager, there is no reward for taking a smart risk that pays off, but there is immense career-ending punishment for a failure. A program manager who delivers a system on time and under budget receives the same promotion consideration as one who played it safe and added two years of unnecessary studies.
- **The "1,000 No's"**: The acquisition bureaucracy contains dozens of review layers -- legal, contracting, engineering, testing, cost, environmental, security -- where any one office can say "no" and delay a program, but no single office has the authority to say "yes" and push a program forward. The result is a veto-ocracy where the path of least career risk is to demand more analysis, add more oversight, and delay decisions.
- **The Impact**: This creates a culture of extreme risk aversion. Program managers avoid making decisions, demand endless studies, and add layers of review to cover themselves. A 2019 study by the Defense Business Board found that the average time to complete a Major Defense Acquisition Program had grown from 7 years in the 1970s to over 15 years, with bureaucratic process rather than technical complexity driving much of the increase.
- **The Accountability Gap**: Program managers typically rotate through assignments every 2-3 years, meaning the person who made the key decisions on a program is rarely still in the role when the consequences of those decisions become apparent. This diffusion of accountability means that no individual bears the cost of bad decisions, which further incentivizes conservative, process-heavy management.

## Root Cause 6: Monopsony and Market Failure

The defense market is a government monopsony (single buyer) purchasing from an oligopoly (few sellers), which creates a fundamentally dysfunctional market structure.

- **No competitive price discovery**: In a normal market, competition among multiple buyers and sellers establishes efficient prices. In defense, the government is the only buyer for most products, and after the post-Cold War consolidation, there are often only one or two sellers. This bilateral monopoly eliminates normal price discipline.
- **Lock-in**: Once a firm wins a development contract, it becomes the sole source for production, upgrades, and sustainment for decades. The government's switching costs are astronomical -- it cannot credibly threaten to move the F-35 to another contractor, giving Lockheed Martin enormous pricing leverage.
- **Barriers to entry**: The combination of complex regulations (over 180,000 pages), security clearance requirements, long payment cycles (60-90+ days), and the need for specialized facilities makes it extremely difficult for new companies to enter the defense market. The "valley of death" -- the gap between a successful technology demonstration and a funded program of record -- kills promising innovations from small firms that cannot sustain operations while waiting for DoD bureaucracy.

## Root Cause 7: Information Asymmetry

Defense contractors possess far more information about the true costs, capabilities, and risks of their products than the government buyers who fund them. This asymmetry pervades every stage of the acquisition process.

- **Technical complexity**: Modern weapons systems involve millions of components and cutting-edge technologies. The F-35, for example, contains over 8 million lines of software code and 300,000+ individual parts. The DoD acquisition workforce has shrunk by approximately 50% since the early 1990s, while the complexity of the systems being acquired has increased dramatically. The government often cannot independently verify whether a contractor's proposed solution is technically feasible or whether its cost estimate is reasonable.
- **Proprietary data**: Contractors control detailed cost and engineering data for their systems, claiming trade secret protections under the FAR and DFARS. This makes independent cost estimation and performance verification extremely difficult. When the government does not own the technical data for a system, it cannot credibly threaten to award sustainment or upgrade contracts to a competitor.
- **Classification as a shield**: The classification of defense programs limits the information available to Congress, the media, and the public. While classification serves legitimate security purposes, it is routinely used to shield programs from scrutiny of costs and performance failures. Members of Congress who lack security clearances cannot review classified program details, and even cleared members often lack the technical expertise to evaluate what they are shown.
- **Contractor-funded research**: Defense firms fund studies at think tanks, universities, and FFRDCs that often validate the need for the firm's products. This creates an echo chamber in which the "expert" analysis available to policymakers is shaped by contractor interests, reinforcing the information advantage contractors hold over both the government and the public.

## Root Cause 8: Perverse Incentives in Contract Types

The dominant contracting mechanisms in defense procurement create incentives that reward cost growth rather than efficiency.

- **Cost-plus contracts**: Under cost-plus-fee contracts, the contractor is reimbursed for all allowable costs plus a negotiated fee (typically 8-15% of costs). While the fee percentage may be fixed, the dollar value of the fee grows as costs rise. A contractor whose program costs $5 billion earns a larger absolute profit than one whose program costs $3 billion, even at the same fee rate.
- **Undefinitized Contract Actions (UCAs)**: The DoD frequently begins work before contract terms are finalized, through "undefinitized" contracts that give contractors broad latitude on costs. GAO has repeatedly flagged the overuse of UCAs as a driver of cost growth, finding that programs with UCAs experience higher cost growth than those with definitized contracts.
- **Profit on overruns**: Even on fixed-price contracts, cost overruns are sometimes socialized back to the government through "equitable adjustment" claims, contract modifications, or the implicit threat that the contractor will exit the market if forced to absorb all losses.

## Causal Chain of a Failing Program

```text
Military service identifies a need for a new weapon
    |
Planners develop overly ambitious requirements ("requirements creep")
    |
JCIDS validates requirements with minimal cost discipline
    |
Contractor and DoD create an optimistic, low-ball cost estimate to get the program funded
    |
The "Iron Triangle" ensures Congress funds the program, with subcontracts distributed across key districts
    |
During development, technical challenges emerge, and new requirements are added
    |
Concurrency: production begins before testing is complete
    |
Costs and schedules begin to slip
    |
Risk-averse bureaucracy adds layers of review, causing further delays
    |
Independent cost estimates (CAPE, CBO) reveal true costs, but are overridden or ignored
    |
Nunn-McCurdy breach triggered, but program is certified as "essential to national security"
    |
The program is now years late and billions over budget, but it is "too big to fail" due to sunk costs, jobs in 40+ states, and the Iron Triangle
    |
Result: A late, over-budget weapon system is delivered to the warfighter, often with reduced capabilities
```

## Interaction Effects

These root causes do not operate in isolation. They interact in ways that make the system highly resistant to reform:

- The **Iron Triangle** ensures that even poorly performing programs survive, while the **conspiracy of optimism** ensures they are born with unrealistic expectations.
- **Requirements creep** drives costs up, while **risk aversion** drives schedules out, and the **annual budgeting cycle** makes it impossible to plan efficiently for either.
- **Information asymmetry** enables contractors to exploit **cost-plus contracting**, while **monopsony/oligopoly** market structure eliminates the competitive pressure that would otherwise discipline prices.
- **Continuing resolutions** compound the **budget cycle mismatch**, making an already dysfunctional process even more unpredictable.

The result is a self-reinforcing system in which each pathology supports and enables the others, creating a network of perverse incentives that is far more durable than any individual reform effort.
