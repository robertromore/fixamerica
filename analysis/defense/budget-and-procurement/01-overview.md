# Budget and Procurement: Overview

## Overview

The U.S. defense budget and the system for procuring weapon systems are the twin pillars that translate national strategy into military capability. With a total national defense budget exceeding $886 billion in FY2025 -- encompassing the Department of Defense, Department of Energy nuclear programs, and other defense-related activities -- this is the largest discretionary component of the federal budget by a wide margin, consuming roughly half of all annual discretionary spending. The process of allocating these vast resources is a complex interplay between the White House (via the Office of Management and Budget), the Department of Defense (through its Planning, Programming, Budgeting, and Execution process), and Congress (through its authorization and appropriations committees). This process is defined by a central tension: balancing investments in the readiness of the current force against the need to fund the development and procurement of next-generation systems (modernization).

The defense acquisition system, the mechanism for spending a large portion of this budget, is widely seen as broken. Major weapon programs are infamous for being years behind schedule and billions over budget. The F-35 Joint Strike Fighter -- at $1.7 trillion in lifecycle costs, the most expensive weapon program in human history -- is the most prominent example, but the pattern extends to the Ford-class aircraft carriers ($13.3 billion for CVN-78, 27% over the original estimate), the Sentinel ICBM replacement (which triggered a critical Nunn-McCurdy breach in 2024 with costs exceeding $130 billion), and the Columbia-class submarine program (now projected at $132+ billion for 12 boats). This is not a new problem; it is a chronic condition rooted in the unique nature of the defense market (a government monopsony buying from an oligopoly of five prime contractors), perverse incentives within the "Iron Triangle" of the DoD, Congress, and industry, and a risk-averse bureaucracy governed by over 180,000 pages of acquisition regulations. Decades of reform efforts -- from the Packard Commission of 1986 to the Section 809 Panel of 2018 to the current Adaptive Acquisition Framework -- have yielded only marginal improvements, and the slow pace of acquisition is now a strategic liability in an era of rapid technological competition with China.

## Scope

This analysis covers the key aspects of the defense budget and procurement systems:

- **The Defense Budget Process**: The Planning, Programming, Budgeting, and Execution (PPBE) process, the role of the National Defense Strategy, the congressional appropriations process, and the impact of continuing resolutions on military readiness and planning.
- **Spending Priorities**: The allocation of funds between personnel, operations and maintenance (readiness), procurement, and R&D (modernization), and how these allocations have shifted over time.
- **The Acquisition System**: The lifecycle of a major weapon system, from requirements generation (JCIDS) through Milestone Decision Authority gates to development, production, and sustainment, including the newer Adaptive Acquisition Framework pathways.
- **Procurement Challenges**: Cost overruns, schedule delays, "requirements creep," the "conspiracy of optimism" in cost estimation, concurrency in development and production, and the "valley of death" for innovative technologies transitioning from prototype to program of record.
- **The Defense Industrial Base**: Market concentration among the "Big Five" prime contractors, the decline of competition, sole-source contracting trends, and the erosion of the small business and mid-tier supplier base.
- **Oversight Mechanisms**: The roles of the Government Accountability Office (GAO), the Congressional Budget Office (CBO), the Cost Assessment and Program Evaluation (CAPE) office, the Director of Operational Test and Evaluation (DOT&E), and the DoD Inspector General.
- **Reform Efforts**: Past and present attempts to make the acquisition system faster, cheaper, and more effective, from the McNamara-era PPBS through Better Buying Power, Middle Tier of Acquisition, and the PPBE Reform Commission.

## Core Tensions and Tradeoffs

- **Readiness vs. Modernization**: Every dollar spent on training, maintenance, and munitions for the current force is a dollar not spent on R&D and procurement for the next generation of weapons. With O&M and personnel consuming over 61% of the budget, the military services face constant pressure to underfund modernization, even as the 2022 National Defense Strategy identifies China as the pacing threat requiring transformational new capabilities.
- **Accountability vs. Speed**: The complex acquisition bureaucracy -- with its DoD 5000-series regulations, milestone reviews, Nunn-McCurdy reporting requirements, and layers of oversight -- is designed to ensure accountability and prevent fraud. But it makes the system incredibly slow, often adding years to program timelines. Efforts to speed up the process, such as Middle Tier of Acquisition authorities, often involve bypassing these controls, creating new risks of waste and mismanagement.
- **Competition vs. Stability**: The DoD needs a stable industrial base capable of surging production in a crisis, which has led to a consolidated market with only five prime contractors receiving the majority of contract dollars. This lack of competition leads to higher prices and less innovation. But breaking up defense monopolies or bringing in new entrants could temporarily disrupt production of critical systems.
- **Requirements vs. Reality**: The military services often set overly ambitious technical requirements for new weapons, leading to "requirements creep" that drives up cost and complexity. The Joint Requirements Oversight Council (JROC) has struggled to impose discipline. This conflicts with the need to field affordable, producible systems quickly -- a tension the "Will to Cost" and "Should Cost" initiatives have tried but largely failed to resolve.
- **Budget Cycle vs. Development Cycle**: The DoD's annual budget cycle is mismatched with the multi-year, often multi-decade, timeline of a major weapon program, creating funding instability. The problem is compounded by the chronic failure of Congress to pass defense appropriations on time, forcing the DoD to operate under continuing resolutions that prohibit new program starts and restrict spending flexibility.

## Key Questions

1. Is the current level of defense spending appropriate for the strategic challenges the nation faces, and is the money being allocated to the right priorities?
2. Why have decades of acquisition reform failed to solve the problem of cost overruns and schedule delays, and what would it take to break this cycle?
3. How can the DoD break out of the "readiness vs. modernization" tradeoff and fund both effectively without simply increasing the topline?
4. What fundamental changes are needed to make the acquisition system faster and more adaptable to software-defined, AI-enabled, and autonomous systems that do not fit the traditional hardware-centric acquisition model?
5. How can meaningful competition be restored in a defense market that has consolidated to the point where sole-source contracting accounts for over a third of all contract dollars?

## The Scale of the Problem

The dysfunction of the defense budget and procurement system is not an abstract policy concern -- it has concrete, measurable consequences:

- **Cost to taxpayers**: The GAO's portfolio of 83+ major defense programs has experienced over $700 billion in cumulative cost growth from initial estimates. This is money that could have funded additional military capability, or been returned to taxpayers, or invested in other national priorities.
- **Delayed capability for warfighters**: When programs run years behind schedule, troops in the field do not receive the equipment they need when they need it. The F-35's 12-year delay to full-rate production meant that the Air Force, Navy, and Marines operated with aging fourth-generation fighters longer than planned, at higher operating cost and lower capability.
- **Strategic risk**: The slow pace of U.S. acquisition is increasingly a liability in the competition with China. The People's Liberation Army is fielding new ships, aircraft, and missiles at a pace that the U.S. acquisition system cannot match. If it takes the United States 15-20 years to develop and field a major weapon system, and China can do it in 5-7 years, the U.S. advantage in military technology will erode regardless of how much money is spent.
- **Opportunity cost**: Every dollar spent on cost overruns for underperforming legacy programs is a dollar not invested in the technologies that will define future military advantage -- artificial intelligence, autonomous systems, directed energy, hypersonic weapons, and space capabilities.
- **Industrial base fragility**: The consolidation of the defense industry to five prime contractors has created single points of failure. If a sole-source contractor experiences production problems (as has occurred with the Sentinel ICBM and the Columbia-class submarine), there is no alternative domestic supplier.

## Vision of Success

A successful budget and procurement system would:

- **Be Strategy-Driven**: The budget would be a clear reflection of the priorities laid out in the National Defense Strategy, with resources shifting decisively toward the capabilities needed for great power competition rather than being locked into legacy programs by political inertia.
- **Be Agile and Fast**: The acquisition system would deliver cutting-edge technology to the warfighter at the "speed of relevance," with software-intensive systems updated in months, not decades, and hardware prototypes fielded in years, not generations.
- **Deliver on Time and on Budget**: Major programs would consistently meet their cost and schedule targets, with realistic baselines, independent cost estimates, and contractors bearing meaningful financial risk for overruns.
- **Be Competitive and Innovative**: The system would foster a dynamic industrial base with genuine competition for major programs, manageable barriers for non-traditional companies and startups, and a "valley of death" that has been bridged rather than merely acknowledged.
- **Be Resilient to Budgetary Disruption**: Multi-year budgeting or other mechanisms would insulate long-term programs from the annual budget cycle and the chronic dysfunction of the congressional appropriations process.
