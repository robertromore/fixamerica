# Budget and Procurement: Opposition to Reform

Decades of attempts to reform the defense budget and acquisition system have failed or produced only marginal gains. This is because any meaningful reform threatens the powerful, entrenched interests of the "Iron Triangle" that benefit from the status quo. Unlike many policy areas where opposition is primarily ideological, resistance to budget and procurement reform is driven primarily by material interests -- profits, jobs, campaign contributions, and career opportunities -- making it both intense and bipartisan.

---

## Opposition from the Defense Industry

### Who They Are

The Big Five prime defense contractors -- Lockheed Martin, RTX (Raytheon), Northrop Grumman, Boeing Defense, and General Dynamics -- are the most powerful opponents of meaningful procurement reform. They are joined by major Tier 1 firms such as L3Harris, Leidos, Huntington Ingalls Industries, and BAE Systems (U.S. operations), as well as the defense trade associations (NDIA, AIA, Professional Services Council) that coordinate industry lobbying.

### Why They Oppose Reform

- **Revenue protection**: The top five primes derive $150-200 billion annually from defense contracts. Reforms that increase competition, tighten cost controls, mandate fixed-price contracts, or reduce sole-source awards directly threaten this revenue.
- **Profit margins**: Defense contractors enjoy profit margins of 8-15% on government contracts, with cost-plus contracts generating higher absolute dollar profits as costs grow. Shifting risk to contractors through fixed-price development contracts -- as the KC-46 tanker program did, forcing Boeing to absorb over $7 billion in losses -- is fiercely resisted.
- **Sole-source positions**: Many major programs (F-35, Sentinel ICBM, B-21 bomber) have only a single qualified contractor. Reforms mandating competitive prototyping, second-sourcing, or government-owned data rights would erode these monopoly positions.
- **Revolving door**: The ability to hire former Pentagon officials is a critical competitive advantage. Restrictions would reduce contractors' insider access and influence.

### How They Oppose Reform

- **Direct lobbying**: ~$140 million annually, employing over 800 registered lobbyists, approximately 500 of whom are former government officials.
- **Campaign contributions**: ~$50 million per election cycle, strategically targeted to Armed Services and Appropriations committee members of both parties.
- **Political engineering**: Strategic distribution of subcontracts across congressional districts to create job-based constituencies. The F-35 supply chain in 45+ states makes it politically untouchable.
- **Think tank funding**: Sponsorship of defense policy research organizations (CSIS, CNAS, Hudson Institute, AEI) that produce analysis generally supportive of higher defense spending and skeptical of procurement restrictions.
- **Trade association advocacy**: NDIA's annual *Vital Signs* report frames the defense industrial base as "fragile" and requiring sustained high spending.
- **Legal challenges**: Bid protest filings at the GAO and Court of Federal Claims to delay unfavorable procurement decisions and deter reform-minded contracting officers.
- **"Astroturf" mobilization**: Organizing defense workers to contact their members of Congress during budget debates, creating the appearance of grassroots opposition to program cuts.

### Key Arguments from Industry

| Argument | Industry's Claim | Counterpoint |
|---|---|---|
| **"Reform is Too Risky"** | The current system, while slow and expensive, ultimately produces the world's most advanced weapon systems. Radical reforms could jeopardize national security. | The current system is the real risk. Its slow pace means technology is often obsolete by the time it is fielded. Its high cost means the DoD cannot afford sufficient quantities. China is acquiring weapons faster and cheaper. |
| **"We Need Budget Stability"** | We can't invest in factories and skilled workers without the stability of large, multi-year, cost-plus contracts. Competition and shorter contracts create uncertainty. | The current "stability" is corporate welfare that insulates contractors from accountability. The KC-46's fixed-price contract forced Boeing to absorb overruns -- proving that contractors can bear risk when required. |
| **"Fixed-Price Contracts Don't Work for R&D"** | Genuinely novel technology development is too uncertain for fixed-price contracting. Forcing fixed-price will cause contractors to pad bids or refuse to bid. | Fixed-price incentive contracts share risk between government and contractor. Commercial aerospace (SpaceX, Airbus) develops complex technologies on fixed-price terms. The issue is not technical uncertainty but contractor preference for cost-plus profits. |
| **"We Must Follow the Process"** | The acquisition regulations exist to ensure fairness, accountability, and stewardship of taxpayer dollars. Bypassing them leads to fraud and waste. | The current process creates its own, much larger waste through massive cost overruns and delays. The MRAP rapid procurement during Iraq demonstrated that streamlined processes can deliver results without fraud. |
| **"Cutting Programs Destroys the Industrial Base"** | Terminating a major program will destroy irreplaceable skills and facilities. Once lost, this capacity cannot be rebuilt. | This argument is routinely deployed to protect any threatened program, regardless of its strategic value. It conflates the health of the industrial base with the survival of specific contractor programs. |

## Opposition from Congress

### Armed Services Committee Members

Members of the Senate and House Armed Services Committees are among the most significant opponents of procurement reform because they combine legislative authority with powerful personal incentives to maintain the status quo.

**Financial incentives**: HASC and SASC members receive 3-5 times more defense industry campaign contributions than non-committee members. In the 2024 cycle, average defense PAC contributions to HASC members exceeded $100,000 per member. Members actively seek these committee assignments when their districts host major defense installations or contractor facilities.

**Constituent incentives**: Defense industry jobs in a member's district create powerful pressure to protect related programs regardless of strategic merit. Senators from submarine-building states (Connecticut, Virginia) resist cost reforms for the Columbia-class. Members from states with ICBM bases (Montana, Wyoming, North Dakota) oppose Sentinel cost scrutiny. HASC members from F-35 production districts have consistently opposed efforts to slow procurement despite GAO recommendations.

### The "Jobs" Argument

| Argument | Congress's Claim | Counterpoint |
|---|---|---|
| **"You Can't Cut My Program"** | This program is vital for national security and provides thousands of good-paying jobs in my district. Cutting it would weaken our defense and devastate my local economy. | The DoD has determined this program is no longer needed or is underperforming. Continuing to fund it for political reasons wastes taxpayer money and diverts funds from systems the warfighter actually needs. |
| **"Congressional Adds Strengthen Defense"** | Congress has unique constitutional authority over defense spending and adds programs the DoD overlooked. | The $24 billion in FY2024 congressional adds were concentrated in the districts of Armed Services and Appropriations committee members and correlated with campaign contributions, not strategic gaps. |

### Appropriations Committee Members

Defense appropriations subcommittee members exercise direct control over spending levels and use their positions to direct spending to their states and districts. The earmark process, while reformed, continues to enable targeted spending. The logrolling dynamic -- members trading votes on each other's preferred programs -- creates a bipartisan coalition that sustains overall defense spending levels regardless of strategic merit.

## Opposition from the DoD Bureaucracy

### Risk-Averse Culture

Parts of the DoD acquisition bureaucracy itself resist reform, not out of corruption but out of institutional self-preservation and genuine belief that the existing process, however slow, provides necessary accountability.

| Argument | Bureaucracy's Claim | Counterpoint |
|---|---|---|
| **"Process Equals Accountability"** | Every layer of review exists because of a past failure. Removing reviews invites new failures that will harm warfighters. | The current process fails to prevent cost overruns and schedule delays -- the very problems it was designed to solve. The process provides the illusion of accountability without the substance. |
| **"Reform Always Fails"** | The acquisition system has been reformed dozens of times and it never works. The real problem is insufficient funding, not procurement dysfunction. | This argument serves to discourage reform and maintain the status quo. The Adaptive Acquisition Framework and Middle Tier of Acquisition have shown that streamlined processes can work when given genuine institutional support. |
| **"We Need More Oversight, Not Less"** | The solution to cost overruns is more reviews, more analysis, and more compliance requirements, not fewer. | Additional oversight has diminishing returns and eventually becomes counterproductive. The GAO has found that some programs have more oversight personnel than engineers, without corresponding improvements in outcomes. |

### The Pre-Revolving Door Dynamic

Current Pentagon officials who anticipate future industry employment have subtle but pervasive incentives to resist reforms that would reduce contractor revenue or increase accountability. This is not typically expressed as overt opposition but rather as reluctance to challenge contractors, favorable interpretation of regulations, and slow-rolling of reform implementation.

## Opposition to Budget Cuts

Proposals to reduce the overall level of defense spending, or to reallocate spending away from legacy programs toward new priorities, face additional opposition from a broader coalition.

| Argument | Proponents' Claim | Counterpoint |
|---|---|---|
| **"The World is Too Dangerous"** | In an era of great power competition with China and Russia, and threats from Iran and North Korea, now is the worst possible time to cut defense spending. | The U.S. already spends more on defense than the next 10 countries combined. The problem is not insufficient spending but inefficient allocation. Spending more on underperforming programs does not make the nation safer. |
| **"Cuts Will Harm Readiness"** | Any reduction in the defense budget will inevitably fall on training, maintenance, and munitions, hollowing out the force. | This is a false choice. Cutting wasteful, underperforming weapon systems and reinvesting in readiness would improve military capability. The Army has stated it does not need additional Abrams tanks, yet Congress keeps funding them. |
| **"We Must Maintain the Arsenal of Democracy"** | The defense industrial base is a national strategic asset that must be preserved through sustained spending. | Preserving the industrial base does not require preserving every program. Targeted investment in critical skills, facilities, and supply chains is more efficient than blanket program protection. |

### Defense Hawks

A bipartisan coalition of defense hawks in Congress, foreign policy commentators, and think tanks funded by defense firms argues that the defense budget should increase, not be reformed. Key organizations and their positions:

- **Heritage Foundation**: Publishes the annual *Index of U.S. Military Strength*, which consistently rates U.S. military capability as "marginal" or "weak" relative to threats, arguing for increased spending.
- **American Enterprise Institute (AEI)**: Advocates for higher defense spending and opposes procurement reforms that would constrain industry revenue or shift risk to contractors.
- **Foundation for Defense of Democracies (FDD)**: Promotes an aggressive military posture that implies higher spending requirements.
- **Center for Strategic and International Studies (CSIS)**: While more moderate, its Defense-Industrial Initiatives Group -- funded in part by defense contractors -- generally frames industrial base concerns in ways that support sustained or increased spending.

While these assessments contain legitimate analysis of genuine threats, the funding relationships and institutional incentives create a structural bias toward recommendations that align with defense industry interests. Think tanks that receive defense industry funding face a conflict of interest in assessing the adequacy of defense spending and the performance of funded programs.

### The Revolving Door Dimension

Former senior Pentagon officials who now serve on defense company boards or as consultants frequently appear as "independent" experts in congressional testimony, media commentary, and think tank publications, arguing against reforms that would constrain their current employers. A 2018 POGO investigation found that 90% of retired three- and four-star generals who went to work for defense contractors did not report their employment as required by law, making it difficult for Congress and the public to assess the independence of their advice.

## The Bipartisan Nature of Opposition

Unlike many policy areas, opposition to budget and procurement reform is genuinely bipartisan. Democrats and Republicans on the Armed Services and Appropriations committees both receive defense industry contributions, both represent districts with defense jobs, and both face the same incentive structure. While there are individual reformers in both parties -- senators and representatives who have pushed for competitive procurement, revolving door restrictions, or program cancellations -- the committee structures and campaign finance dynamics ensure that the median member of the relevant committees in both parties is aligned with industry interests.

This bipartisan alignment is one of the most significant obstacles to reform. Procurement reform cannot be framed as a partisan issue and cannot be advanced through single-party action. It requires building unusual coalitions that combine fiscal conservatives concerned about waste with progressives concerned about corporate influence and centrists focused on good governance.
