# Budget and Procurement: Solutions

Reforming the defense budget and procurement system requires a multi-pronged approach that attacks the perverse incentives of the "Iron Triangle," simplifies the bureaucracy, imposes fiscal discipline, and creates pathways for competition and innovation. No single reform can break the self-reinforcing cycle; the solutions proposed here are designed to be mutually reinforcing, addressing the root causes identified in this analysis.

---

## 1. Reform the Budget Process

### A. Shift to Biennial Budgeting

- **Problem Addressed**: The annual budget process consumes enormous time and energy and creates funding instability. The DoD spends an estimated 25,000 person-years annually preparing its budget, and the chronic failure of Congress to pass appropriations on time (only one on-time enactment since FY2010) forces operations under continuing resolutions that cost $5-10 billion annually in inefficiency.
- **Description**: Shift the DoD to a two-year budget cycle, with detailed appropriations enacted biennially and an adjustment bill in the off year.
- **Implementation**: Congress would pass a two-year defense appropriation bill every other year, covering the full Future Years Defense Program (FYDP). The "off year" would be dedicated to rigorous oversight, performance audits, and long-term strategic planning rather than the annual budget grind.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Provides program managers with more stable, predictable funding. Reduces the churn of budget preparation. Allows Congress to focus alternating years on genuine oversight rather than spending battles. Mitigates (though does not eliminate) the continuing resolution problem.
- **Evidence Base**: The PPBE Reform Commission (2024) recommended a shift toward multi-year budgeting. Several allied nations (United Kingdom, Australia) use biennial or multi-year defense budget frameworks. State governments in 19 states use biennial budgeting.
- **Potential Objection**: Congress may resist giving up annual control over appropriations. Response: The biennial model still requires annual authorization through the NDAA and allows for adjustment bills to address changed circumstances.

### B. Create a "BRAC for Weapons"

- **Problem Addressed**: The Iron Triangle makes it nearly impossible to cancel underperforming or strategically unnecessary weapon programs because of the jobs and political interests they sustain.
- **Description**: Modeled on the Base Realignment and Closure (BRAC) process that successfully closed unneeded military bases, create a "Defense Programs Review Commission" for weapon systems.
- **Implementation**: Charter an independent commission of national security experts, former officials (with appropriate conflict-of-interest screening), and independent analysts. The commission would develop an all-or-nothing list of recommended program terminations, reductions, and restructurings. Congress would be required to hold a simple up-or-down vote on the entire package within 90 days, without amendments.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Breaks the Iron Triangle's grip by making it impossible for individual members of Congress to save their pet projects while voting for the overall package. Allows the DoD to get rid of legacy systems (like unrequested Abrams tank production) and reinvest the savings in higher priorities.
- **Evidence Base**: The BRAC process successfully closed 350+ military installations between 1988 and 2005, overcoming intense local political opposition through the up-or-down vote mechanism. Congress has blocked new BRAC rounds since 2005 precisely because the mechanism works.

### C. End the Continuing Resolution Cycle

- **Problem Addressed**: The chronic failure to pass appropriations on time wastes billions and disrupts planning.
- **Description**: Create an automatic continuing resolution with escalation triggers that impose consequences on Congress for failure to act.
- **Implementation**: If Congress does not enact defense appropriations by October 1, an automatic CR would take effect at 99% of the prior year's level. For every 30 days the CR continues, funding drops by an additional 0.5%. Simultaneously, congressional pay and travel budgets would be suspended during any CR period. New program starts would be authorized (not prohibited, as under current CRs) but at reduced funding levels.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Creates political consequences for inaction. The declining funding trigger gives both parties incentive to reach agreement. Allowing new starts (at reduced levels) during CRs prevents the modernization freeze that current CRs impose.

## 2. Reform the Acquisition Process

### A. Empower Program Managers and Enforce Accountability

- **Problem Addressed**: The current system diffuses responsibility across dozens of review layers, so that no individual is truly accountable for program outcomes.
- **Description**: Give Program Executive Officers (PEOs) and Program Managers (PMs) true authority over their programs and hold them personally accountable for results.
- **Implementation**:
    - **Empowerment**: Give PMs consolidated authority over requirements, budget, and personnel for their program, similar to the "Acquisition Category I Special Access Program" model used for classified programs that typically deliver on time and on budget.
    - **Accountability**: Tie PM career progression directly to program performance. A PM who delivers on time and on budget receives accelerated promotion. A PM whose program suffers a critical Nunn-McCurdy breach is removed and cannot be reassigned to another major program.
    - **Tenure**: Require PMs to stay on a program for at least five years or through a major milestone, ending the 2-3 year rotation cycle that diffuses accountability.
    - **Authority to Say Yes**: Designate the PM as the single decision authority for their program, with review layers providing advice rather than possessing veto power.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Creates clear lines of responsibility. Rewards success and punishes failure. Reduces the bureaucratic veto-ocracy that slows every program.
- **Evidence Base**: GAO has repeatedly identified the short tenure of PMs (average 2.5 years) as a driver of acquisition failure. Special access programs with empowered managers consistently outperform conventional programs on cost and schedule.

### B. Reform Contract Types to Shift Risk to Contractors

- **Problem Addressed**: Cost-plus contracts create incentives for cost growth rather than efficiency, because contractor profit increases in absolute terms as costs rise.
- **Description**: Make fixed-price incentive contracts the default for development and production, with cost-plus reserved for genuinely uncertain early-stage research.
- **Implementation**:
    - **Default to fixed-price**: After Milestone B (start of formal development), require fixed-price incentive fee (FPIF) contracts. The government and contractor agree on a target cost, target profit, and a share ratio (e.g., 60/40) for overruns and underruns.
    - **Cost-plus exception**: Cost-plus contracts would require a written waiver from the Milestone Decision Authority, justified by a determination that the technology risk is genuinely too high for fixed-price and that no commercial analog exists.
    - **Automatic fee reduction**: For any contract type, impose automatic fee reductions when costs exceed the baseline by more than 10%. For overruns exceeding 25%, the contractor absorbs all additional costs.
    - **"Should Cost" reviews**: Require independent "should cost" reviews for all contracts above $100 million, conducted by CAPE or the Defense Contract Audit Agency (DCAA) with access to contractor accounting systems.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Forces contractors to have "skin in the game." Creates powerful incentives to control costs. The KC-46 tanker program demonstrated that fixed-price development contracts can work -- Boeing absorbed $7+ billion in losses, which it would have passed to taxpayers under a cost-plus contract.
- **Potential Objection**: Industry will argue that fixed-price contracts for genuinely novel R&D are inappropriate. Response: The proposal preserves cost-plus for early-stage research (pre-Milestone B). Once technology is mature enough for formal development, fixed-price is appropriate.

### C. Adopt a Software-Centric Acquisition Model

- **Problem Addressed**: The current hardware-focused acquisition model is fundamentally broken for software-intensive systems, which now define modern military capability.
- **Description**: Create a separate, agile pathway for software development that operates on commercial timelines rather than the multi-year hardware acquisition cycle.
- **Implementation**:
    - **Fund Teams, Not Programs**: Instead of funding a specific software "program" with a fixed endpoint, fund standing teams of government and contractor software developers who deliver continuous capability improvements.
    - **DevSecOps**: Use modern DevSecOps practices (agile development, continuous integration, automated security testing) to deliver updated software to the warfighter in weeks or months, not years.
    - **Government-Owned Architecture**: The government should own the open systems architecture and data rights for all major platforms, allowing it to swap in new software from competing vendors. This breaks the lock-in that gives sole-source contractors monopoly power over sustainment and upgrades.
    - **Continuous Authorization to Operate (ATO)**: Replace the cumbersome, one-time ATO security certification process with continuous monitoring and automated compliance testing, modeled on commercial cloud security practices.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Allows the DoD to acquire software at the speed of the commercial sector. The Air Force's Kessel Run program and the Army's Software Factory have demonstrated this model can work; the challenge is scaling it across the enterprise.
- **Evidence Base**: The Defense Innovation Board's 2019 "Software Acquisition and Practices" (SWAP) study documented the failures of the traditional model and recommended the approach described above. The Software Acquisition Pathway in the AAF is a step in this direction but has not been fully implemented.

## 3. Foster a More Competitive Industrial Base

### A. Bridge the "Valley of Death"

- **Problem Addressed**: Innovative small companies with successful prototypes die waiting for the slow DoD contracting process to catch up, while venture capital investors are deterred by the DoD's unpredictable procurement timeline.
- **Description**: Create a dedicated funding mechanism to transition successful prototypes into funded programs of record.
- **Implementation**: Establish a "Transition and Scaling Fund" of $5-10 billion annually. When a company proves its technology through an innovation hub like DIU (Defense Innovation Unit), AFWERX, or NavalX, this fund provides immediate bridge funding to begin initial production while the larger DoD contracting process catches up. The fund would operate under streamlined Other Transaction Authority (OTA) with a 120-day target from prototype validation to production contract.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Prevents promising startups from dying during the 18-36 month gap between prototype success and program of record funding. Signals to venture capital that the DoD is a reliable market. SpaceX, Anduril, and Palantir succeeded in part because they had deep enough venture funding to survive the valley of death -- most companies do not.

### B. Mandate Competitive Prototyping

- **Problem Addressed**: The consolidation of the defense industrial base to five prime contractors has eliminated competitive pressure for major programs.
- **Description**: Require at least two competitive prototypes for all new Major Defense Acquisition Programs before selecting a single contractor for full development and production.
- **Implementation**: For all programs above $5 billion in total acquisition cost, fund at least two contractor teams through the Technology Maturation and Risk Reduction phase (pre-Milestone B). Each team would build and demonstrate a working prototype. The winner of the competition proceeds to EMD and production; the loser's team is maintained at a reduced level to serve as a credible alternative source if the winner fails.
- **Evidence Base**: The YF-22/YF-23 fighter competition produced the F-22. The Great Engine War between GE and Pratt & Whitney for F-16 engines reduced costs by 20%+ and improved quality. The competitive development model consistently produces better outcomes than sole-source awards.

### C. Strengthen Government Data Rights

- **Problem Addressed**: Contractors control proprietary technical data for their systems, creating lock-in that prevents competition for upgrades, sustainment, and future production.
- **Description**: Require the government to retain sufficient technical data rights to enable competition at the subsystem and component level.
- **Implementation**: Mandate government-purpose data rights for all technical data produced under contracts above $10 million. Require modular open systems architecture (MOSA) for all new major programs. Create a centralized data repository for government-owned technical data to facilitate competition.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Breaks the sole-source lock-in that gives contractors monopoly power over sustainment (which accounts for 60-70% of lifecycle costs). Enables competition for upgrades and spare parts, driving down costs.

## 4. Strengthen Independent Oversight

### A. Make Independent Cost Estimates Binding

- **Problem Addressed**: The "conspiracy of optimism" produces unrealistic cost estimates that guarantee overruns.
- **Description**: Elevate the Cost Assessment and Program Evaluation (CAPE) office and make its independent cost estimates the binding baseline for major programs.
- **Implementation**:
    - Elevate the CAPE director to a Senate-confirmed position reporting directly to the Secretary of Defense.
    - Require all major programs to use the CAPE independent cost estimate (not the program office estimate) as the official baseline for Nunn-McCurdy reporting.
    - Require that if a service's cost estimate differs from CAPE's by more than 15%, the service must provide a written justification that is submitted to Congress.
    - Create a public database of actual costs for completed defense programs, enabling more accurate estimation for future programs.
- **Evidence Base**: CBO's independent cost estimates for defense programs have historically been more accurate than DoD's own estimates. GAO has repeatedly found that the DoD underestimates costs for new programs. Making independent estimates binding removes the incentive to game the baseline.

### B. Strengthen DOT&E Independence

- **Problem Addressed**: Industry and the services have repeatedly sought to weaken the Director of Operational Test and Evaluation (DOT&E), the most important independent voice on whether weapons actually work.
- **Description**: Codify DOT&E's independence and authority in law, making it resistant to erosion by future administrations or industry pressure.
- **Implementation**: Require DOT&E sign-off before any major program can proceed past Milestone C into full-rate production. Prohibit the reassignment of DOT&E to a position under the acquisition executive. Increase DOT&E's budget and staffing to keep pace with the growing number and complexity of programs.

## 5. Reform the Acquisition Workforce

- **Problem Addressed**: The DoD acquisition workforce has shrunk by approximately 50% since the early 1990s while program complexity has increased dramatically. The workforce is underpaid relative to industry, creating a talent drain.
- **Description**: Invest in a world-class government acquisition workforce that can match the technical sophistication of the contractors it oversees.
- **Implementation**:
    - **Pay reform**: Create a Defense Acquisition Professional pay scale that offers salaries competitive with the private sector for key roles (program managers, contracting officers, cost analysts, systems engineers). Current GS-scale pay for acquisition professionals is 50-70% of comparable industry positions.
    - **Workforce expansion**: Increase the acquisition workforce by 20% over 5 years, focusing on technical disciplines (software engineering, systems engineering, cost analysis, data analytics).
    - **Retention bonuses**: Tie bonuses to program performance outcomes (cost, schedule, capability delivered), not just to tenure.
    - **Training**: Expand the Defense Acquisition University and create partnerships with leading engineering schools.
    - **Anti-capture rotation**: Rotate acquisition professionals across programs and services every 3-5 years to prevent capture by specific contractors while maintaining the 5-year minimum tenure for program managers.
- **Evidence Base**: GAO has repeatedly identified acquisition workforce shortages as a root cause of procurement failure. The National Security Commission on Artificial Intelligence (2021) recommended significant investment in government technical talent. The DoD cannot effectively oversee $400+ billion in annual contracts with an understaffed, underpaid workforce.

## International Comparisons

Several allied nations have implemented acquisition reforms that offer relevant lessons:

- **Australia**: The Australian Defence Force uses a "smart buyer" model with strong independent cost estimation and has adopted agile acquisition pathways for technology-intensive programs.
- **United Kingdom**: The UK Ministry of Defence's "CAAS" (Cost Assurance and Analysis Service) provides independent cost estimates that are more central to procurement decisions than CAPE's estimates are in the U.S.
- **Israel**: Israel's defense procurement system emphasizes rapid prototyping, iterative development, and close integration of military users with development teams -- a model the U.S. innovation hubs (DIU, AFWERX) are attempting to replicate.
- **France**: The Direction Generale de l'Armement (DGA) maintains significant government-owned technical capacity, reducing information asymmetry with contractors.
