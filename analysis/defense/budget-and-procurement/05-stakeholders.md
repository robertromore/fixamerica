# Budget and Procurement: Stakeholders

The defense budget and acquisition process is dominated by the powerful, interlocking interests of the "Iron Triangle." Understanding the incentives of these key stakeholders -- their motivations, their leverage, and their interconnections -- is crucial to understanding why the system is so resistant to reform and what strategies might succeed in changing it.

## The "Iron Triangle"

This is the core group of stakeholders whose interactions drive the system.

| Stakeholder | Primary Interest | Source of Power |
|---|---|---|
| **Congress** (Armed Services & Appropriations Committees) | Securing jobs and federal spending for their districts/states to ensure re-election; demonstrating "strong on defense" credentials. | **The Funder.** Has the constitutional "power of the purse." Can authorize, fund, add to, or kill programs through the NDAA and defense appropriations bills. |
| **Department of Defense (DoD)** (Military Services, Program Managers, OSD) | Acquiring advanced weapon systems to meet military requirements; increasing their service's budget share and institutional influence; advancing careers through successful program management. | **The Customer.** The DoD is the monopsony buyer; it sets the requirements for all major programs through JCIDS and controls the acquisition process through the DoD 5000 series. |
| **The Defense Industry** (Prime Contractors) | Winning large, long-term, profitable government contracts; maintaining sole-source positions; preserving the revolving door pipeline for talent recruitment and political influence. | **The Supplier.** Possesses the unique technical expertise and facilities to build major systems. Employs approximately 1.7 million workers directly and 3.5 million including supply chain. Spends ~$140 million annually on lobbying and ~$50 million per election cycle on campaign contributions. |

**The Dynamic**: The DoD wants a new weapon system. A contractor (e.g., Lockheed Martin) promises to build it, often spreading subcontracts and jobs across as many congressional districts as possible -- a practice known as "political engineering." The F-35 supply chain extends to over 1,700 suppliers in 45+ states. Members of Congress then fight to fund the program to protect those jobs and the campaign contributions that come with them. This creates a powerful, self-reinforcing coalition that makes it nearly impossible to cancel a major program, no matter how poorly it performs. The Nunn-McCurdy process, which was designed to force cancellation of programs with egregious cost growth, has never actually resulted in the termination of a major weapon system.

## Other Key Stakeholders

### The White House (Office of Management and Budget - OMB)

**Role**: Balancing defense spending against other federal priorities (healthcare, education, infrastructure); enforcing the President's overall fiscal strategy.

**Primary Interest**: Keeping defense spending within the President's topline budget targets while ensuring the budget reflects the administration's strategic priorities.

**Leverage**: OMB acts as the gatekeeper for the President's budget request. It reviews and often cuts the DoD's internal budget submission (the "Program Objective Memorandum" or POM), forcing the services to make tradeoffs. OMB can kill or reduce programs by refusing to include them in the President's Budget.

**Key Tension**: OMB and the DoD are in perpetual tension. The DoD consistently argues it needs more money to meet the threats outlined in the National Defense Strategy. OMB responds that the DoD must fit within overall fiscal constraints and prioritize more effectively within its existing budget. The outcome of this negotiation depends heavily on the President's priorities and the political environment.

### DoD Cost Assessment and Program Evaluation (CAPE)

**Role**: Providing independent, objective cost estimates for major programs to counter the "conspiracy of optimism."

**Primary Interest**: Institutional credibility through accurate, independent analysis that serves the Secretary of Defense's decision-making.

**Leverage**: CAPE provides independent cost estimates directly to the Secretary of Defense, often producing figures significantly higher than the services' own estimates. Its analysis can force programs to be restructured, reduced, or canceled.

**Key Tension**: CAPE's effectiveness depends on the Secretary of Defense's willingness to use its analysis against the services' preferred estimates. Under some Secretaries, CAPE has been a powerful counterweight; under others, its analysis has been sidelined.

### Director, Operational Test and Evaluation (DOT&E)

**Role**: Conducting independent operational testing to determine whether weapons actually work in realistic combat conditions.

**Primary Interest**: Ensuring warfighters receive systems that actually perform as advertised.

**Leverage**: DOT&E reports directly to Congress and the Secretary of Defense. Its annual report provides blunt assessments that can be a major roadblock for a program not performing as promised. DOT&E's finding that the F-35 was not ready for full-rate production delayed that decision for years.

**Key Tension**: The services and industry frequently view DOT&E as an obstacle rather than a quality check. There have been recurring efforts -- some by industry lobbyists, some by the services -- to reduce DOT&E's independence and authority.

### Government Accountability Office (GAO)

**Role**: Serving as Congress's independent auditor and evaluator of defense programs.

**Primary Interest**: Objective assessment of program performance and systemic acquisition issues.

**Leverage**: GAO's annual *Weapon Systems Annual Assessment* is the most authoritative public document on acquisition performance. Its recommendations carry significant weight in congressional debate. GAO also adjudicates bid protests from contractors who challenge contract awards.

**Key Tension**: GAO produces hundreds of recommendations annually, but Congress implements only a fraction. The same members who praise GAO's work in hearings often ignore its recommendations when they conflict with local program interests.

### Congressional Budget Office (CBO)

**Role**: Providing non-partisan, independent cost projections for defense programs and policy options.

**Primary Interest**: Accurate fiscal analysis that supports informed congressional decision-making.

**Leverage**: CBO's long-term cost projections for the defense budget, nuclear modernization, and major acquisition programs are frequently more accurate than the DoD's own estimates and serve as a reality check in budget debates.

### "Good Government" Watchdog Groups

**Key Organizations**: Project on Government Oversight (POGO), Taxpayers for Common Sense, Citizens Against Government Waste, National Priorities Project.

**Primary Interest**: Exposing waste, fraud, and abuse in defense spending; promoting transparency and accountability.

**Leverage**: Uses investigative research, public reporting, congressional testimony, and media engagement to bring attention to failing programs and systemic problems. POGO's revolving door database and analyses of specific program failures have been particularly influential.

**Key Weakness**: These organizations are dramatically outspent by the defense industry. The entire annual budget of the major reform-oriented watchdog groups combined is a fraction of the $140 million the industry spends on lobbying in a single year.

### Defense Workers and Communities

**Role**: The approximately 1.7 million direct defense industry employees and the communities that depend on defense facilities for their economic base.

**Key Characteristics**:

- High-skill, well-compensated workforce: average defense industry salary ~$100,000
- Geographically concentrated in specific regions: Northern Virginia, Southern California, Texas, Connecticut, Alabama, Washington State, Mississippi (shipbuilding), Arizona, Florida
- Communities often economically dependent on a single defense facility or program
- Organized through unions (IAM, UAW, IBEW) in some sectors, particularly shipbuilding and aircraft manufacturing

**Primary Interest**: Job security, good wages, and continued funding for the programs they work on.

**Leverage**: Constituent pressure on members of Congress. When a program faces cuts, workers and their families become a powerful political constituency. Defense firms routinely mobilize their workforce to contact elected officials during budget debates.

**Key Tension**: Defense workers have legitimate interests in job security and community stability. However, these interests are routinely weaponized by contractors and Congress to prevent program reforms or cancellations that would serve the broader public interest. The result is that strategic decisions about weapon systems are often driven by employment considerations rather than military necessity.

### Small and Mid-Tier Defense Firms

**Key Characteristics**: Approximately 20,000 small businesses in the defense supply chain (down from ~40,000 in 1990). Often the source of innovative technologies and approaches. Highly dependent on prime contractors for subcontract opportunities.

**Primary Interest**: Access to DoD contracts without being forced through prime contractors; simpler procurement regulations; fair treatment by primes; protection from predatory acquisition.

**Leverage**: Small Business Administration set-aside requirements (23% target); innovation capabilities that primes sometimes lack; potential allies in reform coalitions.

**Key Tension**: Small and mid-tier firms are squeezed by the consolidation of the prime contractor tier. They depend on primes for subcontracts but are also potential competitors. The "valley of death" between successful prototype and funded program of record disproportionately harms small firms that lack the financial reserves to survive long procurement timelines.

### Taxpayers

**Role**: Fund the entire defense enterprise through federal taxes.

**Primary Interest**: Effective national defense at a reasonable cost; minimizing waste; ensuring defense spending does not crowd out other public investments.

**Leverage**: Voting power (diffuse and hard to organize on defense spending issues); occasional public outrage at waste scandals.

**Key Weakness**: Taxpayer interests are diffuse -- each taxpayer bears a small fraction of the cost of any given weapon program -- while contractor interests are concentrated. This creates a classic collective action problem: the beneficiaries of waste are highly organized and the victims of waste are not. A single member of Congress with an F-35 plant in their district has more political incentive to protect that program than 330 million taxpayers have to scrutinize it.

### Think Tanks and Research Organizations

**Key Organizations**:

- **Defense-industry-funded**: Center for Strategic and International Studies (CSIS), Center for a New American Security (CNAS), Atlantic Council, Hudson Institute, American Enterprise Institute (AEI)
- **Independent/reform-oriented**: Project on Government Oversight (POGO), Stimson Center, Quincy Institute for Responsible Statecraft, Center for Defense Information
- **Federally Funded Research and Development Centers (FFRDCs)**: RAND Corporation, Institute for Defense Analyses (IDA), MITRE Corporation

**Primary Interest**: Influencing policy; maintaining research funding; credibility within the policy community.

**Leverage**: Production of "expert" analysis that shapes policy narratives; personnel who move between think tanks and government; media presence and convening power.

**Key Tension**: Think tanks that receive defense industry funding face a structural conflict of interest. While most maintain editorial independence, the funding relationship creates incentives to produce analysis that validates the need for higher defense spending and the programs of specific contractors.

## Stakeholder Interaction Map

| From | To | Flow |
|---|---|---|
| Defense contractors | Congress | Campaign contributions ($50M/cycle), lobbying ($140M/year), job creation in districts |
| Defense contractors | Pentagon officials | Future employment (revolving door), technical support, lobbying |
| Defense contractors | Think tanks | Research funding, conference sponsorship |
| Congress | DoD/Contractors | Appropriations, authorizations, congressional adds ($24B in FY2024) |
| Pentagon officials | Defense contractors | Contract awards, requirements, oversight (or lack thereof) |
| Pentagon officials (former) | Defense contractors | Expertise, relationships, credibility, access |
| Think tanks | Congress/Pentagon | Policy analysis, threat assessments, reform proposals, testimony |
| Defense workers | Congress | Constituent pressure, voting, mobilization during budget debates |
| Taxpayers | Congress | Voting, advocacy (diffuse and episodic) |
| GAO/CBO/DOT&E | Congress | Independent analysis, cost estimates, program evaluations |

## Power Analysis

| Stakeholder | Political Power | Economic Resources | Information Access | Organizational Capacity |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Big Five primes | Very High | Very High | Very High | Very High |
| Congress (defense committees) | Very High | N/A | High | High |
| Pentagon acquisition officials | High | Low | Very High | Medium |
| OMB | High | N/A | High | Medium |
| GAO/CBO | Medium | Low | High | High |
| Think tanks (industry-funded) | Medium | Medium | High | High |
| Defense workers/unions | Medium | Low | Low | Medium |
| Small/mid-tier firms | Low | Low | Medium | Low |
| Reform watchdog organizations | Low | Low | Medium | Medium |
| Taxpayers (general public) | Low (diffuse) | N/A | Low | Very Low |

The fundamental imbalance in this stakeholder landscape is that the actors who benefit from the current system (contractors, defense committee members, revolving door participants) are highly organized, well-resourced, and concentrated, while those who bear the costs (taxpayers, warfighters who receive late and inadequate equipment, communities denied alternative economic investment) are diffuse and poorly organized.
