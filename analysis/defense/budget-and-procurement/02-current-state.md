# Budget and Procurement: Current State

## The Defense Budget

The U.S. defense budget is composed of several major appropriation titles that fund different aspects of the Department of Defense. The total national defense budget (Budget Function 050) also includes spending by the Department of Energy on nuclear weapons and other defense-related activities outside the DoD.

### DoD Budget Request by Appropriation Title (FY2025)

| Appropriation Title | Amount | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **Operations & Maintenance (O&M)** | $339.8 billion | Funds readiness activities: training, base operations, equipment maintenance, fuel, supplies, and contractor logistics support. |
| **Military Personnel (MILPERS)** | $182.8 billion | Funds pay, allowances, healthcare (TRICARE), and benefits for approximately 1.3 million active-duty uniformed military personnel. |
| **Procurement** | $167.5 billion | Funds the purchase of new weapon systems, vehicles, ships, aircraft, munitions, and other equipment. |
| **Research, Development, Test, & Eval (RDT&E)** | $143.2 billion | Funds basic and applied research, science and technology, prototyping, engineering development, and operational testing. |
| **Military Construction (MILCON)** | $12.5 billion | Funds the construction of new facilities, runways, barracks, and infrastructure on military installations. |
| **Family Housing** | $1.7 billion | Funds the operation, maintenance, and construction of military family housing. |
| **Total DoD Request** | **$849.8 billion** | *Note: This is the discretionary budget for DoD only.* |

*Source: DoD Comptroller, FY2025 Defense Budget Request Overview. The total national defense budget (050 function) is approximately $895 billion, including DOE nuclear programs (~$35 billion) and other defense-related activities.*

### The Readiness vs. Modernization Tradeoff

The budget reflects the constant tension between funding current readiness (O&M) and investing in future capability (RDT&E + Procurement).

- **Readiness (O&M + MILPERS)**: ~$522.6 billion (61.5% of total)
- **Modernization (RDT&E + Procurement)**: ~$310.7 billion (36.6% of total)
- **Infrastructure (MILCON + Housing)**: ~$14.2 billion (1.7% of total)

This allocation has shifted significantly over time. In the Reagan buildup of the 1980s, procurement consumed over 25% of the defense budget. Today it is approximately 20%, while O&M has grown from roughly 30% to 40% of total spending. This reflects the rising cost of maintaining an aging force structure, higher personnel costs driven by pay raises and healthcare, and the increasing expense of sustaining complex legacy systems.

### The Continuing Resolution Problem

The DoD has operated under a full-year appropriation passed on time (before October 1) only once since FY2010. Instead, Congress has relied on **47 continuing resolutions** between FY2010 and FY2024. CRs freeze spending at the prior year's level and prohibit new program starts, creating severe planning disruption:

- **No new starts**: Programs that were scheduled to begin cannot enter the acquisition pipeline, delaying modernization by months or years.
- **Inefficient spending**: Program managers cannot sign optimal multi-year contracts or make bulk purchases, forcing smaller, more expensive transactions.
- **Readiness degradation**: Training exercises and depot maintenance are deferred during CR periods, creating backlogs that compound over time.
- **Industrial base impact**: Contractors face uncertainty about future orders, leading to workforce layoffs and supply chain disruptions.

The Congressional Research Service estimates that CRs cost the DoD between $5 billion and $10 billion annually in inefficiency and delayed programs.

## The Defense Acquisition System

The process for buying major weapon systems is notoriously complex, governed by the DoD 5000 series of regulations (over 180,000 pages of rules across the Federal Acquisition Regulation, the Defense FAR Supplement, and DoD-specific instructions).

### The Acquisition Lifecycle

1. **Requirements Generation (JCIDS)**: The Joint Capabilities Integration and Development System is the process where the military services define the need for a new capability. The Joint Requirements Oversight Council (JROC), chaired by the Vice Chairman of the Joint Chiefs, validates requirements. Critics argue JCIDS produces inflated requirements disconnected from cost and technical reality.
2. **Budgeting (PPBE)**: The Planning, Programming, Budgeting, and Execution process is how the DoD allocates funds for the new program across a five-year Future Years Defense Program (FYDP). The PPBE Reform Commission, which reported in 2024, found the system "fundamentally broken" and recommended major overhauls.
3. **Acquisition (DAS)**: The Defense Acquisition System itself, which includes several key milestones governed by the Milestone Decision Authority (MDA):
    - **Milestone A**: Approves entry into the Technology Maturation & Risk Reduction (TMRR) phase. Requires an Analysis of Alternatives (AoA) and an initial cost estimate.
    - **Milestone B**: Approves entry into the Engineering & Manufacturing Development (EMD) phase. This is the formal start of a program. Requires a certified cost estimate and an approved Acquisition Program Baseline (APB).
    - **Milestone C**: Approves entry into Production & Deployment. Requires successful operational testing (overseen by DOT&E) and a production readiness review.
4. **Sustainment**: The longest and most expensive phase of a weapon system's lifecycle, often accounting for 60-70% of total lifecycle cost. Sustainment includes maintenance, spare parts, upgrades, and depot overhaul.

This linear, milestone-based process was designed for industrial-age hardware programs and is ill-suited for modern, software-intensive systems where capabilities must be updated continuously.

### The Adaptive Acquisition Framework (AAF)

Recognizing the limitations of the traditional model, the DoD introduced the AAF to create six different acquisition "pathways" tailored to different types of programs:

| Pathway | Purpose | Timeline Target | Key Feature |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Urgent Capability Acquisition** | Address urgent operational needs | 6 months - 2 years | Bypasses normal process for wartime or emergency needs |
| **Middle Tier of Acquisition (MTA)** | Rapid prototyping and rapid fielding | 2-5 years | Section 804 authority; limited oversight requirements |
| **Major Capability Acquisition** | Traditional major programs (MDAPs) | 5-15+ years | Full milestone review process; Nunn-McCurdy reporting |
| **Software Acquisition** | Software-intensive systems | Continuous delivery | DevSecOps model; agile development sprints |
| **Defense Business Systems** | IT and business applications | Varies | Tailored for enterprise IT procurement |
| **Acquisition of Services** | Service contracts | Varies | Covers the $200+ billion annual DoD services portfolio |

**Challenge**: While the AAF is a significant structural improvement, the underlying culture of risk aversion and bureaucracy remains a powerful obstacle. Many program managers default to the most conservative interpretation of the new pathways, and the workforce has not been fully retrained to use them effectively. The Middle Tier of Acquisition pathway, designed for rapid prototyping, has been used for over 100 programs but has drawn GAO criticism for insufficient oversight and unclear transition plans to production.

## Acquisition Program Performance

Major Defense Acquisition Programs (MDAPs) have a long history of poor performance on cost and schedule. The GAO's annual Weapon Systems Annual Assessment provides the most comprehensive public accounting.

### Portfolio-Wide Performance

According to GAO's 2024 assessment of 83 major programs:

| Metric | Value | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Total portfolio acquisition cost | $2.1 trillion | GAO, 2024 |
| Total cost growth from initial estimates | $700+ billion | GAO, 2024 |
| Programs with cost growth >25% | 43% of portfolio | GAO, 2024 |
| Programs with schedule delays >2 years | 52% of portfolio | GAO, 2024 |
| Average unit cost growth | ~54% from first estimates | GAO, 2024 |

### Case Studies of Major Programs

**F-35 Joint Strike Fighter (Lockheed Martin)**

| Metric | Original Estimate (2001) | Current Estimate (2024) | Change |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Program Acquisition Cost** | $233 billion | $438 billion | +88% |
| **Total Lifecycle Cost** | ~$1.1 trillion | ~$1.7 trillion | +55% |
| **Unit Cost (F-35A)** | ~$50 million | ~$82 million | +64% |
| **Initial Operational Capability (IOC)** | 2010 (Marines) | 2015 (Marines) | 5-year delay |
| **Full-Rate Production Decision** | 2012 | 2024 | 12-year delay |

The F-35 remains unable to pass full operational testing. The Block 4 modernization effort, which is essential to keeping the aircraft relevant against Chinese and Russian air defenses, is itself over budget and behind schedule. The Technology Refresh 3 (TR-3) hardware/software upgrade has experienced persistent delays and software instability.

*Source: Government Accountability Office (GAO) reports on the F-35 program; DOT&E Annual Reports.*

**Ford-Class Aircraft Carrier (CVN-78, Huntington Ingalls Industries)**

| Metric | Original | Current | Change |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Lead ship cost** | $10.5 billion | $13.3 billion | +27% |
| **Delivery delay** | 2013 (planned) | 2017 (delivered) | 4-year delay |
| **Electromagnetic Aircraft Launch System (EMALS)** | Operational at delivery | Required years of post-delivery fixes | Significant reliability issues |
| **Advanced Weapons Elevators** | 11 elevators at delivery | Last elevator certified in 2023 | 6-year delay |

The Ford-class is a case study in "concurrency" -- beginning production before critical technologies (EMALS catapults, Advanced Arresting Gear, dual-band radar) were mature.

**Sentinel ICBM (GBSD) (Northrop Grumman)**

| Metric | Original Estimate | Current Estimate (2024) | Change |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Program cost** | ~$85 billion | $130+ billion | +53%+ |
| **Nunn-McCurdy status** | N/A | Critical breach (2024) | Cost growth exceeded 50% threshold |
| **IOC** | 2029 | 2032+ (est.) | 3+ year delay |

The Sentinel is a sole-source program -- Northrop Grumman is the only qualified domestic ICBM contractor -- which eliminates competitive pricing pressure.

**Columbia-Class Submarine (General Dynamics Electric Boat / HII)**

| Metric | Original Estimate | Current Estimate | Change |
|---|---|---|---|
| **12-boat program cost** | $109 billion (2014) | $132+ billion | +21% and rising |
| **Lead boat delivery** | 2027 | 2028+ | 12-18 months behind |

### Systemic Performance Issues

- **Cost Growth**: According to the GAO, the total acquisition cost for the DoD's portfolio of major programs has grown by over **$700 billion** from initial estimates.
- **Schedule Delays**: The average MDAP experiences a schedule delay of **2.5 years**.
- **Nunn-McCurdy Breaches**: A "Nunn-McCurdy breach" occurs when a program's unit cost grows by more than 15% (significant breach) or 25% (critical breach) over its baseline. Since 1997, there have been over **100** such breaches, including on major programs like the F-35, the Sentinel ICBM, and the Ford-class aircraft carrier. No major program has ever been canceled solely due to a Nunn-McCurdy breach -- the DoD has invariably certified the program as essential to national security.
- **Concurrency**: The practice of overlapping development and production to accelerate fielding frequently backfires. When problems are discovered during testing, already-produced units must be retrofitted at enormous cost. The F-35 program required hundreds of aircraft to be retrofitted before they could reach full operational capability.

## The Defense Industrial Base

### Market Concentration

The defense industrial base has consolidated dramatically since the post-Cold War "Last Supper" of 1993, when the government actively encouraged mergers. Today, five prime contractors dominate:

| Contractor | Total Revenue (~FY2024) | Defense Revenue | Key Programs |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Lockheed Martin** | ~$71 billion | ~$60 billion | F-35, C-130J, THAAD, Aegis, Trident II |
| **RTX (Raytheon)** | ~$74 billion | ~$37 billion | Patriot, Stinger, Javelin, Pratt & Whitney engines |
| **Northrop Grumman** | ~$40 billion | ~$33 billion | B-21 Raider, Sentinel ICBM, Triton UAV |
| **Boeing Defense** | ~$25 billion | ~$25 billion | KC-46, F/A-18, AH-64 Apache, MQ-25 |
| **General Dynamics** | ~$42 billion | ~$32 billion | Columbia-class sub, Abrams tank, IT systems |

*Sources: Company 10-K filings; Bloomberg.*

### Competition Trends

Approximately **50% of DoD contract dollars** in FY2023 were awarded without competitive bidding, either through sole-source contracts (~37%) or competitions that attracted only a single bidder (~13%). This represents a significant decline from the approximately 65% competitive rate in the early 2000s. In critical market segments -- tactical fighter aircraft, nuclear submarines, ICBMs, strategic bombers -- the U.S. has only one or two domestic suppliers.

### Emerging Competitors

The rise of venture-capital-funded defense technology firms has introduced new dynamics:

- **SpaceX** has disrupted the launch market and challenged the United Launch Alliance (Lockheed Martin/Boeing joint venture), reducing launch costs by over 90%.
- **Anduril Industries** has won contracts for autonomous systems, counter-drone technology, and border surveillance.
- **Palantir Technologies** has challenged legacy IT contractors for intelligence and data analytics work.
- **Shield AI**, **Kratos Defense**, and **Joby Aviation** are entering the autonomous systems and advanced air mobility markets.

Whether these new entrants can meaningfully challenge the dominance of the Big Five for major platform programs -- rather than just competing at the edges in software, services, and small autonomous systems -- remains an open question.

## The Role of Oversight Bodies

### Government Accountability Office (GAO)

The GAO's annual *Weapon Systems Annual Assessment* is the most authoritative public accounting of acquisition program performance. The GAO has produced consistent, detailed recommendations for acquisition reform for decades, but Congress implements only a fraction of them.

### Congressional Budget Office (CBO)

The CBO provides independent cost estimates for defense programs that are frequently more accurate than the DoD's own estimates. CBO's long-term defense spending projections help Congress understand the fiscal implications of current commitments.

### Cost Assessment and Program Evaluation (CAPE)

CAPE provides independent cost estimates and program evaluation directly to the Secretary of Defense, serving as an internal counterweight to the services' "conspiracy of optimism." However, CAPE's influence depends heavily on the Secretary's willingness to use its analysis.

### Director, Operational Test and Evaluation (DOT&E)

DOT&E conducts independent operational testing to determine whether weapons actually work in realistic conditions. Its annual report provides blunt assessments that often contradict optimistic program office claims. Industry and the services have repeatedly sought to weaken DOT&E's independence.
