# Civil-Military Relations: History

## Historical Development

The American model of civil-military relations is unique, shaped by a foundational suspicion of standing armies and a continuous effort to balance military effectiveness with democratic accountability. The history of this relationship is not one of steady progress but of recurring crises, hard-won norms, and periodic erosions that have shaped the institutions and debates of the present day.

---

### The Founding Era (1776-1815): Subordinating the Military

- **Founders' Fear**: The framers of the Constitution were deeply suspicious of a large, permanent "standing army," which they saw as a tool of European monarchical tyranny and a threat to liberty. This fear drew on centuries of British political theory, particularly the writings of James Harrington and the "Country Party" tradition that viewed standing armies as instruments of executive despotism.
- **Constitutional Safeguards**: They embedded civilian control directly into the Constitution through a deliberate division of military authority:
    - **Article I, Section 8**: Gives Congress the power to declare war, raise and support armies, provide and maintain a navy, and make rules for the government and regulation of the land and naval forces. The Appropriations Clause limits military funding to two-year intervals, requiring periodic reauthorization.
    - **Article II, Section 2**: Makes the elected President -- a civilian -- the Commander in Chief of the armed forces, ensuring that the highest military authority resides in an elected official rather than a professional soldier.
    - **Second Amendment**: The "well regulated Militia" clause reflected the founders' preference for citizen-soldiers over a professional standing army.
- **The Newburgh Conspiracy (1783)**: A formative crisis in which Continental Army officers encamped at Newburgh, New York, considered a march on Congress over unpaid wages and pensions. George Washington personally intervened, addressing the officers and persuading them to remain loyal to civilian authority. His dramatic appeal -- reportedly putting on reading glasses with the remark, "Gentlemen, you will permit me to put on my spectacles, for I have not only grown gray but almost blind in the service of my country" -- defused the conspiracy and cemented the principle of military subordination to civilian power.
- **Washington's Resignation (1783)**: Washington's voluntary resignation of his commission as Commander in Chief to Congress was an act without precedent in the Western world. King George III reportedly said that if Washington gave up power voluntarily, he would be "the greatest man in the world." This act established the foundational norm that military power must be returned to civilian authority.

### The 19th and Early 20th Century: A Small, Isolated Force

- **Small Peacetime Military**: For most of its first century, the U.S. maintained a very small professional army (typically fewer than 30,000 soldiers), relying on state militias and rapidly mobilized citizen-soldiers during wartime. This small size was itself a form of civilian control -- a military that small could not threaten the republic.
- **The Civil War (1861-1865)**: The Civil War tested civil-military relations at every level. President Lincoln's relationships with his generals -- dismissing McClellan, elevating Grant -- demonstrated the principle that the civilian commander-in-chief could hire and fire military leaders based on performance and strategic vision. Lincoln also faced the challenge of managing a volunteer force of over 2 million, drawn from civilian life through both volunteering and the first federal draft (the Enrollment Act of 1863).
- **The Root Reforms (1903)**: Secretary of War Elihu Root modernized the Army by creating the Army General Staff, establishing the Army War College, and reorganizing the militia into the National Guard. These reforms professionalized the officer corps while maintaining civilian control through the General Staff system, which placed strategic planning under the Secretary of War's authority.
- **World War I**: The U.S. mobilized 4.7 million troops, mostly through the Selective Service Act of 1917. General John J. Pershing insisted on maintaining an independent American Expeditionary Force rather than integrating U.S. troops into allied commands, a decision that reflected both military and national political priorities. After the war, the military rapidly demobilized, consistent with the American tradition of temporary wartime mobilization.
- **The Interwar Period**: The officer corps remained small and insular. Future leaders like Eisenhower, Marshall, and MacArthur spent decades as mid-grade officers in a peacetime army that was, in Eisenhower's words, "the most shoestring operation in the world."

### World War II and the Cold War: Rise of the National Security State

- **Total Mobilization**: World War II required the mobilization of over 16 million Americans -- approximately 12% of the total population -- the most comprehensive military mobilization in the nation's history. The wartime draft and the shared experience of service across all social classes created a generation of leaders with deep military experience.
- **The "Garrison State" Debate**: Political scientist Harold Lasswell warned in 1941 of a "garrison state" in which permanent mobilization would concentrate power in military specialists and erode democratic governance. This concern proved partially prophetic as the Cold War created a permanent national security establishment.
- **National Security Act of 1947**: A landmark law that created the modern civil-military architecture:
    - Established the **Department of Defense (DoD)** to unify the previously separate War and Navy Departments under a civilian **Secretary of Defense**.
    - Created the **National Security Council (NSC)** to advise the President on integrating foreign, military, and domestic policy.
    - Created the **Central Intelligence Agency (CIA)**.
    - Established the **Joint Chiefs of Staff (JCS)** as a formal advisory body.
    - The first Secretary of Defense, James Forrestal, struggled with the limitations of the position and the resistance of the military services to unification, contributing to his mental breakdown and suicide in 1949.
- **The Truman-MacArthur Controversy (1951)**: The most consequential civil-military crisis of the Cold War era. President Truman fired General Douglas MacArthur for publicly challenging the administration's limited war strategy during the Korean War. MacArthur had advocated bombing China and using nuclear weapons -- positions directly contrary to presidential policy. Truman's decision, though politically costly (MacArthur returned to a hero's welcome and addressed Congress), was a powerful and public affirmation of the principle that the President, not the generals, sets policy. The Senate hearings that followed, led by Senator Richard Russell, produced testimony from General Omar Bradley that the MacArthur strategy would involve the U.S. "in the wrong war, at the wrong place, at the wrong time, and with the wrong enemy."
- **Eisenhower's Warning (1961)**: In his farewell address, President Eisenhower -- a former five-star general -- famously warned of the "unwarranted influence" of the growing "military-industrial complex." Earlier drafts of the speech used the phrase "military-industrial-congressional complex," explicitly naming Congress's role in the dynamic. Eisenhower's warning was rooted in his observation of defense spending that had remained at historically unprecedented peacetime levels (~10% of GDP) and the growing political power of defense contractors.
- **The McNamara Revolution**: Secretary of Defense Robert McNamara (1961-1968) attempted to impose civilian analytical control over the military through systems analysis, the Planning, Programming, and Budgeting System (PPBS), and the "whiz kids" -- civilian analysts who challenged military professional judgment with quantitative methods. While McNamara's reforms strengthened civilian control in some respects, the disastrous application of these methods to Vietnam strategy discredited the approach and deepened military distrust of civilian interference.

### The Vietnam Era and the All-Volunteer Force (1964-1990)

- **A Crisis of Trust**: The Vietnam War created a deep crisis in civil-military relations. The military felt it had been asked to fight a war with political constraints that made victory impossible (the "stab in the back" narrative), while much of the public grew hostile to the military and the draft. General H.R. McMaster's influential book *Dereliction of Duty* (1997) argued that the Joint Chiefs failed in their obligation to provide candid advice to President Johnson, acquiescing in a flawed strategy rather than risking confrontation with civilian leaders.
- **The Draft and Social Division**: The Vietnam-era draft was widely perceived as inequitable, with college deferments disproportionately shielding wealthier Americans. Of the 2.7 million Americans who served in Vietnam, a disproportionate number came from working-class and minority backgrounds, deepening the sense that military sacrifice was not shared equally.
- **The Gates Commission and the All-Volunteer Force (AVF)**: In 1969, President Nixon appointed the Gates Commission (chaired by former Defense Secretary Thomas Gates) to study the feasibility of ending the draft. The commission, influenced by the free-market economic arguments of Milton Friedman, recommended an all-volunteer force. In 1973, the draft was abolished. The creation of the AVF professionalized the military but also began the process of separating it from the broader society, laying the groundwork for the modern civil-military gap.
- **The Abrams Doctrine**: Army Chief of Staff Creighton Abrams deliberately restructured the Army after Vietnam so that combat support functions were concentrated in the Reserves and National Guard. The intent was to ensure that future presidents could not wage a sustained war without mobilizing the Reserves -- which would require public and congressional support, reintroducing a form of democratic accountability.
- **The Weinberger Doctrine (1984)**: Secretary of Defense Caspar Weinberger articulated six conditions for the use of military force, including that forces should be committed only when vital national interests are at stake, with clearly defined objectives, and with the support of the American people and Congress. This doctrine -- later refined by General Colin Powell into the "Powell Doctrine" -- reflected the military's determination to prevent another Vietnam by establishing conditions that would ensure political support before committing to war.
- **Goldwater-Nichols Act of 1986**: This major reform strengthened civilian control and joint-service operations by:
    - Elevating the authority of the **Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff** as the principal military advisor to the President and Secretary of Defense.
    - Keeping the service chiefs out of the direct operational chain of command (which runs from the President through the Secretary of Defense to the combatant commanders).
    - Requiring **joint duty** assignments for promotion to general/flag officer, breaking down service parochialism.
    - Strengthening the authority of **combatant commanders** over forces in their theater.
    - The Act was championed by Senator Barry Goldwater (R-AZ) and Representative Bill Nichols (D-AL) over the fierce opposition of the military services, which resisted the loss of autonomy.

### The Modern Era (1991-Present): A Widening Gap and New Challenges

- **The "Revered but Disconnected" Military**: After the decisive success of the First Gulf War (1991), the military's public prestige soared. However, as < 0.5% of the population served in the post-9/11 wars in Iraq and Afghanistan, the military became an institution that was highly trusted but poorly understood by the public it served.
- **Clinton-Era Tensions**: The 1990s saw significant civil-military friction. President Clinton's lack of military experience and his early confrontation with the military over gays in the military ("Don't Ask, Don't Tell") created a hostile relationship. General Colin Powell, as Chairman of the Joint Chiefs, publicly opposed Clinton's policy, an unusually public assertion of military resistance to civilian direction.
- **The "Revolt of the Generals" (2006)**: During the Iraq War, six retired generals publicly and harshly criticized the civilian leadership of Secretary of Defense Donald Rumsfeld, calling for his resignation. While their frustrations with Rumsfeld's management were widely shared, the spectacle of retired generals organizing public opposition to a sitting civilian defense secretary raised troubling questions about the boundaries of military dissent.
- **The Mattis Exception (2017)**: The nomination of recently retired General James Mattis as Secretary of Defense required a congressional waiver of the 10 U.S.C. 113 requirement that the SecDef be a civilian who has not served in uniform for at least seven years (raised from 10 years in 2008). The waiver was granted -- only the second time since the law's enactment (the first was for George C. Marshall in 1950). The 2021 nomination of retired General Lloyd Austin required a third waiver, prompting renewed debate about whether the norm of civilian SecDefs was eroding.
- **January 6 and Its Aftermath**: The events of January 6, 2021 placed extraordinary pressure on military leaders. Chairman of the Joint Chiefs General Mark Milley took steps to ensure the nuclear chain of command remained under proper civilian control during the transition, and the Joint Chiefs issued an unprecedented public statement affirming that President-elect Biden would be inaugurated on January 20. These actions, while intended to preserve constitutional order, also raised questions about the military's role as an independent guarantor of democratic processes.
- **The Rise of New Domains**: The creation of **U.S. Cyber Command (2010)** and the **Space Force (2019)** institutionalized new warfighting domains whose speed and complexity created new and profound challenges for traditional civilian oversight. The 2023 announcement of the Replicator initiative to field autonomous drone swarms further accelerated the debate over human control of military systems.
