# Civil-Military Relations: Opposition to Reform

Proposals to reform civil-military relations often face resistance from those who believe the current system works well enough, or that proposed changes would harm military effectiveness or infringe on individual rights. Unlike many policy areas where opposition is primarily ideological, resistance to civil-military reform comes from a combination of institutional self-interest, cultural identity, constitutional principle, and genuine strategic concern. This makes the opposition complex and often bipartisan.

---

## Opposition to Increased Civilian Oversight

This opposition often comes from within the military community and its supporters, who are wary of civilian "micromanagement."

### The "Let the Warfighters Fight" Argument

| Argument | Proponents' Claim | Rebuttal / Counterpoint |
|---|---|---|
| **Operational Autonomy** | Military operations, especially in complex domains like cyber, are too fast-paced and technical for civilian generalists to effectively control in real-time. Excessive oversight or "mother-may-I" approval processes will lead to hesitation and defeat on the battlefield. | Civilian control is not about managing tactical details; it's about ensuring that military actions serve strategic, political goals set by elected leaders. The purpose of oversight is to prevent military operations from having unintended and disastrous political consequences. |
| **Erosion of Military Expertise** | If civilians with no military experience are empowered to make key operational or technical decisions, it devalues the deep expertise of the professional officer corps and leads to poor military outcomes. | The role of civilian leaders is not to be military experts, but to ask hard questions, challenge assumptions, and integrate military advice with broader economic and diplomatic considerations. Healthy civil-military relations require a dialogue between experts and elected leaders, not deference by one to the other. |
| **The McNamara Precedent** | Opponents frequently cite Secretary McNamara's disastrous micromanagement of Vietnam War operations as evidence that civilian interference in military affairs produces catastrophic results. | McNamara's failure was not that he exercised civilian oversight but that he did so on the basis of flawed strategic assumptions and dishonest metrics. The lesson of Vietnam is that civilian oversight must be *better*, not that it should be abandoned. |

**Key Opposing Stakeholders**: Some active duty and retired military officers, defense hawks in Congress, commentators who prioritize military effectiveness above all else, and military professional associations.

**Institutional Dimension**: The military services have a long history of resisting civilian oversight initiatives. The uniformed military fought the creation of the Office of the Secretary of Defense, resisted Goldwater-Nichols, and has repeatedly sought to limit the authority of oversight bodies like the Director of Operational Test and Evaluation (DOT&E). This institutional resistance is not necessarily cynical -- it often reflects genuine professional concern about civilian interference in matters of military judgment -- but it consistently tilts against accountability.

## Opposition to Restrictions on Political Activity

This opposition focuses on the free speech rights of retired military personnel and the civic obligations of military leaders.

### The First Amendment Argument

| Argument | Proponents' Claim | Rebuttal / Counterpoint |
|---|---|---|
| **First Amendment Rights** | Retired military officers are private citizens. They have the same First Amendment right as any other citizen to express their political views and endorse candidates. Any restriction on their speech would be unconstitutional. | While they have the legal right, they also have a special responsibility. When a four-star general endorses a candidate, they are not just speaking as a private citizen; they are implicitly lending the military's institutional prestige to a partisan cause, which damages its non-partisan standing. The question is not legality but professional ethics. |
| **"The Stakes are Too High"** | In a time of national crisis or when a particular candidate is seen as a threat to the Constitution, senior military leaders have a moral duty to speak out and warn the public, regardless of norms against political engagement. | This sets a dangerous precedent. It implies that military leaders, not voters, are the ultimate guardians of the republic. If retired generals can endorse candidates they favor as "defending the Constitution," they can also be perceived as opposing candidates they disfavor -- creating a dynamic in which military endorsements become a litmus test for democratic legitimacy. The proper way for retired officers to serve is by providing non-partisan advice and education, not by becoming partisan actors themselves. |
| **Individual vs. Institution** | An individual's political views are distinct from the military's institutional position. Retired officers should not be held to the same standards as active duty personnel. | In practice, the public does not distinguish between "retired General Smith" and "the military." Polls consistently show that the public views military endorsements as carrying institutional weight. A 2021 YouGov/Economist poll found that 47% of Americans said retired military endorsements influenced their view of a candidate's fitness for office. |

**Key Opposing Stakeholders**: Some retired military officers, civil liberties organizations (ACLU), political campaigns seeking high-profile endorsements, and media organizations that benefit from retired generals' commentary.

**Scale of the Problem**: The number of retired generals and admirals making public endorsements has grown dramatically:

| Election Cycle | Approximate Number of Retired Flag Officer Endorsements |
|---|---|
| 1992 | Fewer than 10 |
| 2000 | ~20 |
| 2008 | ~50 |
| 2016 | ~200+ |
| 2020 | ~500+ |

*Sources: Golby et al., "Listening to the Commander in Chief," Armed Forces & Society (2018); media tracking.*

## Opposition to Bridging the Civil-Military Gap

### The "AVF Works" Argument

| Argument | Proponents' Claim | Rebuttal / Counterpoint |
|---|---|---|
| **Professional Superiority** | The All-Volunteer Force is the most professional and effective military in history. The "gap" is a feature, not a bug. A force of dedicated professionals is superior to a conscript force of reluctant amateurs. Conscript armies are less skilled, less cohesive, and less reliable. | While more professional, the AVF's isolation creates a military that is not understood by the society it serves. This makes it harder to recruit, harder for civilians to conduct oversight, and creates a risk of the military's values diverging from the nation's. The gap also makes it politically easier to deploy the force in long wars because the sacrifice is borne by a small, self-selecting population. |
| **National Service is Impractical** | Proposals for mandatory national service to bridge the gap are massively expensive (estimated at $50-100 billion annually for a comprehensive program), bureaucratic, and an infringement on individual liberty. The 13th Amendment prohibits involuntary servitude, and mandatory service may not survive constitutional challenge. | National service does not have to be military conscription. A program with civilian options (like teaching, conservation, or elder care) could foster a shared sense of civic duty and bring Americans from different backgrounds together, without the issues of a military draft. Voluntary programs with strong incentives (full college tuition, federal hiring preference) can achieve participation without coercion. |
| **Elitism Concerns** | Critics from both left and right argue that national service proposals often come from elites who would structure programs to benefit their own priorities. Progressives worry about militarizing civilian service; conservatives worry about expanding government bureaucracy. | These concerns are legitimate design challenges, not reasons to abandon the concept. Programs like AmeriCorps and the Peace Corps demonstrate that civilian service can operate effectively without militarization. |

**Key Opposing Stakeholders**: Fiscal conservatives wary of the cost of national service, libertarians who oppose any mandatory service, some military professionals who prefer the current AVF model, and some progressive groups who oppose anything that might expand military influence.

## Opposition to PME Reform and Civilian Graduate Education Requirements

| Argument | Proponents' Claim | Rebuttal / Counterpoint |
|---|---|---|
| **War Colleges are Sufficient** | The military's war colleges are world-class institutions that produce strategically minded leaders. Requiring civilian graduate education implies that military education is inferior. | War colleges produce valuable education, but they exist within a military culture that can reinforce institutional groupthink. Exposure to civilian academic perspectives, diverse student bodies, and non-military analytical traditions broadens officers' intellectual horizons. |
| **Career Disruption** | Sending officers to civilian universities for 1-2 years disrupts career timelines and reduces operational experience. In an era of strategic competition, the military cannot afford to lose officers from operational billets. | Countries like the UK (which sends officers to civilian universities routinely) and Germany (where many officers earn civilian degrees) demonstrate that military effectiveness is not compromised by civilian education. The investment in broader perspectives pays dividends in strategic thinking and civil-military relations. |
| **Anti-Military Academic Culture** | Some military leaders and conservative commentators argue that civilian universities, particularly elite institutions, are hostile to military perspectives and would expose officers to anti-military ideological environments. | While some campuses have strained relationships with the military (especially during Vietnam-era ROTC bans), the reintegration of ROTC at Harvard, Columbia, and other elite universities since 2011 demonstrates improving conditions. Exposure to critical perspectives strengthens, rather than weakens, officers' intellectual resilience. |

## Opposition to Congressional Oversight Enhancement

| Argument | Proponents' Claim | Rebuttal / Counterpoint |
|---|---|---|
| **Separation of Powers** | The executive branch, not Congress, commands the military. Enhanced congressional oversight risks becoming legislative micromanagement that interferes with the President's authority as Commander in Chief. | The Constitution deliberately divides military authority between the President (command) and Congress (authorization, funding, oversight). Enhanced congressional capacity restores the balance the founders intended, not disrupts it. |
| **Classification Concerns** | Much of what Congress would need to oversee is classified. Expanding congressional access to classified programs increases the risk of leaks that could damage national security. | The Intelligence Committees already handle classified information with appropriate safeguards. The real concern is not leaks but the use of classification to shield programs from accountability -- a well-documented problem. |
| **Speed of Modern Conflict** | Modern warfare, especially cyber operations, moves too fast for congressional oversight. Requiring advance notification or approval for rapid military actions would tie the military's hands. | This is a genuine challenge, but the solution is not to abandon oversight -- it is to develop oversight mechanisms appropriate to the speed of modern operations. Pre-authorized frameworks, real-time notification systems, and ex post review requirements can balance speed with accountability. |

**Key Opposing Stakeholders**: Executive branch officials who resist congressional encroachment on presidential authority, military leaders who prefer operational autonomy, and some legal scholars who advocate for a strong "unitary executive" theory of presidential war powers.

## Rhetorical Strategies of Opposition

Opponents of civil-military reform employ several recurring rhetorical strategies:

- **"Politicizing the Military"**: Any reform proposal can be framed as itself a form of politicization. Proposals to restrict retired officer endorsements are described as "silencing veterans"; proposals to diversify military recruitment are described as "social engineering"; proposals to enhance oversight are described as "undermining the troops."
- **"Now is Not the Time"**: Strategic competition with China and Russia is invoked to argue that any disruption to current arrangements is dangerous. This argument has been made in every era -- there is always a threat that makes reform seem untimely.
- **"If It Ain't Broke"**: The AVF has produced a military that won the Cold War, the Gulf War, and countless smaller operations. Why fix what works? This argument ignores the long-term structural damage the AVF's isolation is doing to democratic accountability.
- **Conflation of Military Effectiveness with Current Arrangements**: Opponents routinely conflate support for the military with support for the *current* civil-military relationship, implying that reform is inherently anti-military.
