# Civil-Military Relations: Reform Roadmap

## Overview

Strengthening civil-military relations requires a long-term, phased approach that focuses first on education and building expertise, then on implementing structural reforms and new programs, and finally on institutionalizing a new equilibrium. The reforms proposed here cannot be achieved through a single legislative act or executive order; they require sustained effort across multiple administrations and Congresses. The roadmap below sequences reforms to build political momentum, establish institutional capacity, and overcome entrenched opposition while minimizing disruption to military effectiveness.

---

## Phase 1: Building the Intellectual Foundation (Years 1-2)

This phase focuses on creating the new institutions and educational programs needed to enhance civilian expertise, reform military education, and establish the evidence base for more ambitious reforms.

### Actions

| Priority | Action | Mechanism | Key Performance Indicator (KPI) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Charter New Institutions** | Congress passes legislation to charter the "Congressional Defense Expertise Institute" and the "National Commission on Military AI." | Federal legislation (NDAA provision) | Institutions are chartered; boards of directors are appointed; initial funding secured. |
| **2. Reaffirm Non-Partisanship Norms** | The Chairman of the Joint Chiefs and all service chiefs issue a joint statement reaffirming the norm of non-partisanship; the statement is incorporated into all officer commissioning and retirement ceremonies. | DoD Directive / JCS policy | Statement issued; incorporated into regulations within 6 months. |
| **3. Reform PME Requirements** | The Secretary of Defense issues a directive changing promotion board precepts to value civilian graduate education equally to war college completion. | SecDef policy directive | New promotion precepts are published and applied to the next promotion cycle. |
| **4. Launch Exchange Program Expansion** | The DoD doubles the number of slots in the Secretary of Defense Executive Fellows program (from ~15 to 30) and launches a pilot "Civilian Defense Fellowship" program (25 civilian professionals placed in DoD roles). | DoD budget request + administrative action | 30 military fellows placed in civilian organizations; 25 civilian fellows placed in DoD. |
| **5. AUMF Reform Introduction** | A bipartisan coalition introduces legislation to repeal the 2001 and 2002 AUMFs and replace them with a new, narrowly scoped authorization framework. | Federal legislation | Bill introduced with at least 10 bipartisan co-sponsors. |
| **6. Congressional Staff Investment** | Congress increases pay for professional staff on the Armed Services and Appropriations Committees by 30% and creates a "defense policy track" with retention incentives. | Congressional appropriation | Staff pay increases enacted; average tenure begins to increase from 3-5 years toward 5-7 years. |
| **7. Transparency Baseline** | DoD publishes a comprehensive report on the state of civil-military relations, including data on veteran representation in government, military political activity, and the civil-military gap. | Congressionally mandated report | Report published and made publicly available. |

### Political Strategy

Phase 1 reforms are designed to be achievable with existing authority or modest legislative changes. Institutional chartering and norm-reaffirmation are difficult for opponents to argue against publicly. The goal is to create visibility for the issues and build the institutional infrastructure needed for more ambitious reforms.

**Goal of Phase 1**: Create the institutional and educational infrastructure needed to bridge the civil-military gap and improve oversight. Establish the evidence base and political constituency for Phase 2 reforms.

## Phase 2: Launching and Scaling New Programs (Years 3-5)

This phase focuses on operationalizing the new institutions, launching the national service initiative, enacting legislative reforms, and beginning to see the first results of institutional changes.

### Actions

| Priority | Action | Mechanism | Key Performance Indicator (KPI) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Open the Congressional Defense Expertise Institute** | The Institute matriculates its first class of 20 congressional staffers in a one-year master's program. First executive education cohort (members of Congress and senior staff) completes a 4-week intensive. | Institutional launch | First class graduates; at least 15 of 20 graduates return to defense committee roles. |
| **2. AI Oversight Commission Operational** | The National Commission on Military AI holds its first public hearing, reviews its first classified AI programs, and issues its inaugural report to Congress. | Commission operations | First report delivered to Congress; at least 3 classified program reviews completed. |
| **3. Launch National Service Fellowship** | Congress passes and funds the "National Service Fellowship Act." The first cohort of 10,000 fellows begins their service year in military and civilian capacities across all 50 states. | Federal legislation + appropriation | 10,000 fellows placed in Year 1; program achieves target demographic and geographic diversity. |
| **4. Expand Civilian Graduate Education** | The number of military officers attending top-tier civilian graduate schools increases by 50% over the baseline. The Defense Scholar Fellowship fully funds 100 officers annually. | DoD budget request + SecDef directive | Enrollment targets met across all services; 100 Defense Scholar Fellows placed. |
| **5. Enact Cooling-Off Period Extension** | Congress passes legislation extending the cooling-off period for retired generals and admirals before accepting paid positions as political campaign surrogates or defense contractor board members from 1 year to 5 years. | Federal legislation | Legislation enacted and signed into law. |
| **6. War Powers Reform** | Congress enacts AUMF reform legislation, repealing the 2001 and 2002 AUMFs and establishing the new authorization framework with sunset clauses. | Federal legislation | New AUMF framework enacted; War Powers Compliance Office established at GAO. |
| **7. Scale Exchange Programs** | The Secretary of Defense Executive Fellows program reaches 50 slots; the Civilian Defense Fellowship reaches 50 slots; the Congressional-Military Exchange places 10 military fellows on Capitol Hill. | DoD budget + congressional appropriation | All programs at target capacity; first post-exchange career outcomes tracked. |

### Political Strategy

Phase 2 requires building a legislative coalition that includes defense reformers, good-government advocates, and members concerned about congressional prerogatives (war powers). The key messaging frame is "strong democracy, strong defense" -- reforms are presented as strengthening both democratic accountability and military effectiveness.

**Coalition targets**:

- **Defense reformers**: Attracted by enhanced oversight, PME reform, and AI governance
- **Civil libertarians**: Attracted by war powers reform and restrictions on military politicization
- **Fiscal conservatives**: Attracted by accountability improvements and efficient use of defense resources
- **Veterans groups**: Attracted by national service programs that honor and extend the tradition of service
- **Technology community**: Attracted by AI ethics commission and civilian-military tech exchange

**Goal of Phase 2**: Move from planning to execution. Begin to build the cadre of expert civilians and broadly-educated officers who will lead the next generation. Enact the key legislative reforms.

## Phase 3: Institutionalizing a New Relationship (Years 6-10)

This phase focuses on seeing the long-term benefits of the reforms as graduates of the new programs move into senior leadership positions and the new institutions become established features of the civil-military landscape.

### Actions

| Priority | Action | Mechanism | Key Performance Indicator (KPI) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. A New Generation of Leaders** | The first graduates of the Congressional Defense Expertise Institute are promoted to staff director and chief of staff positions on Capitol Hill. Defense Scholar Fellows begin reaching O-6 (Colonel/Captain) rank. | Career progression | At least 25% of key defense committee staff are graduates of the program; at least 20% of newly promoted O-7s have civilian graduate degrees. |
| **2. AI Oversight Mature** | Congress enacts permanent legislation based on the National Commission on Military AI's recommendations, establishing ongoing civilian oversight of autonomous weapons and AI-enabled military systems. | Federal legislation | Permanent AI oversight laws enacted; "human control requirements" codified for all lethal autonomous systems. |
| **3. National Service at Scale** | The National Service Fellowship program reaches 50,000 participants annually, with a measurable impact on military recruiting diversity, civic engagement, and public understanding of the military. | Program scaling | The percentage of recruits from under-represented regions increases by 15%; program alumni demonstrate higher civic engagement rates. |
| **4. A Less Partisan Military** | The norm against partisan endorsements by retired senior officers is re-established. The 5-year cooling-off period is effective. Public trust in the military's non-partisanship increases. | Cultural change + legal compliance | The number of public endorsements by retired 3- and 4-star officers decreases by 75% compared to the 2020/2024 cycles; bipartisan public confidence in the military converges. |
| **5. Congressional Capacity Restored** | Armed Services Committee staff have average tenures of 7+ years; at least 50% have completed advanced defense education; Congress exercises meaningful oversight of cyber operations, AI programs, and ongoing military operations. | Institutional capacity | GAO assessment rates congressional oversight capacity as "substantially improved" over baseline. |
| **6. International Leadership** | The U.S. leads NATO allies in establishing shared standards for civilian control of AI-enabled military systems and democratic oversight of new warfighting domains. | International agreements / NATO policy | NATO adopts U.S.-inspired standards for civilian AI oversight; at least 3 bilateral agreements on autonomous weapons governance. |

**Goal of Phase 3**: A new, healthier equilibrium in civil-military relations is achieved, characterized by higher levels of mutual understanding, more effective oversight, renewed commitment to non-partisanship, and democratic accountability mechanisms that keep pace with technological change.

## Risk Assessment

| Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Military institutional resistance to PME and promotion reform | High | Medium | Frame reforms as enhancing military professionalism, not diminishing it; engage service chiefs early |
| New administration reverses executive actions from Phase 1 | Medium | Medium | Prioritize codifying key reforms in legislation rather than executive orders |
| National service program becomes a partisan issue | Medium | High | Design as voluntary with strong incentives; ensure both military and civilian options; gain VSO endorsement |
| AI Commission becomes captured by tech industry interests | Medium | Medium | Strict conflict-of-interest rules; balanced membership; sunset clause requiring reauthorization |
| International crisis diverts attention from reform | High | Medium | Frame reforms as essential to crisis readiness; use crises as evidence for reform needs |
| Cooling-off period legislation faces First Amendment challenge | Low | High | Draft legislation to regulate paid activity (employment, consulting), not speech; ensure strong legal foundation |
| Congressional staff investment undermined by budget politics | Medium | Medium | Link staff pay to defense oversight quality metrics; make the case that better oversight saves money |

---

## Sequencing Rationale

The three-phase approach reflects the political reality that comprehensive civil-military reform cannot be achieved in a single legislative action. Each phase builds on the previous one:

1. **Phase 1 (foundation)** creates the institutional infrastructure, evidence base, and political constituency needed for Phase 2 legislation.
2. **Phase 2 (legislation and programs)** enacts the key legal reforms and launches the programs that will produce the next generation of leaders.
3. **Phase 3 (institutionalization)** sees the long-term benefits as reformed institutions, educated personnel, and restored norms create a self-sustaining equilibrium.

This sequencing also reflects the principle of irreversibility: educational institutions, once established, are difficult to close; norms, once restored, are easier to maintain; and a generation of broadly-educated officers and expert civilians will carry reform values forward regardless of political changes.
