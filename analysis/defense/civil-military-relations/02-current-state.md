# Civil-Military Relations: Current State

## The Civil-Military Gap

The "gap" refers to the measurable divergence in experience, demographics, and political views between the military and the society it serves. This gap is a direct consequence of the All-Volunteer Force (AVF), which eliminated conscription in 1973 and transformed military service from a broadly shared civic obligation into a professional career pursued by a small, self-selecting segment of the population.

### Key Indicators of the Gap

| Metric | Value | Note |
|---|---|---|
| **Share of Population Serving** | < 0.5% (~1.3 million active duty) | The smallest share of the population serving since before World War II. |
| **Share of Population who are Veterans** | ~5% (~16.5 million) | This number is declining as the large cohorts of Vietnam and Cold War veterans age. The VA projects it will fall below 4% by 2040. |
| **Veterans in Congress** | ~18% (97 members) | A historic low, down from a peak of ~75% in the 1970s. Fewer civilian leaders have direct military experience. |
| **Recruits with a Military Parent** | ~30% | A significant portion of the force comes from military families, indicating intergenerational military service patterns. |
| **Recruits with any Family Member who Served** | ~80% | The military is increasingly becoming a "family business," isolated from the general population. |
| **Geographic Concentration** | 5 states produce ~40% of recruits | Texas, California, Florida, Georgia, and North Carolina are consistently the top recruiting states. Rural areas and the Southeast are overrepresented. |
| **Percentage of Young Americans Eligible to Serve** | ~29% (ages 17-24) | Disqualification factors include obesity, drug use, criminal records, and lack of education credentials. |

*Source: DoD, VA, CRS, Pew Research, 2024-2025*

### Demographic Dimensions of the Gap

The civil-military gap has distinct demographic dimensions that compound its effects:

- **Geographic**: Military recruiting stations draw disproportionately from rural areas, small towns, and the South. Communities in the Northeast, urban centers, and the West Coast -- where much of the nation's political, media, and academic elite resides -- have minimal connection to military life. The Rand Corporation's 2020 study found that 44% of military recruits come from just 13% of U.S. counties.
- **Socioeconomic**: While the military is not disproportionately drawn from the poor (the median enlisted recruit comes from a middle-class zip code), it underrepresents both the wealthiest and the most educated segments of society. Graduates of elite universities -- where future political and media leaders are concentrated -- rarely serve.
- **Racial**: The enlisted force is significantly more diverse than the officer corps. African Americans comprise approximately 17% of active duty personnel (compared to 13% of the general population) but hold a smaller share of senior leadership positions. Hispanic representation has been growing but remains below population proportions.
- **Political**: Surveys consistently find that military officers identify disproportionately as Republican and conservative, while the enlisted force more closely mirrors the general population. A 2019 *Military Times* poll found that approximately 45% of active duty troops identified as Republican, compared to about 25% as Democrat and 30% as independent.

## Public Trust and Perception

The public holds the military in high esteem, but this reverence exists alongside a profound lack of knowledge about the institution.

### Public Confidence in U.S. Institutions (2024)

| Institution | "A Great Deal" or "Quite a Lot" of Confidence |
|---|---|
| **The Military** | **60%** |
| Small Business | 57% |
| The Police | 43% |
| The Presidency | 26% |
| The Supreme Court | 23% |
| Newspapers | 15% |
| **Congress** | **8%** |

*Source: Gallup, 2024*

### Trends in Military Confidence

This "confidence gap" between the military and other institutions, especially Congress, is a defining feature of modern civil-military relations. However, the trend line is concerning:

- **Peak**: Public confidence in the military peaked at approximately 82% in 2002-2003, in the patriotic surge following 9/11.
- **Decline**: Confidence has declined steadily from that peak, dropping to approximately 60% by 2024 -- the lowest level since 1997.
- **Partisan Divergence**: The decline has been sharply partisan. Republican confidence in the military remains high (~74%), while Democratic confidence dropped to approximately 42% in 2024, driven in part by perceptions that the military had become politicized and controversies over diversity and inclusion policies.
- **Generational Divide**: Younger Americans (18-34) express significantly lower confidence in the military than older cohorts, reflecting less personal connection to service.

*Sources: Gallup, 2024; Reagan National Defense Survey, 2023-2024*

While high trust is positive, some analysts worry it leads to a lack of critical public scrutiny. The Reagan Institute's 2023 survey found that only 13% of respondents could name a current military leader, suggesting that reverence does not translate into informed engagement.

## Politicization

There is growing concern about the erosion of the military's traditional non-partisanship. This manifests in several distinct forms:

### Retired Officer Endorsements

- **2016 election**: Over 200 retired generals and admirals signed open letters endorsing Donald Trump or Hillary Clinton, a significant escalation from previous cycles.
- **2020 election**: The trend intensified, with retired four-star officers including General James Mattis publicly criticizing the sitting president and others endorsing him.
- **2024 election**: The pattern continued, with both major party campaigns actively soliciting and publicizing retired military endorsements.
- **Historical norm**: Prior to the 1990s, retired senior officers almost universally avoided public partisan statements. The norm against endorsements was considered a professional obligation that extended beyond active service.

### Partisan Identification

- **Officer corps**: Surveys of military personnel show that the officer corps leans significantly Republican, a trend that began during the Vietnam era and accelerated during the Reagan buildup. A landmark 1998-1999 Triangle Institute for Security Studies (TISS) survey found that 64% of military officers identified as Republican, compared to 8% as Democrat.
- **Enlisted force**: More politically diverse, roughly tracking the general population, though with stronger representation of conservative and moderate views.
- **Perception problem**: Regardless of actual partisan distribution, the *perception* of the military as politically aligned damages its institutional credibility. A 2021 Council on Foreign Relations survey found that 56% of Americans believed the military had become "too politicized."

### Use of Military as Political Props

Both political parties have been criticized for using military personnel or settings as backdrops for partisan political events:

- Campaign events at military installations
- Active duty personnel in uniform at political rallies
- Presidential use of military assets (Air Force One flyovers, USS Abraham Lincoln "Mission Accomplished" event) for political messaging
- Congressional members invoking their military service credentials to deflect policy criticism

### The January 6th Dimension

The January 6, 2021 Capitol breach added an alarming new dimension to civil-military politicization. Approximately 81 individuals with military backgrounds were charged in connection with the events. While this represents a tiny fraction of the 18 million living veterans, the overrepresentation of veterans and active duty members in the extremist groups involved prompted a DoD-wide "extremism stand-down" ordered by Secretary of Defense Lloyd Austin in February 2021.

## Congressional Oversight: Capacity and Challenges

Congress's ability to exercise effective civilian control is constrained by several factors:

### Declining Military Expertise in Congress

- With only ~18% of members having military experience (down from ~75% in the 1970s), Congress has lost the experiential foundation for informed defense oversight.
- Congressional staff on the Armed Services and Appropriations Committees are the primary agents of oversight, but they are often young, underpaid, and outmatched by the Pentagon's institutional resources and the defense industry's lobbying apparatus.
- The average tenure of a professional staff member on the Senate Armed Services Committee is approximately 3-5 years, creating a constant knowledge drain.

### War Powers Erosion

- The 2001 Authorization for Use of Military Force (AUMF), passed three days after 9/11, has been used to justify military operations in at least 22 countries over more than two decades, far beyond its original scope.
- Congress has not passed a new AUMF for major military operations since 2002, effectively ceding its war declaration authority to the executive branch.
- Attempts to repeal or replace the 2001 AUMF have repeatedly stalled due to political disagreements over scope and presidential flexibility.

### Budget Complexity

- The annual National Defense Authorization Act (NDAA) and defense appropriations bills involve thousands of line items totaling over $880 billion (FY2025 request). The sheer volume of information overwhelms congressional capacity for detailed review.
- The Pentagon has never passed a full, clean financial audit. The DoD's sixth consecutive audit failure in 2023 means that Congress is authorizing hundreds of billions of dollars for an agency that cannot fully account for how it spends the money.

## Challenges from Emerging Technologies

The speed and complexity of new warfighting domains challenge traditional civilian oversight.

### AI and Autonomous Systems

- **Speed of Warfare**: An AI-driven conflict in which autonomous systems engage in combat at machine speed could unfold faster than a human decision-making cycle. This challenges the ability of the President or Congress to provide meaningful "in-the-loop" control. The DoD's Replicator initiative, announced in 2023, aims to field thousands of autonomous drones, raising new oversight questions.
- **"Expert Deference"**: The technical complexity of AI creates a risk that civilian leaders will simply defer to the judgment of military and technical experts, abdicating their oversight role. A 2023 National Academy of Sciences report warned that "civilian leaders may lack the technical literacy to ask the right questions about AI-enabled military systems."
- **Ethical Oversight**: The DoD adopted Ethical Principles for AI in 2020 (responsible, equitable, traceable, reliable, governable), but translating these principles into technical safeguards and enforceable policies remains a work in progress. DoD Directive 3000.09 requires a "human in the loop" for lethal autonomous weapons decisions, but the definition of meaningful human control is contested.
- **Allies' Approaches**: The UK has established an AI Safety Institute; NATO adopted its first AI strategy in 2021; the EU has pursued a comprehensive regulatory framework. The U.S. approach remains more permissive and less structured.

### Cyber Warfare

- **Blurred Lines**: Cyber operations blur the lines between intelligence gathering and military action, and between foreign and domestic domains. U.S. Cyber Command, established in 2010, conducts operations that may not clearly fit traditional legal categories of "armed conflict."
- **Covert Action**: Many cyber operations are conducted covertly, with limited visibility to Congress and the public, creating an accountability gap. The "Defend Forward" strategy authorizes persistent engagement on adversary networks, often without prior congressional notification.
- **Private Sector Role**: Critical national infrastructure is largely owned and operated by the private sector. Defending it requires a complex civil-military-commercial partnership that is not well-defined. The 2020 SolarWinds hack and the 2021 Colonial Pipeline ransomware attack demonstrated the vulnerability of this arrangement.
- **Escalation Risks**: Cyber operations carry escalation risks that civilian leaders may not fully appreciate. A cyber attack on an adversary's military command-and-control systems could be interpreted as a precursor to nuclear first strike, with catastrophic consequences.

### Space Operations

- **The Space Force**: Established in 2019 as the sixth military branch, the U.S. Space Force represents the militarization of a domain that was previously governed primarily by civilian agencies (NASA) and international treaties.
- **Commercial Dependence**: The military's dependence on commercial satellite systems (Starlink, GPS augmentation) creates oversight challenges. During the 2022 Ukraine conflict, SpaceX's Starlink became a critical military communication tool, raising questions about the authority of a private company over wartime communications.
- **Anti-Satellite (ASAT) Weapons**: China and Russia have tested ASAT weapons that can destroy satellites in orbit. The civilian implications of military operations in space are enormous, yet congressional oversight capacity for space warfare remains limited.

## Professional Military Education (PME)

PME institutions are responsible for teaching future military leaders about their role in a democracy.

### Current Curriculum

- War colleges (e.g., Army War College, Naval War College, Air War College, National War College, Eisenhower School) include courses on civil-military relations, ethics, and constitutional law.
- The curriculum is designed around the Joint Professional Military Education (JPME) framework established by Goldwater-Nichols, which requires officers to complete joint education before promotion to general/flag officer rank.
- Several war colleges use Huntington's *The Soldier and the State* and Feaver's *Armed Servants* as core texts.

### Critiques

- Critics argue that PME has become too focused on operational planning and has not adequately adapted to teaching the complexities of great power competition, technological change, and the military's relationship with society.
- A 2020 congressionally mandated review of PME found "significant shortcomings" in faculty quality, curriculum relevance, and critical thinking development.
- There is ongoing debate about whether PME sufficiently encourages the ability to offer candid, independent advice to civilian leaders. The "say yes to the boss" culture in many military organizations may undermine the educational emphasis on principled dissent.
- The Skelton Panel on PME (2010) recommended greater integration of civilian academic perspectives, but implementation has been uneven across the services.
- Few senior military leaders have graduate degrees from civilian universities; the military's internal education system creates intellectual insularity that can reinforce groupthink.

## International Comparisons

Other democracies manage civil-military relations through distinct models:

- **Germany**: The Bundeswehr operates under the principle of *Innere Fuhrung* ("inner leadership" or "leadership from within"), which emphasizes the soldier as a "citizen in uniform" with democratic responsibilities. Germany's Parliamentary Commissioner for the Armed Forces (*Wehrbeauftragter*) provides an independent ombudsman for military personnel, reporting directly to the Bundestag.
- **United Kingdom**: The UK maintains strong civilian control through the Ministry of Defence, but the tradition of "military advice" is more formalized, with the Chief of the Defence Staff having direct access to the Prime Minister. The UK's military is notably less politicized than the U.S. military.
- **Israel**: The Israel Defense Forces (IDF) represent a case of deep civil-military integration through universal conscription, but the revolving door between senior military command and political leadership is a constant source of tension. Former generals routinely enter politics immediately upon retirement.
- **NATO Framework**: NATO's democratic governance standards require member states to maintain civilian control of the military, with parliamentary oversight and transparent defense budgets. These standards have been instrumental in democratizing the militaries of former Warsaw Pact states.
