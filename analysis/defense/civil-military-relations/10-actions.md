# Civil-Military Relations: What You Can Do

## Overview

Bridging the civil-military gap is a responsibility that falls on both civilians and military personnel. The gap exists not because of malicious intent but because of structural forces -- the All-Volunteer Force, geographic isolation of military communities, and the declining share of Americans with military experience -- that can only be overcome through deliberate, sustained effort. Informed citizens can take several meaningful steps to foster a healthier relationship and strengthen democratic accountability. Because civil-military reform is not a partisan issue, the most effective advocacy is nonpartisan and focused on democratic health, transparency, and shared understanding.

---

## 1. Get Educated

### Understand the Foundational Debates

- **Read Beyond the Headlines**: Seek out high-quality analysis on civil-military relations from the sources listed in the [Resources](09-resources.md) section. Understanding the core theories -- Huntington's "objective civilian control," Feaver's "agency theory," and Cohen's "unequal dialogue" -- provides the intellectual framework for evaluating current events.
- **Key Starting Points**:
    - Samuel P. Huntington, *The Soldier and the State* (1957) -- the foundational text
    - Peter D. Feaver, *Armed Servants* (2003) -- the modern framework
    - Eliot A. Cohen, *Supreme Command* (2002) -- the case for active civilian engagement
    - Rosa Brooks, *How Everything Became War and the Military Became Everything* (2016) -- the contemporary blurring of military and civilian roles
- **Follow Ongoing Debates**: Subscribe to *War on the Rocks* (the leading online publication for civil-military debate), *Lawfare Blog* (law and national security), and the *Texas National Security Review* (academic analysis).

### Learn About the Modern Military

- **Understand the Structure**: Make an effort to understand the organization, culture, and challenges of the modern All-Volunteer Force. Know the difference between the Joint Chiefs (advisory) and combatant commanders (operational). Understand that the military is not monolithic -- each service has distinct cultures, and the officer corps and enlisted force have different experiences and perspectives.
- **Follow Military Media**: Publications like *Army Times*, *Navy Times*, *Air Force Times*, and *Military Times* provide reporting from a military perspective that is often invisible to civilian media consumers.
- **Study the Budget**: The annual National Defense Authorization Act (NDAA) and defense appropriations bills are the most consequential legislation Congress passes each year. Understanding the basic structure of the defense budget -- personnel, operations, procurement, R&D -- is essential to informed citizenship.

### Distinguish Reverence from Understanding

- It is possible to respect and support service members while also critically examining the military as an institution. Avoid the trap of unquestioning reverence, which can stifle accountability.
- When you see military-themed advertising, political messaging, or media coverage, ask: Is this providing genuine information, or is it trading on emotional reverence to avoid substantive scrutiny?
- The Reagan Institute's annual defense survey consistently finds that Americans' confidence in the military does not correspond to actual knowledge of the institution. Informed engagement, not reflexive reverence, is what democratic accountability requires.

## 2. Bridge the Gap Personally

### Engage with Veterans and Military Communities

- **Build Personal Connections**: If you don't have a personal connection to the military, seek one out. Volunteer with a local veterans service organization (VFW, American Legion, Team Rubicon, The Mission Continues). Attend a military appreciation event not as a spectator but as a participant and listener.
- **Hire Veterans**: If you are in a position to hire, actively recruit veterans. Beyond the moral case, veterans bring leadership, discipline, and diverse experience to civilian workplaces. Organizations like Hiring Our Heroes (U.S. Chamber of Commerce) and American Corporate Partners provide resources.
- **Ask and Listen**: Ask veterans about their experiences -- not just combat experiences, but the full range of military life: training, deployments, family separation, bureaucratic frustrations, and the transition to civilian life. Listening is the first step to understanding.

### Encourage Service

- Promote all forms of public service -- including military, AmeriCorps, Peace Corps, Teach for America, and volunteer emergency services -- as an honorable and valuable path for young Americans.
- If you are a parent, educator, or mentor, discuss the possibility of military service as a serious option alongside college and career, regardless of your family's socioeconomic background.
- Support policies that make service accessible and rewarding: expanded GI Bill benefits, improved military family support, and voluntary national service programs.

### Visit and Learn

- Many military bases offer public tours, open houses, and community events. Take advantage of these opportunities to see military life firsthand.
- Military museums (e.g., the National Museum of the Army at Fort Belvoir, the National Museum of the Marine Corps at Quantico, the National WWII Museum in New Orleans) provide a window into the history and culture of the armed forces.
- Attend a military commissioning ceremony, change-of-command ceremony, or retirement ceremony if invited. These events illuminate the values and traditions that shape military culture.

## 3. Demand Accountability from Civilian Leaders

### Ask Specific Questions

At town halls, constituent meetings, and through written communications, ask your elected officials specific questions about their oversight responsibilities:

- **On Oversight Capacity**: "What steps is your office taking to ensure you have the technical expertise to oversee the DoD's AI and cyber programs? Do you support creating a Congressional Defense Expertise Institute?"
- **On Politicization**: "What is your position on the politicization of retired military officers? Do you solicit their endorsements? Do you support extending the cooling-off period before retired generals can serve as paid political surrogates?"
- **On War Powers**: "Do you believe the 2001 AUMF should be repealed or replaced? How do you ensure that authorizations for the use of military force are narrowly defined, have clear objectives, and include sunset clauses?"
- **On the Civil-Military Gap**: "What are you doing to bridge the gap between the military and civilian society? Do you support a voluntary national service program?"
- **On AI and Autonomous Weapons**: "What civilian oversight mechanisms exist for the DoD's autonomous weapons programs? Do you support creating an independent commission to review military AI?"

### Advocate for Congressional Capacity

- Support efforts to increase the pay and professionalism of congressional staff, particularly on the defense committees, to improve their ability to conduct oversight. Congressional staff are the front line of civilian control, and they are chronically underpaid and overworked.
- Advocate for full funding of the Government Accountability Office (GAO), the Congressional Budget Office (CBO), and the Congressional Research Service (CRS) -- the analytical institutions that support congressional oversight.
- Support proposals for a Congressional Defense Expertise Institute or equivalent investment in legislative branch defense expertise.

### Monitor Military Operations

- Track ongoing U.S. military operations, including those conducted under the 2001 AUMF. The Costs of War project at Brown University's Watson Institute provides comprehensive tracking of post-9/11 military operations, their costs, and their human consequences.
- Follow the annual NDAA debate: What programs are being funded? What congressional adds are being made? What oversight provisions are included?
- Engage with the DoD Inspector General's reports, which are publicly available and often document significant accountability failures.

## 4. Uphold Norms

### Challenge Politicization

- When you see politicians using the military as a partisan prop -- whether through rallies at military installations, campaign advertisements featuring military imagery, or demands that military leaders take sides in partisan disputes -- call it out. The norm of a non-partisan military is a civic responsibility that citizens, not just officers, must uphold.
- When retired generals or admirals make partisan endorsements, consider the institutional damage rather than the political content. Regardless of which party benefits, the military's credibility as a neutral institution is diminished.
- Push back against efforts to use military policy (transgender service, DEI programs, vaccination mandates) as partisan wedge issues. These may be legitimate policy debates, but weaponizing them damages the military's internal cohesion and external credibility.

### Promote Nuanced Discussion

- In your own conversations, push back against simplistic stereotypes of the military, whether they are overly heroic ("all troops are heroes") or overly critical ("the military is a tool of imperialism"). Encourage a more nuanced and informed public discourse.
- Challenge the assumption that "supporting the troops" means supporting every military operation, budget request, or institutional decision. Genuine support requires accountability.
- When someone claims that criticizing military leadership or questioning a military operation is "unpatriotic" or "unsupportive of the troops," explain that democratic oversight and accountability *are* forms of patriotic support.

## 6. Electoral Actions

### Candidate Evaluation

When evaluating candidates for Congress or the presidency, consider:

- Do they demonstrate substantive knowledge of defense policy, or do they rely on slogans ("strong defense," "rebuild the military") without specifics?
- Do they have a record of exercising meaningful oversight of the military, or do they simply vote for higher budgets?
- Do they respect the norm of military non-partisanship, or do they seek to weaponize the military for political purposes?
- Do they support war powers reform and congressional accountability for military operations?
- Do they distinguish between supporting service members and rubber-stamping every DoD program?
- If they are veterans, do they use their service credentials to deflect policy criticism, or do they engage substantively with defense policy debates?

### Support Veteran Candidates -- But Evaluate Them on Policy

Veterans running for office bring valuable experience to civil-military governance, but military service alone does not qualify someone for civilian leadership. Evaluate veteran candidates on their policy positions, their understanding of civilian control, and their commitment to democratic accountability -- not simply on their rank or combat record.
