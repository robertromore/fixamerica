# Civil-Military Relations: Stakeholders

The relationship between the military and civilian society is shaped by the interactions of several key groups, each with its own culture, interests, and sources of influence. Unlike many policy areas, the stakeholder landscape in civil-military relations involves actors with fundamentally different organizational cultures, time horizons, and conceptions of duty -- making alignment and accountability especially challenging.

## Key Government Stakeholders

### The President and National Security Council

**Role**: The President serves as Commander in Chief under Article II of the Constitution, the apex of the military chain of command. The National Security Council (NSC) staff coordinates military, diplomatic, and intelligence advice for presidential decision-making.

**Primary Interest**: Ensuring the military is an effective and responsive tool of foreign policy; maintaining political control over military operations; avoiding military entanglements that damage the administration politically.

**Source of Power**: Constitutional authority as Commander in Chief; appointment power over the Secretary of Defense, service secretaries, and senior military leaders; control over the nuclear chain of command.

**Key Tensions**:

- Presidents without military experience (a growing norm -- the last president with significant military service was George H.W. Bush) may feel insecure challenging military advice, leading to either excessive deference or compensating overcontrol.
- The NSC staff has grown from approximately 50 members under Eisenhower to over 400 under recent administrations, creating a parallel power center that can marginalize both the Pentagon's civilian leadership and the uniformed military.

### The Secretary of Defense (SecDef)

**Role**: Translating presidential intent into policy; managing the DoD bureaucracy (~3.4 million military and civilian employees); asserting civilian control over the uniformed military.

**Primary Interest**: Maintaining civilian authority over defense policy; implementing the president's defense agenda; managing inter-service rivalries.

**Source of Power**: Statutory authority over the entire Department of Defense under 10 U.S.C. 113; position in the operational chain of command between the President and combatant commanders.

**Key Tensions**:

- The SecDef must simultaneously serve as the President's agent and as an independent check on military overreach -- a dual role that creates inherent strain.
- The 10 U.S.C. 113 requirement that the SecDef be a civilian who has not served in uniform for at least seven years was established to ensure genuine civilian control. The granting of waivers for Generals Mattis (2017) and Austin (2021) eroded this principle and raised questions about whether the position is becoming a military billet in all but name.
- Historically strong SecDefs (McNamara, Rumsfeld, Gates) have been willing to overrule military advice, sometimes with disastrous consequences (McNamara in Vietnam) and sometimes with beneficial results (Gates canceling the F-22 at 187 aircraft).

### Congress (Armed Services Committees)

**Role**: Authorizing defense programs (Armed Services Committees), appropriating funding (Appropriations Committees), and conducting oversight of the Department of Defense.

**Key Actors**:

- **Senate Armed Services Committee (SASC)**: 27 members; controls the annual National Defense Authorization Act (NDAA)
- **House Armed Services Committee (HASC)**: 60+ members; co-authors the NDAA
- **Senate and House Appropriations Subcommittees on Defense**: Control defense spending levels
- **Senate Foreign Relations Committee and House Foreign Affairs Committee**: Jurisdiction over war powers and security assistance
- **Intelligence Committees**: Oversight of covert military operations and intelligence community

**Primary Interest**: Authorizing and funding the military; oversight; ensuring the military reflects national values; protecting defense jobs in members' districts.

**Source of Power**: Constitutional "power of the purse" and oversight authority; ability to authorize or block weapons programs; confirmation power over senior appointments (Senate); subpoena authority.

**Key Tensions**:

- Members of the Armed Services and Appropriations Committees are the primary recipients of defense industry campaign contributions (3-5x more than non-committee members), creating a potential conflict between oversight responsibilities and financial interests.
- The decline of military veterans in Congress (from ~75% in the 1970s to ~18% today) means fewer members have the experiential basis to challenge military claims or evaluate strategy.
- Congressional staff on defense committees are often young, underpaid (~$55,000-$80,000 for professional staff members), and serve short tenures, creating a persistent knowledge gap relative to the Pentagon and defense industry.

### The Uniformed Military

**Role**: Maintaining military readiness and effectiveness; providing expert military advice to civilian leaders; executing lawful orders; preserving institutional health and professional standards.

**Key Actors**:

| Position | Primary Function |
|---|---|
| **Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff (CJCS)** | Principal military advisor to the President and SecDef under Goldwater-Nichols; does not exercise operational command |
| **Service Chiefs** (Army, Navy, Marine Corps, Air Force, Space Force) | Organize, train, and equip their respective services; advise on service-specific capabilities |
| **Combatant Commanders** (INDOPACOM, EUCOM, CENTCOM, etc.) | Exercise operational command over forces in their geographic or functional areas |
| **Senior Enlisted Advisors** | Represent the perspectives and welfare of the enlisted force |

**Primary Interest**: Military readiness and effectiveness; maintaining institutional autonomy in professional matters (promotion, doctrine, training); expanding or protecting service budget share; preserving the military's reputation and public trust.

**Source of Power**: Monopoly on military expertise and operational command of forces; statutory advisory role (10 U.S.C. 151-155); public trust and credibility; institutional permanence (military careers span decades, political appointees serve 2-4 years).

**Key Tensions**:

- **Duty to Advise vs. Duty to Obey**: Military leaders have both an obligation to provide candid, independent advice to civilian leaders *and* an obligation to execute lawful orders even when they disagree with the decision. The boundary between these obligations is the most fraught issue in civil-military relations. General H.R. McMaster's *Dereliction of Duty* argued that the Joint Chiefs failed in Vietnam by not forcefully presenting their objections; critics counter that the military's role is to advise, not to overrule.
- **Institutional Interests**: Each service competes for budget share, mission roles, and organizational survival. These institutional interests can distort the advice provided to civilian leaders, as services advocate for programs and force structures that serve institutional rather than national strategic needs.

### Internal Government Conflicts

- **SecDef vs. Service Chiefs**: The classic tension. The SecDef attempts to impose strategic and budgetary discipline from a civilian, enterprise-wide perspective, while the service chiefs advocate for their own service's budget, culture, and programs. Secretary Gates's cancellation of the F-22 over Air Force objections and his push for mine-resistant vehicles (MRAPs) over Army resistance illustrate this dynamic.
- **President/NSC vs. Joint Chiefs**: The President may desire a military option that the uniformed leadership believes is ill-advised, too risky, or unsustainable. This creates a tension between the military's duty to provide candid advice and its duty to execute lawful orders. The 2009 Afghanistan strategy review, in which President Obama felt the military was trying to "box him in" to a troop surge, exemplifies this tension.
- **Congress vs. DoD**: Congress may block DoD efforts to cut a weapon system or close a base to protect local jobs, an example of political interests overriding strategic ones. The repeated blocking of Base Realignment and Closure (BRAC) rounds since 2005 and Congress's insistence on funding Abrams tanks the Army did not request are prominent examples.

## Societal Stakeholders

### The American Public

**Primary Interest**: National security without excessive cost or entanglement; trust that the military is competent and non-partisan.

**Source of Power**: Public opinion, which shapes the political environment for both civilian and military leaders; voting power; willingness (or unwillingness) to volunteer for military service.

**Key Characteristic**: The public's relationship with the military is characterized by high trust combined with low knowledge -- a combination that inhibits effective democratic accountability. The Reagan Institute's 2023 survey found that while 71% of Americans expressed confidence in the military, only 13% could name a current military leader, and fewer than 25% could identify any ongoing U.S. military operation.

### The Media

**Primary Interest**: Reporting on military operations, budgets, and scandals; acting as a public watchdog.

**Source of Power**: Ability to shape public perception and drive political accountability through investigative journalism and editorial coverage.

**Key Tensions**: The military controls access to information and operational areas through embedding policies and classification. The post-9/11 trend toward embedded journalism created access but also fostered co-dependence and identification with military units that could compromise journalistic independence. The decline of dedicated Pentagon correspondents at major news outlets has reduced the institutional capacity for sustained, expert military reporting.

### Veterans and Veterans Service Organizations (VSOs)

**Key Actors**: American Legion, Veterans of Foreign Wars (VFW), Disabled American Veterans (DAV), Iraq and Afghanistan Veterans of America (IAVA), Concerned Veterans for America.

**Primary Interest**: Ensuring benefits and recognition for service; acting as a bridge between the military and civilian society; advocating for veterans' policy priorities.

**Source of Power**: High moral authority; powerful lobbying and grassroots networks; significant membership (American Legion: ~1.6 million; VFW: ~1.3 million); presence in virtually every congressional district.

**Key Role in Civil-Military Relations**: VSOs serve as one of the few institutional bridges between military and civilian culture. However, they also have a vested interest in maintaining the military's exalted public image, which can inhibit critical oversight.

### The "Attentive Public" (Think Tanks, Academia, Policy Experts)

**Key Actors**: Center for Strategic and International Studies (CSIS), Center for a New American Security (CNAS), RAND Corporation, Brookings Institution, Council on Foreign Relations, War on the Rocks, Quincy Institute for Responsible Statecraft, the Triangle Institute for Security Studies (TISS).

**Primary Interest**: Analyzing and debating civil-military issues; shaping elite opinion and policy proposals; maintaining credibility and funding.

**Source of Power**: Expertise and influence in policy circles and the media; personnel who move between think tanks and government positions.

**Key Tension**: Think tanks that receive defense industry funding face structural conflicts of interest in assessing military programs and civil-military issues. The same organizations that study civil-military relations also depend on relationships with the Pentagon and defense industry for access, funding, and career opportunities for their staff.

## Emerging Stakeholders in the Tech Era

### Major Tech Companies

**Key Actors**: Microsoft, Google, Amazon (AWS), SpaceX, Palantir, Oracle, Anduril Industries.

**Primary Interest**: Securing lucrative defense contracts (especially for cloud, AI, and space); avoiding employee backlash over military work; maintaining brand reputation.

**Source of Power**: Ownership and control of the core digital infrastructure (cloud, AI models, satellite networks) upon which the future military depends. Unlike traditional defense contractors, these firms have commercial businesses that dwarf their defense revenue, giving them leverage the government has not previously faced.

### Defense Tech Startups and Venture Capital

**Key Actors**: Anduril Industries, Shield AI, Kratos Defense, Rebellion Defense, Primer AI; venture capital firms including Andreessen Horowitz, Lux Capital, and Founders Fund.

**Primary Interest**: Winning DoD contracts to scale their technology; achieving profitable exits (acquisition or IPO); disrupting traditional defense contractors.

**Source of Power**: Agility and cutting-edge technology that the traditional defense industry lacks; access to venture capital funding that enables rapid development.

### AI Ethics and Arms Control Community

**Key Actors**: Campaign to Stop Killer Robots, International Committee for Robot Arms Control, Future of Life Institute, AI Now Institute, Human Rights Watch Arms Division.

**Primary Interest**: Preventing the development of fully autonomous lethal weapons ("killer robots"); ensuring meaningful human control over the use of force; establishing international norms and treaties.

**Source of Power**: Moral authority; influence in international forums (UN Convention on Certain Conventional Weapons); relationships with progressive legislators; media visibility.

## The DoD-Silicon Valley Culture Clash

The new conflict dynamic between the Pentagon and the tech sector creates a distinctive stakeholder tension:

- **DoD Wants**: Reliable, secure, long-term technology solutions; exclusive or priority access to cutting-edge capabilities; compliance with classification and security requirements.
- **Silicon Valley Wants**: To move fast, break things, and avoid getting bogged down in bureaucracy; to maintain commercial market optionality; to avoid employee backlash over military work.
- **The Conflict**: This culture clash makes partnership difficult. Tech company employees may protest working on military projects (e.g., Google and Project Maven in 2018; Microsoft employees protesting HoloLens use by the military in 2019), while the DoD's slow, risk-averse procurement process frustrates startups. The Pentagon's Chief Digital and Artificial Intelligence Office (CDAO), established in 2022, and the Defense Innovation Unit (DIU) represent attempts to bridge this gap, but the cultural differences remain profound.

## Power Analysis

| Stakeholder | Political Power | Information Access | Organizational Capacity | Permanence |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| President/NSC | Very High | Very High | High | Low (4-8 years) |
| Secretary of Defense | High | Very High | High | Low (2-4 years avg.) |
| Congress (defense committees) | Very High | Medium | Medium | Medium (turnover) |
| Uniformed Military (JCS, Service Chiefs) | High | Very High | Very High | High (institutional) |
| Major Tech Companies | Growing | High (proprietary) | High | High |
| Veterans/VSOs | Medium | Low | High | High |
| Think Tanks | Medium | Medium | Medium | Medium |
| General Public | Low (diffuse) | Low | Very Low | N/A |
| Arms Control/Ethics Community | Low | Low | Low-Medium | Medium |

The fundamental imbalance in civil-military relations is temporal: political appointees serve for 2-4 years, while the military institution is permanent. This gives the military an inherent advantage in any sustained bureaucratic contest -- it can wait out civilian leaders it disagrees with, a dynamic that scholars call "slow-rolling."
