# Civil-Military Relations: Resources

This section provides a curated list of foundational academic works, key reports, important policy documents, and organizations that focus on the study of U.S. civil-military relations.

---

## Foundational Academic Works

- **Huntington, Samuel P. *The Soldier and the State: The Theory and Politics of Civil-Military Relations*. Cambridge, MA: Harvard University Press, 1957.**
    - The seminal, foundational text of the field. Huntington's theory of "objective civilian control," which advocates for a professional, autonomous, and non-partisan military, remains the starting point for all modern debate. Huntington argued that the best way to ensure civilian control was to maximize military professionalism, creating a distinct military sphere that would voluntarily subordinate itself to civilian authority.

- **Janowitz, Morris. *The Professional Soldier: A Social and Political Portrait*. Glencoe, IL: Free Press, 1960.**
    - A classic sociological study of the military as a profession, focusing on the internal culture and values of the officer corps. Janowitz advocated for a "constabulary" model in which the military remains integrated with civilian society rather than insulated from it -- a direct counterpoint to Huntington's vision.

- **Feaver, Peter D. *Armed Servants: Agency, Oversight, and Civil-Military Relations*. Cambridge, MA: Harvard University Press, 2003.**
    - A modern counterpoint to Huntington. Feaver's "agency theory" model posits that the relationship is a constant negotiation between a civilian "principal" and a military "agent." It focuses on the mechanisms civilians use to monitor and control the military, including "intrusive" oversight (audits, inspections) and "non-intrusive" oversight (relying on professional norms).

- **Kohn, Richard H. "The Erosion of Civilian Control of the Military in the United States Today." *Naval War College Review* 55, no. 3 (2002): 9-59.**
    - An influential essay that raised alarms about a post-Cold War crisis in civil-military relations, arguing that the military had become too influential and politically active. Kohn, a historian who served as chief of Air Force History, provided one of the first systematic accounts of how norms of non-partisanship had eroded.

- **McMaster, H.R. *Dereliction of Duty: Johnson, McNamara, the Joint Chiefs of Staff, and the Lies That Led to Vietnam*. New York: HarperCollins, 1997.**
    - McMaster's doctoral dissertation argued that the Joint Chiefs of Staff failed in their constitutional duty to provide candid military advice to President Johnson during the escalation of the Vietnam War, acquiescing in a flawed strategy rather than risking confrontation with civilian leaders. The book has profoundly influenced how military officers think about their obligation to "speak truth to power."

- **Cohen, Eliot A. *Supreme Command: Soldiers, Statesmen, and Leadership in Wartime*. New York: Free Press, 2002.**
    - Cohen argues against the Huntington model, contending that the most effective wartime leaders (Lincoln, Clemenceau, Churchill, Ben-Gurion) exercised deep, sustained, and sometimes intrusive involvement in military affairs. He calls this "unequal dialogue" -- civilian leaders must engage substantively with military strategy, not simply defer to professional expertise.

- **Brooks, Rosa. *How Everything Became War and the Military Became Everything*. New York: Simon & Schuster, 2016.**
    - Examines how the post-9/11 expansion of military roles into nation-building, humanitarian assistance, development, and diplomacy has blurred the line between military and civilian functions, with dangerous implications for democratic governance.

- **Golby, Jim, Kyle Dropp, and Peter Feaver. "Listening to the Commander in Chief: Individual-Level Preferences for Military Action and the Role of Military Cues." *Armed Forces & Society* 44, no. 1 (2018): 86-116.**
    - Empirical research demonstrating that retired military endorsements significantly influence public opinion on military policy and political candidates, providing the evidence base for concerns about military politicization.

## Key Historical and Analytical Works

- **Bacevich, Andrew J. *The New American Militarism: How Americans Are Seduced by War*. New York: Oxford University Press, 2005.**
    - Argues that a culture of militarism has pervaded American society since the Vietnam era, manifesting in uncritical reverence for the military and a willingness to use force as a first resort.

- **Ricks, Thomas E. *The Generals: American Military Command from World War II to Today*. New York: Penguin Press, 2012.**
    - A study of the decline of accountability in senior military leadership, arguing that the U.S. military has lost the willingness to relieve underperforming generals that characterized World War II-era leadership.

- **Urben, Heidi A. "Civil-Military Relations in a Time of War: Party, Politics, and the Profession of Arms." PhD diss., Georgetown University, 2010.**
    - One of the most comprehensive empirical studies of political attitudes within the active duty military, documenting the officer corps' conservative lean and its implications for civil-military relations.

- **Schake, Kori, and James Mattis, eds. *Warriors and Citizens: American Views of Our Military*. Stanford: Hoover Institution Press, 2016.**
    - A collection of essays examining the civil-military gap from multiple perspectives, based on original survey research comparing military and civilian attitudes.

## Key Reports and Government Sources

- **National Defense Strategy (NDS) and National Security Strategy (NSS)**
    - These quadrennial strategic documents frame the missions, threats, and priorities that shape civil-military relations, including guidance on the relationship between civilian strategy and military planning.

- **GAO Reports on Professional Military Education (PME)**
    - The Government Accountability Office periodically reviews the curriculum and effectiveness of the military's war colleges and other PME institutions, most recently in GAO-20-323.

- **Congressional Research Service. "Military Officer Personnel Management: Key Concepts and Statutory Provisions." CRS Report R44496.**
    - A comprehensive overview of the legal framework governing military officer careers, promotions, and assignments, including the role of civilian authority in shaping the officer corps.

- **National Security Commission on Artificial Intelligence. *Final Report*. Washington, DC: NSCAI, 2021.**
    - The most comprehensive governmental assessment of AI's implications for national security, including significant discussion of civilian oversight challenges.

- **Department of Defense. *DoD Directive 3000.09: Autonomy in Weapon Systems*. Updated 2023.**
    - The primary DoD policy governing human control over autonomous and semi-autonomous weapons systems.

- **Reagan National Defense Survey (Annual).**
    - Annual survey of public attitudes toward the military, defense spending, and national security. The most comprehensive ongoing tracking of public confidence in the military.

- **Triangle Institute for Security Studies (TISS). *Project on the Gap Between the Military and Civilian Society*. Multiple publications, 1998-2010.**
    - The most significant academic survey project on civil-military attitudes, producing foundational data on partisan identification, cultural attitudes, and mutual perceptions.

## Think Tanks and Research Organizations

### Academic Centers

- **Triangle Institute for Security Studies (TISS)**: A consortium of universities (Duke, UNC-Chapel Hill, NC State) that is the leading academic center for the study of civil-military relations. TISS produced the landmark surveys that documented the growing partisan gap in the officer corps.
- **Center for Civil-Military Relations, Naval Postgraduate School**: Part of the Defense Security Cooperation University; focuses on international civil-military relations and security sector governance.
- **Clements Center for National Security, University of Texas at Austin**: Hosts research and conferences on civil-military relations, military history, and defense policy.

### Policy Think Tanks

- **Center for Strategic and International Studies (CSIS)**: Publishes reports and hosts events on civil-military relations, military professionalism, and defense governance. The International Security Program frequently addresses civil-military issues.
- **Center for a New American Security (CNAS)**: The Military, Veterans, and Society Program focuses on the health of the All-Volunteer Force, the civil-military gap, and military family policy.
- **RAND Corporation**: Produces extensive research on military personnel policy, recruitment, civil-military relations, and military culture. Key reports include studies on military recruitment demographics and the civil-military gap.
- **Brookings Institution**: The Foreign Policy program publishes analyses of civil-military relations, war powers, and defense governance.
- **Council on Foreign Relations**: Hosts events and publishes articles on civil-military relations, including special reports on military politicization and war powers reform.
- **Quincy Institute for Responsible Statecraft**: Advocates for democratic accountability in military affairs, including war powers reform and reduced executive authority over the use of force.

### Online Publications

- **War on the Rocks**: The leading online publication for civil-military relations debate, featuring articles by scholars, practitioners, and military officers. Essential reading for following current debates.
- **Lawfare Blog**: Focuses on law, national security, and the intersection of military and civilian authority, including war powers and AI governance.
- **Texas National Security Review**: A peer-reviewed journal publishing academic-quality analysis on defense and security issues, including civil-military relations.
- **The Bridge (West Point Modern War Institute)**: Features articles on military leadership, ethics, and civil-military dynamics written by active duty officers and scholars.

## Key Legal Authorities

- **Article I, Section 8, U.S. Constitution** -- Congressional war powers, military funding, and regulation
- **Article II, Section 2, U.S. Constitution** -- Commander in Chief clause
- **10 U.S.C. 113** -- Secretary of Defense civilian requirement (7-year cooling period)
- **10 U.S.C. 151-155** -- Joint Chiefs of Staff organization and advisory role (Goldwater-Nichols)
- **10 U.S.C. 162-167** -- Combatant commands and chain of command
- **50 U.S.C. 1541-1548** -- War Powers Resolution (1973)
- **Authorization for Use of Military Force (2001)** -- P.L. 107-40
- **DoD Directive 1344.10** -- Political Activities by Members of the Armed Forces
- **DoD Directive 3000.09** -- Autonomy in Weapon Systems
- **Uniform Code of Military Justice (UCMJ), Article 88** -- Contempt toward officials (applicable to commissioned officers)
