# Civil-Military Relations: Root Causes

## Systemic Analysis

The current strains in U.S. civil-military relations are not the result of a single failure but of several long-term, interlocking trends. These include the structural separation of the military from society, the increasing complexity of warfare, the erosion of shared norms in a polarized political environment, the expanding role of the private sector in warfighting, and the institutional weakening of congressional oversight capacity. Each root cause reinforces the others, creating a self-perpetuating cycle that makes incremental reform difficult.

## Root Cause 1: The All-Volunteer Force (AVF) and the "Warrior Caste"

The decision to end conscription in 1973 and create the AVF is the single most important root cause of the modern civil-military gap.

### Professionalism at the Cost of Separation

The AVF created a more professional, experienced, and effective military force. However, by making service a career choice rather than a common civic duty, it severed the primary link between the military and the broader society.

- **The Self-Reproducing Force**: With few external incentives to join, the military has increasingly relied on recruiting from within its own community. With ~80% of recruits having a family member who served, the force has become a "warrior caste" -- a subculture with its own values, traditions, and experiences that are increasingly alien to the rest of the country. The Rand Corporation found that military recruitment is concentrated in just 13% of U.S. counties.
- **Lack of Shared Experience**: With < 0.5% of the population serving, very few civilians -- including political elites, journalists, and academics -- have any direct experience with the military. This leads to a public that either romanticizes the military or misunderstands it, and a political class that lacks the experiential knowledge to conduct effective oversight.
- **Elite Disengagement**: The gap is sharpest among elites. Students at Ivy League and other top-tier universities, where future political leaders, media figures, and corporate executives are concentrated, almost never serve in the military. ROTC programs were expelled from many elite campuses during Vietnam and only slowly returned (Columbia University reinstated ROTC in 2011, Harvard in 2016). A 2020 study found that fewer than 0.5% of graduates from Ivy League schools entered military service.
- **The "Praise but Don't Join" Culture**: Americans express high admiration for the military but are unwilling to serve or to encourage their children to serve. A 2023 DoD survey found that only 9% of eligible young Americans expressed interest in military service, the lowest figure since tracking began.

### Intergenerational Effects

The AVF has created a self-reinforcing cycle: military families produce military recruits, who form military families, who produce the next generation of recruits. Over time, this creates a force whose values, political attitudes, and cultural experiences diverge further from civilian society with each generation. The military's physical concentration on remote bases further isolates service members and their families from civilian communities.

## Root Cause 2: The Increasing Complexity and Speed of Warfare

As war becomes more technological, it becomes less accessible to civilian understanding and control.

### The "Expertise" Gap

Civilian leaders are, by design, generalists. They cannot be expected to have deep technical expertise in hypersonic missile design, quantum computing, or cyber network defense. This creates an "expertise gap" where they become heavily reliant on the advice of uniformed military and technical experts.

- **Institutional Knowledge Drain**: Congressional staff turnover means that the institutional memory for defense oversight is shallow. By the time a staffer develops real expertise in a program, they often leave for higher-paying private sector positions, frequently with the defense industry itself.
- **Pentagon Information Advantage**: The Department of Defense produces an overwhelming volume of briefings, reports, and data that civilian overseers must process. The asymmetry of information between the DoD -- with its hundreds of thousands of acquisition professionals, analysts, and subject matter experts -- and a congressional committee staff of perhaps 30-50 people is enormous.

### The Risk of Deference

This expertise gap can lead to "expert deference," where civilian leaders feel unqualified to challenge the military's recommendations. This subtly erodes the principle of civilian control, as de facto decision-making power shifts to the experts.

- **Historical Examples**: Secretary of State Dean Acheson later acknowledged that during the Korean War, civilian leaders often deferred to military judgments they did not fully understand. More recently, the 2009-2010 Afghanistan strategy review saw allegations that General Stanley McChrystal's team leaked their troop request to pressure President Obama into accepting the military's preferred option.
- **The "McChrystal Affair"**: General McChrystal's 2009 speech in London publicly advocating for a troop surge, before President Obama had made a decision, was widely viewed as an attempt to box in the President. McChrystal was later relieved for disparaging comments about civilian officials published in *Rolling Stone*.

### The Challenge of Machine Speed

AI-enabled and cyber warfare operate at speeds that defy human cognition. A traditional oversight model where a President or Congress deliberates before authorizing action is unworkable when a conflict can escalate in milliseconds. This creates immense pressure to pre-delegate authority to automated systems or to military commanders, further removing civilians from the decision loop.

- **Pre-Delegation Precedent**: During the Cold War, certain nuclear release authorities were pre-delegated to military commanders in case the President was killed in a first strike. The expansion of pre-delegation into AI and cyber domains raises analogous concerns about civilian control.
- **The "Flash War" Scenario**: Analysts have described scenarios in which AI-controlled military systems on opposing sides interact and escalate faster than human decision-makers can intervene -- analogous to financial "flash crashes" but with lethal consequences.

## Root Cause 3: Political Polarization and the Erosion of Norms

The military, once seen as a bastion of non-partisanship, is being pulled into the country's broader political polarization.

### The Politicization of the Officer Corps

- **Historical Norm**: From World War II through the 1980s, senior military officers assiduously avoided public political statements. General George C. Marshall reportedly never even voted, to preserve his reputation for nonpartisanship. This norm began to erode in the 1990s.
- **Growing Partisan Identification**: The Triangle Institute for Security Studies (TISS) surveys documented a dramatic shift: in the 1976 survey, 33% of military officers identified as Republican and 12% as Democrat; by 1998-1999, 64% identified as Republican and only 8% as Democrat. This shift has continued, though the partisan composition of the enlisted force remains more balanced.
- **Endorsement Culture**: The practice of retired generals endorsing presidential candidates began modestly (31 retired generals endorsed Bob Dole in 1996) and escalated dramatically (200+ endorsements in 2016). Each escalation normalizes the next, eroding the institutional norm against political activity.

### The Public's Partisan Lens

The public increasingly views the military through a partisan lens. Gallup surveys show that Republican confidence in the military has remained relatively stable (~74% in 2024), while Democratic confidence has dropped sharply (from ~68% in 2020 to ~42% in 2024). This divergence was driven by politicized debates over:

- The military's response to the January 6 Capitol breach
- Diversity, equity, and inclusion (DEI) initiatives in the military
- The military's COVID-19 vaccination mandate
- "Critical Race Theory" allegations related to military education programs
- The chaotic withdrawal from Afghanistan in August 2021

### Politicians Using the Military

Politicians from both parties increasingly use the military as a political symbol, either by attacking its leadership for being too "woke" or too "politicized," or by using troops in uniform as a backdrop for partisan events. This treats the military as just another political interest group rather than a neutral instrument of the state.

- **Weaponization of Military Policy**: Issues like the transgender service ban, DEI programs, and the vaccine mandate became partisan flashpoints that forced military leaders into politically visible positions regardless of which side they took.
- **Congressional Holds**: Individual senators have used holds on military promotions to advance political agendas (e.g., Senator Tommy Tuberville's 2023 hold on approximately 450 general and flag officer promotions over the DoD's abortion travel policy), directly using military personnel as leverage in partisan disputes.

## Root Cause 4: The Rise of the Private Sector in Warfighting

### From Supplier to Operator

For most of history, the private sector built weapons and sold them to the government. Today, in domains like cyber and space, private companies are not just suppliers; they are integral to warfighting itself.

- **Cloud Computing**: The DoD's Joint Warfighting Cloud Capability (JWCC) contract distributes sensitive military computing across Amazon Web Services, Microsoft Azure, Google Cloud, and Oracle -- making private companies custodians of classified military data and operational capabilities.
- **Satellite Communications**: SpaceX's Starlink provides military communications in active conflict zones (as demonstrated in Ukraine), yet the terms of service and operational control rest with a private company, not the government.
- **AI Development**: The most advanced AI capabilities are being developed in the private sector (by OpenAI, Google DeepMind, Anthropic, and others), not in government laboratories. The DoD must negotiate for access to these capabilities rather than developing them in-house.

### Accountability Gap

This creates a new civil-military-commercial relationship that is not well understood or governed. How does Congress oversee a military operation that relies on a private company's network? Who is accountable when a commercial satellite used by the military is attacked? What happens when a tech company CEO makes a unilateral decision about military communications (as Elon Musk did when he initially restricted Starlink access for a Ukrainian military operation near Crimea)?

- **The Google Project Maven Controversy (2018)**: Google employees protested the company's participation in Project Maven, a DoD AI program for analyzing drone surveillance footage. Google withdrew from the contract -- demonstrating that private companies can effectively veto military capabilities based on employee or shareholder pressure.
- **Regulatory Vacuum**: There is no comprehensive legal framework governing the military's dependence on commercial technology platforms. The existing defense acquisition regulations were designed for a world of government-owned, government-operated weapons systems, not one in which critical military capabilities run on commercial cloud infrastructure.

## Root Cause 5: Institutional Weakening of Congressional Oversight

### Capacity Decline

Congress's ability to serve as an effective check on military power has eroded significantly:

- **Staff Reductions**: Congressional committee staffing has been cut significantly since the 1990s. The House Armed Services Committee's professional staff is roughly half the size it was during the Cold War, while the complexity and budget of the military have grown enormously.
- **Term Limits and Turnover**: In the House, committee assignments and chairmanships turn over more frequently than in past decades, preventing members from developing deep expertise.
- **War Powers Abdication**: Congress has effectively ceded its war declaration power to the executive through the open-ended 2001 AUMF, which has been used to authorize military operations far beyond its original scope.
- **Budget Dysfunction**: The regular budget process has broken down, with the government frequently operating on continuing resolutions rather than enacted appropriations. This dysfunction reduces Congress's ability to exercise its "power of the purse" as a meaningful oversight tool.

## Causal Chain of Eroding Civilian Control

```text
All-Volunteer Force Creates a Socially Isolated Military
    |
Public and Elites Lack Military Experience, Creating a "Knowledge Gap"
    |
Warfare Becomes More Technologically Complex (AI, Cyber, Space)
    |
Knowledge Gap Widens into an "Expertise Gap"
    |
Civilian Leaders Defer to Military/Technical Experts
    |
Private Sector Becomes Critical to Warfighting, Adding New Unaccountable Actors
    |
Politicization Erodes Trust in Both Civilian and Military Leadership
    |
Congress Lacks Capacity and Will to Exercise Robust Oversight
    |
Effective Civilian Oversight Weakens
```

## Interaction Effects

These root causes do not operate in isolation. They interact in ways that make the system resistant to reform:

- The **AVF's social isolation** deepens the **expertise gap** because civilian leaders have no personal military experience to draw on when evaluating military advice.
- **Technological complexity** amplifies the **private sector's role**, because the most advanced capabilities are developed commercially, further removing military operations from direct government control.
- **Political polarization** makes it harder for Congress to exercise oversight, because any criticism of military leadership is immediately weaponized by the opposing party.
- The **erosion of norms** against politicization makes retired officers more willing to enter partisan politics, which further erodes public confidence in military nonpartisanship, creating a feedback loop.
