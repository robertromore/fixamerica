# Civil-Military Relations: Solutions

Strengthening U.S. civil-military relations requires a multi-faceted approach that aims to bridge the gap between the military and society, enhance civilian expertise, adapt oversight mechanisms for the 21st century, and rebuild the norms of non-partisanship that are essential to democratic accountability. The solutions proposed here address the root causes identified in this analysis and are designed to be mutually reinforcing.

---

## 1. Bridge the Civil-Military Gap

### A. Establish a National Service Program

**Problem Addressed**: The All-Volunteer Force has created a military that is socially isolated from the society it serves. Fewer than 0.5% of Americans serve, and ~80% of recruits come from military families, creating a self-reproducing "warrior caste" disconnected from the broader public (Root Cause 1).

**Proposal**: Create a voluntary, incentive-driven national service program that encourages all young Americans to complete one year of service in either a military or civilian capacity.

**Key Provisions**:

- Establish a "National Service Fellowship" that offers significant benefits in exchange for one year of service:
    - Full college tuition at any public university (or equivalent credit at private institutions)
    - Federal student loan forgiveness for existing debt
    - Hiring preference for federal civil service positions
    - A post-service stipend of $10,000
- Civilian service options would include: public school teaching assistants, conservation corps, elder care, disaster response, and infrastructure maintenance
- Military service would remain a multi-year commitment but would be part of the same national service framework, with enhanced benefits reflecting the longer commitment
- Program administration through an expanded Corporation for National and Community Service, renamed the "National Service Administration"

**Evidence Base**:

- The current AmeriCorps program (approximately 75,000 participants annually) demonstrates the viability of civilian national service, but its scale is far too small to bridge the civil-military gap.
- Germany's former *Wehrpflicht* (military conscription) with a civilian alternative (*Zivildienst*) provided a model for dual-track national service before it was suspended in 2011. Polling shows a majority of Germans now support reinstating some form of mandatory service.
- Israel's universal service model, while specific to its security context, demonstrates that shared service creates a common civic identity across social classes.
- A 2023 Gallup poll found that 63% of Americans support a voluntary national service program with strong incentives.

**Anticipated Impact**: Fosters a shared sense of civic duty; exposes a broader cross-section of society to service; creates a larger pool of future civilian leaders with some connection to the military or public service; reduces the military's reliance on recruiting from within its own community.

### B. Expand and Incentivize Civil-Military Exchange Programs

**Problem Addressed**: Military and civilian leaders operate in separate professional worlds with little understanding of each other's cultures, constraints, and decision-making processes (Root Causes 1 and 2).

**Proposal**: Dramatically expand programs that place mid-career military officers in civilian organizations and vice-versa.

**Key Provisions**:

- Increase funding for the **Secretary of Defense Executive Fellows** program (currently ~15 slots per year) to 50 slots, placing officers in Fortune 500 companies, major technology firms, and leading research universities for a one-year fellowship
- Create a parallel **Civilian Defense Fellowship** to bring 50 mid-career civilian professionals (from tech, finance, law, and public policy) into the Pentagon for a one-year "tour of duty" with meaningful responsibility and appropriate security clearances
- Establish a **Congressional-Military Exchange** placing 10 military fellows per year on congressional defense committee staffs, and 10 congressional staffers per year in combatant command headquarters
- Create a **Media-Military Fellowship** placing 5 journalists per year in military units (not as embedded reporters, but as working members of the command) and 5 military public affairs officers in major news organizations

**Evidence Base**:

- The UK's interchange program between the military and civil service is widely regarded as strengthening both institutions.
- The existing White House Fellows program demonstrates that placing mid-career professionals in government roles produces lasting cross-sector relationships.
- The Defense Innovation Unit (DIU) in Silicon Valley has shown that co-location of military and civilian tech professionals improves mutual understanding.

**Anticipated Impact**: Builds personal relationships and cross-cultural understanding between the military and key civilian sectors; creates a cadre of civilians with defense expertise and military officers with civilian-sector sophistication.

## 2. Enhance Civilian Expertise and Oversight

### A. Create a Congressional Defense Expertise Institute

**Problem Addressed**: Congress lacks the institutional expertise to effectively oversee the modern military. Committee staff are underpaid, undertrained, and serve short tenures, creating a persistent knowledge gap that undermines the legislative branch's constitutional oversight role (Root Cause 5).

**Proposal**: Create a dedicated, non-partisan educational institution -- a "Congressional War College" -- to provide advanced, in-depth education on defense policy, budgeting, and technology to congressional staffers and members.

**Key Provisions**:

- Charter a new, independent educational institution reporting to the legislative branch, modeled on the National War College but serving Congress
- Offer a one-year, full-time master's degree program for senior committee and personal staff, with participants continuing to draw their congressional salary plus a supplemental stipend
- Offer shorter executive education programs (2-4 weeks) for members of Congress and senior staff
- Curriculum to include: defense strategy, military operations, weapons acquisition, budgeting, technology assessment, civil-military relations theory, classified briefings, and war-gaming exercises
- Faculty drawn from a mix of academics, former military officers, and former senior defense officials
- Graduates would receive a pay premium and promotion preference within the congressional staff system

**Evidence Base**:

- The military's own Professional Military Education system produces officers who spend years studying strategy, operations, and defense policy. Congress has no equivalent investment in its own oversight workforce.
- The Congressional Research Service and Congressional Budget Office provide excellent analytical support, but they cannot substitute for the deep expertise that comes from sustained, full-time education.
- Other countries (UK, Germany, France) invest more heavily in parliamentary defense expertise through dedicated research services and staff training programs.

**Anticipated Impact**: Creates a permanent cadre of highly knowledgeable civilian staffers on Capitol Hill, improving the quality of legislation and oversight; reduces the knowledge asymmetry between Congress and the Pentagon.

### B. Establish a Civilian Oversight Board for Military AI and Autonomous Systems

**Problem Addressed**: The speed, complexity, and opacity of AI and autonomous weapons systems are outpacing existing civilian oversight mechanisms. Civilian leaders face an "expertise gap" that risks leading to uncritical deference to military and technical experts (Root Causes 2 and 4).

**Proposal**: Create an independent, external body to provide technical and ethical oversight of the DoD's development and use of AI and autonomous systems.

**Key Provisions**:

- Charter a "National Commission on Military AI" composed of 15 members: 5 leading civilian AI researchers, 3 ethicists/philosophers, 2 military technology experts, 2 international law scholars, and 3 former senior military leaders
- The commission would have statutory authority to review classified AI programs and autonomous weapons development
- Required to provide semiannual public reports to Congress and the President on the state of military AI, including assessments of ethical compliance, civilian control mechanisms, and international comparison
- Authority to issue binding "human control requirements" for any AI system that could autonomously select and engage targets
- Sunset provision of 10 years, with reauthorization required by Congress

**Evidence Base**:

- The National Security Commission on Artificial Intelligence (NSCAI, 2019-2021), chaired by former Google CEO Eric Schmidt, demonstrated the value of external expert review but lacked ongoing authority.
- The UK's AI Safety Institute provides a model for independent government AI oversight.
- DoD Directive 3000.09 (Autonomy in Weapon Systems) establishes human control requirements but relies on internal DoD compliance rather than independent external verification.

**Anticipated Impact**: Addresses the "expertise gap" by providing civilian leaders with an independent source of technical advice; ensures that ethical considerations are integrated into AI development from the start; provides a mechanism for democratic accountability over the most consequential military technology decisions.

### C. Strengthen War Powers and AUMF Reform

**Problem Addressed**: Congress has effectively ceded its constitutional war powers authority to the executive branch through open-ended Authorizations for Use of Military Force (Root Cause 5).

**Proposal**: Replace the 2001 and 2002 AUMFs with a new, narrowly scoped authorization framework.

**Key Provisions**:

- Repeal the 2001 and 2002 AUMFs
- Require a new, specific AUMF for any sustained military operation (defined as more than 60 days or more than 1,000 troops deployed) that names the enemy, defines the geographic scope, sets objectives, and includes a 3-year sunset clause requiring reauthorization
- Require 48-hour advance notification to the Gang of Eight (congressional leadership and intelligence committee chairs) for any new military action, with full committee notification within 14 days
- Create a dedicated War Powers Compliance Office within the Government Accountability Office to monitor and report on executive compliance with war powers requirements
- Establish a legal framework for congressional oversight of cyber operations, including mandatory reporting of significant cyber incidents and offensive cyber campaigns

**Evidence Base**:

- The 2001 AUMF has been used to justify military operations in at least 22 countries, far beyond its original scope.
- Bipartisan proposals for AUMF reform have been introduced in every Congress since 2013 but have failed due to lack of political will.
- Allied nations (UK, France, Germany) require parliamentary authorization for major military deployments, demonstrating that democratic oversight and military effectiveness are compatible.

## 3. Reform Professional Military Education (PME)

### A. Mandate Civilian Graduate Education for Senior Officers

**Problem Addressed**: The military's internal education system creates intellectual insularity that can reinforce groupthink and reduce the officer corps' ability to engage with civilian perspectives (Root Cause 1).

**Proposal**: Require that a significant portion of future generals and admirals earn a graduate degree from a civilian university, rather than solely from a military war college.

**Key Provisions**:

- Change promotion board precepts to require that, by 2035, at least 40% of newly promoted one-star generals/admirals must have a master's degree from a top-50 civilian university in a relevant field (e.g., public policy, international relations, engineering, computer science)
- Create a funded fellowship program (Defense Scholar Fellowship) that covers tuition, provides a salary supplement, and guarantees a meaningful follow-on assignment upon completion
- Expand the existing RAND Pardee fellowship, Stanford's Hoover National Security Affairs Fellowship, and similar programs to accommodate more military fellows
- Ensure that civilian graduate education is valued equally to war college completion in promotion board deliberations

**Evidence Base**:

- The UK's military sends officers to civilian universities (London School of Economics, King's College London) as a routine part of professional development. British officers are widely regarded as among the most strategically literate in the world.
- General David Petraeus's PhD from Princeton's Woodrow Wilson School is frequently cited as an example of how civilian graduate education can broaden an officer's strategic perspective.
- A 2020 study by the Military Education Coordination Conference found that officers with civilian graduate degrees were rated more highly on "strategic thinking" by their superiors.

**Anticipated Impact**: Exposes future military leaders to diverse, civilian perspectives during their formative mid-career years; reduces intellectual insularity; improves their ability to think critically about strategy and policy; creates personal relationships between military officers and future civilian leaders.

### B. Strengthen and Codify Norms of Non-Partisanship

**Problem Addressed**: The erosion of the norm against political activity by senior military leaders, both active and retired, threatens the military's standing as a trusted, non-partisan institution (Root Cause 3).

**Proposal**: Rebuild the norm that senior military leaders, both active and retired, should remain outside of partisan politics, through a combination of legislation, institutional policy, and cultural reinforcement.

**Key Provisions**:

- **Legislation**: Pass a law extending the "cooling-off period" before retired generals and admirals can accept paid positions as political campaign surrogates, lobbyists, or on defense contractor boards from one year to five years. This would not restrict their free speech but would limit their ability to monetize their military credentials in partisan or commercial contexts.
- **Institutional Policy**: The Chairman of the Joint Chiefs and the service chiefs should issue a joint public statement reaffirming the norm of non-partisanship, to be read at every change-of-command ceremony and incorporated into every officer's retirement counseling.
- **Promotion Incentives**: Modify promotion board precepts to consider any evidence of partisan political activity by active duty officers as a disqualifying factor for promotion to general/flag officer rank.
- **Bipartisan Commitment**: The President and leaders of both parties should publicly commit to not using active duty military personnel as political props, not soliciting endorsements from retired senior officers, and not appointing recently retired generals to senior political positions without a genuine seven-year cooling-off period.

**Evidence Base**:

- The tradition of military non-partisanship was robust from World War II through the 1980s. General George C. Marshall reportedly never voted, and President Eisenhower did not register with a political party until he began running for office. This tradition can be restored.
- Survey data shows that the public's trust in the military declines when it is perceived as politically aligned. A 2021 Council on Foreign Relations survey found that 56% of Americans believed the military had become "too politicized."
- International models: The UK's military maintains a strong tradition of non-partisanship, with strict rules against political activity for both active and retired officers. Germany's *Innere Fuhrung* concept explicitly connects military service to democratic citizenship rather than partisan identity.

**Anticipated Impact**: Helps restore the military's standing as a trusted, non-partisan institution; reduces the perception that the military is aligned with one political party; strengthens public confidence across the political spectrum.
