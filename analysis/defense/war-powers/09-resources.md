# War Powers: Resources

## Constitutional and Legal Foundations

### Primary Sources

- **U.S. Constitution**, Article I, Section 8 (congressional war powers); Article II, Section 2 (Commander in Chief)
- **War Powers Resolution of 1973**, P.L. 93-148, 50 U.S.C. 1541-1548
- **Authorization for Use of Military Force (2001)**, P.L. 107-40, 115 Stat. 224
- **Authorization for Use of Military Force Against Iraq Resolution of 2002**, P.L. 107-243, 116 Stat. 1498 (repealed 2023)
- **National Defense Authorization Act for FY2024**, P.L. 118-1 (containing 2002 AUMF repeal)

### Key Supreme Court Cases

- *Bas v. Tingy*, 4 U.S. (4 Dall.) 37 (1800): Congress can authorize limited as well as general war
- *Little v. Barreme*, 6 U.S. (2 Cranch) 170 (1804): The President must comply with congressional limits on the use of force
- *The Prize Cases*, 67 U.S. (2 Black) 635 (1863): The President can respond to insurrection without a declaration of war
- *Youngstown Sheet & Tube Co. v. Sawyer*, 343 U.S. 579 (1952): Justice Jackson's three-zone framework for executive power; presidential power is at its lowest ebb when acting contrary to congressional will
- *INS v. Chadha*, 462 U.S. 919 (1983): Legislative veto is unconstitutional; affects WPR Section 5(c)
- *Hamdi v. Rumsfeld*, 542 U.S. 507 (2004): Congress authorized detention of enemy combatants under the 2001 AUMF; "a state of war is not a blank check for the President"
- *Hamdan v. Rumsfeld*, 548 U.S. 557 (2006): Military commissions must comply with congressional requirements and the Geneva Conventions
- *Zivotofsky v. Clinton*, 566 U.S. 189 (2012): Not all disputes between branches over foreign affairs are political questions

### Key Lower Court Cases

- *Campbell v. Clinton*, 203 F.3d 19 (D.C. Cir. 2000): Members of Congress lacked standing to challenge Kosovo operations
- *Doe v. Bush*, 323 F.3d 133 (1st Cir. 2003): Challenge to Iraq War dismissed as political question
- *Smith v. Obama*, 217 F. Supp. 3d 283 (D.D.C. 2016): Army captain lacked standing to challenge ISIS operations under 2001 AUMF

## Books

### Constitutional History and Analysis

- Edelson, Chris. *Emergency Presidential Power: From the Drafting of the Constitution to the War on Terror*. Madison: University of Wisconsin Press, 2013. Analysis of how emergency powers have been used to expand executive authority beyond constitutional limits.
- Fisher, Louis. *Presidential War Power*. 3rd ed. Lawrence: University Press of Kansas, 2013. The definitive scholarly treatment of the constitutional allocation of war powers and the history of executive overreach. Fisher, a former Congressional Research Service specialist, provides the most comprehensive account of how presidential war power has expanded.
- Ramsey, Michael D. *The Constitution's Text in Foreign Affairs*. Cambridge: Harvard University Press, 2007. Originalist analysis of the constitutional text governing foreign affairs and war powers.
- Yoo, John. *The Powers of War and Peace: The Constitution and Foreign Affairs After 9/11*. Chicago: University of Chicago Press, 2005. Presents the executive supremacy argument, contending that the President has broad inherent authority to initiate military operations. Widely criticized by other scholars but influential in executive branch legal culture.

### Modern Practice and Reform

- Glennon, Michael J. *National Security and Double Government*. New York: Oxford University Press, 2014. Argues that national security policy is effectively controlled by a permanent national security bureaucracy regardless of which party holds the White House.
- Goldsmith, Jack. *Power and Constraint: The Accountable Presidency After 9/11*. New York: W.W. Norton, 2012. Analysis of the interplay between executive power and institutional constraints in the post-9/11 era.
- Goldsmith, Jack. *The Terror Presidency: Law and Judgment Inside the Bush Administration*. New York: W.W. Norton, 2007. Inside account of legal decision-making in the Bush administration after 9/11.
- Waxman, Matthew C. "The Power to Wage War Successfully." *Columbia Law Review* 117, no. 3 (2017): 613-686. Analysis of how the "power to wage war successfully" has been used to justify expanding executive authority.
- Waxman, Matthew C. *Governing the Use of Force in International Relations*. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 2023. Comprehensive analysis of domestic and international legal frameworks governing the use of force.

### Historical Studies

- Howell, William and Jon Pevehouse. *While Dangers Gather: Congressional Checks on Presidential War Powers*. Princeton: Princeton University Press, 2007. Empirical study of how Congress influences military operations through institutional mechanisms short of formal votes.
- Kriner, Douglas. *After the Rubicon: Congress, Presidents, and the Politics of Waging War*. Chicago: University of Chicago Press, 2010. Analysis of how congressional behavior shapes the political environment for military operations.
- Schlesinger, Arthur M., Jr. *The Imperial Presidency*. Boston: Houghton Mifflin, 1973. Classic analysis of the expansion of presidential power, written in the context of Vietnam and Watergate.

## Reports and Policy Papers

- Baker, James A., III, and Warren Christopher, Co-Chairs. *National War Powers Commission Report*. Miller Center of Public Affairs, University of Virginia, 2008. Bipartisan commission recommending comprehensive replacement of the War Powers Resolution.
- Brennan Center for Justice. "Reclaiming the War Power." 2019. Analysis of WPR failures and reform proposals.
- Costs of War Project, Watson Institute, Brown University. "United States Counterterrorism Operations, 2018-2020." Comprehensive documentation of the geographic scope of post-9/11 military operations.
- Daskal, Jennifer and Stephen I. Vladeck. "After the AUMF." *Harvard National Security Journal* 5, no. 1 (2014): 115-146. Analysis of legal options for addressing the 2001 AUMF.
- Project on Government Oversight (POGO). "War Powers and Congressional Oversight." Multiple reports (2019-2024).
- Quincy Institute for Responsible Statecraft. Various policy papers on war powers reform, military restraint, and congressional oversight (2020-2025).

## Academic Journals

Key journals covering war powers scholarship:

- *Harvard Law Review* -- Constitutional analysis of executive and congressional powers
- *Yale Law Journal* -- War powers, national security law
- *Columbia Law Review* -- Use of force, separation of powers
- *Harvard National Security Journal* -- Focused on national security legal issues
- *Journal of National Security Law and Policy* -- Specialized journal for national security legal analysis
- *Presidential Studies Quarterly* -- Executive power and presidential authority

## News and Analysis Sources

- *The New York Times* -- Investigative reporting on military operations and civilian casualties (notably the "Civilian Casualty Files" series, 2021)
- *The Washington Post* -- Coverage of war powers debates and congressional action
- *Foreign Affairs* -- Long-form analysis of U.S. military policy and strategy
- *Politico* -- Congressional war powers politics and legislation tracking
- Bureau of Investigative Journalism -- Independent tracking of drone strikes and civilian casualties
- Airwars -- Independent monitoring of civilian harm from airstrikes globally
