# Foreign Interventions: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

The legislative framework governing U.S. foreign military interventions is defined primarily by the Constitution (Article I, Section 8 and Article II, Section 2), the War Powers Resolution of 1973 (50 U.S.C. 1541-1548), and the 2001 Authorization for Use of Military Force (P.L. 107-40). This framework has proven deeply inadequate. Congress has not declared war since 1942, has not voted on a new AUMF since 2002, and has never enforced the War Powers Resolution's withdrawal provisions. The result is a system in which the President exercises virtually unchecked authority to initiate and sustain military operations abroad.

The two bills proposed here address the two fundamental failures of the current framework:

1. **The Military Intervention Reform Act** addresses the *front end* of intervention: establishing criteria that must be met before military force is used, requiring congressional authorization, and mandating exit planning.
2. **The Post-Intervention Accountability Act** addresses the *back end*: requiring independent after-action reviews, mandating declassification of intervention records, and creating mechanisms for institutional learning.

Together, these bills aim to restore the constitutional balance between the executive and legislative branches on questions of war and peace, and to create the accountability mechanisms necessary to prevent the repetition of past failures.

**Key principles guiding this legislation**:

- Congressional authorization must be specific, time-limited, and subject to regular renewal
- The decision to use military force must be based on rigorous analysis, not political expediency
- Every intervention must have defined objectives, an exit strategy, and a post-conflict plan before operations begin
- Independent review of completed interventions must be mandatory, not discretionary
- Historical records must be declassified on a reasonable timeline to enable democratic accountability
- Civilian harm must be systematically minimized, investigated, and compensated

---

## Constitutional Amendments

No constitutional amendments are required for this legislation. The existing constitutional framework provides sufficient authority:

- **Article I, Section 8** grants Congress the power "To declare War" and "To make Rules for the Government and Regulation of the land and naval Forces." The proposed legislation falls squarely within these enumerated powers.
- **Article II, Section 2** designates the President as "Commander in Chief." The legislation does not seek to direct military operations but rather to establish the conditions under which Congress authorizes military force and the accountability mechanisms that apply after operations conclude.
- **The War Powers Resolution** (1973) already establishes the principle that Congress may legislate constraints on the introduction of armed forces into hostilities. The proposed legislation strengthens and clarifies these existing authorities.

The one area of potential constitutional tension is the automatic funding cutoff provision, which operates through the congressional power of the purse (Article I, Section 9) to enforce the authorization requirement. Courts have generally held that Congress's spending power may be used to condition executive action, and the Supreme Court in *Youngstown Sheet and Tube Co. v. Sawyer*, 343 U.S. 579 (1952), established that presidential power is at its lowest ebb when acting contrary to the expressed will of Congress.

## Federal Legislation

### Bill 1: The Military Intervention Reform Act

**Purpose**: To establish mandatory criteria for the initiation of significant military operations abroad, require congressional authorization within 60 days, mandate exit strategy and post-conflict planning, and provide enforcement mechanisms through automatic funding restrictions.

**Key Provisions**:

1. **Intervention Criteria Certification**: Before initiating any significant military operation, the President must certify in writing to Congress that nine specified criteria have been assessed.
2. **Congressional Authorization Requirement**: Operations lasting more than 60 days require affirmative congressional authorization through an expedited floor vote.
3. **Exit Strategy Mandate**: No significant military operation may proceed without defined objectives, success criteria, and a withdrawal plan.
4. **Post-Conflict Planning**: Requires a funded post-conflict plan developed in coordination with the State Department, USAID, and relevant international organizations.
5. **Cost Transparency**: Requires the Congressional Budget Office to produce independent cost estimates, including projected long-term obligations.
6. **Automatic Funding Cutoff**: If Congress does not authorize operations within 60 days, funding is automatically suspended with a 30-day wind-down period.
7. **Self-Defense Exception**: Preserves the President's authority to respond to imminent armed attacks, with a requirement to submit the intervention criteria certification within 14 days.

**Draft Text**:

```text
[119th CONGRESS, 2nd Session]

AN ACT

To establish criteria and procedures for the initiation of significant
military operations abroad and to require congressional authorization
for sustained military engagements.

Be it enacted by the Senate and House of Representatives of the
United States of America in Congress assembled,

SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Military Intervention Reform Act
of 2026".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) The Constitution vests the power to declare war exclusively
    in Congress under Article I, Section 8.
(2) Since 1945, the United States has engaged in more than 200
    instances of armed force abroad without a formal declaration
    of war (Congressional Research Service, 2024).
(3) The 2001 Authorization for Use of Military Force has been
    used to justify operations in at least 22 countries against
    more than 41 groups, far exceeding any reasonable reading of
    its original scope.
(4) The post-9/11 wars have cost the United States approximately
    $8 trillion and contributed to more than 900,000 deaths
    (Brown University Costs of War Project, 2024).
(5) Multiple interventions have suffered from inadequate planning,
    threat inflation, mission creep, and the absence of exit
    strategies, resulting in strategic failure and unnecessary
    loss of life.
(6) Congress has a constitutional obligation to deliberate and
    vote on the use of military force, and structural reforms
    are necessary to ensure this obligation is fulfilled.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:
(a) SIGNIFICANT MILITARY OPERATION.--The term "significant
    military operation" means--
    (1) the introduction of United States Armed Forces into
        hostilities or into situations where imminent involvement
        in hostilities is clearly indicated by the circumstances;
    (2) any deployment of United States Armed Forces abroad that
        is expected to exceed 60 days in duration; or
    (3) any military operation abroad with projected costs
        exceeding $50,000,000.
(b) HOSTILITIES.--The term "hostilities" means any situation in
    which United States Armed Forces are actively engaged in
    exchanges of fire with hostile forces, conducting offensive
    military operations, or operating in circumstances where
    there is a significant risk of engagement.
(c) INTERVENTION CRITERIA CERTIFICATION.--The term "intervention
    criteria certification" means the written certification
    described in Section 4 of this Act.
(d) SELF-DEFENSE EXCEPTION.--The term "self-defense" means the
    use of military force to repel an armed attack against the
    territory of the United States, its possessions, or its
    Armed Forces, or to address an imminent threat of such
    attack, as defined in customary international law.

SEC. 4. INTERVENTION CRITERIA CERTIFICATION.

(a) IN GENERAL.--Before initiating any significant military
    operation, the President shall submit to the Speaker of the
    House of Representatives, the President pro tempore of the
    Senate, and the chairs and ranking members of the Committees
    on Armed Services and Foreign Relations/Affairs of both
    chambers, a written certification addressing each of the
    following criteria:
    (1) VITAL NATIONAL INTEREST.--An identification and
        explanation of the vital national security interest of
        the United States that necessitates the use of military
        force.
    (2) CLEAR OBJECTIVES.--A statement of the specific military
        objectives of the operation and the criteria by which
        success will be measured.
    (3) DIPLOMATIC ALTERNATIVES.--A description of the
        diplomatic, economic, and other non-military measures
        that have been attempted or considered, and an
        explanation of why military force is necessary.
    (4) COST AND RISK ASSESSMENT.--A comprehensive estimate of
        the projected financial costs (including long-term
        veterans' care and interest on any borrowing), the
        expected duration of operations, the projected risk of
        escalation, and the probability of achieving stated
        objectives.
    (5) EXIT STRATEGY.--A description of the conditions under
        which military operations will be concluded and United
        States Armed Forces withdrawn, including specific,
        measurable benchmarks for withdrawal.
    (6) POST-CONFLICT PLAN.--A description of the plan for
        post-conflict stabilization, reconstruction, or
        transition, developed in coordination with the
        Department of State, the United States Agency for
        International Development, and relevant international
        organizations, including a budget estimate.
    (7) INTERNATIONAL SUPPORT.--A description of the
        international support for the operation, including any
        United Nations Security Council authorization, coalition
        partners, and allied government positions.
    (8) FORCE REQUIREMENTS.--An assessment of the force levels,
        capabilities, and duration required to achieve stated
        objectives.
    (9) LEGAL AUTHORITY.--An identification of the specific
        constitutional or statutory authority under which the
        operation will be conducted.
(b) CLASSIFICATION.--The certification may be submitted in
    classified form to the extent necessary to protect sources,
    methods, and operational security, but an unclassified
    summary must be made available to all members of Congress
    and to the public within 48 hours.
(c) GAO ASSESSMENT.--Not later than 30 days after the
    submission of a certification under subsection (a), the
    Comptroller General of the United States shall submit to
    Congress an independent assessment of the accuracy and
    completeness of the certification, including the cost
    estimates.

SEC. 5. CONGRESSIONAL AUTHORIZATION REQUIREMENT.

(a) 60-DAY LIMIT.--No significant military operation may
    continue for more than 60 days after the date on which
    United States Armed Forces are introduced into hostilities,
    unless Congress has enacted a specific authorization for
    such operation.
(b) EXPEDITED PROCEDURES.--
    (1) Upon submission of a request for authorization by the
        President or introduction of an authorization resolution
        by any member, the resolution shall be referred to the
        appropriate committee of each chamber.
    (2) The committee shall report the resolution, with or
        without amendment, not later than 15 calendar days after
        the date of referral.
    (3) If the committee fails to report the resolution within
        15 days, the resolution shall be discharged from the
        committee and placed on the calendar of the respective
        chamber.
    (4) Debate on the resolution shall be limited to 20 hours
        in each chamber, equally divided between proponents and
        opponents.
    (5) A vote on final passage shall occur not later than
        3 calendar days after the conclusion of debate.
(c) AUTOMATIC FUNDING RESTRICTION.--If Congress does not enact
    a specific authorization within the 60-day period described
    in subsection (a):
    (1) No funds appropriated or otherwise made available to the
        Department of Defense or any other Federal agency may be
        obligated or expended for the continuation of the
        operation, except as necessary for the safe and orderly
        withdrawal of United States Armed Forces.
    (2) The withdrawal shall be completed not later than 30 days
        after the expiration of the 60-day period.
(d) NO SILENCE AS CONSENT.--Congressional inaction shall not be
    construed as authorization for the continuation of military
    operations.

SEC. 6. SELF-DEFENSE EXCEPTION.

(a) Nothing in this Act shall be construed to limit the
    authority of the President to use military force in
    self-defense as defined in Section 3(d) of this Act.
(b) If the President initiates military operations under the
    self-defense exception, the President shall:
    (1) Notify Congress within 48 hours, consistent with the
        War Powers Resolution (50 U.S.C. 1541-1548); and
    (2) Submit the intervention criteria certification
        described in Section 4 within 14 calendar days.
(c) Operations initiated under the self-defense exception
    remain subject to the 60-day authorization requirement
    of Section 5.

SEC. 7. QUARTERLY REPORTING.

(a) Not later than 90 days after the commencement of any
    significant military operation, and quarterly thereafter,
    the President shall submit to Congress an unclassified
    report containing:
    (1) The number of United States Armed Forces deployed in
        connection with the operation;
    (2) All countries in which operations are being conducted;
    (3) All groups against which military force has been used;
    (4) The legal authorities under which operations are
        conducted;
    (5) An estimate of civilian casualties resulting from the
        operation;
    (6) Cumulative costs of the operation; and
    (7) An assessment of progress toward achieving the stated
        objectives and exit criteria.
(b) The Comptroller General shall review each report for
    accuracy and completeness and submit findings to Congress.

SEC. 8. JUDICIAL REVIEW.

(a) STANDING.--Any member of Congress may bring an action in
    the United States District Court for the District of
    Columbia to enforce the provisions of this Act.
(b) EXPEDITED REVIEW.--Any action brought under this section
    shall be heard by a three-judge panel and shall be
    expedited in every way possible.

SEC. 9. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 90 days after the date of enactment.

SEC. 10. SEVERABILITY.

If any provision of this Act, or the application of such
provision to any person or circumstance, is held to be
unconstitutional, the remainder of this Act, and the
application of such provision to other persons or
circumstances, shall not be affected.
```

**Explanation**: This bill addresses the core dysfunction identified in the root causes analysis: the absence of meaningful criteria for intervention decisions and the erosion of congressional war power. The intervention criteria certification creates a structured decision-making process that forces honest assessment of costs, risks, and alternatives. The 60-day authorization requirement with automatic funding cutoff provides a genuine enforcement mechanism, unlike the current War Powers Resolution, which has never been enforced. The self-defense exception preserves legitimate presidential authority while preventing its abuse to circumvent congressional authorization.

**Potential Challenges**:

- **Executive resistance**: Every president since Nixon has contested the constitutionality of the War Powers Resolution, and the automatic funding cutoff will face similar objections. However, Congress's power of the purse is among its most clearly established constitutional authorities.
- **Judicial standing**: Courts have historically been reluctant to adjudicate war powers disputes as "political questions." The explicit grant of standing in Section 8 may not survive judicial review, though the trend in recent cases (*Zivotofsky v. Clinton*, 2012) has been to narrow the political question doctrine.
- **Evasion through classification**: The executive branch may attempt to evade the certification requirement by classifying information necessary for meaningful congressional review. The requirement for an unclassified summary addresses this partially.
- **Definition disputes**: The definition of "hostilities" has been a persistent source of executive-legislative conflict (e.g., the Obama administration's claim that the Libya operation did not constitute "hostilities").

**Refinements**:

- Consider adding a specific provision addressing cyber operations and proxy warfare (e.g., arming and training foreign forces), which increasingly substitute for direct military intervention
- The $50 million cost threshold should be indexed to inflation
- Consider establishing a classified legislative annex process for situations where the full certification cannot be made public

### Bill 2: The Post-Intervention Accountability Act

**Purpose**: To establish an independent body for reviewing completed military interventions, mandate declassification of intervention records, and create mechanisms for institutional learning.

**Key Provisions**:

1. **Independent Post-Intervention Review Board**: Creates a 7-member independent board to conduct comprehensive reviews of completed interventions.
2. **Mandatory Review Triggers**: Reviews are triggered automatically when an intervention lasts more than 1 year or costs more than $10 billion.
3. **Subpoena Authority**: The Board has subpoena authority for documents and testimony and access to classified information.
4. **Declassification Mandate**: Intervention-related records are automatically declassified 10 years after the conclusion of operations, with narrow exemptions.
5. **Institutional Learning**: Requires integration of review findings into military education curricula and policy planning.
6. **Comply or Explain**: Executive agencies must respond to Board recommendations within 180 days, either implementing them or publicly explaining why they disagree.

**Draft Text**:

```text
[119th CONGRESS, 2nd Session]

AN ACT

To establish an Independent Post-Intervention Review Board, require
declassification of intervention records, and create mechanisms for
institutional accountability and learning.

Be it enacted by the Senate and House of Representatives of the
United States of America in Congress assembled,

SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Post-Intervention Accountability
Act of 2026".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(1) The United States has conducted multiple major military
    interventions since 1945, many of which have failed to
    achieve their stated objectives at enormous cost in lives
    and resources.
(2) No permanent, independent mechanism exists to conduct
    comprehensive reviews of completed military interventions
    and publish findings for public accountability.
(3) Critical information about intervention decisions,
    intelligence assessments, and operational outcomes remains
    classified for decades, preventing democratic accountability
    and institutional learning.
(4) The Special Inspector General for Afghanistan Reconstruction
    (SIGAR) demonstrated the value of independent oversight but
    was limited in scope to a single intervention.
(5) Systematic after-action review, as practiced by the National
    Transportation Safety Board for aviation accidents, has
    proven effective in identifying failures and preventing
    their recurrence.

SEC. 3. ESTABLISHMENT OF THE INDEPENDENT POST-INTERVENTION
REVIEW BOARD.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.--There is established as an independent
    agency of the United States Government the Independent
    Post-Intervention Review Board (hereinafter "the Board").
(b) COMPOSITION.--
    (1) The Board shall consist of 7 members, of whom:
        (A) 3 shall be appointed by the President;
        (B) 2 shall be appointed by the Speaker of the House
            of Representatives and the Minority Leader of the
            House of Representatives (1 each); and
        (C) 2 shall be appointed by the Majority Leader of
            the Senate and the Minority Leader of the Senate
            (1 each).
    (2) No more than 4 members shall be affiliated with the
        same political party.
    (3) Members shall serve staggered 7-year terms.
    (4) No individual may serve as a member who, during the
        5-year period preceding appointment, was employed by
        the Department of Defense, any intelligence agency,
        any defense contractor with annual government
        contracts exceeding $100,000,000, or any entity
        that received more than 25 percent of its funding
        from such contractors.
(c) QUALIFICATIONS.--Members shall have demonstrated expertise
    in national security policy, international relations,
    military affairs, intelligence, international law, or
    conflict resolution.
(d) CHAIR.--The President shall designate one member as Chair,
    subject to Senate confirmation.
(e) COMPENSATION.--Members shall be compensated at Level IV of
    the Executive Schedule (5 U.S.C. 5315).

SEC. 4. MANDATORY REVIEW.

(a) TRIGGERS.--The Board shall initiate a comprehensive review
    of any military intervention that:
    (1) lasts more than 1 year; or
    (2) incurs costs exceeding $10,000,000,000.
(b) DISCRETIONARY REVIEW.--The Board may, by majority vote,
    initiate a review of any military intervention, including
    covert action programs, regardless of duration or cost.
(c) TIMING.--A mandatory review shall commence not later than
    180 days after the conclusion of the intervention or the
    withdrawal of the majority of United States Armed Forces,
    whichever occurs first.

SEC. 5. POWERS OF THE BOARD.

(a) SUBPOENA AUTHORITY.--The Board may issue subpoenas for the
    attendance of witnesses and the production of documents
    relevant to any review.
(b) ACCESS TO CLASSIFIED INFORMATION.--Members and designated
    staff of the Board shall have access to all classified
    information relevant to any review, including information
    classified at the TOP SECRET/SCI level.
(c) TESTIMONY.--The Board may require the testimony of any
    current or former Federal employee, including military
    officers and intelligence officials, relevant to any
    review.
(d) COOPERATION.--All Federal agencies shall cooperate fully
    with the Board and provide requested documents and access
    within 30 days of a request.
(e) ENFORCEMENT.--Failure to comply with a Board subpoena or
    request may be enforced through the United States District
    Court for the District of Columbia.

SEC. 6. REVIEW PROCESS AND REPORTS.

(a) SCOPE.--Each review shall examine:
    (1) the decision-making process that led to the
        intervention, including intelligence assessments,
        policy deliberations, and the consideration of
        alternatives;
    (2) the stated objectives and the degree to which they
        were achieved;
    (3) the accuracy of cost estimates and intelligence
        assessments provided to Congress and the public;
    (4) the execution of military operations, including
        civilian harm and compliance with the laws of
        armed conflict;
    (5) the effectiveness of post-conflict planning and
        execution;
    (6) the total costs of the intervention, including
        long-term obligations; and
    (7) lessons learned and recommendations for policy,
        legal, and institutional reforms.
(b) PUBLIC REPORT.--The Board shall publish a public report
    not later than 2 years after initiating each review.
    The report may include a classified annex for information
    that cannot be publicly disclosed without damage to
    national security, but the Board shall make every effort
    to maximize the unclassified content.
(c) CONGRESSIONAL TESTIMONY.--Within 60 days of publishing a
    report, the Board Chair shall testify before the relevant
    committees of both chambers of Congress.

SEC. 7. COMPLY OR EXPLAIN.

(a) Within 180 days of the publication of a Board report, the
    heads of all Federal agencies to which recommendations are
    directed shall submit to Congress a written response that
    either:
    (1) describes the actions taken or planned to implement
        the recommendation; or
    (2) explains in detail why the agency disagrees with the
        recommendation and what alternative actions, if any,
        it will take.
(b) The Board shall publish all agency responses and, if
    appropriate, a follow-up assessment.

SEC. 8. DECLASSIFICATION OF INTERVENTION RECORDS.

(a) AUTOMATIC DECLASSIFICATION.--All records of the Federal
    Government relating to the decision to initiate, conduct,
    or conclude a military intervention shall be automatically
    declassified 10 years after the conclusion of the
    intervention, unless:
    (1) the Director of National Intelligence certifies that
        specific documents, if disclosed, would reveal
        intelligence sources or methods that remain active
        and the disclosure of which would cause demonstrable
        harm to national security; or
    (2) the Secretary of Defense certifies that specific
        documents, if disclosed, would compromise ongoing
        military operations.
(b) EXEMPTION REVIEW.--Any exemption under subsection (a)
    shall be reviewed by the Board every 3 years and shall
    expire after 25 years unless renewed by the Director of
    National Intelligence or Secretary of Defense with a
    written justification provided to Congress.
(c) SCOPE.--The records subject to this section include, but
    are not limited to:
    (1) intelligence assessments and estimates;
    (2) policy memoranda and decision documents;
    (3) diplomatic communications;
    (4) military operational plans and after-action reports;
    (5) cost analyses and budget documents; and
    (6) civilian casualty assessments and investigations.

SEC. 9. INSTITUTIONAL LEARNING.

(a) CURRICULUM INTEGRATION.--The Secretary of Defense shall
    ensure that the findings and recommendations of Board
    reports are integrated into the curricula of the National
    Defense University, the service war colleges, and the
    professional military education system within 1 year of
    publication.
(b) LESSONS LEARNED DATABASE.--The Board shall maintain a
    publicly accessible database of findings, recommendations,
    and lessons learned from all completed reviews.
(c) PLANNING INTEGRATION.--The Department of Defense and the
    Department of State shall certify to Congress annually
    that Board findings have been considered in current
    operational planning and policy development.

SEC. 10. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There are authorized to be appropriated to the Board
$25,000,000 for each fiscal year to carry out its functions
under this Act.

SEC. 11. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect upon the date of enactment.
The Board shall be fully constituted not later than 180 days
after the date of enactment.

SEC. 12. SEVERABILITY.

If any provision of this Act, or the application of such
provision to any person or circumstance, is held to be
unconstitutional, the remainder of this Act, and the
application of such provision to other persons or
circumstances, shall not be affected.
```

**Explanation**: This bill addresses the persistent failure to learn from past interventions. The NTSB model has been remarkably successful in aviation safety because it combines independence, technical expertise, subpoena authority, and public reporting. Applying this model to military interventions creates an institutional mechanism that did not previously exist: a permanent, independent body with the authority and mandate to assess what happened, why, and what should change. The declassification provisions address the information asymmetry that allows administrations to control the narrative about interventions for decades after they conclude.

**Potential Challenges**:

- **Executive privilege claims**: The executive branch may resist Board subpoenas and information requests by invoking executive privilege, particularly for presidential decision-making documents. The legislation's cooperation requirements and enforcement provisions address this, but enforcement may ultimately require judicial resolution.
- **Confirmation battles**: Board member nominations could become politicized. The staggered appointment process and bipartisan requirements mitigate this but do not eliminate it.
- **Timeliness**: The 2-year timeline for completing reviews may be insufficient for the most complex interventions. The Board should have discretion to extend this timeline.
- **Classification disputes**: The 10-year automatic declassification timeline will face resistance from intelligence agencies. The exemption process provides a safety valve, but the Board must be vigilant against blanket exemptions that undermine the provision's purpose.

**Refinements**:

- Consider extending the Board's mandate to include covert action programs, which are currently subject to limited congressional oversight
- Establish a dedicated FOIA processing unit within the Board to handle public access requests for intervention records
- Consider a provision requiring the Board to conduct "real-time" assessments of ongoing interventions (similar to SIGAR's role in Afghanistan) rather than waiting for their conclusion

## State Model Legislation

Foreign military intervention is exclusively a federal responsibility under the Constitution. However, states can take several relevant actions:

### State National Guard Deployment Transparency Act

**Purpose**: Require state-level transparency about the deployment of state National Guard units to foreign operations.

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the "[State] National Guard Deployment
Transparency Act".

SECTION 2. REPORTING REQUIREMENT.
(a) The Adjutant General shall submit to the Governor and the
    state legislature a quarterly report on all federalized
    deployments of [State] National Guard units to foreign
    military operations, including:
    (1) the number of personnel deployed;
    (2) the location and mission of deployment;
    (3) the federal legal authority under which the deployment
        is conducted;
    (4) casualties sustained; and
    (5) the estimated duration of deployment.
(b) The report shall be made publicly available except for
    information that would compromise operational security.

SECTION 3. LEGISLATIVE NOTIFICATION.
The Governor shall notify the presiding officers of both
chambers of the state legislature within 72 hours of receiving
notice that [State] National Guard units will be federalized
for deployment to foreign military operations.
```

**Adaptations**: States may add provisions requiring legislative hearings on Guard deployments, resolutions regarding the state legislature's position on specific military operations, or mechanisms for communicating state-level concerns to the state's congressional delegation.

## Regulatory Framework

### Department of Defense

**Existing Authority**: DoD Directive 3000.09 (Civilian Harm Mitigation and Response); DoD Policy on Civilian Casualties; Executive Order on Civilian Casualties.

**Proposed Regulation**: Codify existing civilian harm mitigation policies into binding regulations with the following additions:

- Mandatory pre-operation civilian harm assessments for all planned strikes
- Independent civilian casualty investigation unit reporting outside the operational chain of command
- Standardized civilian casualty reporting across all combatant commands
- Ex gratia payment program with streamlined claims procedures

### Congressional Budget Office

**Existing Authority**: Congressional Budget and Impoundment Control Act of 1974.

**Proposed Enhancement**: Direct CBO to produce comprehensive annual estimates of total intervention costs, including direct operational costs, long-term veterans' care obligations, interest on war-related borrowing, and economic opportunity costs.

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

**War Powers**: The proposed legislation operates within Congress's enumerated powers under Article I, Section 8 (declare war, regulate the armed forces, power of the purse). The automatic funding cutoff is the most constitutionally assertive provision but is grounded in Congress's well-established spending power. The Supreme Court has never directly addressed the constitutionality of the War Powers Resolution's withdrawal provision, and the proposed legislation strengthens the enforcement mechanism to avoid the Resolution's historical ineffectiveness.

**Executive Power**: The legislation preserves the President's inherent authority to respond to armed attacks in self-defense. The intervention criteria certification is a procedural requirement, not a substantive limitation on the Commander-in-Chief power. Presidents retain discretion over how to conduct authorized operations.

**Separation of Powers**: The judicial review provision (Military Intervention Reform Act, Section 8) may face political question doctrine objections. However, the trend in recent Supreme Court jurisprudence has been to narrow the political question doctrine (*Zivotofsky v. Clinton*, 566 U.S. 189 (2012)), and the provision of explicit statutory standing strengthens the case for justiciability.

### Preemption Questions

These are exclusively federal matters. No preemption issues arise, as foreign military intervention is a federal power under the Constitution's allocation of war and foreign affairs authority to the national government.

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- **Automatic funding cutoff**: The primary enforcement mechanism for the authorization requirement
- **Judicial review**: Available to individual members of Congress
- **Subpoena authority**: The Review Board can compel production of documents and testimony
- **"Comply or explain"**: Agencies must publicly respond to Board recommendations
- **Declassification timelines**: Automatic, with narrow and reviewable exemptions

### Sunset and Review Provisions

- The Military Intervention Reform Act does not include a sunset clause, as its provisions are intended to be permanent structural reforms. However, Section 5 requires that any specific military authorization include a sunset clause of no more than 3 years.
- The Post-Intervention Accountability Act authorizes appropriations annually, providing Congress with ongoing control over the Board's funding and operations.

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Military Intervention Reform Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Classification abuse | The President may classify the intervention criteria certification to limit congressional and public review, while providing only a minimal unclassified summary | High |
| Definition evasion | Executive may argue that operations do not constitute "hostilities" (as Obama administration claimed for Libya) or do not meet the $50M/60-day thresholds | Critical |
| Self-defense stretching | The self-defense exception could be interpreted broadly to cover preemptive or preventive operations that do not involve an imminent armed attack | High |
| Proxy warfare gap | Operations conducted through proxy forces, CIA covert action, or private military contractors may fall outside the definition of "significant military operation" | High |
| Congressional inaction by design | Members may deliberately avoid voting to exploit the ambiguity, despite the "no silence as consent" provision | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Enforcement depends on judicial willingness | Courts may decline to adjudicate war powers disputes on political question grounds | Historical judicial reluctance to enter war powers disputes |
| No retroactive application | Does not address the existing 2001 AUMF or currently ongoing operations | Political compromise necessary to pass the bill |
| Cyber operations gap | Does not clearly cover offensive cyber operations, which may substitute for kinetic military operations | Rapid evolution of cyber warfare doctrine |
| Intelligence community operations | CIA-conducted drone strikes and covert action may not be covered by the "armed forces" language | Distinct legal frameworks for Title 10 (military) and Title 50 (intelligence) operations |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Anti-classification abuse**: Amend the bill to require that the unclassified summary include, at minimum, the country/region of operations, the general nature of the threat, the estimated cost range, the expected duration, and the legal authority cited. If the executive branch provides only a minimal summary, the GAO assessment (Section 4(c)) should flag the inadequacy.
2. **Broadened definitions**: Include specific language that "hostilities" encompasses any situation in which U.S. forces or U.S.-directed forces are exposed to hostile fire, conducting offensive operations, or providing direct targeting support for operations by other forces. Address the Libya precedent explicitly.
3. **Self-defense limitation**: Define "imminent" attack using the Caroline test standard from international law (necessity of self-defense is "instant, overwhelming, and leaving no choice of means, and no moment for deliberation"), preventing the expansive redefinition of "imminent" employed in the Obama-era targeted killing white papers.
4. **Proxy warfare inclusion**: Add a provision covering operations in which the U.S. provides direct targeting intelligence, operational planning, or command and control support to foreign military forces or non-state armed groups, even if U.S. forces are not directly engaged.
5. **Cyber operations**: Add a provision defining "significant cyber operations" that cause physical destruction, casualties, or significant economic damage as equivalent to significant military operations for the purposes of this Act.

### Post-Intervention Accountability Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Executive privilege blanket | The executive branch may invoke executive privilege to shield entire categories of presidential decision-making documents from Board review | High |
| Covert action exemption | The Board's authority over covert action programs is ambiguous, potentially allowing CIA operations to escape review | High |
| Declassification exemption abuse | Intelligence agencies may request blanket exemptions from the 10-year declassification requirement, citing ongoing sources and methods | Medium |
| Board capture | Over time, Board members may be drawn from the same foreign policy establishment they are meant to oversee | Medium |
| Funding vulnerability | Congress could defund or underfund the Board to limit its effectiveness | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| 2-year report timeline may be slow | Lessons may not be applied to subsequent interventions in time | Comprehensive reviews are inherently time-consuming |
| No enforcement power beyond "comply or explain" | Agencies may explain away recommendations without implementing changes | Separation of powers limits on independent agency authority |
| Backward-looking only | Board reviews completed interventions but cannot intervene in ongoing ones | Designed to avoid interference with operational command authority |
| Limited international scope | Does not require the Board to assess impacts on foreign populations or consult with affected communities | Domestic focus of the legislation |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Executive privilege limitation**: Include a provision modeled on the Intelligence Community Whistleblower Protection Act that requires the executive branch to provide a privilege log for any withheld documents and allows the Board to challenge privilege claims before a designated federal judge.
2. **Covert action clarification**: Explicitly state that the Board's review authority extends to all U.S. government military and paramilitary operations, including covert action programs conducted under Title 50, with appropriate security clearances for Board members and staff.
3. **Declassification safeguards**: Require that exemption requests be item-specific (not category-wide), reviewed by an independent classification review panel, and published (in redacted form) so that the scope of exemptions is publicly known.
4. **Board independence**: Add a provision requiring that no more than 2 Board members may have served in the same presidential administration, and establish a public financial disclosure requirement.
5. **Real-time monitoring**: Consider adding a provision authorizing the Board to conduct interim assessments of ongoing interventions that have exceeded 3 years, modeled on SIGAR's role in Afghanistan.

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| Executive branch resistance across administrations | Build bipartisan support; include automatic enforcement mechanisms that do not depend on executive cooperation |
| Judicial reluctance to adjudicate war powers | Provide explicit statutory standing and expedited review procedures; build a legislative record addressing political question concerns |
| Institutional culture resistant to accountability | Use mandatory curriculum integration and "lessons learned" database to embed accountability into professional development |
| Classification system used to prevent accountability | Mandatory declassification timelines with narrow, reviewable exemptions; strengthen FOIA enforcement for national security records |
| Proxy warfare and cyber operations outpace legal framework | Build flexibility into definitions; require periodic review and update of covered activities |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

Both bills should be subject to comprehensive congressional review after 10 years to assess effectiveness and make necessary adjustments. The Post-Intervention Accountability Act should include a provision requiring the Board itself to publish a self-assessment of its effectiveness after its first 5 years of operation.
