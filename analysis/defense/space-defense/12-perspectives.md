# Space Defense: Political Perspectives Analysis

## Overview

Space defense policy sits at a unique intersection of national security, technology, international law, and environmental sustainability. Unlike most defense issues, it generates unusual cross-partisan alignments: both hawks and environmentalists have reasons to oppose debris-generating ASAT tests, and both pro-business conservatives and progressives have reasons to question the military-industrial complex's approach to space acquisition. The debate centers on how aggressively to weaponize space, how much to regulate commercial operators, whether arms control is possible in orbit, and how to balance military dominance with long-term sustainability.

## Perspective Definitions

| Perspective | Core Values | Policy Orientation |
|-------------|-------------|-------------------|
| Conservative | National security, military strength, free enterprise | Space dominance, strong Space Force, minimal commercial regulation |
| Liberal | International cooperation, sustainability, regulation | Norms-based approach, debris regulation, multilateral frameworks |
| Progressive | Demilitarization, environmental protection, public goods | Oppose weaponization, strong debris standards, international treaties |
| Libertarian | Limited government, free markets, property rights | Commercial space freedom, minimal regulation, oppose government monopoly |
| Constitutionalist | Enumerated powers, Senate treaty authority, oversight | Congressional authorization for space weapons, treaty process |
| Populist | National sovereignty, anti-elite, industrial jobs | American space dominance, domestic manufacturing, oppose foreign dependence |
| Centrist | Pragmatism, bipartisan solutions, balanced investment | Resilient architectures, norms and deterrence, public-private partnership |
| Religious Right | Stewardship, moral order, national security | Support strong defense, stewardship of creation (space environment) |
| Democratic Socialist | Public investment, anti-militarism, international solidarity | Redirect military space spending to civil programs, oppose weaponization |

## Perspective Analyses

### Conservative Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 4/5 - Mostly Consistent**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Consistently prioritize military space dominance and oppose regulatory constraints on commercial operators; motivations align with stated principles of strong defense and free enterprise. |
| Principle consistency | Internal tension between supporting massive Space Force budgets (government spending) and opposing regulation of commercial space (free market). Generally resolved in favor of defense spending. |
| Goalpost stability | Stable. Support for Space Force funding and opposition to arms control have been consistent across Republican administrations and congressional terms. |
| Zero-sum behavior | View space as a domain to be dominated; frame competition with China as existential. Skeptical of cooperation that constrains U.S. freedom of action. |

**Justification:** Conservatives approach space defense through a national security lens, consistently supporting strong military capabilities and opposing constraints on U.S. freedom of action. Their positions are predictable: fund the Space Force, resist arms control, minimize regulation of commercial operators, and compete aggressively with China. The internal tension between pro-defense spending and anti-government-spending instincts is real but typically resolved in favor of defense.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Republican support for the creation of the Space Force in the FY2020 NDAA, championed by the Trump administration.
- Heritage Foundation and Hudson Institute publications consistently advocating for offensive counterspace capabilities and opposing ASAT moratoria.
- Republican members of the House and Senate Armed Services Committees consistently increasing Space Force budget requests above administration proposals.

## Compromise Proposals

### Compromise 1: *Space Awareness and Debris Prevention First*

**Bridges:** Conservative + Liberal + Centrist + Constitutionalist + Progressive + Religious Right

**Core Agreement:** All perspectives agree that the U.S. needs better awareness of what is happening in orbit and that the debris environment is worsening. This compromise packages the least controversial elements of both bills into a standalone first step.

**Policy Description:**

A standalone bill combining SSA enhancement, debris mitigation standards, and allied SSA cooperation -- without the more controversial ASAT moratorium or military modernization provisions. This approach builds bipartisan momentum and establishes the institutional infrastructure for more ambitious legislation later.

**Concern Mapping:**

- **Durability:** High. SSA and debris are low-controversy, high-value issues where all sides benefit.

### Compromise 3: *International ASAT Norm with Deterrence Safeguards*

**Bridges:** Liberal + Centrist + Constitutionalist + Progressive + Democratic Socialist

**Core Agreement:** Codifying the ASAT moratorium with a meaningful presidential waiver, combined with robust deterrence provisions, satisfies both the arms control community and those who insist on maintaining military flexibility.

**Policy Description:**

Codify the ASAT moratorium with a presidential waiver requiring certification that: (1) an adversary has conducted a destructive ASAT test, (2) national security requires a U.S. test, and (3) no alternative exists. ## Strategic Implications

### Most Viable Coalition

The most viable path forward is the **Compromise 1 coalition** (SSA + Debris), which commands the broadest support (6/9 perspectives) with the highest consistency ratio. This approach:

- Avoids the most polarizing issues (ASAT moratorium, military modernization budget).
- Delivers tangible improvements to space safety and awareness.
- Builds institutional infrastructure (SSA sharing, debris standards) that makes future legislation easier to implement.
- Establishes a bipartisan track record on space policy that facilitates more ambitious legislation later.

### Key Obstacles

- **Conservative-Progressive Divide on Militarization**: The fundamental disagreement about whether the U.S. should prioritize military dominance or restraint in space cannot be resolved within a single bill. The compromise approach sequences legislation to avoid forcing this choice prematurely.
- **Populist Skepticism of International Cooperation**: Populist opposition to international norms and institutions is a structural obstacle to any framework that relies on multilateral cooperation. This can be partially addressed by framing norms as serving American interests (protecting U.S. satellites) rather than constraining American power.
- **Cost of Transition**: The proliferated architecture mandate requires significant upfront investment during a period of competing defense priorities. This fiscal reality may delay or scale back the most ambitious provisions.

### Low Consistency Partners

The two perspectives with mixed consistency (Populist at 3/5 and Religious Right at 3/5) require different engagement strategies:

- **Populist**: Frame space security in terms of American jobs, domestic manufacturing, and competition with China. Avoid framing that emphasizes international cooperation or multilateral institutions.
- **Religious Right**: Use stewardship framing for debris and sustainability issues. Defer to their conservative alliance on military matters but appeal to their distinctive values on environmental protection of space.

### Recommended Approach

1. **Phase 1**: Introduce and pass the SSA + Debris compromise (Compromise 1) as a standalone, bipartisan bill. Build the coalition, demonstrate success, and establish the institutional infrastructure.
2. **Phase 2**: Include the proliferated architecture mandate with domestic manufacturing provisions (Compromise 2) in the next NDAA, leveraging the defense authorization process where space funding has bipartisan support.
3. **Phase 3**: Pursue the ASAT moratorium codification (Compromise 3) once the international diplomatic campaign has gained sufficient momentum (50+ nations) to demonstrate that the moratorium is becoming an international norm, not a unilateral U.S. constraint.

This sequenced approach builds coalitions incrementally, avoids forcing premature confrontation on the most divisive issues, and delivers tangible results at each stage.
