# Space Defense: Solutions

Securing the space domain requires a multi-layered strategy that builds resilient architectures, improves awareness, mitigates debris, establishes international norms, and integrates commercial capabilities into a coherent national defense posture. No single solution is sufficient; the interacting root causes of space instability demand a portfolio approach that addresses threats, vulnerabilities, governance gaps, and environmental risks simultaneously.

---

## 1. Build Resilient Satellite Architectures

The single most important strategic shift in space defense is moving from a small number of irreplaceable, exquisite satellites to distributed, redundant constellations that are resilient to attack.

**A. Accelerate Proliferated LEO Constellations**

- **Description**: Shift from small numbers of large, expensive, irreplaceable satellites in high orbits to large numbers of smaller, cheaper satellites in low Earth orbit. A distributed architecture ensures that the loss of any individual satellite does not degrade the overall capability, rendering an adversary's ASAT investment futile.
- **Implementation**: Fully fund and accelerate the Space Development Agency's Proliferated Warfighter Space Architecture (PWSA). Expand the concept beyond missile tracking and data transport to communications and ISR missions. The PWSA is organized in two-year development cycles (tranches), with Tranche 0 and 1 satellites already on orbit and Tranches 2-4 in development. Each tranche adds capability and capacity.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Fundamentally changes the adversary's cost calculus. Instead of needing to destroy 3-5 satellites to blind a constellation, an adversary would need to destroy hundreds -- an operationally impractical proposition that would generate catastrophic amounts of debris, harming the adversary's own space assets.
- **International Comparison**: The UK's Skynet 6 program and France's Syracuse IV system are exploring similar disaggregated approaches. NATO's Space Advisory Group has endorsed the principle of resilient, distributed architectures.
- **Cost Estimate**: The SDA has awarded contracts totaling approximately $5-8 billion for Tranches 0-2, a fraction of the cost of traditional national security satellite programs (SBIRS cost approximately $19 billion for 6 GEO satellites).

**B. Invest in On-Orbit Maneuver and Hardening**

- **Description**: Equip high-value satellites with greater maneuvering capability to evade threats, and harden them against electronic warfare, laser dazzle, and cyber intrusion.
- **Implementation**: Require that all new national security satellite programs include anti-jam communications links (e.g., protected waveforms on AEHF and its successor ESS), radiation-hardened components, autonomous threat-avoidance maneuver capability, cybersecurity-by-design (including encrypted command links and anomaly detection), and laser-hardened optical sensors where applicable.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Increases the difficulty and cost of attacking any individual satellite, complementing the resilience provided by proliferated architectures. Autonomous maneuver capability is particularly important for responding to co-orbital threats where reaction time may be measured in hours rather than days.
- **Key Programs**: The Space Force's Tactically Responsive Space (TacRS) office is developing rapid maneuver capabilities. The Protected Tactical SATCOM (PTS) program is designed to provide anti-jam communications for tactical forces.

**C. Develop Rapid Reconstitution Capability**

- **Description**: Pre-position replacement satellite components and develop the launch infrastructure to rapidly replace destroyed or degraded satellites within hours to days rather than months to years.
- **Implementation**: Invest in responsive launch programs. The Space Force's Victus Nox mission demonstrated a satellite launch within 27 hours of call-up in 2024. Pre-position satellite buses and payloads at launch sites. Contract with commercial launch providers (SpaceX, Rocket Lab, ULA) for emergency launch capacity under pre-negotiated "space surge" agreements.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Ensures that even if an adversary successfully degrades a constellation, the U.S. can rapidly restore capability, denying the adversary any lasting advantage. This changes the adversary's calculus from "destroy the satellite, gain permanent advantage" to "destroy the satellite, achieve temporary disruption at the cost of international condemnation and debris generation."
- **Target**: Achieve the capability to launch a replacement satellite within 24 hours of call-up by 2028, and within 12 hours by 2032.

**D. Develop Alternative PNT**

- **Description**: Reduce dependence on GPS by developing and fielding alternative positioning, navigation, and timing (PNT) systems that can function when GPS is jammed or denied.
- **Implementation**: Invest in multiple alternative PNT technologies: improved inertial navigation systems (fiber-optic and quantum inertial measurement units), celestial navigation (automated star trackers), signals of opportunity (using commercial broadcast signals for navigation), chip-scale atomic clocks, and eLoran (a modernized version of the LORAN radio navigation system). The DoD PNT Oversight Council should establish requirements for GPS-denied operations across all services.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Reduces the single-point-of-failure risk posed by GPS dependence. Even if GPS is jammed in a theater of operations, U.S. forces retain navigation and timing capability through alternative means.

## 2. Enhance Space Situational Awareness

You cannot defend what you cannot see. Comprehensive, persistent, and precise awareness of the space environment is the foundation of all other space defense efforts.

**A. Modernize the Space Surveillance Network**

- **Description**: Upgrade the ground-based and space-based sensors that track objects in orbit to handle the rapidly growing number of objects and detect threats at greater range and sensitivity.
- **Implementation**: The Space Fence (a powerful S-band radar on Kwajalein Atoll, built by Lockheed Martin at ~$1.5 billion, now operational since 2020) represents a major upgrade. Additional investments should include: next-generation optical telescopes for deep-space tracking, additional phased-array radars to increase geographic coverage, space-based SSA sensors (satellites that track other satellites), and integration with allied sensor networks. Fund a second Space Fence site (proposed for Australia) to provide southern hemisphere radar coverage.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Improves the ability to detect threats (co-orbital ASAT approaches, debris conjunctions, electronic warfare emissions) earlier, providing more time for avoidance maneuvers and informed decision-making.

**B. Develop Space Domain Awareness Satellites**

- **Description**: Deploy dedicated satellites in GEO and cislunar space that can observe activities in orbit, providing persistent, all-weather surveillance of the space domain from above.
- **Implementation**: Expand the Geosynchronous Space Situational Awareness Program (GSSAP) -- a constellation of maneuverable satellites in near-GEO orbit that inspect and characterize objects in the geosynchronous belt. Develop new sensors for cislunar awareness as activities extend to the Moon and beyond (Artemis, Chinese lunar missions, commercial lunar operations). The Space Force's "Silent Barker" program (a collaboration with the NRO) is developing space-based sensors to track objects in deep space.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Provides the ability to characterize objects in orbit (determine whether a satellite is active, maneuvering, or threatening), detect hostile intent, and attribute actions -- essential for deterrence and response. Cislunar awareness closes a growing blind spot as activity extends beyond GEO.

**C. Share SSA Data Internationally**

- **Description**: Expand the sharing of space situational awareness data with allies, partners, and commercial operators to create a comprehensive, global picture of the space environment.
- **Implementation**: Accelerate the transition of basic space traffic management to the Department of Commerce's Office of Space Commerce and its Open Architecture Data Repository. Expand SSA sharing agreements with CSpO partners and additional nations. Support commercial SSA providers (LeoLabs, ExoAnalytic, Numerica) who can serve as intermediaries for data the U.S. cannot share directly due to classification.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Increases the number of sensors and perspectives tracking the space environment. Creates a "neighborhood watch" effect where irresponsible behavior (ASAT tests, dangerous proximity operations, debris-generating events) is more likely to be detected and attributed, raising the reputational cost of norm violations.

## 3. Mitigate the Debris Threat

Debris is the space equivalent of environmental pollution -- a threat to the long-term usability of the domain that requires both prevention (mitigation) and cleanup (active removal).

**A. Enforce and Strengthen Debris Mitigation Standards**

- **Description**: Require all U.S.-licensed satellite operators to comply with updated debris mitigation guidelines, and work to internationalize these standards.
- **Implementation**: Codify debris mitigation requirements in federal law (not just FCC rules, which can be changed by future commissions). Key requirements should include: the FCC's 5-year post-mission disposal rule (adopted in 2022, requiring operators to deorbit satellites within 5 years of end-of-mission, down from the previous 25-year guideline); collision avoidance capability (the ability to maneuver to avoid tracked debris); passivation requirements (venting fuel and discharging batteries at end of life to prevent explosions); and trackability requirements (equipping small satellites with radar reflectors or transponders). Support the development of binding international guidelines through UN COPUOS.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Reduces the rate of debris accumulation and establishes clear, enforceable expectations for responsible behavior. Prevents the "race to the bottom" dynamic in which operators who cut corners on debris mitigation gain a cost advantage.

**B. Invest in Active Debris Removal (ADR)**

- **Description**: Fund the development and demonstration of technologies to remove existing debris from orbit -- particularly large, dangerous objects in congested orbital bands.
- **Implementation**: Provide government funding for ADR technology demonstration missions. Multiple approaches are under development: robotic capture (grappling debris with robotic arms), net capture, harpoon systems, laser ablation (using ground-based or space-based lasers to alter debris orbits), and electrodynamic tethers. Establish a priority list of debris objects for removal based on collision probability, orbital altitude, and object mass (the approximately 500 derelict rocket bodies and defunct satellites in the 700-1,000 km band pose the greatest cascading collision risk). Develop an international cost-sharing framework for ADR.
- **Key Programs**: ESA's ClearSpace-1 mission (planned for 2026) will demonstrate debris capture technology. JAXA's Commercial Removal of Debris Demonstration (CRD2) program funds Astroscale to demonstrate debris capture in LEO. The U.S. should match or exceed these efforts.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Addresses the existing debris population, not just future debris generation. Could reduce the risk of Kessler syndrome in critical orbital bands. NASA modeling suggests that removing as few as 5-10 large derelict objects per year from the 700-1,000 km band could stabilize the debris environment.

**C. Pursue an International ASAT Testing Moratorium**

- **Description**: Build on the 2022 U.S. commitment and the 2023 UN General Assembly resolution to establish a broad, international moratorium on destructive direct-ascent ASAT missile tests.
- **Implementation**: Continue diplomatic efforts to recruit additional nations to the moratorium (currently over 40 endorsements). Explore whether a moratorium can be formalized in a UN Security Council resolution (though China and Russia would likely veto) or multilateral agreement among like-minded nations. Develop verification mechanisms using national technical means (U.S. space surveillance can detect ASAT launches and satellite breakups), commercial SSA data, and allied sensor networks.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Prevents the single most destructive category of debris-generating events. Establishes a critical norm against the most destabilizing form of space weapons testing. Even if China and Russia do not formally join, the existence of the norm raises the political cost of future testing.

## 4. Establish International Space Security Norms

In the absence of a comprehensive space weapons treaty (which is politically impossible in the near term), norms of responsible behavior represent the most viable path to reducing instability.

**A. Lead the Development of "Rules of the Road"**

- **Description**: Work with allies and partners to develop and promote a set of norms for responsible behavior in space, focusing on the most destabilizing activities.
- **Implementation**: Proposed norms should include:
    - No destructive ASAT testing that generates long-lived debris.
    - No dangerous proximity operations against other nations' spacecraft without prior notification and consent. Define a "keep-out zone" distance for uninvited approaches.
    - No interference with nuclear early warning satellites (given the catastrophic escalation risk -- blinding a nation's missile warning system could be interpreted as a prelude to a nuclear first strike).
    - Transparency measures: pre-launch notification of orbital parameters, notification of planned proximity operations, publication of national space policies and doctrines.
    - Data sharing on space debris and conjunction warnings as a public good.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Creates a framework for judging state behavior in space, enabling collective diplomatic and economic responses to norm violations. Even without enforcement mechanisms, norms shape expectations and raise the cost of defection.

**B. Strengthen the Outer Space Treaty Framework**

- **Description**: While a new comprehensive space treaty is unlikely in the near term, work to strengthen the existing framework through additional protocols, transparency mechanisms, and interpretive guidelines.
- **Implementation**: Pursue a protocol to the Outer Space Treaty specifically addressing debris-generating activities. Develop confidence-building measures (pre-launch notifications, orbital parameter transparency, voluntary proximity operations notifications) bilaterally with China and Russia. Support the Woomera Manual project, which is developing a comprehensive guide to how international law applies to military space operations.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Incrementally builds the legal and normative framework without requiring the politically impossible task of negotiating a comprehensive new treaty. Creates the analytical foundation for determining when state behavior in space constitutes a violation of existing international law.

## 5. Integrate Commercial Space Capabilities

The commercial space revolution is the most significant structural change in the space domain since the Cold War. Harnessing it for national security while managing its risks is essential.

**A. Establish a Commercial Space Integration Framework**

- **Description**: Develop clear legal, contractual, and operational frameworks for integrating commercial satellite services into national defense.
- **Implementation**: Establish standard contracts for commercial SATCOM, ISR, and SSA services that address wartime obligations, indemnification, replacement, and security requirements. Create a "Commercial Space Reserve" program (analogous to the Civil Reserve Air Fleet, or CRAF, which guarantees commercial airlines government contracts in exchange for priority access during crises) where commercial satellite operators agree to provide priority capacity during national emergencies in exchange for peacetime contracts and guaranteed purchase commitments. Establish clear legal guidance on the status of commercial satellites under the law of armed conflict.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Provides legal clarity for commercial providers, ensures military access to commercial capabilities during conflict, and creates incentives for commercial operators to invest in resilience and hardening. The CRAF model has proven effective in aviation for over 70 years and could be adapted for space.

**B. Diversify the Launch and Satellite Industrial Base**

- **Description**: Reduce the concentration risk created by dependence on a small number of commercial providers, particularly SpaceX.
- **Implementation**: Maintain at least two National Security Space Launch (NSSL) providers (currently SpaceX and ULA). Actively support new entrants -- Rocket Lab (Neutron medium-lift vehicle), Blue Origin (New Glenn heavy-lift vehicle), and Relativity Space (Terran R) -- through government launch contracts, Commercial Augmentation Space Reserve agreements, and technology investment. Invest in domestic production of critical satellite components (solar cells, reaction wheels, star trackers, radiation-hardened processors) to reduce supply chain vulnerabilities and foreign dependencies, particularly on Chinese rare earth elements.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Reduces single points of failure in the space industrial base, ensures competitive pressure on pricing, and provides the surge capacity needed in a crisis. Having three or more qualified launch providers would substantially reduce the risk posed by a fleet-wide grounding of any single vehicle.

**C. Establish Cybersecurity Standards for Space Systems**

- **Description**: The 2022 Viasat cyberattack demonstrated that ground segments and command links are among the most vulnerable elements of the space architecture. Mandatory cybersecurity standards are needed.
- **Implementation**: Require all national security satellite systems (government and commercial) to meet defined cybersecurity baselines for ground stations, command-and-control links, and inter-satellite links. Mandate encrypted communications, continuous monitoring, anomaly detection, and incident response planning. The Space Force's Commercial Satellite Communications Office should include cybersecurity requirements in all commercial SATCOM contracts.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Raises the bar for cyberattack on the space architecture, reducing the vulnerability of both military and commercial systems to the type of attack that disrupted Viasat's KA-SAT network.
