# Space Defense: Reform Roadmap

Securing the space domain requires a phased approach that prioritizes building resilient architectures and establishing norms in the near term, scaling capabilities and deepening alliances in the medium term, and achieving sustainable space security over the long term. Each phase builds on the accomplishments of the previous one, creating compounding benefits over time. The roadmap is designed to be adaptive -- specific timelines may shift based on adversary actions, budget realities, and technological developments, but the strategic direction and priorities remain constant.

---

## Phase 1: Foundations and Quick Wins (Years 1-2)

This phase focuses on immediate legislative, executive, and diplomatic actions that establish the policy framework and begin the most urgent capability improvements. The emphasis is on actions that can be accomplished quickly, create political momentum for further reforms, and demonstrate U.S. leadership to allies and adversaries.

| Priority | Action | Lead Agency | Key Performance Indicator (KPI) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Codify ASAT Testing Moratorium** | Congress passes legislation codifying the U.S. commitment not to conduct destructive direct-ascent ASAT missile tests, making it durable beyond any single administration. | Congress / State Department | Law is enacted. Diplomatic campaign recruits at least 10 additional nations to the moratorium within 12 months (from ~40 to 50+). |
| **2. Accelerate Proliferated LEO** | Fully fund and accelerate the Space Development Agency's PWSA Tranche 2 and Tranche 3 satellite procurements. Protect SDA funding from reprogramming or cuts in annual budget negotiations. | DoD / SDA / Space Force | PWSA Tranche 2 achieves initial operational capability on schedule. Tranche 3 contracts are awarded. At least 100 PWSA satellites on orbit by end of Phase 1. |
| **3. Strengthen Debris Mitigation** | Pass the Space Sustainability Act codifying debris mitigation standards (including the FCC's 5-year disposal rule) into federal law. Establish debris mitigation as a condition of all government satellite contracts. | Congress / FCC / Commerce | Law is enacted. All new U.S.-licensed satellite operators comply with updated standards. Debris mitigation plan required for all government satellite contracts. |
| **4. Expand SSA Sharing** | Expand space situational awareness data sharing agreements with all CSpO partners and at least 10 additional allied nations. Accelerate the Department of Commerce Open Architecture Data Repository. | DoD / Commerce / State | New SSA sharing agreements are signed. Department of Commerce Open Architecture Data Repository reaches initial operating capability. |
| **5. Pass Space Force Modernization Act** | Congress passes legislation authorizing commercial space integration frameworks (including a Commercial Space Reserve pilot program), space cyber defense requirements for all national security satellite systems, and acquisition reform measures to protect the SDA model. | Congress / DoD | Law is enacted. Space Force issues implementing guidance within 180 days. Commercial Space Reserve pilot program launched with at least 3 commercial operators. |
| **6. Establish Space Cybersecurity Baseline** | DoD issues a directive requiring all national security satellite systems (government and commercial) to meet defined cybersecurity standards for ground stations, command links, and data systems. | DoD CIO / Space Force | Directive issued. Compliance assessment of existing systems completed within 12 months. All new contracts include cybersecurity requirements. |

**Goal of Phase 1**: Establish the legal and policy foundations for sustainable space security. Demonstrate U.S. leadership on norms. Begin the architectural shift from exquisite to resilient constellations. Create the institutional framework for commercial integration.

## Phase 2: Scaling Capability and Alliances (Years 3-5)

This phase focuses on delivering new capabilities at scale, deepening allied cooperation, demonstrating new technologies, and building the institutional infrastructure for long-term space security.

| Priority | Action | Lead Agency | Key Performance Indicator (KPI) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Achieve PWSA IOC** | The Proliferated Warfighter Space Architecture reaches initial operational capability for missile tracking and data transport, providing the first operational demonstration of the proliferated, resilient architecture. | SDA / Space Force | PWSA provides persistent global missile tracking coverage for ballistic and hypersonic threats. Data transport layer operational with latency targets met. At least 300 PWSA satellites on orbit. |
| **2. Demonstrate Active Debris Removal** | Fund and execute the first U.S. government-supported active debris removal demonstration mission, targeting a high-priority debris object in the 700-1,000 km band. | NASA / DARPA / Space Force | ADR demonstration mission successfully captures or de-orbits a tracked debris object. Technology readiness validated for operational deployment. Lessons learned incorporated into follow-on operational program. |
| **3. Establish Commercial Space Reserve** | Full-scale establishment of the Commercial Space Reserve program modeled on the Civil Reserve Air Fleet. Commercial operators agree to provide priority satellite communications, imagery, and launch capacity during national emergencies. | DoD / Commerce | Framework fully established. At least 5 major commercial operators enrolled (including SATCOM, ISR, and launch providers). First combined government-commercial tabletop exercise and live exercise conducted. |
| **4. Formalize Allied Space Operations** | Expand the Combined Space Operations (CSpO) initiative to include formal combined space operations plans, shared doctrine, interoperable systems, and combined space exercises. Bring NATO's Space Centre to full operational capability. | Space Command / State / NATO | Combined space operations plan published. First combined live exercise conducted. At least 2 additional nations join CSpO (target: India, South Korea, or Nordic nations). NATO Space Centre conducts first collective defense scenario exercise. |
| **5. Pursue UN Norms Resolution** | Lead diplomatic effort to adopt a UN General Assembly resolution establishing norms against debris-generating ASAT tests, dangerous proximity operations, and interference with nuclear early warning satellites. | State Department / UN Mission | Resolution passes with support from at least 100 nations. Key space-faring nations (India, Japan, EU members) co-sponsor. |
| **6. Develop Responsive Launch** | Achieve responsive launch capability: the ability to launch a replacement satellite within 24 hours of call-up, demonstrated through regular exercises. | Space Force / SpaceX / Rocket Lab | Space Force conducts at least 2 responsive launch exercises per year, demonstrating 24-hour launch readiness. Pre-positioned satellite buses and payloads at designated launch sites. |
| **7. Field Alternative PNT** | Initial fielding of alternative PNT capabilities across joint force, reducing critical dependence on GPS for navigation and timing. | DoD / PNT Oversight Council | GPS-denied navigation capability demonstrated in joint exercises. MGUE (M-code GPS receivers) fielded to at least 50% of priority platforms. Alternative timing sources deployed for critical infrastructure. |
| **8. Develop Cislunar Awareness** | Begin deployment of sensors capable of tracking objects and detecting activities between GEO and the Moon. | Space Force / DARPA / NRO | Cislunar surveillance prototype deployed and demonstrating tracking capability beyond GEO. Initial cislunar space domain awareness architecture defined. |

**Goal of Phase 2**: Deliver resilient satellite constellations at scale, demonstrate debris cleanup, formalize allied space cooperation, advance international norms from non-binding commitments toward broadly adopted standards, and close critical capability gaps in responsive launch, alternative PNT, and cislunar awareness.

## Phase 3: Sustainable Space Security (Years 6-10)

This phase focuses on achieving the long-term vision of a stable, sustainable, and secure space domain where the U.S. and its allies maintain a durable competitive advantage.

| Priority | Action | Lead Agency | Key Performance Indicator (KPI) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Resilient Architecture Achieved** | The U.S. national security space architecture is sufficiently distributed and redundant that the loss of any 10% of satellites does not degrade any critical mission below operational thresholds. | Space Force / SDA | Wargames and exercises demonstrate that adversary ASAT attacks cannot achieve strategically meaningful degradation of U.S. space capabilities. PWSA at full operational capability with 500+ satellites. |
| **2. Operational ADR Capability** | Active debris removal moves from demonstration to operational capability, with regular missions removing high-priority debris objects from congested orbital bands. International cost-sharing framework established. | NASA / Commerce / International | ADR program removes at least 10 high-priority debris objects per year. Debris population in the 700-1,000 km band stabilizes and begins to decline. International partners contribute to ADR costs and operations. |
| **3. International Norms Enforced** | The international community has established and begun enforcing consequences for violations of space security norms. | State / UN / Allied governments | At least one case of collective diplomatic or economic response to a norm violation (e.g., coordinated sanctions or diplomatic consequences for a debris-generating ASAT test or dangerous proximity operation). |
| **4. Cislunar Domain Awareness** | U.S. and allied space surveillance extends to cislunar space, providing persistent awareness of activities between GEO and the Moon. | Space Force / NRO / DARPA | Cislunar surveillance network operational with persistent tracking of objects beyond GEO. Integrated into Combined Space Operations Center. |
| **5. Next-Generation Space Force** | The Space Force has fully matured as an independent service with its own culture, doctrine, acquisition processes, and career pathways optimized for the space domain. | Space Force | Space Force personnel retention rates meet or exceed other services. Acquisition timelines for new satellite programs reduced by 50% compared to 2020 baseline. Guardian career development model fully implemented. |
| **6. Space Traffic Management Mature** | The Department of Commerce has fully assumed responsibility for civilian space traffic management, operating a comprehensive system for conjunction warnings, spectrum coordination, and operator communication. | Commerce / DoD | Commerce's Open Architecture Data Repository is the authoritative global source for civilian space traffic coordination. Military resources freed to focus on warfighting space domain awareness. |
| **7. Responsive Reconstitution Proven** | The U.S. can launch and deploy replacement satellites within 12 hours of a call-up order, with pre-positioned assets and multiple launch providers. | Space Force / Commercial | Responsive reconstitution demonstrated in exercises at 12-hour timescale. Multiple launch providers qualified for emergency launch. Satellite buses and payloads pre-positioned at 3+ launch sites. |

**Goal of Phase 3**: The United States has achieved a sustainable competitive advantage in space through resilient architectures, allied partnerships, and international norms, while the orbital debris environment is stabilized and beginning to improve. Space conflict is deterred because the combination of resilience, reconstitution, and norms makes attacks strategically futile and politically costly.

## Contingency Planning

The roadmap must account for disruptive events that could accelerate or alter the timeline:

- **Major ASAT Test by Adversary**: If China or Russia conducts another debris-generating ASAT test, the U.S. should be prepared to escalate diplomatic pressure, accelerate norms adoption, and use the event to build support for binding restrictions.
- **Kessler Syndrome Event**: A cascading collision event in a critical orbital band would require emergency ADR deployment and potentially temporary restrictions on launches to affected altitudes. Contingency plans should be developed in advance.
- **Major Cyberattack on Space Systems**: A cyberattack on critical satellite systems (beyond the scale of the 2022 Viasat incident) could accelerate cybersecurity standards implementation and drive demand for more resilient architectures.
- **Conflict Involving Space**: Any armed conflict in which space assets are deliberately targeted would fundamentally change the political dynamics, likely accelerating both U.S. space defense spending and international support for binding norms.
