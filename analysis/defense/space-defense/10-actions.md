# Space Defense: What You Can Do

Space defense may seem like the exclusive province of the military and intelligence community, but citizens, professionals, and organizations all have meaningful roles to play in shaping U.S. space policy, supporting sustainability, and building the workforce the nation needs. Space security affects everyone -- GPS navigation, weather forecasting, financial transactions, emergency services, and national defense all depend on satellites that are increasingly threatened. The decisions made about space defense over the next decade will shape the space environment for generations.

---

## 1. For Individuals: Educate and Advocate

### Understand the Stakes

- **Learn the Basics**: Space security is not an abstract technical subject -- it directly affects daily life. GPS provides the timing signals that synchronize financial markets, coordinate the electrical grid, and enable cell phone networks. Weather satellites provide the data that drives forecasts for agriculture, disaster preparedness, and transportation. The loss or degradation of these capabilities would have immediate, cascading consequences for the U.S. economy and public safety.
- **Understand the Threats**: The space domain faces real and growing threats from adversary ASAT weapons, the accumulating debris environment, and the challenges of governing a domain that is simultaneously military, commercial, scientific, and international. Understanding these threats -- and the tradeoffs involved in addressing them -- enables informed advocacy.
- **Recognize the Tradeoffs**: Space defense involves genuine tensions between deterrence and debris, military capability and arms control, commercial freedom and regulation, transparency and secrecy. Informed citizens should resist simplistic framing and engage with the real complexity of the issues.

### Contact Your Representatives

- **Support the ASAT Testing Moratorium**: Ask your senators and representatives to support legislation codifying the U.S. commitment not to conduct destructive ASAT tests. The moratorium is a bipartisan issue -- it protects both military assets and the civilian space economy. Emphasize that the U.S. has already proven its ASAT capability and does not need further debris-generating tests.
- **Support Space Sustainability Funding**: Advocate for appropriations for NASA's Orbital Debris Program Office, the Space Force's SSA networks, the Department of Commerce's space traffic management role, and active debris removal technology development. Debris mitigation is a long-term investment that protects the usability of the space domain for all.
- **Support Resilient Architecture Funding**: Advocate for continued funding of the Space Development Agency's Proliferated Warfighter Space Architecture. Proliferated constellations are the single most important architectural decision for space defense, and they face opposition from incumbents who profit from the exquisite-satellite model.
- **Engage on Commercial Space Regulation**: When Congress considers legislation affecting commercial space regulation (debris mitigation standards, licensing, spectrum management), urge a balanced approach that supports innovation while protecting the shared orbital environment.

### Stay Informed

- **Follow the Debate**: Track space policy developments through resources like SpaceNews (the industry publication of record), The Space Review, and reports from CSIS and the Secure World Foundation. The Space Force's official website and social media provide updates on operations and programs.
- **Read the Annual Threat Assessments**: The CSIS *Space Threat Assessment* and the Secure World Foundation's *Global Counterspace Capabilities* report are published annually and are freely available online. These provide the most comprehensive unclassified picture of adversary space threats.
- **Attend Public Events**: Organizations like the Secure World Foundation, the Space Foundation, and the American Institute of Aeronautics and Astronautics (AIAA) host conferences, webinars, and public events on space security topics. Many are free or low-cost and available online.

## 2. For Professionals and Students: Build the Space Workforce

The space enterprise -- military, intelligence, and commercial -- faces critical workforce shortages that threaten the nation's ability to compete in and secure the space domain. Building the space workforce is a national security imperative.

### Education Pathways

- **Pursue STEM Education**: The Space Force and the broader space industry face critical workforce shortages in engineering (electrical, mechanical, aerospace, software), physics, data science, cybersecurity, and space operations. Educational pathways from community colleges (many of which now offer space-related technical programs) to graduate programs can lead to space careers.
- **Space-Specific Programs**: Several universities offer specialized programs in space security, space policy, and space engineering that prepare students for careers at the intersection of technology and national security. Key programs include:
    - **Air Force Institute of Technology (AFIT)**: The Space Force's graduate school for space systems engineering and operations.
    - **Johns Hopkins University Applied Physics Laboratory (APL)**: Research and graduate education in space systems.
    - **George Washington University, Space Policy Institute**: Graduate program focused on space policy and international space law.
    - **MIT Department of Aeronautics and Astronautics**: Leading graduate program in space systems engineering.
    - **University of Colorado Boulder, Aerospace Engineering Sciences**: Strong connections to the space defense community in Colorado Springs and the broader space industry.
    - **Naval Postgraduate School**: Offers space systems programs for military officers.
- **Cybersecurity Education**: Given the growing importance of defending space systems against cyberattack, cybersecurity professionals with an understanding of space systems are in particularly high demand.

### Career Opportunities

- **Join the Space Force or Reserves**: The Space Force is the smallest military branch and actively recruiting. It offers career tracks in space operations, intelligence, engineering, cybersecurity, and acquisitions. Civilian professionals with relevant skills can also serve through the Air Force Reserve or Air National Guard space units.
- **Work in Commercial Space**: The commercial space industry is growing rapidly and offers career opportunities in satellite engineering, launch operations, mission planning, data analytics, ground systems engineering, and policy. Major employers include SpaceX, United Launch Alliance, Northrop Grumman, L3Harris, Lockheed Martin, Planet Labs, Maxar, Rocket Lab, and scores of startups.
- **Work in Government**: Civilian careers at the Space Force, Space Command, NRO, NASA, the Department of Commerce (Office of Space Commerce), the FCC (Space Bureau), and intelligence agencies offer the opportunity to shape space policy from within government.
- **Engage in Research**: Academic researchers can contribute to critical areas including orbital mechanics, debris modeling, active debris removal technology, space situational awareness algorithms, remote sensing, space weather, space law and governance, and deterrence theory applied to space.

## 3. For Businesses: Contribute to Space Security

### Satellite Operators

- **Comply with Debris Mitigation Standards**: Satellite operators should comply with or exceed FCC and international debris mitigation guidelines, including the 5-year post-mission disposal rule. Design satellites with end-of-life disposal capability (either controlled reorbiting or de-orbit). Implement passivation procedures (venting remaining fuel, discharging batteries) at end of mission to prevent accidental explosions that generate debris.
- **Share SSA Data**: Commercial satellite operators should participate in data sharing programs with the Department of Commerce and the Space Force to improve the collective picture of the orbital environment. LeoLabs, ExoAnalytic Solutions, and other commercial SSA providers demonstrate that private-sector data can complement government tracking.
- **Implement Collision Avoidance**: Maintain the capability to maneuver satellites to avoid tracked debris. Monitor conjunction data messages from the 18th Space Defense Squadron and commercial SSA providers. Establish procedures for evaluating and executing avoidance maneuvers.
- **Plan for Resilience**: Design satellite constellations with resilience in mind -- distributed architectures, redundant ground stations, encrypted command links, and cyber-hardened systems.

### Businesses That Depend on Space

- **Assess Space Vulnerabilities**: Companies that depend on GPS (financial services, logistics, transportation, agriculture, energy), satellite communications (maritime, aviation, remote operations), or satellite imagery (agriculture, insurance, resource management) should assess their vulnerabilities to space service disruption.
- **Develop Contingency Plans**: Create business continuity plans for degraded or denied space environments. Identify alternative PNT sources, backup communications pathways, and manual procedures that can function when space services are unavailable.
- **Invest in Alternative Technologies**: Support the development and deployment of alternative PNT technologies (e.g., eLoran, chip-scale atomic clocks), terrestrial backup communications, and other systems that reduce single-point-of-failure dependence on space.

### Workforce Development

- **Support Workforce Development**: Space companies and companies that depend on space services can partner with universities, community colleges, and trade schools to build the talent pipeline the industry needs. Internship programs, scholarships, and research partnerships help develop the next generation of space professionals.
- **STEM Outreach**: Support programs that introduce K-12 students to space science, engineering, and policy. Organizations like the Space Foundation, the Challenger Center, and Students for the Exploration and Development of Space (SEDS) provide structured programs.

## 4. Engage in the Policy Debate

### Key Policy Issues for Public Engagement

- **Support the ASAT Testing Moratorium**: The 2022 U.S. commitment not to conduct destructive ASAT tests was a major step. Urge other nations and your own representatives to formalize and expand this norm. Emphasize that debris-generating ASAT tests threaten all nations' space assets, including the testing nation's own.
- **Advocate for Space Sustainability Funding**: Active debris removal technology and improved space surveillance need sustained public investment. Support appropriations for NASA's Orbital Debris Program, the Space Force's SSA networks, the Department of Commerce's space traffic management role, and the SDA's PWSA.
- **Participate in Public Comment**: Federal agencies (FCC, Department of Commerce, DoD) routinely seek public comment on space policy rules and regulations. The FCC's rulemaking on debris mitigation, the Commerce Department's development of the Open Architecture Data Repository, and DoD's commercial space integration policies all include public comment opportunities. Informed public engagement improves policy outcomes.
- **Support International Cooperation**: Advocate for U.S. participation in multilateral space governance forums (UN COPUOS, the Conference on Disarmament) and for expansion of allied space cooperation through the Combined Space Operations initiative and NATO's Space Centre.
- **Demand Transparency**: While legitimate classification concerns exist, the public has a right to understand how space defense dollars are spent and what threats the nation faces in space. Support congressional oversight of space programs and advocate for declassification of SSA data that does not compromise sources and methods.

## What Effective Engagement Looks Like

Space defense advocacy is most effective when it:

- **Is specific**: Rather than "support space defense," ask for specific actions: "support the ASAT testing moratorium codification bill," "fund the PWSA at the requested level," or "support the 5-year debris disposal rule."
- **Is bipartisan**: Space security is not a partisan issue. GPS protects both military operations and the civilian economy. Debris threatens all nations. Frame arguments in terms that resonate across the political spectrum.
- **Is informed**: Policymakers respond to constituents who demonstrate knowledge of the issues. Reading the CSIS Space Threat Assessment or the Secure World Foundation's counterspace report provides the background needed for credible engagement.
- **Is sustained**: Space defense is not a single vote or a single bill. It is an ongoing set of policy decisions that require sustained attention across budget cycles and administrations. Regular engagement with elected officials, attendance at hearings and public events, and ongoing education are more effective than one-time actions.
