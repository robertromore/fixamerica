# Cyber Warfare: Root Causes

## Systemic Analysis

Cyberspace is an inherently unstable and conflict-prone domain. This is not due to any single policy failure, but to the fundamental technical and structural characteristics of the domain itself. These characteristics create a strong and persistent advantage for offensive actors, while the political, economic, and institutional structures of the United States create additional vulnerabilities that adversaries actively exploit. Understanding these root causes is essential to designing policies that can improve stability rather than merely react to the latest breach.

## 1. The Offense-Dominant Nature of Cyberspace

The core problem is that it is easier, cheaper, and more effective to attack in cyberspace than it is to defend. This structural imbalance is unlike any other warfighting domain and is the single most important factor driving cyber instability.

- **The Attacker's Advantage**: An attacker only needs to find one vulnerability to succeed. A defender must protect against all possible vulnerabilities across all systems, all the time -- an impossible task. Modern enterprise networks contain millions of lines of code, thousands of devices, and countless configuration settings, any one of which could contain an exploitable flaw. Microsoft alone patches over 1,000 vulnerabilities per year across its product line; organizations that fall even weeks behind on patching create openings for attackers.

- **Asymmetric Costs**: The cost of entry for a cyber operation is extremely low compared to traditional warfare. A small team of skilled hackers operating from a rented office can develop tools that achieve strategic effects -- stealing the security clearance records of 21 million federal employees (OPM hack) or shutting down a major fuel pipeline (Colonial Pipeline) -- at a cost that would not even register as a rounding error in a conventional military budget. This asymmetry empowers weaker states and non-state actors. North Korea, a country that cannot reliably feed its population, has built a cyber program capable of stealing billions of dollars from the global financial system.

- **Zero-Day Exploits**: The existence of "zero-day" vulnerabilities -- flaws in software that are unknown to the defender and for which no patch exists -- means that even a perfectly configured and updated system can be compromised. The market for zero-day exploits is a thriving gray market where individual vulnerabilities in widely used software can sell for $500,000 to $2.5 million (Zerodium pricing, 2024). The U.S. government, through the NSA and CIA, is one of the largest purchasers of zero-day exploits, creating a structural tension between offense (hoarding vulnerabilities for operational use) and defense (disclosing them so they can be patched).

- **The Expanding Attack Surface**: The attack surface grows relentlessly. The proliferation of Internet of Things (IoT) devices, cloud computing, operational technology (OT) systems in critical infrastructure, and remote work environments has massively expanded the number of potential entry points for attackers. By 2025, an estimated 75 billion IoT devices are connected globally, many with minimal or no security features.

## 2. The Attribution Problem

It is extremely difficult to quickly and definitively prove who is responsible for a cyberattack. This problem strikes at the heart of deterrence theory and is a primary driver of the instability in cyberspace.

- **Technical Obstacles**: Attackers can route their operations through multiple countries, use compromised infrastructure belonging to innocent third parties, employ off-the-shelf tools rather than custom malware, deploy false flags mimicking other nations' tradecraft, and use encrypted communications to coordinate. China's Volt Typhoon campaign specifically used "living off the land" techniques -- legitimate system administration tools already present on the target networks -- precisely to avoid the malware signatures that enable attribution.

- **Plausible Deniability**: The technical difficulty of attribution provides states with plausible deniability that does not exist in other domains. If a Russian tank crosses the border into a neighboring country, attribution is trivial. If Russian intelligence conducts a cyberattack routed through servers in Brazil, using tools that mimic Chinese tradecraft, attribution is a complex analytical judgment that may take weeks or months and may never achieve certainty. States like Russia and China exploit this dynamic to conduct aggressive operations while maintaining the ability to deny responsibility.

- **The Challenge to Deterrence**: Traditional deterrence theory relies on a credible threat of retaliation. If the victim of an attack cannot be certain who the attacker is, they cannot credibly threaten to retaliate, and the deterrent effect is severely weakened. For example, if the U.S. power grid is attacked during a crisis, but the technical evidence points to servers in multiple countries and could implicate several adversaries, would a President risk military escalation by retaliating against Russia or China based on incomplete evidence?

- **Improving but Imperfect Capabilities**: U.S. attribution capabilities have improved significantly. The government has publicly attributed major operations to specific nation-states (and in some cases, specific military units and individual operatives) with increasing speed and confidence. The 2018 indictment of 12 GRU officers for the DNC hack and the 2020 indictment of specific Sandworm operators demonstrate this capability. However, the speed of attribution still lags far behind the speed of cyber operations, and public attribution is a policy decision as much as a technical one.

## 3. The Entanglement of Civilian and Military Infrastructure

Unlike other warfighting domains, cyberspace is almost entirely owned and operated by the private sector. This creates a fundamental structural vulnerability that has no parallel in the physical domains of warfare.

- **A Shared Domain**: The same physical infrastructure -- fiber optic cables, routers, cloud data centers, cell towers -- carries both military and civilian traffic. The DoD relies on commercial ISPs (AT&T, Verizon, Lumen) for much of its communications, on commercial cloud providers (AWS, Microsoft Azure, Google Cloud, Oracle) for data storage and processing, and on private companies for its power, water, and logistics. The Defense Information Systems Agency (DISA) operates military networks, but these networks are deeply interconnected with commercial infrastructure.

- **Critical Infrastructure Vulnerability**: The 16 critical infrastructure sectors designated under Presidential Policy Directive 21 -- including energy, water, transportation, healthcare, financial services, and communications -- are overwhelmingly privately owned and were designed for efficiency and reliability, not for resilience against state-level cyberattacks. Many operational technology (OT) systems controlling physical processes (power generation, water treatment, manufacturing) run legacy software that was never designed to be connected to the internet and cannot be easily updated or patched.

- **The Risk of Collateral Damage**: The entanglement of civilian and military networks means that a military cyberattack can easily have unintended and catastrophic consequences for civilians. The 2017 NotPetya attack, aimed at Ukraine, spread globally through the supply chain and caused over $10 billion in damage to companies like Maersk, FedEx, and Merck that had no connection to the intended target. This collateral damage risk complicates both offensive planning and deterrence calculations.

- **The Public-Private Defense Dilemma**: The government cannot defend this infrastructure on its own. It needs the cooperation of private companies. However, these companies have different incentives (profit vs. national security), different legal obligations (to shareholders vs. to the nation), and are often wary of government mandates or surveillance on their networks. The result is a patchwork of voluntary and mandatory cybersecurity measures that varies dramatically across sectors and leaves significant gaps.

## 4. The Lack of International Norms and Arms Control

The absence of a clear, enforceable international framework for state behavior in cyberspace creates a permissive environment for escalation and conflict.

- **No "Rules of the Road"**: There is no broad international consensus on what constitutes a legitimate act of espionage versus an illegal act of war in cyberspace. Is the theft of millions of security clearance records (OPM hack) an acceptable intelligence operation or an act of aggression? Is the pre-positioning of destructive malware on an adversary's power grid (Volt Typhoon) preparation for war or a legitimate form of deterrence? International law provides general principles (sovereignty, non-intervention, proportionality), but their application to cyber operations is deeply contested.

- **The UN GGE Process**: The United Nations Group of Governmental Experts (GGE) has produced consensus reports (2013, 2015, 2021) affirming that existing international law applies to cyberspace and establishing voluntary norms of responsible state behavior. However, these norms are non-binding, lack enforcement mechanisms, and are routinely violated by the same nations that endorsed them. Russia and China both signed the 2015 GGE report while simultaneously conducting massive cyber espionage and disruptive operations.

- **The Impossibility of Traditional Arms Control**: Cyber weapons are just lines of code. They can be replicated infinitely, transferred instantly, and concealed on ordinary computers. Traditional arms control, which relies on counting and inspecting physical objects like missiles, tanks, or nuclear warheads, is fundamentally impossible in cyberspace. There is no way to verify that a state has destroyed or limited its cyber weapons arsenal, and any agreement to do so would be unverifiable and therefore unenforceable.

- **Divergent Visions of Internet Governance**: The United States and its allies promote a model of a "free, open, and secure" internet, while Russia and China advocate for "cyber sovereignty" -- the principle that each state has the right to control the internet within its borders. These fundamentally different visions make it difficult to establish shared norms because the parties cannot agree on the underlying governance framework.

- **The "Vulnerabilities Equities Process" (VEP)**: The U.S. government faces a constant dilemma. When it discovers a new software vulnerability, it can either disclose it to the software vendor so it can be patched (improving defense for everyone), or it can keep it secret and develop it into a new offensive weapon. This choice between offense and defense is a core tension in cyber policy. The VEP, formalized by a 2017 White House charter, is intended to manage this tradeoff, but critics argue it is biased toward retention for offensive use. The 2017 Shadow Brokers leak of NSA hacking tools -- which were subsequently used in the WannaCry and NotPetya attacks -- demonstrated the catastrophic risk when retained vulnerabilities are compromised.

## 5. Workforce and Institutional Capacity Gaps

The government's ability to operate effectively in cyberspace is constrained by persistent shortages of qualified personnel and institutional structures that were not designed for the speed and complexity of the cyber domain.

- **The Talent Gap**: The United States faces a cybersecurity workforce shortage of over 500,000 positions across government and the private sector. The military services and intelligence agencies struggle to recruit and retain top cyber talent when the private sector offers salaries two to four times higher. A skilled offensive cyber operator who earns $120,000 in the military can command $300,000-$500,000 at a major tech company or cybersecurity firm.

- **Institutional Fragmentation**: Responsibility for cybersecurity is fragmented across multiple agencies -- USCYBERCOM for military operations, NSA for intelligence, CISA for civilian government and critical infrastructure, FBI for law enforcement, Secret Service for financial crimes, and multiple sector-specific regulators. This fragmentation creates coordination challenges, bureaucratic turf battles, and gaps that adversaries exploit.

- **Decision-Making Speed**: Cyber operations unfold at machine speed -- an attack can compromise thousands of systems in minutes -- but the U.S. national security decision-making process was designed for the slower tempo of conventional operations. The interagency deliberation, legal review, and senior-level approval required for significant cyber operations can take days or weeks, creating a structural disadvantage against adversaries who operate with less oversight and faster decision cycles.

## 6. Perverse Economic Incentives in the Technology Sector

The economic incentives in the technology industry systematically prioritize speed-to-market and features over security, creating the vulnerabilities that adversaries exploit.

- **Security as a Cost Center**: For most technology companies, security is a cost that reduces profit margins rather than a feature that generates revenue. The competitive pressure to ship products quickly and cheaply creates incentives to underinvest in security, testing, and secure coding practices.

- **Externalized Costs**: When a software vulnerability leads to a breach, the costs are borne primarily by the victim (the user), not by the software vendor. This externalization of costs means that vendors do not face the full economic consequences of their security failures, weakening the incentive to invest in security. The 2023 National Cybersecurity Strategy's proposal to shift liability to software vendors is the most significant policy attempt to address this misalignment.

- **Legacy Systems**: Critical infrastructure operators rely on operational technology (OT) and industrial control systems (ICS) that were designed decades ago without cybersecurity in mind. These systems are often too expensive to replace, too critical to take offline for updates, and too deeply embedded in industrial processes to secure retroactively. The result is a vast installed base of inherently vulnerable systems controlling the nation's most critical physical processes.

## Causal Chain of Cyber Instability

```text
Low barrier to entry and attacker's advantage (Root Cause 1)
    |
    v
States and non-state actors develop sophisticated offensive capabilities
    |
    v
The attribution problem makes it difficult to deter these attacks (Root Cause 2)
    |
    v
The entanglement of civilian and military networks creates high collateral damage risk (Root Cause 3)
    |
    v
The lack of international norms prevents clear "rules of the road" (Root Cause 4)
    |
    v
Workforce gaps and institutional fragmentation limit the defender's ability to respond (Root Cause 5)
    |
    v
Perverse economic incentives ensure that new vulnerabilities are continuously created (Root Cause 6)
    |
    v
Result: A domain of constant, low-level conflict with a persistent risk of
catastrophic escalation, where defensive improvements are perpetually outpaced
by offensive innovation
```

## Interaction Effects

These root causes do not operate in isolation. They interact in ways that make the problem highly resistant to any single intervention:

- The **offense-dominant nature** of cyberspace is amplified by the **perverse economic incentives** in the technology sector that continuously create new vulnerabilities to exploit.
- The **attribution problem** enables states to exploit the **lack of international norms** by conducting aggressive operations while maintaining plausible deniability.
- The **entanglement of civilian and military infrastructure** means that **workforce gaps** in the private sector directly affect national security, because the government cannot secure infrastructure it does not own.
- The **institutional fragmentation** of U.S. cybersecurity responsibilities complicates the **public-private defense dilemma** because companies must navigate relationships with multiple government agencies, each with different authorities, cultures, and requirements.
- The **lack of international norms** interacts with the **offense-dominant nature** of the domain to create a spiraling competition in offensive capabilities, as states develop ever-more-sophisticated tools without agreed-upon limits.

The result is a self-reinforcing system where each vulnerability feeds the others, creating a security environment that deteriorates faster than any single policy intervention can address.
