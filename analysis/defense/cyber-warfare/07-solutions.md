# Cyber Warfare: Solutions

## Overview

Strengthening U.S. cyber deterrence and defense requires a multi-layered strategy that enhances resilience, clarifies red lines, deepens public-private partnership, builds workforce capacity, and leads an international effort to establish norms of behavior. No single solution can address the systemic challenges identified in this analysis; the solutions must be mutually reinforcing and address the root causes simultaneously. The proposals below draw on the recommendations of the Cyberspace Solarium Commission (2020), the 2023 National Cybersecurity Strategy, and the broader cybersecurity policy community.

---

## 1. Strengthen Deterrence

### A. Adopt a Declaratory Policy of Proportional Cross-Domain Response

- **Problem Addressed**: The threat of a "cyber-for-cyber" response is often not a credible deterrent because the U.S. has more to lose in cyberspace than many adversaries (Root Cause 1).
- **Description**: The U.S. should formally declare that a significant cyberattack on its critical infrastructure could be met with a response in any domain -- cyber, economic, diplomatic, or conventional military -- at a time and place of the U.S. government's choosing. This expands the deterrence calculus beyond the cyber domain.
- **Implementation**: The President should issue a formal declaratory policy statement, analogous to the nuclear deterrence posture. This would be reinforced by wargaming and demonstrating the capability to link cyber triggers to economic sanctions (Treasury/OFAC), diplomatic isolation (State Department), or conventional military options (DoD). The 2018 DoD Cyber Strategy hinted at cross-domain response, but a clear, public declaration has not been made.
- **Evidence Base**: The U.S. has used cross-domain responses ad hoc -- imposing sanctions on Russian entities after election interference, issuing indictments against Chinese military hackers after OPM -- but these responses have been inconsistent and delayed. A formal declaratory policy would systematize this approach.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Increases the credibility of U.S. deterrence by expanding the range of possible responses, forcing adversaries to calculate a much broader set of risks before conducting significant cyber operations.

### B. Invest in Cyber Resilience ("Deterrence by Denial")

- **Problem Addressed**: The best way to deter an attack is to make it likely to fail. Current critical infrastructure is insufficiently resilient to withstand state-level cyber operations (Root Causes 3 and 6).
- **Description**: Invest heavily in the resilience of U.S. critical infrastructure so that it can withstand and rapidly recover from a major cyberattack, denying adversaries the ability to cause lasting disruption.
- **Implementation**:
    - Use federal funds (e.g., from the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act's $1 billion State and Local Cybersecurity Grant Program) and regulatory minimum standards to require critical infrastructure sectors to build redundancy, segmentation, and out-of-band recovery systems.
    - Mandate that energy, water, and telecommunications operators maintain the ability to operate critical functions in a degraded or disconnected mode for a minimum of 72 hours.
    - Require critical infrastructure operators to conduct annual tabletop exercises simulating state-level cyberattacks on their systems, with results reported to their sector risk management agency (SRMA).
    - Invest in "analog fallback" capabilities for the most critical systems (e.g., manual overrides for water treatment, grid operations) so that systems can continue to function even when digital controls are compromised.
- **Evidence Base**: Ukraine's power grid resilience in 2022-2024, despite sustained Russian cyberattacks, demonstrates that investment in resilience can significantly reduce the impact of cyber operations. The sector-specific CIP standards for the bulk electric system (NERC CIP) have improved energy sector security, though they remain insufficient for state-level threats.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Reduces the incentive for an adversary to attack by denying them the ability to cause catastrophic, long-term disruption. Makes the U.S. a harder target.

### C. Demonstrate Consequences for Major Cyber Operations

- **Problem Addressed**: Adversaries have learned that the U.S. response to major cyber operations is often slow, inconsistent, and limited to proportional cyber responses or symbolic sanctions (Root Cause 4).
- **Description**: Establish a track record of imposing meaningful consequences for significant cyber operations, including sanctions, indictments, diplomatic actions, and, when appropriate, offensive cyber operations.
- **Implementation**: Develop pre-planned response packages for specific scenarios (e.g., ransomware attack on critical infrastructure, theft of classified information, disruption of elections) that can be executed rapidly upon attribution. These packages should include options across multiple domains (cyber, economic, diplomatic, legal).
- **Evidence Base**: The 2018 USCYBERCOM operation against the Internet Research Agency during the midterm elections, the FBI's seizure of DarkSide ransomware proceeds after Colonial Pipeline, and the coordinated international takedown of the LockBit ransomware group demonstrate that consequences can be imposed. However, these responses have been ad hoc rather than systematic.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Creates a pattern of consequences that adversaries must factor into their decision-making, strengthening deterrence over time.

## 2. Reform the Public-Private Partnership

### A. Create a Cyber National Guard

- **Problem Addressed**: The nation's top cyber talent resides in the private sector, and the government lacks the ability to rapidly scale cyber defense capacity during a major crisis (Root Cause 5).
- **Description**: Create a new, specialized reserve force -- a "Cyber National Guard" -- that allows private sector cybersecurity professionals to serve part-time and be activated during national cyber emergencies.
- **Implementation**:
    - Establish a new reserve component (potentially within the existing National Guard framework) where cybersecurity professionals can be commissioned directly based on skills rather than traditional military qualifications.
    - Members would train on government systems for a limited time each year (equivalent to the traditional one-weekend-per-month, two-weeks-per-year model).
    - Activation authority would rest with the Governor (for state-level incidents) or the President (for national-level crises), similar to existing National Guard activation authorities.
    - Provide incentives including student loan forgiveness, continuing education benefits, and legal protections for civilian employment (similar to USERRA protections for other reservists).
- **Evidence Base**: The concept was recommended by the Cyberspace Solarium Commission and has been introduced in Congress multiple times (the Civilian Cybersecurity Reserve Act). Estonia's Cyber Defence Unit, a volunteer force of civilian IT professionals integrated into the national defense structure, provides a successful international model. Israel's reserve system, which leverages Unit 8200 alumni, demonstrates how military-trained cyber professionals can maintain connections to the defense establishment while working in the private sector.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Provides a surge capacity of elite technical talent during a crisis. Deepens the relationship and trust between the government and the tech sector. Creates a pipeline for cybersecurity professionals who gain government experience.

### B. Legislate a Comprehensive Liability Framework

- **Problem Addressed**: Private companies underinvest in cybersecurity because they do not bear the full costs of security failures (Root Cause 6). Information sharing is impeded by liability concerns (Root Cause 3).
- **Description**: Create a comprehensive legal framework that shifts incentives toward security investment and removes barriers to information sharing.
- **Implementation**:
    - **Safe harbor for information sharing**: Expand the liability protections in the Cybersecurity Information Sharing Act of 2015 to provide a stronger "safe harbor" from lawsuits and regulatory penalties for companies that share cyber threat information with CISA or sector-specific agencies in good faith.
    - **Software liability**: Implement the National Cybersecurity Strategy's proposal to establish a legal standard of care for software security, holding vendors liable for shipping products with known vulnerabilities or failing to follow recognized secure development practices (such as NIST's Secure Software Development Framework).
    - **Critical infrastructure minimum standards**: Give CISA regulatory authority to establish and enforce minimum cybersecurity standards for the most critical infrastructure sectors, with penalties for non-compliance.
- **Evidence Base**: The European Union's NIS2 Directive (2022) establishes mandatory cybersecurity requirements for essential and important entities across the EU, providing a model for mandatory standards. The SEC's 2023 cyber incident disclosure rule for public companies demonstrates that regulatory mandates can change corporate behavior.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Removes a major barrier to information sharing, giving the government better visibility into adversary campaigns. Shifts the economic incentives in the technology industry toward security investment.

### C. Establish a Sector-Based Cyber Defense Framework

- **Problem Addressed**: Cybersecurity regulation is fragmented across dozens of agencies with different standards and approaches (Root Cause 5).
- **Description**: Rationalize and harmonize cybersecurity regulation under a coherent national framework, with CISA as the central coordinator and sector risk management agencies implementing sector-specific requirements.
- **Implementation**:
    - Designate CISA as the lead authority for developing cross-sector cybersecurity performance standards, with SRMAs adapting these standards to sector-specific contexts.
    - Establish a "one-stop shop" for incident reporting, replacing the current patchwork of reporting requirements (CIRCIA, SEC, HHS, state breach notification laws) with a single federal reporting mechanism administered by CISA.
    - Create a National Cyber Incident Response Plan (updated from the current 2016 version) with clear roles, responsibilities, and coordination procedures for federal agencies, state and local governments, and the private sector.
- **Evidence Base**: The Cyberspace Solarium Commission recommended regulatory harmonization as a top priority. The current fragmented approach imposes compliance costs of hundreds of millions of dollars annually on companies that operate across multiple sectors, diverting resources from actual security.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Reduces compliance burden while raising the baseline of security. Creates clarity for the private sector about who to call and what is expected.

## 3. Clarify Roles and Responsibilities

### A. Address the Dual-Hat Structure

- **Problem Addressed**: The dual-hat arrangement creates a potential conflict of interest between military operations and intelligence collection, concentrates power, and complicates oversight (Root Cause 5).
- **Description**: Either formally separate the leadership of USCYBERCOM and the NSA or establish conditions under which the dual hat would be maintained with enhanced oversight.
- **Implementation**:
    - **Option A (Separation)**: Formally separate the leadership with two separate four-star generals, each with distinct mission and chain of command. This would require building USCYBERCOM's independent capability for intelligence and operational planning.
    - **Option B (Conditional Maintenance)**: Maintain the dual hat with conditions: (1) enhanced congressional oversight through joint Armed Services/Intelligence Committee briefings; (2) a formal conflict resolution process when military and intelligence equities conflict; (3) an independent inspector general with jurisdiction over both organizations; (4) periodic review with a sunset provision requiring congressional reauthorization.
- **Evidence Base**: The question has been debated since USCYBERCOM's creation. The FY2017 NDAA included a provision to separate the dual hat, but the conditions for separation (USCYBERCOM achieving specified capabilities) have not been certified as met. Most military and intelligence leaders have opposed separation, arguing that the current threat environment requires the synergy the dual hat provides.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Either option would strengthen civilian oversight and create clearer lines of authority and accountability.

### B. Empower CISA as the Lead Domestic Agency

- **Problem Addressed**: The current ambiguity about roles creates confusion for the private sector and turf battles within the government (Root Cause 5).
- **Description**: Clarify that CISA is the lead federal agency for domestic cybersecurity and the primary interface for the private sector.
- **Implementation**:
    - Increase CISA's budget from ~$3 billion to ~$5 billion over 5 years, with targeted investment in incident response capacity, threat intelligence sharing, and technical assistance to state, local, tribal, and territorial (SLTT) governments.
    - Formally designate CISA as the lead agency for responding to any domestic cyber incident, with the DoD/USCYBERCOM acting in a supporting role unless directed by the President in a national-level crisis.
    - Establish CISA regional offices with dedicated incident response teams, modeled on FEMA's regional structure, to provide hands-on assistance to critical infrastructure operators and SLTT governments.
- **Evidence Base**: The Cyberspace Solarium Commission recommended significantly increasing CISA's budget and authorities. CISA's current budget (~$3 billion) is dwarfed by the scale of the mission -- protecting the cybersecurity of the entire federal civilian government and coordinating critical infrastructure defense across 16 sectors. By comparison, the NSA's budget is estimated at $12+ billion.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Strengthens civilian control over domestic cybersecurity. Provides a clearer, more trusted "front door" for private companies to work with the government.

## 4. Build the Cyber Workforce

### A. Launch a National Cyber Workforce Initiative

- **Problem Addressed**: The persistent shortage of qualified cybersecurity professionals undermines both government and private sector defense (Root Cause 5).
- **Description**: A comprehensive, multi-pronged approach to closing the cybersecurity workforce gap.
- **Implementation**:
    - **Education pipeline**: Fund cybersecurity education from K-12 through graduate school, including CyberPatriot competitions, cybersecurity-focused community college programs, and expanded CyberCorps Scholarship for Service slots.
    - **Skills-based hiring**: Implement the White House's guidance to adopt skills-based hiring for cybersecurity positions across the federal government, removing unnecessary degree requirements that exclude talented individuals with non-traditional backgrounds.
    - **Military-to-civilian pipeline**: Create a seamless transition program for military cyber operators leaving active duty, with guaranteed civilian cybersecurity positions in federal agencies or critical infrastructure organizations.
    - **Diversity and inclusion**: Invest in programs that recruit cybersecurity talent from underrepresented communities, including HBCUs, Hispanic-Serving Institutions, and community colleges.
    - **Competitive compensation**: Expand the use of the Cyber Excepted Service (CES) and other special hiring authorities to offer salaries competitive with the private sector for the most critical government cyber positions.
- **Evidence Base**: The 2023 National Cyber Workforce and Education Strategy identified a gap of over 500,000 cybersecurity positions in the United States. Israel's mandatory military service, which channels talented young people through Unit 8200 and other cyber units before they enter the private sector, has created one of the world's strongest cybersecurity ecosystems. While the U.S. cannot replicate mandatory service, it can create incentive structures that achieve similar talent development outcomes.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Over a 10-year horizon, could significantly reduce the workforce gap, improve the quality of cyber defense across government and the private sector, and create a pipeline of cyber talent with both government and private sector experience.

## 5. Promote International Norms

### A. Lead a Coalition to Define and Enforce "Red Lines"

- **Problem Addressed**: The absence of clear, enforceable international norms creates a permissive environment for escalation (Root Cause 4).
- **Description**: While a formal treaty is unlikely, the U.S. can lead a coalition of like-minded nations to establish clear norms of behavior in cyberspace with mechanisms for accountability.
- **Implementation**:
    - Launch a major diplomatic initiative to get a critical mass of nations to publicly pledge to a set of norms, building on the 2015 UN GGE report. Priority norms:
        - No attacks on civilian critical infrastructure in peacetime (power, water, hospitals, financial systems).
        - No attacks on nuclear command and control systems.
        - No interference with election infrastructure.
        - No harboring of criminal cyber actors who target other nations' critical infrastructure.
    - Establish a joint attribution and response mechanism among allied nations, so that violations are met with coordinated consequences (sanctions, diplomatic isolation, joint cyber operations).
    - Create an international Cyber Incident Response Fund to assist developing nations that are victims of state-sponsored cyberattacks, building support for the norms coalition.
- **Evidence Base**: The Paris Call for Trust and Security in Cyberspace (2018) attracted over 1,200 signatories (nations, companies, civil society organizations) but lacks enforcement mechanisms. The 2021 UN GGE report established 11 voluntary norms. The U.S.-China bilateral agreement on cyber-enabled IP theft (2015) temporarily reduced Chinese commercial espionage, demonstrating that diplomatic pressure can change behavior. NATO's 2016 recognition of cyberspace as an operational domain and its 2021 agreement that a cyberattack could trigger Article 5 provide a foundation for collective defense.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Creates a clear standard for judging state behavior. Allows for collective international condemnation and sanctions against nations that violate the norms, isolating them diplomatically and economically. Even if adversaries do not comply, establishing norms provides the legal and political basis for escalating responses.

### B. Build Partner-Nation Cyber Capacity

- **Description**: Invest in building the cybersecurity capabilities of allied and partner nations, so they can better defend their own networks and contribute to collective security.
- **Implementation**:
    - Expand USCYBERCOM's hunt-forward mission, deploying teams to partner nations to identify and disrupt threats (over 50 such missions conducted as of 2024).
    - Fund cybersecurity capacity building through USAID, the State Department's Bureau of Cyberspace and Digital Policy, and military-to-military partnerships.
    - Share declassified threat intelligence with partner nations to help them understand and defend against adversary campaigns.
- **Evidence Base**: USCYBERCOM's hunt-forward operations in Ukraine before the 2022 invasion are credited with significantly improving Ukraine's cyber resilience. Similar operations in Montenegro, North Macedonia, and other partner nations have helped identify and remove adversary malware.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Strengthens the collective defense of the allied cyber ecosystem, reduces the number of platforms available for adversary operations, and builds political support for the international norms coalition.

## 6. Strengthen Supply Chain Security

### A. Mandate Software Bill of Materials (SBOM)

- **Problem Addressed**: Software supply chain attacks (SolarWinds, Log4Shell, MOVEit) have demonstrated that vulnerabilities in widely used software components can cascade to thousands of downstream users (Root Cause 6).
- **Description**: Require all software sold to the federal government -- and eventually all critical infrastructure operators -- to include a Software Bill of Materials (SBOM) that lists all components, libraries, and dependencies.
- **Implementation**:
    - Build on Executive Order 14028 (2021), which required SBOMs for software sold to the federal government.
    - Extend the SBOM requirement to all software used in critical infrastructure sectors, with implementation timelines phased by sector criticality.
    - Develop automated tools for SBOM analysis that can quickly identify when a newly discovered vulnerability affects a component used in critical systems.
    - Create a national SBOM repository maintained by CISA that enables rapid identification of affected systems when new vulnerabilities are discovered.
- **Evidence Base**: The Log4Shell vulnerability (December 2021) demonstrated the problem: a critical vulnerability in the Log4j Java logging library, which was embedded in hundreds of thousands of software products, required weeks of effort to identify all affected systems because there was no comprehensive inventory of which products used Log4j. An SBOM regime would have enabled rapid identification of affected systems within hours rather than weeks.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Dramatically reduces the time needed to identify and remediate supply chain vulnerabilities. Creates transparency in the software supply chain that enables better risk management by both government and private sector buyers.

### B. Establish Trusted Supplier Programs

- **Description**: Create a certification program for critical software and hardware suppliers that verifies their adherence to secure development practices, supply chain integrity, and vulnerability disclosure policies.
- **Implementation**:
    - Develop certification criteria based on the NIST Secure Software Development Framework (SSDF) and the Cybersecurity Maturity Model Certification (CMMC) framework.
    - Require certification for suppliers to the federal government and incentivize certification for suppliers to critical infrastructure operators through procurement preferences and liability safe harbors.
    - Conduct periodic audits and vulnerability assessments of certified suppliers.
- **Evidence Base**: The DoD's CMMC program, while criticized for implementation complexity, demonstrates the concept of supply chain cybersecurity certification. The defense industrial base's experience with CMMC implementation provides lessons for a broader program.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Raises the security baseline of the software and hardware supply chain. Creates market incentives for vendors to invest in security by making certification a competitive advantage.

## 7. Address Operational Technology and Legacy System Risk

### A. Critical Infrastructure OT Modernization Program

- **Problem Addressed**: Operational technology (OT) systems controlling critical infrastructure (power grids, water treatment, manufacturing) run legacy software that was never designed to be connected to the internet and cannot be easily secured (Root Cause 6).
- **Description**: Create a federal program to help critical infrastructure operators modernize their OT systems, with a focus on the most vulnerable and most critical sectors.
- **Implementation**:
    - Establish a $5 billion Critical Infrastructure Modernization Fund (over 10 years) to provide grants and low-interest loans to critical infrastructure operators for OT security upgrades.
    - Prioritize funding for small and medium operators (particularly water utilities and rural electric cooperatives) that lack the resources for self-funded modernization.
    - Require funded projects to implement network segmentation between IT and OT environments, deploy OT-specific monitoring tools, and establish tested recovery plans.
    - Create an OT Security Center of Excellence within CISA to develop and disseminate best practices for securing operational technology in critical infrastructure.
- **Evidence Base**: The Volt Typhoon campaign targeted OT systems at water treatment facilities and power utilities that were running legacy systems with minimal security. Iranian hackers compromised PLCs at water utilities using default passwords. These incidents demonstrate that OT security cannot be left to market forces alone, particularly for smaller operators with limited resources.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Reduces the most critical vulnerabilities in the nation's physical infrastructure. Addresses the "weakest link" problem by providing resources to the operators least able to defend themselves.

## Solution Interaction Map

The proposed solutions are designed to be mutually reinforcing:

- **Deterrence** (Solutions 1A, 1B, 1C) depends on **resilience** (Solution 1B) and **workforce** (Solution 4A) to be credible. An adversary will not be deterred by threats of retaliation if the U.S. cannot withstand the initial attack or lacks the personnel to respond.
- **Public-private partnership** (Solutions 2A, 2B, 2C) depends on **regulatory harmonization** (Solution 2C) to reduce friction and **workforce** (Solution 4A) to provide the government with credible technical partners for the private sector.
- **International norms** (Solution 5A) depends on **demonstrating consequences** (Solution 1C) to show that violations have costs, and on **partner capacity building** (Solution 5B) to expand the coalition of nations capable of contributing to collective defense.
- **Supply chain security** (Solution 6A, 6B) and **OT modernization** (Solution 7A) address the root vulnerabilities that make deterrence necessary in the first place, reducing the overall attack surface.
