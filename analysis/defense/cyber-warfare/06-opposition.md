# Cyber Warfare: Opposition

## Overview

The U.S. government's assertive cyber strategy, particularly its "defend forward" doctrine, the development of offensive capabilities, and proposals for mandatory cybersecurity regulation, faces opposition from multiple directions. Unlike many defense policy areas where opposition is primarily ideological, resistance to cyber reforms comes from a diverse coalition: privacy advocates who fear expanded surveillance, fiscal conservatives who question the cost of new mandates, the technology industry which resists liability shifts and regulation, foreign policy realists who worry about escalation, and parts of the national security establishment itself that resist structural reorganization. Understanding these opposition dynamics is essential because they shape the boundaries of what is politically achievable.

---

## The Escalation and Stability Critique

This argument focuses on the risk that proactive U.S. cyber operations could lead to unintended conflict with nuclear-armed adversaries. It is advanced primarily by arms control advocates, some international law scholars, and foreign policy realists.

### "Defend Forward is Provocative"

**Proponents' Claim**: Actively operating on foreign networks, even with partner consent, can be interpreted by adversaries like Russia and China as an act of aggression or espionage. This could provoke a response and lead to an escalatory spiral that could spill over into conventional or even nuclear conflict. The 2019 *New York Times* report that USCYBERCOM had placed implants in Russia's power grid was cited by Russian officials as a dangerous provocation. Russian strategic doctrine treats attacks on critical infrastructure as a potential trigger for nuclear escalation, making U.S. pre-positioning in Russian networks particularly risky.

**Rebuttal**: Cyberspace is a domain of constant contact where adversaries are already operating aggressively against U.S. networks. Passively waiting for attacks to hit U.S. shores is a losing strategy. Persistent engagement is necessary to disrupt adversary campaigns and impose costs, which raises the threshold for their aggression and enhances deterrence. The experience of defending Ukraine's networks before and during the 2022 invasion validates this approach -- USCYBERCOM hunt-forward teams deployed to Ukraine helped identify and remove Russian malware before it could be activated, contributing to Ukraine's surprising cyber resilience.

### "Offensive Tools Create Instability"

**Proponents' Claim**: Developing and using offensive cyber weapons signals that such attacks are a legitimate tool of statecraft, encouraging proliferation. Stuxnet, while achieving its immediate objective against Iran's nuclear program, accelerated Iran's development of its own offensive capabilities, leading to the Shamoon attack against Saudi Aramco within two years. The Shadow Brokers leak of NSA tools in 2016-2017 demonstrated that offensive capabilities can be stolen and repurposed by other actors, as seen when the EternalBlue exploit was used in both the WannaCry and NotPetya attacks, causing billions in global damage. Each new offensive tool the U.S. develops normalizes cyber warfare and contributes to a global arms race.

**Rebuttal**: Adversaries are already developing and using these tools regardless of U.S. policy. Russia conducted destructive cyberattacks against Ukraine's power grid years before the U.S. adopted "defend forward." China was conducting massive cyber espionage campaigns against the U.S. defense industrial base long before Stuxnet. The U.S. cannot unilaterally disarm. A credible offensive capability is essential to deter adversaries by holding their own systems at risk. The Vulnerabilities Equities Process (VEP), while imperfect, provides a framework for managing the risks of developing and retaining offensive capabilities.

### "The Attribution Problem Makes Retaliation Too Risky"

**Proponents' Claim**: Because attribution is difficult and sometimes uncertain, retaliating for a cyberattack carries a risk of miscalculation -- striking the wrong country or escalating based on incomplete evidence. Academic research by Brandon Valeriano and Benjamin Jensen at the Marine Corps University has shown that restraint rather than retaliation has been the dominant response to cyberattacks historically, suggesting that the international community has developed an implicit norm of restraint that the U.S. should preserve rather than undermine. In a crisis, the pressure to respond quickly could lead to attribution errors with catastrophic consequences.

**Rebuttal**: While attribution is hard, it has improved dramatically in recent years. The U.S. has publicly attributed major operations to specific military units and even individual operatives in Russia, China, Iran, and North Korea. The 2018 indictment of 12 GRU officers for the DNC hack, and the subsequent indictments of Sandworm operators, demonstrate that attribution can be achieved with high confidence for major state-sponsored operations. The threat of a response, even if not immediate, is a necessary component of deterrence. The historical pattern of restraint that Valeriano and Jensen document has not deterred further aggression -- in fact, the lack of consequences has arguably emboldened adversaries.

### "Pre-Positioning Risks Accidental Conflict"

**Proponents' Claim**: The discovery that both the U.S. and China have pre-positioned access on each other's critical infrastructure creates a hair-trigger dynamic. U.S. implants in Russia's power grid (per the 2019 *New York Times* report) and China's Volt Typhoon campaign on U.S. infrastructure create a situation where a miscalculation, a technical malfunction, or a rogue operator could trigger a crisis. This mirrors Cold War "launch on warning" instabilities, but without the communication channels, hotlines, and crisis management protocols that were developed for nuclear deterrence. There is no "cyber hotline" between the U.S. and China or Russia that could be used to de-escalate a cyber incident before it spirals.

**Rebuttal**: Pre-positioning is a necessary component of deterrence by threat of punishment. The alternative -- developing offensive capabilities but not pre-positioning access -- would mean that the U.S. could not respond quickly enough to impose consequences, undermining deterrence credibility. The key is to maintain clear communication channels and establish "red lines" that, if crossed, would trigger a response. The development of crisis communication channels for cyberspace is a worthy diplomatic goal, but it should complement rather than replace deterrent capability.

**Key Opposing Stakeholders**: Carnegie Endowment for International Peace (Cyber Policy Initiative), Quincy Institute for Responsible Statecraft, academic researchers at MIT, Stanford, and Columbia studying cyber escalation dynamics, and some former senior officials (including former NSA Director Admiral Michael Rogers, who has expressed caution about escalatory risks).

## The Civil Liberties and Privacy Critique

This critique focuses on the impact of military cyber operations and cybersecurity authorities on the privacy of U.S. citizens. It represents one of the most politically significant sources of opposition because it unites progressive civil libertarians with libertarian conservatives.

### "Defend Forward Enables Domestic Surveillance"

**Proponents' Claim**: The line between foreign and domestic networks is technically meaningless -- internet traffic crosses borders continuously. Military operations to "defend forward" will inevitably sweep up the communications of U.S. persons, creating a backdoor for domestic surveillance by the military and the NSA. The Section 702 program, which permits warrantless surveillance of foreign targets, has been documented to incidentally collect the communications of hundreds of thousands of Americans annually. FBI agents have repeatedly queried the Section 702 database for information on U.S. persons in ways that violated legal restrictions. The FISA Court documented over 278,000 improper queries by the FBI in 2021 alone. These violations demonstrate that existing safeguards are inadequate to prevent the misuse of foreign intelligence authorities for domestic surveillance.

**Rebuttal**: There are strict legal and policy firewalls between foreign intelligence collection (NSA's mission) and domestic law enforcement (FBI's mission). Any incidental collection of U.S. person data is handled according to minimization procedures approved by the FISA Court. The 2024 reauthorization of Section 702 included additional safeguards, including a requirement for FBI agents to receive court approval before accessing Section 702 data in criminal investigations (with exceptions for imminent threats and national security). These reforms demonstrate that the oversight system, while imperfect, is capable of self-correction.

### "The Dual Hat Concentrates Too Much Power"

**Proponents' Claim**: The arrangement where the same person leads both USCYBERCOM (a military command) and the NSA (an intelligence agency) erodes the distinction between military operations and intelligence gathering. It creates a powerful, secretive organization that controls both the collection of intelligence and the use of that intelligence for military operations, with minimal external oversight. The combined entity has a budget estimated at $25+ billion and employs tens of thousands of people, making it one of the most powerful organizations in the federal government. This concentration of power is reminiscent of the concerns that led to the original separation of the CIA from the military in 1947.

**Rebuttal**: The dual-hat arrangement creates essential synergy. The NSA's intelligence provides the insights needed for CYBERCOM to act, and CYBERCOM's operations provide new access for the NSA to collect intelligence. This integration is necessary to operate at the speed of relevance in cyberspace, where operations unfold in minutes, not months. Separating the dual hat could slow operations, duplicate infrastructure, and reduce effectiveness at a time when the threat from China and Russia is accelerating. Congress can enhance oversight of the dual-hat arrangement without separating it.

### "Cyber Incident Reporting is Surveillance in Disguise"

**Proponents' Claim**: Mandatory incident reporting requirements (CIRCIA) and government-industry information sharing programs create channels through which the government can monitor private sector networks and collect data on private companies' systems, customers, and operations. This information could be used for regulatory enforcement, economic espionage, or political purposes beyond cybersecurity. The government's track record of expanding surveillance authorities beyond their original purpose (from PATRIOT Act National Security Letters to Section 702 query abuse) provides grounds for concern about mission creep.

**Rebuttal**: Incident reporting is essential for the government to understand the threat landscape and provide timely warnings to other potential victims. CIRCIA includes specific protections limiting how reported data can be used, including exemptions from FOIA disclosure, restrictions on use in regulatory enforcement, and protections against the reported information being used in lawsuits against the reporting entity. The alternative -- government ignorance about the threats facing critical infrastructure -- is far more dangerous than the risks of data misuse that can be mitigated through legal safeguards.

**Key Opposing Stakeholders**: American Civil Liberties Union (ACLU), Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF), Center for Democracy & Technology (CDT), Brennan Center for Justice, and a bipartisan coalition in Congress including Senators Ron Wyden (D-OR), Rand Paul (R-KY), and Mike Lee (R-UT), as well as Representatives Zoe Lofgren (D-CA) and Warren Davidson (R-OH).

## The Technology Industry and Regulatory Critique

This opposition comes primarily from the technology industry and its allies, who resist mandatory cybersecurity standards, liability shifts, and increased government oversight of private sector security practices.

### "Mandatory Standards Stifle Innovation"

**Proponents' Claim**: Government-mandated cybersecurity standards are inevitably rigid, slow to update, and based on compliance checkboxes rather than actual security outcomes. They create a floor that becomes a ceiling, discouraging companies from investing beyond the minimum requirements. The rapid pace of technological change means that standards are obsolete almost as soon as they are published. Companies will focus on checking compliance boxes rather than genuinely improving their security posture. The cybersecurity industry's own standards bodies (NIST, ISO, SANS) produce better, more adaptable guidance than government regulators could.

**Rebuttal**: Voluntary approaches have been tried for over two decades and have failed to produce adequate security. The 2003 National Strategy to Secure Cyberspace relied on voluntary cooperation, and two decades later, critical infrastructure remains deeply vulnerable. The 2023 National Cybersecurity Strategy's finding that market forces alone have not produced sufficient cybersecurity investment is well-documented by repeated breaches of critical infrastructure. Mandatory standards do not preclude additional voluntary measures, and they prevent the "race to the bottom" where companies that underinvest in security gain a competitive advantage over responsible firms.

### "Liability Shifts Will Kill Innovation"

**Proponents' Claim**: The National Cybersecurity Strategy's proposal to shift legal liability for insecure software from consumers to vendors will make software development prohibitively expensive, discourage open-source development, drive innovation offshore to jurisdictions without liability requirements, and expose small software companies to crippling lawsuits. The software industry has long operated on the principle that no software can be made perfectly secure, and holding vendors liable for every vulnerability is unrealistic. The end-user license agreements (EULAs) that disclaim liability for software defects exist for a reason -- software is too complex for any vendor to guarantee its security.

**Rebuttal**: Software is the only major product category where the manufacturer bears essentially no liability for defects that cause harm. Automobiles, medical devices, pharmaceuticals, and building materials are all subject to product liability regimes that have driven dramatic improvements in safety over decades. The goal is not to require perfect security but to establish a reasonable standard of care -- incentivizing vendors to follow secure development practices, conduct adequate testing, and promptly patch known vulnerabilities rather than shipping insecure products and externalizing the costs to users and the broader economy. A well-designed liability framework with safe harbors for vendors who follow recognized practices (e.g., NIST's Secure Software Development Framework) would drive improved security without crushing innovation.

### "Government Access Compromises Security"

**Proponents' Claim**: Government requests for encryption backdoors, expanded access to private networks, and mandatory incident reporting create attack surfaces that adversaries can exploit. Any backdoor built for the government can potentially be accessed by foreign hackers. The Salt Typhoon breach of U.S. telecom providers provided a devastating real-world example: Chinese hackers gained access to the very wiretap systems that the government required telecom companies to maintain under the Communications Assistance for Law Enforcement Act (CALEA). The lesson of Salt Typhoon is that government-mandated access points become high-value targets for adversaries, and the security costs of maintaining them may outweigh the intelligence benefits.

**Rebuttal**: The current administration is not proposing encryption backdoors -- indeed, in the wake of Salt Typhoon, senior officials (including CISA Director Jen Easterly) recommended that Americans use end-to-end encrypted communications. Incident reporting involves sharing information about attacks, not providing ongoing access to networks. The Salt Typhoon example is a legitimate concern that argues for re-evaluating the design of lawful access systems, but it does not argue against cybersecurity regulation or information sharing more broadly.

### "Regulation Fragments the Market"

**Proponents' Claim**: The proliferation of cybersecurity regulations from multiple agencies -- CISA, SEC, HHS, FERC, TSA, FTC, state attorneys general, and foreign regulators (EU NIS2, GDPR) -- creates a compliance burden that consumes resources better spent on actual security. A company operating across multiple sectors may face dozens of overlapping and sometimes contradictory cybersecurity requirements, each with different definitions, reporting timelines, and enforcement mechanisms. A 2023 industry survey estimated that major corporations spend $3-5 million annually on cybersecurity compliance activities that do not directly improve their security posture.

**Rebuttal**: Regulatory fragmentation is a real and legitimate problem, and the solution should be harmonization under a coherent national framework (as recommended by the Cyberspace Solarium Commission), not the elimination of regulation. The Office of the National Cyber Director (ONCD) has launched a cybersecurity regulatory harmonization initiative to address this concern. The goal should be a single, comprehensive set of requirements that are mapped to the NIST Cybersecurity Framework, with sector-specific additions only where genuinely necessary.

**Key Opposing Stakeholders**: TechNet, Information Technology Industry Council (ITI), BSA - The Software Alliance, CompTIA, the U.S. Chamber of Commerce, and major cloud providers (particularly on liability issues). Libertarian-oriented think tanks (Cato Institute, Mercatus Center, American Enterprise Institute) provide intellectual support for anti-regulation arguments.

## The Fiscal and Institutional Critique

### "We Can't Afford It"

**Proponents' Claim**: Building a fully staffed Cyber Mission Force, funding CISA at the recommended levels ($5+ billion annually), investing in critical infrastructure hardening across 16 sectors, and creating a Cyber National Guard would cost tens of billions of dollars annually in a period of fiscal constraint and trillion-dollar deficits. Unlike kinetic military capabilities (aircraft carriers, fighter jets), cybersecurity investments produce no visible, tangible outputs that justify the expenditure to voters and Congress. The effectiveness of cybersecurity spending is inherently difficult to measure -- success is measured by attacks that do not happen.

**Rebuttal**: The cost of inaction far exceeds the cost of investment. A single major cyberattack on the power grid or financial system could cause hundreds of billions of dollars in economic damage and potentially cost lives. The Colonial Pipeline attack alone caused billions in economic disruption from a single criminal ransomware group. The 2023 National Cybersecurity Strategy estimated that inadequate cybersecurity costs the U.S. economy over $200 billion annually in theft, fraud, and disruption. The proposed investments represent a small fraction of the DoD's $880+ billion annual budget.

### "Reorganization Creates Chaos"

**Proponents' Claim**: Proposals to separate the dual hat, elevate CISA, create new agencies, or reorganize the relationship between DoD and DHS will create bureaucratic upheaval, destroy existing relationships that have been painstakingly built over years, and create gaps in defense that sophisticated adversaries will immediately exploit. Any major reorganization takes 3-5 years to stabilize, during which the organization's effectiveness degrades. The current structure, while imperfect, at least has the benefit of established relationships and operational familiarity.

**Rebuttal**: Organizational dysfunction imposes its own costs. The fragmented structure creates coordination failures, turf battles, and gaps that adversaries already exploit. The Cyberspace Solarium Commission -- which included senior military, intelligence, and civilian officials -- recommended specific organizational reforms precisely because the current structure is inadequate for the threat. Any reorganization involves transition risks, but the risks of maintaining a dysfunctional status quo against an accelerating threat are greater.

### "Workforce Competition is a Losing Battle"

**Proponents' Claim**: Government can never match private sector cyber salaries -- a senior penetration tester earning $120,000 in the military can command $300,000-$500,000 at a major tech company. Investing in government cyber workforce programs is throwing money at a problem that the government cannot solve because it will always lose the compensation competition. The most talented cyber professionals will always flow to the highest bidder.

**Rebuttal**: Not all cyber professionals are motivated solely by salary. Mission, patriotism, the opportunity to work on unique problems, training and educational benefits, work-life balance, job security, and retirement benefits make government service attractive to many talented individuals. The Cyber Excepted Service (CES), scholarship-for-service programs, and military service commitments demonstrate that government can attract talent with the right combination of incentives. Israel's Unit 8200 -- which recruits top talent into mandatory military service and then benefits from a lifelong alumni network that maintains ties to the defense establishment -- provides an international model for how government service can anchor a broader cybersecurity ecosystem. Estonia's volunteer Cyber Defence Unit demonstrates that patriotic motivation can attract skilled volunteers who serve without any expectation of matching private sector pay.

**Key Opposing Stakeholders**: Fiscal hawks in both parties, some DoD officials who resist reorganization that would reduce their authority, USCYBERCOM and NSA leaders who oppose splitting the dual hat, and institutional defenders who prefer incremental improvement over structural reform.

## The Militarization of Cyberspace Critique

### "The Internet Should Not Be a Battlefield"

**Proponents' Claim**: A close partnership between the DoD and major tech companies for "defend forward" operations effectively militarizes the internet. It turns private companies into instruments of state power and makes their global platforms a battlefield. This threatens the internet's role as a neutral, global space for communication, commerce, and innovation. Countries like India, Brazil, and Indonesia -- which are important partners but also wary of U.S. tech dominance -- may respond to the militarization of cyberspace by pursuing digital sovereignty measures that fragment the global internet. The concept of the internet as a "global commons" is fundamentally incompatible with treating it as a military domain.

**Rebuttal**: The internet is already a battlefield -- adversaries have been conducting offensive operations through it for decades. Russia used it to interfere in U.S. elections, China used it to steal trillions in intellectual property, and both have pre-positioned destructive capabilities on American infrastructure. The question is not whether cyberspace is contested, but whether the U.S. will defend itself effectively within it. The government cannot defend the nation without the cooperation of the companies that own the infrastructure, and framing that cooperation as "militarization" plays into adversary narratives designed to weaken Western cyber defenses. The distinction between defending the internet and militarizing it is important but should not be used to prevent necessary defensive cooperation.

### "The VEP is Biased Toward Offense"

**Proponents' Claim**: The government's Vulnerabilities Equities Process for deciding whether to disclose a software vulnerability or keep it for offensive use is structurally biased toward retention because the agencies with the strongest voices in the process (NSA, CIA, USCYBERCOM) have operational incentives to retain vulnerabilities. This leaves U.S. companies and citizens vulnerable to attack from the very flaws the government is hoarding. The 2017 Shadow Brokers leak of NSA tools proved that retained vulnerabilities can escape government control and be weaponized against the public. The EternalBlue exploit, which the NSA had reportedly used for years before it was stolen, was subsequently used in the WannaCry and NotPetya attacks that caused billions in damage.

**Rebuttal**: The VEP has been reformed and made more transparent since the 2017 White House charter. The process involves representatives from multiple agencies with defensive equities, including CISA, the Department of Commerce, and the Department of State, alongside the offensive agencies. The government has stated that the "vast majority" of vulnerabilities discovered through the VEP process are disclosed to vendors for patching. However, critics are right that the tension between offense and defense is inherent and cannot be fully resolved as long as the government maintains offensive capabilities. Greater transparency about the VEP's outcomes (aggregate statistics on disclosure vs. retention decisions) could help address concerns without compromising operational security.

**Key Opposing Stakeholders**: Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF), Access Now, the Internet Society, the Web Foundation, some tech companies (particularly those with global user bases who resist being seen as instruments of U.S. state power), the European Union (which promotes a vision of technology regulation distinct from U.S. approaches), and advocates for a global, open internet.

## Bipartisan Nature of Opposition

Unlike many defense policy areas where opposition breaks along partisan lines, opposition to various aspects of U.S. cyber policy is genuinely bipartisan but fragmented:

- **Privacy opposition**: Unites progressive civil libertarians (ACLU, Brennan Center) with libertarian-conservative privacy advocates (Rand Paul, the Cato Institute) against expanded surveillance authorities. This coalition has repeatedly demonstrated its ability to block or constrain surveillance legislation, including forcing significant amendments to the 2024 Section 702 reauthorization.

- **Anti-regulation opposition**: Unites the technology industry with free-market conservatives against mandatory cybersecurity standards and liability shifts. This coalition has significant financial resources ($140+ million in annual tech sector lobbying) and strong relationships with both parties.

- **Anti-escalation opposition**: Unites foreign policy restraint advocates (Quincy Institute) with some progressive Democrats against aggressive "defend forward" operations. This coalition is the weakest because national security hawks in both parties generally support assertive cyber operations.

- **Fiscal opposition**: Unites deficit hawks in both parties against the cost of expanding cyber forces and agencies. In an era of trillion-dollar deficits, any new spending program faces fiscal headwinds regardless of its merits.

This fragmented, cross-cutting opposition means that no single coalition blocks all cyber reforms, but different coalitions can block different elements of a comprehensive cyber strategy. The challenge for policymakers is to build majorities for each component by addressing the legitimate concerns of each opposition group -- for example, pairing mandatory standards with regulatory harmonization (to address industry concerns), coupling offensive authorities with enhanced oversight (to address privacy concerns), and demonstrating cost-effectiveness (to address fiscal concerns).
