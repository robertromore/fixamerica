# Cyber Warfare: Overview

## Overview

Cyberspace is a critical domain of national security, underpinning the U.S. economy, critical infrastructure, and military operations. It is also a domain of constant conflict. The United States faces persistent cyberattacks from state and non-state actors who seek to steal data, disrupt critical services, and undermine democratic processes. The scale of this threat is staggering: the FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3) received over 880,000 complaints in 2023 alone, with estimated losses exceeding $12.5 billion, and those figures capture only reported criminal activity, not the far more consequential state-sponsored campaigns that target government networks, defense industrial base systems, and critical infrastructure.

In response, the Department of Defense (DoD) has adopted a more assertive cyber strategy, moving from a reactive, defensive posture to a proactive doctrine of "persistent engagement" and "defending forward." This strategy, executed by U.S. Cyber Command (USCYBERCOM), involves actively hunting for adversary activity on allied and partner networks to disrupt threats before they reach U.S. shores. The 2023 DoD Cyber Strategy reaffirmed this approach and expanded it, emphasizing the lessons learned from supporting Ukraine's cyber defense against Russian operations and the growing threat from China's pre-positioning of malware on U.S. critical infrastructure networks.

This proactive stance is necessary in an offense-dominant domain, but it creates significant strategic and legal challenges. It blurs the line between intelligence gathering and military action, raises risks of unintended escalation, and creates complex dependencies on the private sector, which owns and operates the vast majority of the infrastructure at risk. The discovery of China's Volt Typhoon campaign in 2023 -- in which Chinese state-sponsored hackers pre-positioned access on water treatment facilities, power grids, and transportation systems across the United States -- demonstrated that the threat to the homeland is not theoretical but active and ongoing.

The central challenge for U.S. policy is to deter significant attacks in cyberspace without triggering a catastrophic conflict, while simultaneously building the resilience to withstand attacks that cannot be deterred.

## Scope

This analysis covers the key aspects of U.S. military cyber operations and the broader national cybersecurity landscape:

- **U.S. Cyber Command (USCYBERCOM)**: The mission, structure, budget, and evolution of the lead military command for cyberspace operations, including the Cyber Mission Force and its expansion under CYBERCOM 2.0.
- **Strategic Doctrine**: The shift to "persistent engagement" and "defend forward," the 2018 and 2023 DoD Cyber Strategies, and the 2023 National Cybersecurity Strategy.
- **Offensive and Defensive Operations**: The capabilities and legal frameworks for both defending DoD networks and conducting offensive cyber operations, including the authorities granted under National Security Presidential Memorandum 13 (NSPM-13).
- **Adversary Threats**: The capabilities, doctrines, and major campaigns of China, Russia, Iran, and North Korea, as well as the growing ransomware threat from criminal actors with state ties.
- **Critical Infrastructure Defense**: The role of the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA), sector risk management agencies, and the public-private partnership in protecting the 16 critical infrastructure sectors.
- **Cyber Workforce**: The persistent shortage of qualified cybersecurity professionals in both government and the private sector, and efforts to close the gap.
- **Deterrence and Escalation**: The challenge of applying traditional deterrence theory to the cyber domain, including cross-domain deterrence and deterrence by denial.
- **International Norms**: Diplomatic efforts to establish rules of responsible state behavior in cyberspace, including the UN Group of Governmental Experts (GGE) process and the Paris Call for Trust and Security in Cyberspace.

## Core Tensions and Tradeoffs

- **Defend Forward vs. Sovereignty**: The doctrine of "defending forward" on foreign networks, even with partner consent, can be seen by adversaries as an intrusion into their sovereign territory, increasing escalation risks. Russia and China have explicitly condemned U.S. "defend forward" operations as destabilizing.
- **Offense vs. Defense**: Every offensive cyber tool or vulnerability the U.S. develops and keeps secret is one that cannot be disclosed to the private sector to patch defenses. This is the "vulnerabilities equities process" (VEP) dilemma. The 2017 leak of NSA tools by the Shadow Brokers group -- which were subsequently used in the WannaCry and NotPetya attacks -- demonstrated the catastrophic consequences when offensive tools are compromised.
- **Security vs. Civil Liberties**: The tools used to monitor foreign adversary networks can also sweep up the communications of U.S. persons, creating a fundamental tension with privacy rights. The debate over Section 702 of the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act, reauthorized in 2024, exemplifies this tension.
- **Attribution vs. Response**: It can be difficult and time-consuming to definitively attribute a cyberattack. This "attribution problem" complicates efforts to respond in a timely and proportionate manner, though U.S. attribution capabilities have improved significantly in recent years.
- **Public-Private Partnership**: The government needs the private sector's cooperation to defend critical infrastructure, but private companies are often wary of government intrusion, liability concerns, and the cost of meeting mandated security standards. The private sector owns approximately 85% of U.S. critical infrastructure.
- **Speed vs. Oversight**: Cyber operations unfold at machine speed. The traditional national security decision-making process -- interagency deliberation, legal review, presidential authorization -- is often too slow for the cyber domain, creating pressure to delegate authority downward and reduce oversight.

## Key Questions

1. How can the U.S. effectively deter a major cyberattack when the threat of a proportional "cyber-for-cyber" response may not be credible against adversaries who have less to lose in cyberspace?
2. What is the proper division of labor between the government (DoD, CISA) and the private sector in defending critical infrastructure, and should the government mandate minimum cybersecurity standards for critical sectors?
3. Are the current legal and policy frameworks (e.g., AUMFs, presidential authorities, NSPM-13) adequate for governing offensive cyber operations, or does Congress need to create a new statutory framework for cyber warfare?
4. How can the U.S. and its allies establish international norms of behavior to prevent catastrophic escalation in cyberspace, given that major adversaries (China, Russia) have a fundamentally different view of internet governance and cyber sovereignty?
5. How should the U.S. address the persistent cyber workforce shortage, and can it compete with private sector compensation to retain top talent in government and military cyber roles?

## Vision of Success

A successful U.S. cyber warfare policy would:

- **Maintain a Credible Deterrent**: Adversaries are convinced that the costs of launching a significant cyberattack against U.S. critical infrastructure far outweigh any potential benefits, because the U.S. has both the capability and the demonstrated will to impose unacceptable costs in response -- through cyber, economic, diplomatic, or conventional means.
- **Achieve Cyber Resilience**: The U.S. can withstand and rapidly recover from a major cyberattack, ensuring the continuity of government and essential services. Critical infrastructure operators have implemented robust segmentation, redundancy, and out-of-band recovery capabilities.
- **Foster a Seamless Public-Private Defense**: The government and the private sector work in close partnership, sharing threat information and coordinating defensive actions in real-time through organizations like CISA's Joint Cyber Defense Collaborative (JCDC) and sector-specific Information Sharing and Analysis Centers (ISACs).
- **Build a World-Class Cyber Workforce**: The U.S. has closed the cybersecurity workforce gap through sustained investment in education, training, and retention programs that make government and military cyber careers competitive with the private sector.
- **Establish International Norms**: The U.S. leads a global coalition in establishing and enforcing clear "rules of the road" for state behavior in cyberspace, with meaningful consequences for violations.
