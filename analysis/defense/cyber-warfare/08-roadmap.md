# Cyber Warfare: Reform Roadmap

## Overview

Strengthening U.S. cyber defenses and deterrence requires a phased approach that prioritizes clarifying policy and roles in the short term, building new institutions and partnerships in the medium term, and achieving long-term resilience and international stability. The roadmap below sequences reforms to account for political feasibility, institutional capacity, and the urgency of the threat. Each phase builds on the previous one, creating a foundation for more ambitious reforms while delivering near-term improvements.

---

## Phase 1: Policy and Partnership Foundations (Years 1-2)

### Objective

Establish a clear and credible deterrence posture, remove barriers to public-private partnership, and implement reforms achievable through executive action or bipartisan consensus.

### Actions

| Priority | Action | Mechanism | Lead Actor | KPI |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Issue Declaratory Policy** | The President issues a formal declaratory policy on cross-domain deterrence for significant cyberattacks on U.S. critical infrastructure. | Presidential statement / NSC policy directive | White House / NSC | Policy is publicly released, briefed to allies, and integrated into wargaming. |
| **2. Pass Liability Shield Legislation** | Congress passes legislation providing strengthened "safe harbor" for companies that share cyber threat information with CISA, expanding on the 2015 CISA Act. | Legislation | Congress / CISA | Law is enacted. CISA reports a measurable increase (25%+) in voluntary information sharing from the private sector within 12 months. |
| **3. Implement CIRCIA Regulations** | CISA finalizes and implements the regulations for the Cyber Incident Reporting for Critical Infrastructure Act of 2022. | Rulemaking | CISA | Final rule published. Reporting system operational with 90%+ compliance from covered entities within 12 months. |
| **4. Expand Hunt-Forward Operations** | Increase the pace and scope of USCYBERCOM hunt-forward deployments to allied and partner nations, with particular focus on Indo-Pacific partners. | Military operations / bilateral agreements | USCYBERCOM / State Department | 30+ hunt-forward missions per year in 25+ countries. Measurable increase in partner-nation cyber resilience. |
| **5. Launch Norms Initiative** | The State Department, through the Bureau of Cyberspace and Digital Policy, launches a diplomatic initiative to build a coalition around "red lines" for state behavior in cyberspace. | Diplomatic engagement | State Department / CDP | First international conference held. Draft declaration of norms produced with endorsements from 20+ nations. |
| **6. Address Regulatory Fragmentation** | ONCD leads an interagency process to harmonize cybersecurity reporting requirements across federal agencies. | Executive order / interagency coordination | ONCD / OMB | Unified reporting framework published. Number of distinct federal cybersecurity reporting requirements reduced by 50%. |
| **7. Cyber Workforce Quick Wins** | Expand Cyber Excepted Service (CES) to additional DoD and civilian agencies. Remove degree requirements for entry-level federal cyber positions. Increase CyberCorps Scholarship for Service slots by 50%. | Executive order / agency policy | OPM / ONCD / NSF | 500+ additional federal cyber hires in Year 1. Time-to-hire for cyber positions reduced to 90 days. |

### Political Strategy

Phase 1 reforms are designed to be achievable with existing authority or minimal legislative changes. Transparency measures (CIRCIA implementation, reporting harmonization) are difficult for opponents to argue against publicly. The liability shield legislation addresses a long-standing industry concern, creating a transactional opportunity to build goodwill for more ambitious Phase 2 reforms. The declaratory policy costs nothing but signals resolve.

### Risk Mitigation

| Risk | Mitigation |
|---|---|
| Industry lobbies against CIRCIA implementation details | Engage industry in rulemaking process; ensure reporting burden is proportionate to entity size |
| Allies resist norms initiative as U.S.-dominated | Co-lead initiative with a European partner (e.g., France, Estonia) and ensure genuine multilateral input |
| Workforce reforms blocked by OPM bureaucracy | Use direct presidential direction and agency-specific authorities to bypass standard hiring processes |

## Phase 2: Building Resilience and New Institutions (Years 3-5)

### Objective

Move from policy to practice by building the new institutions, regulatory frameworks, and resilience capabilities needed for a more defensible nation.

### Actions

| Priority | Action | Mechanism | Lead Actor | KPI |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Establish Cyber National Guard** | Congress passes legislation authorizing and funding a new Cyber National Guard reserve component with direct commissioning authority for cybersecurity professionals. | Legislation | Congress / DoD / National Guard Bureau | First cohort of 500 private sector experts commissioned and trained. Activation exercised in at least one national-level exercise. |
| **2. Implement Critical Infrastructure Standards** | CISA, using expanded regulatory authority granted by Congress, issues binding minimum cybersecurity standards for the energy, water, and telecommunications sectors, building on existing frameworks (NERC CIP, TSA directives). | Legislation + rulemaking | Congress / CISA / SRMAs | Standards finalized and published for three sectors. 80%+ compliance within 24 months of publication. |
| **3. Empower CISA** | Congress increases CISA's budget to ~$5 billion and formally designates it as the lead agency for domestic cyber incident response. CISA establishes regional offices modeled on FEMA's structure. | Legislation / appropriations | Congress / CISA | Budget appropriated. 10 regional offices established. National Cyber Incident Response Plan updated and exercised. |
| **4. Software Liability Framework** | Congress passes legislation establishing a reasonable standard of care for software security, with a phased implementation timeline and safe harbor for vendors who follow recognized secure development practices (e.g., NIST SSDF). | Legislation | Congress / Commerce / NIST | Legislation enacted. First enforcement actions within 3 years. Measurable decrease in vulnerabilities in covered software products. |
| **5. Address the Dual Hat** | The Secretary of Defense conducts a formal review of the dual-hat arrangement with recommendations to Congress. If conditions for separation are met, implement with a 2-year transition period. If not, implement enhanced oversight measures. | DoD review + potential legislation | SecDef / Congress | Review completed and recommendations delivered to Congress within Year 3. Implementation plan within Year 4. |
| **6. Solidify International Norms** | The U.S. and at least 30 nations formally sign a joint declaration on norms of responsible state behavior in cyberspace, with a commitment to coordinated attribution and sanctions for violations. | Diplomatic agreement | State Department / NSC | Declaration signed and published. First coordinated attribution and sanctions action conducted in response to a norms violation. |
| **7. Cyber Workforce Scale-Up** | Implement the full National Cyber Workforce Initiative, including expanded education programs, military-to-civilian pipeline, and competitive compensation. | Multiple mechanisms | ONCD / DoD / OPM / NSF / Education | 25,000 additional cybersecurity professionals in federal service. Workforce diversity increased measurably (30%+ women, increased minority representation). |

### Political Strategy

Phase 2 requires building legislative coalitions. Key messaging frames:

- **For fiscal conservatives**: Mandatory standards reduce the multi-billion-dollar cost of cyberattacks and ransomware payments borne by the economy. The Cyber National Guard is cost-effective compared to expanding the active duty force.
- **For progressives**: Empowering CISA as a civilian agency strengthens democratic oversight. Software liability protects consumers. Workforce programs create pathways for diverse talent.
- **For industry**: The liability shield and reporting harmonization reduce compliance burden. The software liability framework includes safe harbors. The Cyber National Guard addresses the workforce gap that industry itself identifies as its top challenge.
- **For national security hawks**: Stronger deterrence, expanded hunt-forward operations, and critical infrastructure resilience directly address the China threat.

## Phase 3: Achieving a Resilient and Stable Cyber Domain (Years 6-10)

### Objective

See the long-term benefits of a more resilient infrastructure, a stronger workforce, and a more stable international environment. Achieve a state of "deterrence by denial" where the U.S. can withstand a major cyberattack without catastrophic consequences.

### Actions

| Priority | Action | Mechanism | Lead Actor | KPI |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Resilient Critical Infrastructure** | The energy, water, telecommunications, and financial sectors have broadly implemented binding cybersecurity standards, including segmented networks, redundant control systems, and tested recovery plans. | Regulatory enforcement / private sector investment | CISA / SRMAs / private sector | A major joint exercise (national cyber exercise) successfully demonstrates that critical infrastructure can withstand and recover from a simulated state-level cyberattack within 72 hours. |
| **2. Operational Cyber National Guard** | The Cyber National Guard is fully operational, with 2,000+ members across all 50 states, and has been successfully activated to assist in responding to at least one major incident. | Operations | DoD / National Guard Bureau | Guard declared at Full Operational Capability (FOC). Average response time for national-level activation is under 48 hours. |
| **3. Closed Workforce Gap** | The federal cybersecurity workforce gap has been reduced by 75%, with competitive compensation, diverse recruitment, and a robust pipeline from education to government service. | Multiple programs | ONCD / OPM / DoD | Federal cyber vacancy rate below 10%. Retention rates above 80% for critical cyber positions. |
| **4. Enforced International Norms** | The international coalition successfully imposes coordinated sanctions and other consequences on a state that violates the established "red lines" in cyberspace. | Diplomatic coordination | State Department / NSC / allies | First coordinated sanctions have measurable economic impact on the target nation. Norms coalition has expanded to 50+ nations. |
| **5. Mature Software Liability Regime** | The software liability framework is fully implemented, with measurable reductions in vulnerability density in covered software products and a functioning safe harbor for responsible vendors. | Legal enforcement / market incentives | Courts / FTC / Commerce | 30%+ reduction in critical vulnerability disclosures for covered products. Security investment by software vendors increased measurably. |
| **6. Integrated Public-Private Defense** | The JCDC and sector ISACs operate as a seamless, real-time information sharing and coordinated response network. Government and private sector responders regularly conduct joint exercises and share threat intelligence with minimal friction. | Institutional maturation | CISA / JCDC / ISACs / private sector | Average time from government threat advisory to private sector implementation reduced to under 24 hours for critical vulnerabilities. |

### Success Criteria for Phase 3

The ultimate measure of success is a shift in the strategic calculus of adversaries. A Phase 3 world would be one where:

- China's pre-positioning campaigns (like Volt Typhoon) are detected and remediated quickly enough to deny them strategic value.
- Ransomware attacks on critical infrastructure decline significantly because improved defenses make them less profitable and the government imposes meaningful consequences.
- The U.S. can weather a major cyberattack without catastrophic disruption to essential services or the economy.
- International norms are sufficiently established that violations carry real political and economic costs.

## Risk Assessment

| Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Technology industry mobilizes against software liability legislation | High | High | Phase implementation with safe harbors; engage industry in developing reasonable standard of care |
| Critical infrastructure operators resist mandatory standards as costly | High | Medium | Provide federal funding for small and medium operators; allow phased implementation; tie standards to risk levels |
| International norms initiative fails to attract broad coalition | Medium | Medium | Start with a core group of like-minded nations and expand; demonstrate consequences for violations |
| New administration reverses executive actions from Phase 1 | Medium | Medium | Prioritize codifying reforms in legislation; build institutional capacity that is resistant to political reversal |
| Major cyber crisis shifts priorities before reforms are implemented | Medium | High | Frame reforms as essential to crisis response; use any crisis as a catalyst for reform (as Colonial Pipeline accelerated TSA directives) |
| Adversaries escalate in response to "defend forward" operations | Low-Medium | Very High | Maintain clear communication channels; ensure escalation management is integrated into all offensive planning; conduct wargaming |
| Cyber National Guard faces recruiting challenges | Medium | Medium | Offer compelling incentives (student loan forgiveness, continuing education); partner with major tech companies that support reserve service |

## Sequencing Rationale

The three-phase approach reflects the political and institutional reality that comprehensive reform cannot be achieved in a single legislative action:

1. **Phase 1 (policy and quick wins)** creates the evidence base, removes barriers, and builds political support for more ambitious Phase 2 legislation.
2. **Phase 2 (legislation and institution-building)** creates the legal framework, regulatory authority, and institutional capacity needed for Phase 3 systemic changes.
3. **Phase 3 (systemic resilience)** implements the deep structural changes that require sustained institutional capacity and political commitment built in Phases 1 and 2.

Each phase is designed to deliver tangible benefits that justify continued investment and build political support for the next phase. The sequence also reflects the principle of "irreversibility" -- transparency measures, institutional capacity, and regulatory frameworks, once established, are difficult to dismantle without appearing to favor insecurity.
