# Cyber Warfare: Stakeholders

## Overview

Cybersecurity is a "whole-of-society" problem, involving a complex web of government agencies, private companies, international actors, and civil society organizations. Unlike most national security domains where stakeholders are primarily governmental, the cyber domain is distinguished by the central role of the private sector, which owns the majority of the infrastructure at risk and employs the majority of the technical talent capable of defending it. Understanding these stakeholders -- their motivations, their leverage, and their conflicts -- is essential to designing effective policy.

## U.S. Government Stakeholders

### Military and Intelligence

#### U.S. Cyber Command (USCYBERCOM)

- **Primary Interest**: Military Operations. Conducting offensive and defensive cyber operations to support military objectives and defend the nation.
- **Role**: The lead organization for military cyber effects and defending DoD networks. Operates through the Cyber Mission Force (133 teams, ~6,800 personnel) and service component commands (Army Cyber Command, Fleet Cyber Command/10th Fleet, 16th Air Force, Marine Forces Cyberspace Command).
- **Budget**: Approximately $14.5 billion (FY2025) for cyber activities across the DoD.
- **Key Leadership**: Commander is a four-star general, currently dual-hatted as Director of the NSA.

#### National Security Agency (NSA)

- **Primary Interest**: Intelligence Gathering. Collecting foreign signals intelligence (SIGINT) from cyberspace and providing cybersecurity guidance for national security systems.
- **Role**: Provides the intelligence that enables USCYBERCOM's operations. The "dual-hat" leadership structure creates a deep, symbiotic relationship. The NSA's Cybersecurity Directorate (established 2019 under Anne Neuberger, later Deputy National Security Advisor for Cyber) publishes threat advisories and security guidance for the defense industrial base and government.
- **Budget**: Estimated $12+ billion annually (classified).
- **Key Tension**: The NSA's mission is to maintain persistent access to foreign networks for intelligence collection. USCYBERCOM's mission sometimes requires using that access for military effects, which can "burn" intelligence sources.

#### Defense Information Systems Agency (DISA)

- **Primary Interest**: Network Operations. Operating and defending the DoD's global communications infrastructure, including the Defense Information Network (DoDIN).
- **Role**: Manages the classified and unclassified networks that connect DoD globally and provides cybersecurity services (endpoint security, cloud security) to the entire Department.

#### CIA Directorate of Digital Innovation

- **Primary Interest**: Clandestine Cyber Operations. Conducting covert cyber operations in support of intelligence collection and covert action.
- **Role**: Conducts Title 50 cyber operations that are distinct from USCYBERCOM's Title 10 military operations, with different oversight mechanisms and authorities.

### Civilian Government

#### Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA)

- **Primary Interest**: Homeland Defense. Defending federal civilian government networks (.gov) and coordinating the defense of U.S. critical infrastructure.
- **Role**: The lead civilian agency for domestic cybersecurity. Operates the National Cybersecurity and Communications Integration Center (NCCIC), manages the Known Exploited Vulnerabilities (KEV) catalog, runs the Joint Cyber Defense Collaborative (JCDC), and coordinates with sector-specific agencies.
- **Budget**: Approximately $3 billion (FY2025).
- **Workforce**: Approximately 3,100 employees (2024), widely considered insufficient for the scope of the mission.
- **Key Programs**: Shields Up initiative, Cyber Hygiene Services (free vulnerability scanning for critical infrastructure), Cybersecurity Performance Goals (CPGs).

#### Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI)

- **Primary Interest**: Law Enforcement. Investigating cybercrime, attributing malicious cyber activity, and disrupting criminal and state-sponsored cyber operations.
- **Role**: Pursues criminal charges against hackers and state-sponsored actors. Operates the Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3), conducts joint operations with international law enforcement (e.g., the takedown of the Hive and LockBit ransomware groups), and provides victim notification.
- **Workforce**: Approximately 1,000 agents in the Cyber Division, plus analysts and support staff.
- **Key Operations**: Colonial Pipeline ransom recovery ($2.3 million seized via blockchain tracing), Hive ransomware takedown (2023), LockBit disruption (2024).

#### Office of the National Cyber Director (ONCD)

- **Primary Interest**: Policy Coordination. Synchronizing cyber policy across the entire federal government.
- **Role**: Created by Congress in 2021. Advises the President on cybersecurity policy, coordinates the National Cybersecurity Strategy, and ensures a whole-of-government approach. The first National Cyber Director was Chris Inglis (2021-2023), followed by Harry Coker Jr.
- **Budget**: Approximately $22 million (small coordinating office).
- **Key Products**: National Cybersecurity Strategy (2023), National Cyber Workforce and Education Strategy (2023), implementation plans.

#### Department of State, Bureau of Cyberspace and Digital Policy (CDP)

- **Primary Interest**: Diplomacy. Leading U.S. efforts to establish international norms for responsible state behavior in cyberspace and building partner-nation capacity.
- **Role**: Established in 2022. Leads diplomatic engagement on cyber issues, coordinates sanctions and other international responses to malicious cyber activity, and participates in UN processes (GGE and Open-Ended Working Group).

#### Congress

- **Primary Interest**: Oversight and Funding. Authorizing and appropriating the budget for cyber forces and programs, and overseeing cyber operations.
- **Key Committees**: Senate and House Armed Services Committees (authorize the cyber budget through the NDAA); Intelligence Committees (oversee NSA and covert cyber operations); Homeland Security Committees (oversee CISA).
- **Key Initiative**: The Cyberspace Solarium Commission (2019-2021), a congressionally mandated bipartisan body that produced 82 recommendations for improving U.S. cybersecurity. Approximately 75% of the recommendations have been implemented or are in progress, including the creation of the ONCD, CIRCIA, and the strengthening of CISA.

#### Sector Risk Management Agencies (SRMAs)

- **Primary Interest**: Sector-Specific Regulation. Overseeing cybersecurity in their respective critical infrastructure sectors.
- **Key Agencies**: FERC/NERC (bulk electric system), TSA (transportation), SEC (public companies and financial markets), HHS (healthcare), EPA (water systems), Treasury/OCC (banking), NRC (nuclear).
- **Key Tension**: The quality and rigor of cybersecurity oversight varies dramatically across sectors. The energy sector's NERC CIP standards are the most mature, while the water sector has virtually no enforceable cybersecurity requirements despite serving as a target in recent Iranian and Chinese operations.

### Key Internal Government Conflicts

#### USCYBERCOM vs. NSA (The "Dual Hat")

The arrangement where one four-star general leads both organizations is the most consequential structural decision in U.S. cyber policy. Proponents argue it creates essential operational synergy -- the NSA's intelligence provides the targeting data for CYBERCOM's operations, and CYBERCOM's operations provide new access for the NSA to collect intelligence. Critics argue it creates a conflict between the military mission (delivering effects that may burn intelligence access) and the intelligence mission (maintaining persistent access for long-term collection), and that it concentrates too much power in one individual with insufficient civilian oversight. Congress has repeatedly debated and deferred the decision to split the dual hat. The FY2017 NDAA included provisions for separation, but the conditions have not been certified as met.

#### DoD vs. CISA/DHS

A persistent turf battle exists over who is ultimately responsible for defending the nation in cyberspace. The DoD's "defend forward" mission involves operating in foreign cyberspace to disrupt threats before they reach U.S. networks, but "defending forward" can sometimes mean operating on domestic networks or taking actions that affect domestic infrastructure. CISA, as the lead civilian agency, asserts its primacy for domestic cybersecurity. The boundary between "domestic" and "foreign" cyberspace is technically meaningless -- the internet is globally interconnected -- creating ongoing jurisdictional friction. The 2023 National Cybersecurity Strategy attempted to clarify roles but did not fully resolve the tension.

#### FBI vs. USCYBERCOM/NSA

Law enforcement and intelligence/military agencies often have conflicting objectives when responding to the same cyber incident. The FBI may want to preserve evidence, build a criminal case for prosecution, and coordinate with international law enforcement partners. USCYBERCOM may want to immediately disrupt an adversary's infrastructure, potentially destroying the evidence the FBI needs. These conflicts played out during the responses to the SolarWinds breach, the Colonial Pipeline attack, and various ransomware operations. Coordination has improved but remains imperfect.

#### Regulators vs. CISA

CISA aspires to be the central coordinator for critical infrastructure cybersecurity, but sector-specific regulators guard their regulatory turf. FERC, SEC, HHS, TSA, and other agencies each maintain their own cybersecurity requirements and resist subordination to CISA's coordinating authority. The result is a fragmented regulatory landscape where different sectors face different standards, creating inconsistency, compliance burden for companies operating across sectors, and gaps that adversaries can exploit.

## Private Sector Stakeholders

### Critical Infrastructure Owners and Operators

#### Energy Sector

- **Key Actors**: Duke Energy, Southern Company, Exelon, Dominion Energy, pipeline operators (Colonial Pipeline, Kinder Morgan, Energy Transfer).
- **Primary Interest**: Maintaining reliable operations and regulatory compliance while managing cybersecurity costs.
- **Cybersecurity Posture**: The most regulated sector, with NERC CIP standards providing a baseline. However, operational technology (OT) systems often run legacy software that cannot be easily updated, and the convergence of IT and OT networks creates new vulnerabilities.
- **Key Vulnerability**: The energy sector is the foundational infrastructure on which all other sectors depend. A successful attack on the power grid would have cascading effects across the entire economy.

#### Financial Services

- **Key Actors**: JPMorgan Chase, Goldman Sachs, Bank of America, Mastercard, Visa, SWIFT, major exchanges.
- **Primary Interest**: Protecting the integrity and availability of financial systems and customer data.
- **Cybersecurity Posture**: The best-defended sector in the private sector. Financial institutions spend an estimated $10-15 billion annually on cybersecurity. The FS-ISAC is the most mature and effective information-sharing organization. JPMorgan Chase alone spends approximately $700 million annually on cybersecurity.
- **Key Vulnerability**: The concentration of financial infrastructure (clearinghouses, payment processors, SWIFT) creates systemic risk.

#### Healthcare

- **Key Actors**: Hospital systems (HCA Healthcare, CommonSpirit Health, Ascension), insurers (UnitedHealth Group), pharmacy benefit managers (Change Healthcare/Optum), medical device manufacturers.
- **Primary Interest**: Maintaining patient care and protecting sensitive health data (HIPAA compliance).
- **Cybersecurity Posture**: Among the most vulnerable sectors. Healthcare organizations combine sensitive personal data, life-critical systems (medical devices, electronic health records), legacy technology, and limited IT budgets. The 2024 Change Healthcare attack demonstrated the sector's systemic vulnerability.
- **Key Vulnerability**: Ransomware attacks on hospitals directly endanger patient care. Studies have shown that hospital mortality rates increase during active ransomware incidents.

#### Telecommunications

- **Key Actors**: AT&T, Verizon, T-Mobile, Lumen Technologies.
- **Primary Interest**: Protecting network integrity and customer communications.
- **Cybersecurity Posture**: Increasingly targeted by state-sponsored espionage. The Salt Typhoon breach revealed that compromise of telecom providers gives adversaries access to wiretap systems and communications metadata for government officials, law enforcement, and millions of citizens.
- **Key Vulnerability**: The CALEA-mandated lawful intercept infrastructure creates a single point of compromise that adversaries can target.

#### Water and Wastewater Systems

- **Key Actors**: Approximately 150,000 water systems nationwide, ranging from large municipal utilities to small rural systems.
- **Primary Interest**: Maintaining safe water supply.
- **Cybersecurity Posture**: Among the least resourced and most vulnerable critical infrastructure operators. Small and medium water utilities often lack dedicated cybersecurity staff, and many rely on legacy SCADA systems with minimal security.
- **Key Vulnerability**: Iranian-linked hackers compromised water utility programmable logic controllers (PLCs) in 2024 (Aliquippa, PA), and Volt Typhoon pre-positioned access on water systems. The sector has virtually no enforceable cybersecurity standards.

### Technology Companies

#### Major Tech Platforms (Microsoft, Google, Amazon, Apple)

- **Primary Interest**: Protecting their platforms and customers while selling cybersecurity services and cloud infrastructure.
- **Source of Power**: These companies have more visibility into global cyber threats than most governments. Microsoft alone blocks billions of attacks per day and publishes detailed threat intelligence reports identifying state-sponsored campaigns. AWS, Microsoft Azure, and Google Cloud host a significant portion of government and critical infrastructure data. Their security decisions affect billions of users worldwide.
- **Key Tension**: Tech companies resist being seen as instruments of U.S. state power, particularly when they operate globally and serve customers in countries that are U.S. adversaries. They have provided critical support to Ukraine's cyber defense, but this has complicated their position in other markets.

#### Cybersecurity Industry (CrowdStrike, Palo Alto Networks, Mandiant/Google Cloud, SentinelOne, Fortinet)

- **Primary Interest**: Selling security products and services. The global cybersecurity market is projected to exceed $300 billion by 2027.
- **Source of Power**: These companies are often the first responders to major incidents and have deep technical knowledge of adversary tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs). CrowdStrike and Mandiant (now part of Google Cloud) have conducted forensic investigations of many of the most significant cyber incidents. Their threat intelligence reports are often the primary public source of information about state-sponsored campaigns.
- **Key Role**: The cybersecurity industry serves as a de facto extension of national cyber defense, providing capabilities that the government cannot replicate at scale.

#### Software Vendors

- **Primary Interest**: Selling software products while balancing speed-to-market with security investment.
- **Key Tension**: Software vendors occupy a privileged position in the supply chain, and a vulnerability in their products can cascade to thousands of downstream users (SolarWinds, MOVEit, Log4j). The 2023 National Cybersecurity Strategy's proposal to shift legal liability for insecure software from consumers to vendors directly threatens this stakeholder group and has generated significant industry opposition.

## External Stakeholders

### Adversary States

#### China

- **Primary Interest**: (1) Steal intellectual property to accelerate economic and military development; (2) pre-position destructive access on U.S. critical infrastructure for use in a Taiwan contingency; (3) conduct espionage against government officials and decision-makers.
- **Key Actors**: PLA Information Support Force (formerly Strategic Support Force), Ministry of State Security (MSS), contracted hackers.
- **Scale**: FBI Director Wray testified that Chinese state-sponsored hackers outnumber FBI cyber agents "at least 50 to 1." China's hacking program is assessed to be "larger than that of every other major nation combined."

#### Russia

- **Primary Interest**: (1) Disruptive/destructive attacks as tools of coercive statecraft; (2) election interference and information operations to undermine democratic institutions; (3) intelligence collection against Western governments.
- **Key Actors**: GRU Unit 26165 (APT28/Fancy Bear), GRU Unit 74455 (Sandworm), SVR (APT29/Cozy Bear), FSB (Turla, Berserk Bear).
- **Distinctive Characteristic**: Russia is the only nation-state that has demonstrated a repeated willingness to conduct destructive cyberattacks (NotPetya, Ukraine grid attacks, Viasat). Russia also tolerates and sometimes directs ransomware gangs that provide plausible deniability.

#### Iran

- **Primary Interest**: Using cyber as an asymmetric tool to counter U.S. and Israeli power, retaliate for perceived provocations, and project regional influence.
- **Key Actors**: IRGC-affiliated groups (Cyber Av3ngers, Charming Kitten/Phosphorus), MOIS.
- **Distinctive Characteristic**: High risk tolerance and willingness to target critical infrastructure (water utilities, energy systems) with destructive intent.

#### North Korea

- **Primary Interest**: Revenue generation through cyber theft (estimated $1.5-2 billion stolen 2017-2024) to fund nuclear and missile programs, circumventing international sanctions.
- **Key Actors**: Lazarus Group, APT38, Kimsuky.
- **Distinctive Characteristic**: The only nation-state that uses cyber operations primarily for financial theft. Also operates an IT worker fraud scheme deploying thousands of North Korean workers under false identities to freelance for Western companies.

### Non-State Actors

#### Ransomware Groups

- **Key Actors**: LockBit (disrupted 2024), BlackCat/ALPHV, Cl0p, Royal/BlackSuit, Akira.
- **Primary Interest**: Financial profit through extortion.
- **Scale**: Ransomware payments exceeded $1.1 billion in 2023 (Chainalysis). Many groups operate "ransomware-as-a-service" (RaaS) models that enable less-skilled affiliates to conduct attacks.
- **State Nexus**: Many groups operate from Russia with varying degrees of state tolerance or direction, providing the Russian government with plausible deniability for operations that have strategic-level impact.

#### Hacktivists

- **Key Actors**: IT Army of Ukraine, pro-Russian groups (Killnet, NoName057(16)), Anonymous affiliates.
- **Primary Interest**: Political goals, often aligned with state interests.
- **Impact**: Limited technical capability but can generate media attention. Some "hacktivist" groups serve as fronts for state intelligence operations.

### Allies and Partners

#### Five Eyes Partners (UK GCHQ/NCSC, Australia ASD, Canada CSE, New Zealand GCSB)

- **Interest**: Deepest intelligence-sharing partnership in cyberspace. Joint operations, shared threat intelligence, and coordinated public attribution.
- **Leverage**: Provide global coverage and additional operational capacity. The UK's National Cyber Security Centre (NCSC) is considered a model for government cybersecurity agencies.

#### NATO

- **Interest**: Collective defense in cyberspace. NATO declared cyberspace an operational domain in 2016 and agreed that a cyberattack could trigger Article 5 (collective defense).
- **Leverage**: Provides political legitimacy for collective responses and platforms for USCYBERCOM hunt-forward operations. The NATO CCDCOE in Tallinn is a leading center for cyber defense research and doctrine.

#### Indo-Pacific Partners (Japan, South Korea, Australia, India)

- **Interest**: Regional cybersecurity cooperation against Chinese and North Korean threats.
- **Growing Importance**: USCYBERCOM has expanded hunt-forward operations in the Indo-Pacific, and bilateral cyber cooperation agreements with Japan, South Korea, and Australia have deepened significantly.

### Civil Society

#### Privacy and Civil Liberties Organizations

- **Key Actors**: ACLU, EFF, Center for Democracy & Technology (CDT), Brennan Center for Justice.
- **Primary Interest**: Protecting the privacy and civil liberties of U.S. citizens from government surveillance enabled by cyber authorities.
- **Leverage**: Legal advocacy, public awareness campaigns, litigation challenging surveillance authorities (including successful challenges to bulk metadata collection and influence over Section 702 reform).

#### Academic and Research Community

- **Primary Interest**: Independent research on cybersecurity policy, technology, and international norms.
- **Key Contributions**: The Tallinn Manual (international law), the Belfer Center's National Cyber Power Index, Carnegie's Cyber Operations Tracker, and numerous studies on deterrence theory, escalation dynamics, and the effectiveness of cyber operations.

## Stakeholder Power Analysis

| Stakeholder | Political Power | Economic Resources | Information Access | Organizational Capacity |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| USCYBERCOM/NSA | Very High | High | Very High | Very High |
| CISA | Medium (growing) | Medium | High | Medium |
| FBI Cyber Division | Medium | Medium | High | High |
| Major Tech Companies | High | Very High | Very High | Very High |
| Critical Infrastructure Owners | Medium | High (varies) | Medium | Medium |
| Cybersecurity Industry | Medium | High | Very High | High |
| Congress (relevant committees) | Very High | N/A | Medium | High |
| Adversary States (China, Russia) | N/A | High | High | High |
| Civil Society/Privacy Groups | Low-Medium | Low | Medium | Medium |
| General Public | Low (diffuse) | N/A | Very Low | Very Low |

The fundamental power imbalance in this stakeholder landscape is that the entities best positioned to defend cyberspace (major tech companies, large critical infrastructure operators, government agencies) often have misaligned incentives, while the entities most affected by insecurity (the general public, small businesses, healthcare providers, state and local governments) lack the resources and organizational capacity to drive reform.
