# Cyber Warfare: What You Can Do

## Overview

While much of cyber warfare is conducted by classified government agencies and invisible to the public, citizens, businesses, and communities have a critical role to play in national cyber defense. Cyberspace is unique among national security domains in that individual and corporate actions can have a direct impact on national security. Every unpatched system, reused password, and unreported phishing email creates an opportunity for adversaries. Conversely, a citizenry and business community that practices strong cybersecurity fundamentals dramatically raises the cost and difficulty of cyberattacks for adversaries at all levels.

---

## 1. For Individuals: Practice Cyber Hygiene

National resilience starts with individual security. The vast majority of successful cyberattacks -- including many state-sponsored operations -- begin with simple techniques like phishing, credential theft, and exploitation of known vulnerabilities. Personal cybersecurity practices can make it significantly harder for adversaries to succeed.

### Priority Actions

- **Use Strong, Unique Passwords**: Use a password manager (e.g., 1Password, Bitwarden, Dashlane) to create and store complex, unique passwords for every account. Reused passwords are the single most common vector for account compromise. When a credential database is breached at one service, attackers immediately test those credentials against other services -- a technique called "credential stuffing."

- **Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)**: Turn on MFA for all important accounts, especially email, financial services, and social media. Use an authenticator app (Google Authenticator, Authy) or a hardware security key (YubiKey) rather than SMS-based MFA, which can be defeated through SIM-swapping attacks. MFA blocks over 99% of automated account compromise attempts (Microsoft Security, 2023).

- **Update Your Software**: Keep your operating system, web browser, and applications updated to ensure you have the latest security patches. Enable automatic updates wherever possible. Many of the most damaging cyberattacks -- including WannaCry (2017) -- exploited vulnerabilities for which patches were already available. CISA's Known Exploited Vulnerabilities (KEV) catalog lists vulnerabilities being actively exploited; the patches for these should be applied immediately.

- **Be Skeptical of Phishing**: Phishing remains the most common initial access vector for both criminal and state-sponsored hackers. Be wary of unsolicited emails, texts, or calls asking for personal information or urging you to click links or open attachments. Verify requests through a separate, trusted channel. Spear-phishing -- targeted phishing tailored to specific individuals -- is used in nearly all major state-sponsored intrusions.

- **Secure Your Home Network**: Change the default password on your home router and Wi-Fi network. Use WPA3 encryption if available. Segment IoT devices (smart thermostats, cameras, speakers) onto a separate network from your primary computers and phones. Many IoT devices have minimal security and can serve as entry points into your home network.

- **Back Up Your Data**: Maintain regular backups of important data, stored offline or in a separate cloud account. This is the most effective defense against ransomware -- if you can restore your data from backup, paying a ransom becomes unnecessary.

- **Recognize and Report Suspicious Activity**: If you receive a suspicious email or notice unusual activity on your accounts, report it. Many organizations have phishing reporting tools. Report cybercrime to the FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3) at ic3.gov.

### Why Individual Action Matters for National Security

The 2020 SolarWinds attack began with the compromise of a single company's software build process. The 2021 Colonial Pipeline attack succeeded because of a single compromised password on a legacy VPN account that lacked MFA. State-sponsored hackers routinely target individuals -- government employees, defense contractors, think tank researchers, journalists -- as stepping stones to higher-value targets. When individuals practice strong cybersecurity, they deny adversaries the easy entry points that enable even the most sophisticated campaigns.

## 2. For Businesses: Build a Culture of Security

### Priority Actions

- **Implement a Security Framework**: Adopt a recognized cybersecurity framework as the foundation for your security program. The NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF 2.0, updated 2024) is the most widely used in the United States and provides a structured approach to identifying, protecting against, detecting, responding to, and recovering from cyber threats. For small businesses, CISA's Cybersecurity Performance Goals (CPGs) provide a more accessible starting point.

- **Train Your Employees**: Your employees are your first line of defense and your most common vulnerability. Conduct regular training on how to spot phishing attacks, report suspicious activity, and practice good security hygiene. Simulated phishing exercises can identify employees who need additional training. Companies that conduct regular security awareness training reduce their risk of successful phishing by 50-75% (SANS Institute, 2023).

- **Segment Your Networks**: Separate critical systems (financial data, customer data, operational technology) from general-purpose networks. Network segmentation limits the ability of an attacker who compromises one system to move laterally to more sensitive systems. This is particularly important for companies that operate industrial control systems or operational technology.

- **Plan for Incidents**: Develop and regularly exercise an incident response plan. Know who to call, what to do, and how to recover before an incident occurs. A well-rehearsed plan can reduce the average cost of a breach by over $2 million (IBM Cost of a Data Breach Report, 2024).

- **Report Incidents**: If you experience a significant cyber incident, report it to CISA (cisa.gov/report) and the FBI (ic3.gov). Your information can help the government identify and disrupt broader adversary campaigns, potentially protecting other organizations from the same threat. Under CIRCIA, critical infrastructure operators will be required to report significant incidents within 72 hours and ransomware payments within 24 hours.

- **Invest in Resilience**: Do not focus exclusively on prevention. Invest in robust backup and recovery systems, offline backup copies, and tested disaster recovery procedures so you can get back online quickly after an attack. Assume that a sufficiently determined and resourced attacker will eventually breach your defenses; the question is whether you can recover quickly enough to limit the damage.

- **Assess Your Supply Chain**: Evaluate the cybersecurity practices of your key suppliers and vendors. The SolarWinds, Kaseya, and MOVEit attacks all demonstrated that attackers can reach their ultimate targets through trusted third-party software and services. Include cybersecurity requirements in vendor contracts and conduct periodic assessments.

### For Small and Medium Businesses

Small and medium businesses are disproportionately affected by cyberattacks because they lack the resources for dedicated security teams. Key resources:

- **CISA Cybersecurity Performance Goals (CPGs)**: Free, prioritized baseline practices designed for organizations with limited resources.
- **SBA Cybersecurity Resources**: The Small Business Administration provides guidance tailored to small business needs.
- **NIST Small Business Cybersecurity Corner**: Simplified guidance based on the NIST framework.
- **Local FBI Field Offices**: Can provide briefings on current threats to businesses in your area.
- **Small Business Development Centers (SBDCs)**: Many offer free cybersecurity assessments and guidance.

## 3. Engage in the Policy Debate

### Advocate for Effective Cybersecurity Policy

Citizens can influence the policy decisions that shape America's cyber defenses:

- **Contact Your Elected Officials**: Advocate for specific policy changes:
    - Support full funding for CISA and the Office of the National Cyber Director.
    - Support the creation of a Cyber National Guard or civilian cyber reserve.
    - Urge implementation of the remaining Cyberspace Solarium Commission recommendations.
    - Support software liability reform that incentivizes vendors to build more secure products.
    - Demand that critical infrastructure in your community meets mandatory cybersecurity standards.

- **Advocate for Privacy and Oversight**: Support organizations that advocate for strong privacy laws and robust oversight of government surveillance activities. Effective cybersecurity policy must balance security needs with civil liberties protections. Support reforms to Section 702 of FISA that strengthen privacy safeguards while preserving intelligence capabilities.

- **Support Public-Private Partnerships**: Encourage your company and industry associations to participate in information sharing programs like those run by CISA and sector-specific ISACs. If you work for a critical infrastructure operator, advocate for your organization to join the Joint Cyber Defense Collaborative (JCDC).

- **Ask Questions at Town Halls**: When your elected officials hold town halls or public forums, ask what they are doing to:
    - Secure critical infrastructure (water, power, healthcare) in your community against cyberattacks.
    - Address the cybersecurity workforce shortage.
    - Establish clear international rules for state behavior in cyberspace.
    - Ensure adequate oversight of government cyber operations.

## 4. For Defense and Critical Infrastructure Workers

If you work in the defense industrial base, critical infrastructure, or government:

- **Follow Your Organization's Security Policies**: Security policies exist for a reason. Using unauthorized devices, sharing credentials, or bypassing security controls -- even for convenience -- creates vulnerabilities that sophisticated adversaries actively seek to exploit.

- **Report Suspicious Activity Immediately**: If you notice unusual network behavior, receive a suspicious email, or observe potential insider threats, report it through your organization's established channels. Early reporting is critical -- the average "dwell time" for a state-sponsored attacker on a compromised network is weeks to months, during which time they can exfiltrate massive amounts of data or pre-position destructive capabilities.

- **Maintain Awareness of the Threat**: Stay informed about current cyber threats relevant to your sector. CISA publishes sector-specific alerts and advisories. Your organization may provide additional threat briefings. Understanding how adversaries operate makes you better able to recognize and report suspicious activity.

- **Consider Cyber Service**: If you have cybersecurity skills, consider military service (active duty or reserve), government civilian service, or participation in a future Cyber National Guard. The Cyber Excepted Service (CES) offers competitive compensation for government cyber positions, and the CyberCorps Scholarship for Service provides full scholarships for cybersecurity study in exchange for government service commitments.

## 5. For Educators and Academic Institutions

- **Integrate Cybersecurity into Curriculum**: From basic digital literacy in K-12 to advanced cybersecurity programs at the university level, education is the long-term solution to the workforce gap.
- **Support CyberPatriot and Similar Programs**: CyberPatriot, the National Cyber League, and similar competitions engage students in cybersecurity and build a pipeline of future talent.
- **Pursue Research on Cyber Policy**: Independent academic research on cyber deterrence, international law in cyberspace, and the effectiveness of cybersecurity policies is essential for evidence-based policymaking.
- **Disclose Defense and Intelligence Funding**: If your institution receives cybersecurity research funding from defense or intelligence agencies, ensure transparent disclosure in publications and testimony.

## 6. For State and Local Officials

State and local governments are responsible for critical services (water, power, emergency services, elections) that are increasingly targeted by cyber adversaries:

- **Assess Your Community's Cyber Risk**: Identify the critical infrastructure in your jurisdiction and assess its cybersecurity posture. Many water utilities, school districts, and local governments lack basic cybersecurity protections.
- **Apply for Federal Cybersecurity Grants**: The State and Local Cybersecurity Grant Program (authorized by the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act) provides $1 billion in funding for state and local cybersecurity improvements. Ensure your jurisdiction is applying for and effectively using these funds.
- **Participate in CISA Programs**: CISA offers free cybersecurity assessments, vulnerability scanning, and incident response support to state and local governments. These services are available at no cost and can significantly improve your community's security posture.
- **Secure Election Infrastructure**: Work with CISA and your state election officials to ensure that election systems in your jurisdiction meet current security standards, including paper ballot backups, post-election audits, and network segmentation.
- **Develop a Cyber Incident Response Plan**: Ensure your jurisdiction has a plan for responding to a major cyber incident affecting critical services, including communication procedures, mutual aid agreements with neighboring jurisdictions, and contact information for federal agencies (CISA, FBI).

## Effectiveness Tips

- **Start with the basics**: The most impactful actions are the simplest -- MFA, patching, strong passwords, phishing awareness. Do not let the complexity of the threat landscape prevent you from taking these foundational steps.
- **Think of cybersecurity as a civic duty**: Just as citizens maintain their property to prevent fire spread or get vaccinated to protect community health, practicing good cybersecurity protects not just yourself but the broader community and nation.
- **Be specific when engaging officials**: General complaints about "cybersecurity" are less effective than specific concerns about named infrastructure, specific programs, or concrete policy proposals.
- **Be persistent**: The adversaries operate 24/7/365. Advocacy for better cybersecurity policy and personal cybersecurity practice must be continuous, not episodic.
- **Build coalitions**: Cybersecurity is a rare area of genuine bipartisan agreement. Frame cybersecurity as a shared national interest that transcends partisan divides.
