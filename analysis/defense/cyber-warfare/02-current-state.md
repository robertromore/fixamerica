# Cyber Warfare: Current State

## U.S. Cyber Forces and Doctrine

### U.S. Cyber Command (USCYBERCOM)

- **Mission**: To direct, synchronize, and coordinate cyberspace planning and operations to defend and advance national interests in collaboration with domestic and international partners.
- **Structure**: USCYBERCOM is one of the 11 unified combatant commands of the Department of Defense. The Commander of USCYBERCOM is also dual-hatted as the Director of the National Security Agency (NSA). USCYBERCOM was elevated to a full unified combatant command in 2018 under the leadership of General Paul Nakasone, signaling its growing importance alongside geographic and other functional commands.
- **Cyber Mission Force (CMF)**: The CMF is the operational arm of USCYBERCOM, consisting of approximately 6,800 personnel organized into 133 teams.
    - **National Mission Teams (13 teams)**: Defend the U.S. from strategic cyber threats by operating outside DoD networks to disrupt adversary campaigns.
    - **Combat Mission Teams (27 teams)**: Support other combatant commands (e.g., INDOPACOM, EUCOM) with integrated cyber effects during military operations.
    - **Cyber Protection Teams (68 teams)**: Defend DoD networks, systems, and weapons platforms from intrusion and attack.
    - **Support Teams (25 teams)**: Provide analytic and planning support to the operational teams.

### CYBERCOM 2.0

In 2023, USCYBERCOM announced a major force restructuring initiative known as "CYBERCOM 2.0," intended to move beyond the original 133-team CMF structure established in 2013. Key elements include:

- **Expanded Force Structure**: Growth beyond the 6,800-person CMF to incorporate additional capabilities in areas such as artificial intelligence, data analytics, and hunt-forward operations.
- **Service-Provided Components**: A clearer framework for how each military service (Army Cyber Command, Fleet Cyber Command/10th Fleet, 16th Air Force, Marine Forces Cyberspace Command) generates and provides trained cyber forces to USCYBERCOM.
- **Integration with Joint Force**: Deeper integration of cyber effects with conventional military operations, including improved planning tools and real-time coordination with combatant commands.
- **Partnerships**: Expanded "hunt forward" partnerships with allied nations, building on the model of deploying USCYBERCOM teams to allied countries (over 50 such missions conducted across 20+ countries as of 2024) to identify and disrupt threats on partner networks.

### Strategic Doctrine

- **Persistent Engagement**: This doctrine, articulated by General Nakasone and scholar Dr. Michael Fischerkeller, posits that to be effective in cyberspace, the U.S. must continuously engage with adversaries in the "gray zone" below the threshold of armed conflict. Unlike traditional deterrence, which assumes a distinction between peace and war, persistent engagement recognizes that cyberspace is a domain of constant contact where adversaries are continuously probing, exploiting, and positioning.
- **Defend Forward**: A component of persistent engagement, this involves taking action in foreign cyberspace to stop threats before they reach U.S. networks. This is done with the consent of allied and partner nations on whose networks USCYBERCOM operates. Notable defend-forward operations include support to Ukraine before and during the 2022 Russian invasion, operations to disrupt Russian Internet Research Agency activities targeting the 2018 and 2020 U.S. elections, and hunt-forward missions to allied nations in Eastern Europe, the Indo-Pacific, and Latin America.
- **2023 DoD Cyber Strategy**: The updated strategy, released in summary form in September 2023, emphasizes four priorities: (1) defend the nation by operating in and through cyberspace; (2) prepare to fight and win the nation's wars in cyberspace; (3) protect the cyber domain with allies and partners; and (4) build enduring advantages in cyberspace through workforce development and technology investment.

### National Cybersecurity Strategy (2023)

The White House released the National Cybersecurity Strategy in March 2023, representing the most comprehensive articulation of U.S. cyber policy to date. The strategy is organized around five pillars:

1. **Defend Critical Infrastructure**: Expand minimum cybersecurity requirements for critical sectors and modernize federal network defenses.
2. **Disrupt and Dismantle Threat Actors**: Use all instruments of national power to make malicious actors incapable of threatening U.S. interests.
3. **Shape Market Forces to Drive Security and Resilience**: Shift liability for software and services to the entities best positioned to reduce risk, and promote privacy-preserving data practices.
4. **Invest in a Resilient Future**: Reduce systemic vulnerabilities, prioritize cybersecurity R&D, and build a diverse, robust cyber workforce.
5. **Forge International Partnerships to Pursue Shared Goals**: Build coalitions to counter threats, increase partner capacity, and promote responsible state behavior.

The strategy's most significant policy shift is Pillar 3's proposal to shift legal liability for insecure software products from consumers to the companies that develop them -- a fundamental reorientation of incentives that the technology industry has resisted.

## Adversary Cyber Capabilities

The U.S. faces a diverse set of state-sponsored cyber threats, each with distinct motivations, capabilities, and risk tolerances.

### China (The "Pacing Threat")

China represents the most comprehensive and well-resourced cyber threat to the United States. The People's Liberation Army Strategic Support Force (now reorganized as the Information Support Force) and the Ministry of State Security (MSS) conduct cyber operations across the full spectrum.

| Capability | Intent / Doctrine | Key Campaigns |
|---|---|---|
| **Cyber Espionage** | Stealing intellectual property and military technology on a massive scale to fuel economic and military growth. U.S. officials estimate China has stolen hundreds of billions of dollars in IP. | **2015 OPM Hack**: Theft of security clearance records for over 21 million federal employees. **APT10 (Cloud Hopper)**: Compromise of managed IT service providers to access dozens of client organizations globally. |
| **Pre-Positioning for Conflict** | Developing capabilities to disrupt or destroy U.S. critical infrastructure in a crisis or conflict over Taiwan. This is the most concerning development in the threat landscape. | **Volt Typhoon (2023-present)**: A campaign that pre-positioned malware on U.S. water treatment, power grid, transportation, and communications networks, using "living off the land" techniques to evade detection. **Salt Typhoon (2024)**: Compromise of major U.S. telecommunications providers (AT&T, Verizon, Lumen) to access wiretap systems and communications metadata. |
| **Information Operations** | Using social media and other platforms to sow discord and influence public opinion, particularly on issues related to Taiwan, Hong Kong, and Xinjiang. | Active campaigns using inauthentic social media accounts targeting U.S. audiences, including the "Spamouflage" operation. |

**Scale of Chinese Cyber Operations**: FBI Director Christopher Wray testified in 2024 that China's hacking program is "larger than that of every other major nation combined." Chinese state-sponsored hackers outnumber FBI cyber personnel by "at least 50 to 1."

### Russia (The "Acute Threat")

Russia's cyber capabilities are among the most advanced and operationally proven, with a track record of destructive attacks and election interference.

| Capability | Intent / Doctrine | Key Campaigns |
|---|---|---|
| **Disruptive/Destructive Attacks** | Using cyberattacks as a tool of coercive statecraft, often integrated with military operations and political coercion. | **2017 NotPetya Attack**: A destructive malware attack disguised as ransomware that caused over $10 billion in collateral damage globally, targeting Ukrainian businesses but spreading to Maersk, FedEx, and Merck. **Whispergate/HermeticWiper (2022)**: Data-wiping attacks against Ukrainian government systems launched concurrent with the ground invasion. |
| **Election Interference** | Using hacking and information operations to interfere in foreign democratic processes, undermining confidence in democratic institutions. | **2016 U.S. Election**: GRU (military intelligence) hacking of DNC and DCCC servers, combined with information operations through the Internet Research Agency. **SolarWinds/Nobelium (2020)**: SVR (foreign intelligence) supply chain attack compromising thousands of government and private sector networks. |
| **Critical Infrastructure Targeting** | Maintaining persistent access to foreign critical infrastructure for potential future disruption, particularly in the energy sector. | **Ukraine Power Grid Attacks (2015, 2016)**: First confirmed cyberattacks to cause power outages, affecting approximately 230,000 customers. **Sandworm/Viasat Attack (2022)**: Disruption of satellite internet across Europe at the start of the Ukraine invasion. |

**Key Russian Actors**: GRU Unit 26165 (APT28/Fancy Bear), GRU Unit 74455 (Sandworm), SVR (APT29/Cozy Bear), and FSB (Turla, Berserk Bear).

### Iran

Iran's cyber capabilities have grown significantly since the Stuxnet attack on its nuclear program, driven by a desire for asymmetric capability against the U.S. and regional rivals.

| Capability | Intent / Doctrine | Key Campaigns |
|---|---|---|
| **Destructive Attacks** | Using destructive malware against regional adversaries and the U.S. homeland, often in retaliation for perceived provocations. | **2012 Shamoon Attack**: Data-wiping attack against Saudi Aramco that destroyed over 30,000 workstations. **2024 Water Utility Attacks**: Iranian-linked group "Cyber Av3ngers" compromised programmable logic controllers (PLCs) at water utilities in Pennsylvania and other states. |
| **Espionage and Influence** | Targeting defense, telecommunications, and energy sectors for intelligence, and conducting influence operations around U.S. elections. | **Operation Phosphorus/Charming Kitten**: Long-running espionage campaigns targeting think tanks, academics, journalists, and government officials. |
| **Ransomware as Cover** | Using ransomware-style attacks for destructive purposes, sometimes through proxy criminal groups. | Iranian nationals indicted in 2022 for ransomware attacks against critical infrastructure including a children's hospital. |

### North Korea

North Korea is unique among state cyber threats in its primary use of cyber operations for revenue generation to fund its nuclear and missile programs.

| Capability | Intent / Doctrine | Key Campaigns |
|---|---|---|
| **Financial Theft** | Stealing from financial institutions and cryptocurrency exchanges to generate revenue for the regime, circumventing international sanctions. Estimated $1.5-2 billion stolen through cyber operations from 2017 to 2024. | **2016 Bangladesh Bank Heist**: Attempted theft of $1 billion from the SWIFT system, successfully stealing $81 million. **Cryptocurrency theft**: Multiple campaigns stealing from DeFi platforms and exchanges, including the $620 million Axie Infinity/Ronin Bridge hack (2022). |
| **Disruptive Attacks** | Using destructive malware to punish perceived insults or provocations against the regime. | **2014 Sony Pictures Attack**: Destructive attack and data theft in retaliation for the film *The Interview*, attributed to the Lazarus Group. |
| **IT Worker Fraud** | Deploying North Korean IT workers under false identities to freelance for Western companies, generating revenue and potentially gaining insider access. | FBI warnings that thousands of North Korean IT workers are embedded in Western companies, generating hundreds of millions in annual revenue for the regime. |

### Criminal Ransomware Ecosystem

The ransomware threat has evolved from isolated criminal activity into a sophisticated ecosystem with significant national security implications.

- **Scale**: Ransomware payments exceeded $1.1 billion in 2023 (Chainalysis), with attacks affecting hospitals, schools, water utilities, and local governments.
- **State Nexus**: Many ransomware groups operate from Russia with varying degrees of state tolerance or direction. Groups like Conti, LockBit, REvil, and BlackCat/ALPHV have conducted attacks with strategic-level impact while enjoying effective safe harbor.
- **Critical Infrastructure Impact**: The 2021 Colonial Pipeline ransomware attack, which forced the shutdown of the largest U.S. fuel pipeline for six days and triggered fuel shortages across the Southeast, demonstrated that even criminal actors can cause strategic-level disruption.
- **Targeting of Healthcare**: Ransomware attacks on hospitals have directly endangered patient care. The 2024 Change Healthcare attack disrupted prescription processing and insurance claims for months across the U.S. healthcare system.

## Legal and Policy Framework

U.S. cyber operations are governed by a complex mix of public laws, executive orders, and classified presidential directives.

### Key Authorities

| Authority | Description |
|---|---|
| **Title 10 (Armed Forces)** | Authorizes the DoD to conduct military operations, including in cyberspace. Offensive cyber operations (also called "cyber effects operations") are generally considered a Title 10 activity when intended to degrade, disrupt, or destroy adversary capabilities. |
| **Title 50 (Intelligence)** | Authorizes the intelligence community (including the NSA) to conduct intelligence gathering activities, including through cyber means. "Covert action" in cyberspace is a Title 50 activity requiring a presidential finding and congressional notification. The line between Title 10 and Title 50 activities in cyberspace is often blurry and hotly debated. |
| **National Security Presidential Memorandum 13 (NSPM-13)** | A 2018 directive that reportedly delegated broader authority to the Commander of USCYBERCOM to conduct offensive cyber operations without needing lengthy White House interagency approval, replacing the more restrictive Presidential Policy Directive 20 (PPD-20) from 2012. The classified details of NSPM-13 have been the subject of significant congressional debate. |
| **Section 1642, FY2019 NDAA** | Authorized the Secretary of Defense to conduct clandestine military cyber activities in cyberspace to "deter, safeguard, or defend against attacks or malicious cyber activities" by Russia, China, North Korea, and Iran. Considered a statutory endorsement of "defend forward." |
| **Executive Order 14028 (2021)** | "Improving the Nation's Cybersecurity" -- required federal agencies to adopt zero-trust architecture, improve software supply chain security, and establish a Cyber Safety Review Board (CSRB). |
| **Cyber Incident Reporting for Critical Infrastructure Act (CIRCIA) of 2022** | Requires critical infrastructure owners to report significant cyber incidents to CISA within 72 hours and ransomware payments within 24 hours. CISA's implementing regulations are still being finalized as of 2025. |

### The Title 10/Title 50 Debate

The distinction between military cyber operations (Title 10) and intelligence activities (Title 50) has significant implications for oversight, legal authority, and operational conduct:

- **Title 10 operations** are subject to the law of armed conflict, congressional oversight through the Armed Services Committees, and specific rules of engagement.
- **Title 50 covert actions** require a presidential finding, notification to the intelligence committees, and adherence to different legal frameworks.
- In practice, many cyber operations do not fit neatly into either category. The "dual-hat" arrangement, where the same commander leads both USCYBERCOM (Title 10) and NSA (Title 50), further blurs the distinction and has been both praised for enabling operational synergy and criticized for complicating oversight.

## Public-Private Partnership

The defense of cyberspace is a shared responsibility, as the private sector owns and operates an estimated 85% of U.S. critical infrastructure.

### Key Challenges

- **Information Sharing**: The government has access to classified threat intelligence that private companies need, while companies have visibility into attacks on their networks that the government needs. Sharing this information quickly and effectively is a major challenge. Declassification timelines and liability concerns create friction that slows the flow of information in both directions.
- **Liability Concerns**: Companies are often hesitant to share information about breaches for fear of regulatory penalties, lawsuits from customers or shareholders, and reputational damage. The Cybersecurity Information Sharing Act of 2015 (CISA, not to be confused with the agency) provided some liability protections, but many companies consider them insufficient.
- **Misaligned Incentives**: The government's priority is national security, while a private company's priority is shareholder value. A company may not want to make costly security investments that do not have a clear return on investment. Security is a cost center, not a revenue generator, for most firms.
- **Regulatory Fragmentation**: Different critical infrastructure sectors are overseen by different sector risk management agencies (SRMAs), each with different regulatory approaches. The energy sector is regulated by FERC/NERC, financial services by OCC/SEC, healthcare by HHS, and so on. There is no single, coherent national standard for critical infrastructure cybersecurity.
- **Small and Medium Businesses**: While large corporations can afford sophisticated cybersecurity, many small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) in the supply chain lack the resources to implement robust defenses, creating the "weakest link" problem.

### Key Initiatives

- **CISA's Joint Cyber Defense Collaborative (JCDC)**: Launched in 2021, the JCDC brings together government agencies and private sector partners to develop joint cyber defense plans. Its initial focus has been on coordinating responses to major vulnerabilities (such as the Log4Shell vulnerability in December 2021) and developing defensive playbooks for critical infrastructure sectors.
- **Information Sharing and Analysis Centers (ISACs)**: Industry-specific organizations such as the Financial Services ISAC (FS-ISAC), Electricity ISAC (E-ISAC), and Health ISAC that share threat information among member companies and coordinate sector-specific responses.
- **CISA Known Exploited Vulnerabilities (KEV) Catalog**: A public catalog of vulnerabilities that CISA has confirmed are being actively exploited, with mandated patching timelines for federal agencies. Many private sector organizations have also adopted the KEV catalog as a patching priority guide.
- **Cybersecurity Performance Goals (CPGs)**: Cross-sector cybersecurity performance goals published by CISA as voluntary benchmarks for critical infrastructure owners, designed to be achievable and impactful baseline practices.
- **Sector-Specific Initiatives**: NERC Critical Infrastructure Protection (CIP) standards for the bulk electric system; TSA security directives for pipeline and aviation cybersecurity (issued after the Colonial Pipeline attack); SEC cyber incident disclosure rules for public companies (2023).

## Cyber Workforce

The persistent shortage of qualified cybersecurity professionals represents one of the most significant structural challenges to U.S. cyber defense.

### The Workforce Gap

| Metric | Value | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Global cybersecurity workforce gap | ~3.4 million unfilled positions | ISC2 Cybersecurity Workforce Study, 2024 |
| U.S. cybersecurity workforce gap | ~500,000+ unfilled positions | CyberSeek/NICE, 2024 |
| DoD cyber workforce target | ~7,800+ (CYBERCOM 2.0) | USCYBERCOM, 2024 |
| Average time to fill a cybersecurity position (federal) | 6-12 months | OPM, 2024 |
| Private sector cybersecurity salary premium over government | 30-50%+ for comparable roles | CyberSeek, 2024 |

### Key Challenges

- **Competition with Private Sector**: Federal government pay scales (GS system) cannot match private sector compensation for top cybersecurity talent. A senior penetration tester or threat intelligence analyst can earn $200,000-$400,000+ in the private sector, far exceeding government pay.
- **Security Clearance Bottleneck**: Military and intelligence cyber roles require security clearances, and the clearance process can take 6-18 months, during which candidates are often hired away by private sector employers.
- **Retention**: Even when the government recruits top talent, retaining them beyond their initial commitment is difficult. The military services lose many of their best-trained cyber operators to private sector firms and government contractors after their initial enlistment or service obligation.
- **Diversity**: The cybersecurity workforce remains predominantly white and male, with women representing approximately 25% of the workforce and Black and Hispanic workers significantly underrepresented. This limits the talent pool and deprives the field of diverse perspectives.

### Key Initiatives

- **National Cyber Workforce and Education Strategy (2023)**: The White House strategy to address the cyber workforce gap through expanding access to cyber education, increasing workforce diversity, and leveraging skills-based hiring practices.
- **CyberCorps Scholarship for Service**: An NSF-funded program that provides scholarships for cybersecurity study in exchange for government service commitments.
- **DoD Cyber Excepted Service (CES)**: A specialized personnel system that allows DoD cyber organizations to hire at competitive salaries outside the normal GS system.
- **Cyber Auxiliary/Reserve Concepts**: Various proposals for a "Cyber National Guard" or civilian reserve force that could mobilize private sector expertise during major incidents.

## Budget Landscape

U.S. government spending on cybersecurity has grown significantly but remains a small fraction of overall defense and national security spending.

### Federal Cybersecurity Spending

| Category | FY2025 Request | Trend | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| DoD cyber activities (including USCYBERCOM) | ~$14.5 billion | +10% YoY | DoD, 2024 |
| CISA budget | ~$3 billion | +15% YoY | DHS, 2024 |
| NSA (estimated, classified) | ~$12+ billion | Stable | Public estimates |
| FBI Cyber Division | ~$800 million | +5% YoY | DOJ, 2024 |
| Total federal civilian cybersecurity (non-DoD) | ~$13 billion | +12% YoY | OMB, 2024 |
| State and Local Cybersecurity Grant Program | $1 billion (total, over 4 years) | New program (IIJA) | DHS, 2024 |

### Context

- Total U.S. government cybersecurity spending across all agencies is approximately $20-25 billion annually.
- By comparison, the private sector spends an estimated $150-200 billion annually on cybersecurity in the United States.
- The global cybersecurity market is projected to exceed $300 billion by 2027 (Gartner, 2024).
- CISA's budget of ~$3 billion is widely considered insufficient for its mission of coordinating the cybersecurity of the entire federal civilian government and all 16 critical infrastructure sectors. The Cyberspace Solarium Commission recommended at least $400 million in additional annual funding for CISA, and some experts have called for doubling the budget.

## International Comparison

The U.S. approach to cyber warfare and cybersecurity exists within a global context where other major powers and allies have developed their own institutions, doctrines, and capabilities.

### Allies

- **United Kingdom**: The UK's National Cyber Security Centre (NCSC), established in 2016 as part of GCHQ, is widely considered a model for government cybersecurity agencies. The UK also established the National Cyber Force (NCF) in 2020 as a joint MoD-GCHQ offensive cyber capability. The UK's approach is notable for its public-facing posture -- the NCSC publishes extensive guidance and threat intelligence for both government and the private sector.

- **Israel**: Israel's Unit 8200 (military signals intelligence) and the Israel National Cyber Directorate (civilian coordination) have created one of the world's most effective national cybersecurity ecosystems. Israel benefits from mandatory military service, which channels talented young people through Unit 8200 before they enter the private sector, creating a lifelong network of cyber expertise. The Israeli cybersecurity industry (with over 500 cybersecurity companies) generates approximately $10 billion in annual exports.

- **Estonia**: A pioneer in cyber defense following the 2007 Russian cyberattacks, Estonia hosts the NATO CCDCOE and has developed a Cyber Defence Unit within its Defence League -- a volunteer force of civilian IT professionals who train regularly and can be mobilized for national defense. Estonia's model of integrating civilian cyber expertise into national defense is frequently cited as a template for the proposed U.S. Cyber National Guard.

- **Australia**: The Australian Signals Directorate (ASD) has adopted an approach similar to the U.S. "defend forward" doctrine. The 2023 Australian Cyber Security Strategy committed A$2.3 billion to cybersecurity over seven years. Australia is also a key Five Eyes partner for cyber intelligence sharing and joint operations.

- **France**: The Agence Nationale de la Securite des Systemes d'Information (ANSSI) serves as France's civilian cybersecurity agency. France has been a strong advocate for international cyber norms and co-launched the Paris Call for Trust and Security in Cyberspace in 2018. France's Commandement de la Cyberdefense (COMCYBER) conducts military cyber operations.

### Adversaries

- **China**: China reorganized its military cyber capabilities in 2024, transitioning from the Strategic Support Force to the Information Support Force. China's approach integrates cyber espionage, information operations, and electronic warfare under a unified command concept. China is estimated to have the largest state-sponsored hacking program in the world, with tens of thousands of dedicated cyber operators across the PLA, MSS, and contracted hackers.

- **Russia**: Russia's cyber capabilities are distributed across multiple intelligence agencies (GRU, SVR, FSB) and military units, creating a diverse but sometimes uncoordinated threat. Russia's doctrine of "information confrontation" treats cyber operations as one element of a broader information warfare strategy that includes propaganda, disinformation, and electronic warfare. Russia's tolerance of criminal ransomware groups operating from its territory adds a volatile dimension to the threat.

- **Iran**: Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) and Ministry of Intelligence and Security (MOIS) have developed significant offensive capabilities, particularly in destructive attacks and influence operations. Iran's capabilities have grown rapidly since Stuxnet, and the country has demonstrated a high risk tolerance, targeting critical infrastructure in the U.S., Saudi Arabia, and Israel.

- **North Korea**: The Reconnaissance General Bureau (RGB) oversees North Korea's cyber operations, which are uniquely focused on revenue generation. North Korean cyber operators are estimated to have stolen $1.5-2 billion in cryptocurrency and from financial institutions between 2017 and 2024. North Korea also operates a large-scale IT worker fraud program that generates hundreds of millions in annual revenue.
