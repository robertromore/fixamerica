# Alliances and Partnerships: History

## Historical Development

The U.S. system of alliances is a product of the post-World War II era, designed to contain the Soviet Union and prevent a return to global conflict. Its evolution reflects the changing geopolitical landscape, from the bipolar Cold War to the unipolar moment and back to great power competition. Before 1949, the United States had maintained a near-continuous policy of avoiding "entangling alliances," a tradition dating to George Washington's Farewell Address of 1796 and reinforced by the Senate's rejection of the Treaty of Versailles and the League of Nations in 1919-1920. The creation of NATO represented a fundamental departure from nearly 170 years of American strategic tradition.

## Pre-Cold War Foundations

### The Atlantic Charter and Wartime Cooperation (1941-1945)

The intellectual and institutional foundations of the postwar alliance system were laid during World War II. The Atlantic Charter, issued by President Franklin Roosevelt and Prime Minister Winston Churchill in August 1941 -- before the U.S. even entered the war -- articulated shared Anglo-American principles that would undergird the postwar order: self-determination, free trade, freedom of the seas, and collective security. The wartime alliance among the United States, United Kingdom, and Soviet Union demonstrated the operational feasibility of multinational military cooperation, while also revealing the political frictions inherent in alliances among states with divergent interests.

The Bretton Woods Conference (1944) and the founding of the United Nations (1945) created the economic and diplomatic architecture of the postwar order, but the security architecture required a more direct instrument: mutual defense treaties.

### The Vandenberg Resolution (1948)

The key domestic political breakthrough enabling U.S. alliance commitments was Senate Resolution 239, introduced by Senator Arthur Vandenberg (R-MI), chairman of the Senate Foreign Relations Committee. The resolution, adopted 64-4 on June 11, 1948, declared the Senate's support for U.S. participation in "regional and other collective arrangements for individual and collective self-defense" consistent with the UN Charter. This bipartisan endorsement of peacetime alliances cleared the constitutional path for NATO and subsequent treaty commitments, overcoming the isolationist tradition that had blocked U.S. participation in the League of Nations.

## The Cold War: Forging the Alliance System (1949-1991)

### The Creation of NATO (1949)

| Year | Development | Impact on Alliance Policy |
|---|---|---|
| 1949 | **Founding of NATO** | The North Atlantic Treaty, signed in Washington on April 4, 1949, by 12 nations, created a formal collective defense commitment, binding the U.S. to the security of Western Europe. Article 5 stated that an armed attack against one member "shall be considered an attack against them all." The original signatories were the U.S., Canada, UK, France, Belgium, Netherlands, Luxembourg, Norway, Denmark, Iceland, Italy, and Portugal. |
| 1952 | **Greece and Turkey join NATO** | Extended the alliance's southern flank to the Eastern Mediterranean, adding two nations on the front line with the Soviet Union. |
| 1955 | **West Germany joins NATO** | The accession of the Federal Republic of Germany -- ten years after the end of World War II -- was deeply controversial but essential to building a credible conventional defense of Western Europe. The Soviet Union responded by creating the Warsaw Pact. |

The first Supreme Allied Commander Europe (SACEUR) was General Dwight D. Eisenhower, who took command in 1951 and established SHAPE in Paris (later relocated to Mons, Belgium, after France withdrew from NATO's integrated military command in 1966). The early years of NATO were focused on building conventional forces to deter a Soviet ground offensive in Central Europe, though the alliance quickly became dependent on the U.S. nuclear guarantee as the primary deterrent.

### Indo-Pacific Alliance Formation (1951-1962)

| Year | Development | Impact on Alliance Policy |
|---|---|---|
| 1951 | **ANZUS Treaty** | The Australia, New Zealand, and United States Security Treaty solidified the alliance with key Pacific partners. New Zealand was later effectively suspended from ANZUS in 1986 over its anti-nuclear policy. |
| 1951 | **U.S.-Philippines Mutual Defense Treaty** | Signed one day before ANZUS, cementing the U.S. relationship with its former colony and securing access to major military bases at Clark Air Base and Subic Bay Naval Station. |
| 1953 | **U.S.-South Korea Mutual Defense Treaty** | Signed after the Korean War armistice, this treaty committed the U.S. to the defense of South Korea and established the basis for a long-term U.S. troop presence. President Syngman Rhee had demanded the treaty as a condition for accepting the armistice rather than continuing to fight for unification. |
| 1954 | **Southeast Asia Treaty Organization (SEATO)** | Created by the Manila Pact, SEATO included the U.S., UK, France, Australia, New Zealand, Philippines, Thailand, and Pakistan. Unlike NATO, it lacked a standing military force and an integrated command structure, making it largely ineffective. It was disbanded in 1977. |
| 1960 | **U.S.-Japan Treaty of Mutual Cooperation and Security** | Revised an earlier 1951 treaty, this became the cornerstone of U.S. strategy in East Asia, granting the U.S. basing rights in Japan in exchange for a security guarantee. The treaty's ratification triggered massive protests in Tokyo (the "Anpo" protests), forcing the cancellation of President Eisenhower's planned visit and the resignation of Prime Minister Kishi Nobusuke. |
| 1962 | **Thanat-Rusk Communique** | A joint statement by Thai Foreign Minister Thanat Khoman and Secretary of State Dean Rusk affirming that the U.S. obligation to Thailand under SEATO was individual as well as collective, effectively creating a bilateral defense commitment that survived SEATO's dissolution. |

During this era, Secretary of State John Foster Dulles constructed the "hub-and-spokes" system of bilateral alliances in Asia, deliberately choosing bilateral treaties over a multilateral NATO-style pact. The rationale was partly strategic -- Asian allies had different threat perceptions and less in common with each other than the European allies did -- and partly political, as a multilateral Asian pact would have been more difficult to ratify in the Senate and would have given the U.S. less control over alliance decisions.

### The Cold War Alliance in Practice

The Cold War alliance system was tested repeatedly but never through the invocation of Article 5:

- **The Berlin Crises (1948-49, 1958-61)**: The Berlin Blockade and the construction of the Berlin Wall tested allied solidarity, with the 1948-49 airlift demonstrating Western resolve.
- **The Suez Crisis (1956)**: The U.S. opposition to the Anglo-French-Israeli invasion of Egypt exposed a major intra-alliance rift and demonstrated that NATO solidarity did not extend to colonial adventures outside the treaty area.
- **France's Withdrawal from NATO's Military Command (1966)**: President Charles de Gaulle withdrew France from NATO's integrated military command structure (though not from the treaty itself), expelled NATO headquarters from Paris, and pursued an independent nuclear deterrent (*force de frappe*). France did not rejoin NATO's military command until 2009 under President Nicolas Sarkozy.
- **The Cuban Missile Crisis (1962)**: The crisis demonstrated both the centrality of the U.S. nuclear guarantee and the allies' lack of influence over U.S. decisions that could lead to nuclear war on their territory.

## The Post-Cold War: A Search for Purpose (1991-2001)

- **"Out of Area or Out of Business"**: With the collapse of the Soviet Union, NATO faced an existential crisis. Senator Richard Lugar's famous formulation captured the debate: NATO would either go "out of area" (beyond the North Atlantic Treaty's geographic scope) or go "out of business." It adapted by taking on "out-of-area" missions, most notably leading the peacekeeping interventions in the **Balkans** -- the 1995 Implementation Force (IFOR) in Bosnia and the 1999 Kosovo Force (KFOR), both of which involved sustained NATO combat and peacekeeping operations.
- **NATO Enlargement**: A major debate emerged over whether to expand NATO eastward. The "Partnership for Peace" (PfP) program was created in 1994 as a halfway house for former Warsaw Pact states. In 1999, the **Czech Republic, Hungary, and Poland** became the first former Warsaw Pact members to join the alliance, a move seen by Russia as a major provocation. A second wave in 2004 added the three Baltic states (Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania), along with Bulgaria, Romania, Slovakia, and Slovenia -- bringing NATO to Russia's borders.
- **The Russia Question**: Whether Western leaders made implicit or explicit promises to Soviet leader Mikhail Gorbachev that NATO would not expand eastward remains one of the most contentious historical disputes in international relations. Declassified documents show that Secretary of State James Baker told Gorbachev in February 1990 that NATO's jurisdiction "would not shift one inch eastward," though whether this constituted a binding commitment is disputed. Russia consistently cites this as a foundational grievance.
- **The Indo-Pacific in the Unipolar Moment**: In Asia, the end of the Cold War did not produce the same existential questioning. The bilateral alliances with Japan and South Korea retained clear purposes due to the persistence of North Korea's threat and the uncertain trajectory of a rising China. The 1996 Taiwan Strait Crisis -- in which China conducted missile tests near Taiwan and the U.S. deployed two carrier battle groups in response -- reinforced the importance of the U.S. military presence in the Western Pacific.

## The Post-9/11 Era: Counter-Terrorism and "Coalitions of the Willing" (2001-2014)

- **Invocation of Article 5**: Following the September 11th attacks, NATO invoked its collective defense clause, Article 5, for the first and only time in its history, on September 12, 2001. NATO AWACS aircraft flew patrol missions over the United States -- the alliance defending American airspace.
- **The War in Afghanistan**: NATO led the International Security Assistance Force (ISAF) in Afghanistan from 2003 to 2014, its largest and longest operational deployment. At its peak, ISAF comprised over 130,000 troops from 51 nations. The mission strained alliance cohesion as "national caveats" -- restrictions individual allies placed on what their forces could do -- created a two-tier alliance in which some nations fought in the most dangerous areas (the U.S., UK, Canada, Denmark, Netherlands) while others restricted their forces to relatively safe regions.
- **The Iraq War (2003)**: The U.S. decision to invade Iraq without UN Security Council authorization and over the vocal opposition of France and Germany led to the deepest transatlantic rift since Suez. Secretary of Defense Donald Rumsfeld's dismissive distinction between "Old Europe" (France and Germany) and "New Europe" (Eastern European allies who supported the war) damaged intra-alliance trust. The U.S. instead relied on a "coalition of the willing," undermining the primacy of formal alliances like NATO.
- **Libya (2011)**: NATO's intervention in Libya under Operation Unified Protector exposed deep capability gaps among European allies. Defense Secretary Robert Gates's valedictory speech warned that NATO risked "collective military irrelevance" as European allies ran low on precision-guided munitions within weeks of the operation's start and relied heavily on U.S. intelligence, refueling, and surveillance assets.

## The Return of Great Power Competition (2014-Present)

| Year | Development | Impact on Alliance Policy |
|---|---|---|
| 2014 | **Russia's Annexation of Crimea** | This event was a major wake-up call for NATO, ending the post-Cold War "holiday from history." It led to the Wales Summit's reaffirmation of the 2% spending target, creation of the Very High Readiness Joint Task Force (VJTF), and the establishment of the Enhanced Forward Presence in the Baltic states and Poland. |
| 2016 | **Permanent Court of Arbitration rules against China in South China Sea** | The tribunal found China's "nine-dash line" claims without legal basis, but China rejected the ruling. The case highlighted the rule-of-law dimension of alliance competition in the Indo-Pacific. |
| 2017 | **"America First" Rhetoric** | The Trump administration's questioning of the value of NATO and its strong demands on burden-sharing created significant friction and concern among allies about the reliability of the U.S. security guarantee. President Trump's refusal to explicitly endorse Article 5 at the 2017 NATO summit (later corrected) sent shockwaves through allied capitals. |
| 2021 | **AUKUS Agreement Announced** | The creation of a new, technology-focused security pact with the UK and Australia signaled a major U.S. commitment to countering China in the Indo-Pacific, though it angered France, which lost a A$90 billion submarine contract with Australia. Paris recalled its ambassadors from Washington and Canberra -- unprecedented in the history of French-American relations. |
| 2022 | **Russia's Full-Scale Invasion of Ukraine** | This event completely reinvigorated NATO, proving its continued relevance. It led to unprecedented sanctions on Russia, over $100 billion in military aid to Ukraine from allied nations, and a surge in defense spending across Europe. Germany announced a special EUR 100 billion defense fund (*Sondervermogen*) within days of the invasion. |
| 2023-24 | **Finland and Sweden Join NATO** | Russia's invasion prompted two historically non-aligned Nordic nations to join NATO, a major strategic setback for Moscow -- effectively doubling NATO's border with Russia and bringing the entirety of the Baltic Sea (except the Russian exclave of Kaliningrad and St. Petersburg) within NATO territory. Finland brought a well-equipped military of 280,000 wartime strength and over 900 artillery pieces. |
| 2024 | **U.S. Forces Japan Restructured** | The upgrade of USFJ to a joint force command represented the most significant structural change to the U.S.-Japan alliance since its founding, enabling deeper operational integration for potential contingencies including a Taiwan conflict. |

This era is defined by the dual challenge of revitalizing Cold War alliances to deter Russia while also building new partnerships and adapting existing ones to compete with China. The central strategic question is whether the alliance system designed for one adversary in one theater can be adapted to confront two major adversaries in two theaters simultaneously.
