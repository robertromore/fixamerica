# Alliances and Partnerships: History

## Historical Development

The U.S. system of alliances is a product of the post-World War II era, designed to contain the Soviet Union and prevent a return to global conflict. Its evolution reflects the changing geopolitical landscape, from the bipolar Cold War to the unipolar moment and back to great power competition.

---

### The Cold War: Forging the Alliance System (1949-1991)

| Year | Development | Impact on Alliance Policy |
|---|---|---|
| 1949 | **Founding of NATO** | The North Atlantic Treaty created a formal collective defense commitment, binding the U.S. to the security of Western Europe. This was a historic departure from America's traditional avoidance of "entangling alliances." |
| 1951 | **ANZUS Treaty** | The Australia, New Zealand, and United States Security Treaty solidified the alliance with key Pacific partners. |
| 1953 | **U.S.-South Korea Mutual Defense Treaty** | Signed after the Korean War armistice, this treaty committed the U.S. to the defense of South Korea and established the basis for a long-term U.S. troop presence. |
| 1960 | **U.S.-Japan Treaty of Mutual Cooperation and Security** | Revised an earlier treaty, this became the cornerstone of U.S. strategy in East Asia, granting the U.S. basing rights in Japan in exchange for a security guarantee. |

During this era, the alliances were clearly defined and focused on a single, overarching threat: the Soviet Union and its communist allies.

---

### The Post-Cold War: A Search for Purpose (1991-2001)

- **"Out of Area or Out of Business"**: With the collapse of the Soviet Union, NATO faced an existential crisis. It adapted by taking on "out-of-area" missions, most notably leading the peacekeeping interventions in the **Balkans** in the 1990s.
- **NATO Enlargement**: A major debate emerged over whether to expand NATO eastward. The "Partnership for Peace" program was created in 1994, and in 1999, the **Czech Republic, Hungary, and Poland** became the first former Warsaw Pact members to join the alliance, a move seen by Russia as a major provocation.

---

### The Post-9/11 Era: Counter-Terrorism and "Coalitions of the Willing" (2001-2014)

- **Invocation of Article 5**: Following the September 11th attacks, NATO invoked its collective defense clause, Article 5, for the first and only time in its history in solidarity with the United States.
- **The War in Afghanistan**: NATO led the International Security Assistance Force (ISAF) in Afghanistan for over a decade. This long, difficult counter-insurgency mission strained alliance cohesion and resources.
- **The Iraq War (2003)**: The U.S. decision to invade Iraq without a broad international consensus led to a major transatlantic rift, particularly with France and Germany. The U.S. instead relied on a "coalition of the willing," undermining the primacy of formal alliances like NATO.

---

### The Return of Great Power Competition (2014-Present)

| Year | Development | Impact on Alliance Policy |
|---|---|---|
| 2014 | **Russia's Annexation of Crimea** | This event was a major wake-up call for NATO, ending the post-Cold War "holiday from history." It led to the creation of the NATO Response Force and the establishment of the Enhanced Forward Presence in the Baltic states and Poland. |
| 2017 | **"America First" Rhetoric** | The Trump administration's questioning of the value of NATO and its strong demands on burden-sharing created significant friction and concern among allies about the reliability of the U.S. security guarantee. |
| 2021 | **AUKUS Agreement Announced** | The creation of a new, technology-focused security pact with the UK and Australia signaled a major U.S. commitment to countering China in the Indo-Pacific, though it angered France, which lost a submarine contract with Australia. |
| 2022 | **Russia's Full-Scale Invasion of Ukraine** | This event completely reinvigorated NATO, proving its continued relevance. It led to unprecedented sanctions on Russia, massive military aid to Ukraine, and a surge in defense spending across Europe. |
| 2023-24 | **Finland and Sweden Join NATO** | Russia's invasion prompted two historically non-aligned Nordic nations to join NATO, a major strategic setback for Moscow and a significant strengthening of the alliance. |

This era is defined by the dual challenge of revitalizing Cold War alliances to deter Russia while also building new partnerships and adapting existing ones to compete with China.
