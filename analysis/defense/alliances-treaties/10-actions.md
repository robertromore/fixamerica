# Alliances and Partnerships: What You Can Do

The debate over the cost and purpose of U.S. alliances is a fundamental part of the broader discussion about America's role in the world. These commitments affect the federal budget, the risk of military conflict, and the global balance of power. Informed citizens can play a crucial role in shaping this debate, which ultimately determines whether the United States maintains, strengthens, or scales back the alliance system that has been the foundation of its global strategy for over 75 years.

---

## 1. Understand the Commitments

### Know What a Treaty Means

- Understand that a mutual defense treaty like NATO's Article 5 is a solemn commitment to treat an attack on an ally as an attack on the United States. Article 5 states that each party shall take "such action as it deems necessary, including the use of armed force, to restore and maintain the security of the North Atlantic area." This is the most serious promise a nation can make short of going to war itself.
- Note the critical nuance: Article 5 does not require an automatic military response. Each member decides what action it "deems necessary." This language was deliberately chosen in 1949 to satisfy the U.S. Senate's concern about preserving Congress's constitutional war-making authority. The commitment is real, but its precise form is a matter of political judgment, not legal automaticity.
- Understand the difference between NATO's collective defense treaty, bilateral defense treaties (Japan, South Korea, Australia, Philippines, Thailand), and non-treaty partnerships like AUKUS and the Quad. Each carries different levels of obligation and different political dynamics.

### Follow the Burden-Sharing Debate

- Look up the latest NATO defense spending figures, published annually by NATO's Public Diplomacy Division. Understand which allies are meeting the 2% of GDP target and which are not, but also understand the limitations of the 2% metric (it measures inputs, not outputs; it is distorted by GDP fluctuations; it ignores non-spending contributions like host-nation support and basing access).
- Compare U.S. defense spending (~3.4% of GDP, ~$886 billion) with that of major allies: the UK (~$75 billion), France (~$60 billion), Germany (~$97 billion), Japan (~$50 billion, rising to $75+ billion by 2027), and South Korea (~$47 billion). Consider these figures in the context of each country's GDP, threat environment, and non-defense contributions to the alliance.
- Follow the evolving debate over whether 2% is sufficient. Poland (4%+) and some analysts now argue that 3% should be the minimum. Understand the political and economic constraints that make meeting higher targets difficult for many allies.

### Learn the Geography

- Understand why the security of the "Suwalki Gap" -- the 65-kilometer corridor between Poland and Lithuania, bordered by Russian exclave Kaliningrad to the west and Belarus to the east -- is critical for defending the Baltic states. If Russia were to seize the Suwalki Gap, the three Baltic NATO members would be cut off from land reinforcement.
- Understand why the "First Island Chain" (running from Japan through Taiwan to the Philippines) is central to deterring Chinese aggression in the Western Pacific. Control of this chain determines whether China can project naval power into the open Pacific or is contained within the Western Pacific.
- Understand the strategic importance of the Strait of Malacca (through which roughly 25% of global trade passes), the South China Sea (where China has built artificial islands with military installations), and the Taiwan Strait (one of the world's most important semiconductor supply chain chokepoints).

### Understand the Constitutional Framework

- The Constitution gives the Senate the power of "advice and consent" on treaties, requiring a two-thirds vote for ratification (Article II, Section 2). This means that a mutual defense treaty, once ratified, has the force of law.
- The question of whether the President can unilaterally withdraw from a ratified treaty without Senate approval is constitutionally unsettled. In *Goldwater v. Carter* (1979), the Supreme Court dismissed a challenge to President Carter's unilateral termination of the U.S.-Taiwan defense treaty without reaching the merits. The NATO Commitment Act (if passed) would address this ambiguity legislatively.
- Congress also controls the power of the purse: it can fund or defund military activities in support of alliances, which gives it practical leverage over alliance policy even if the President has broad executive authority over foreign affairs.

## 2. Engage with Your Representatives

Congress authorizes funding for U.S. military activities in support of alliances, ratifies new treaties, and conducts oversight of alliance policy. Your representatives' votes directly shape the alliance system.

### Ask Specific Questions

- **At a town hall or in a letter, ask your representative or senator**: "Do you believe the U.S. should prioritize deterring Russia in Europe or China in Asia, and how should our alliance commitments reflect that choice?" This is the central strategic question of our time and most members of Congress have not articulated a clear position.
- **On burden-sharing**: "What is your position on NATO burden-sharing? Do you support conditioning the U.S. presence in Europe on allies meeting their 2% spending commitments, or do you believe the U.S. benefits from the alliance regardless of what allies spend?"
- **On specific legislation**: "Do you support the NATO Commitment Act, which would require Senate consent before the President can withdraw from NATO?" and "Do you support fully funding the Pacific Deterrence Initiative to strengthen our Indo-Pacific alliances?"
- **On Taiwan**: "What is your position on the U.S. commitment to Taiwan? Do you support providing Taiwan with the arms it needs to defend itself, and do you believe the U.S. should explicitly commit to Taiwan's defense?" This question forces representatives to engage with the most consequential alliance question of the coming decade.

### Support Funding for Alliance Initiatives

- Voice your support (or opposition, if that is your informed view) for funding streams like the **European Deterrence Initiative (EDI)** and the **Pacific Deterrence Initiative (PDI)**, which are the primary mechanisms for resourcing U.S. commitments in those theaters.
- Engage with the annual **National Defense Authorization Act (NDAA)** process, which is where Congress makes specific decisions about troop levels, weapons procurement, and alliance policy. The NDAA is typically debated in committee in the spring and on the floor in the summer.
- Track your representatives' votes on alliance-related measures using resources like GovTrack.us, Congress.gov, or the websites of advocacy organizations.

### Contact Committee Members

If your representative or senators serve on the **Senate Foreign Relations Committee**, **Senate Armed Services Committee**, **House Foreign Affairs Committee**, or **House Armed Services Committee**, they have an outsized influence on alliance policy. These members are particularly important targets for constituent engagement.

## 3. Support Transatlantic and Transpacific Ties

The strength of alliances is built on a foundation of shared values, mutual understanding, and people-to-people relationships. These intangible connections are what give alliances their political durability beyond any single government's tenure.

### Engage with Allied Cultures and Perspectives

- Follow allied media to understand how allies perceive the alliance and the United States: the **BBC** (UK), **Deutsche Welle** (Germany), **France 24** (France), **NHK World** (Japan), **The Japan Times**, **The Hankyoreh** (South Korea), **The Australian**. Understanding allied perspectives on burden-sharing, U.S. reliability, and shared threats will make you a more informed participant in the domestic debate.
- Support cultural exchange programs: the **Fulbright Program** (which sends Americans to allied countries and brings allied citizens here), the **Japan Exchange and Teaching (JET) Program**, NATO's **public diplomacy programs**, and university exchange partnerships. These programs build the personal relationships and mutual understanding that sustain alliances across generations.
- Participate in **sister city** programs that connect American communities with communities in allied nations. Over 2,000 U.S. cities have sister city relationships with communities abroad through Sister Cities International.

### Engage with Allied Military Members and Veterans

- Support organizations like the **Atlantic Council's Millennium Fellowship** and the **German Marshall Fund's Transatlantic Fellows** program, which build relationships among emerging leaders from allied nations.
- If you are a veteran, engage with allied veterans' organizations and joint military professional education programs that build cross-national military-to-military relationships.

## 4. Participate in the Strategic Debate

### Engage with Think Tanks and Research Organizations

- Follow the work of organizations that analyze U.S. alliances across the spectrum of views:
    - **Pro-alliance strengthening**: Center for Strategic and International Studies (CSIS), Center for a New American Security (CNAS), Atlantic Council, Council on Foreign Relations (CFR), Brookings Institution
    - **Pro-restraint/retrenchment**: Quincy Institute for Responsible Statecraft, Cato Institute, Defense Priorities
    - **Non-partisan research**: RAND Corporation, Congressional Research Service, Government Accountability Office
- Many of these organizations host free public events, publish accessible policy briefs, and maintain active social media presences that allow citizens to engage with the latest analysis.

### Advocate for Your Position

- Whether you believe the U.S. should double down on its alliances, reform them significantly, or pull back from its global commitments, make your voice heard through:
    - **Letters to the editor** of local and national newspapers
    - **Op-eds and blog posts** articulating your informed view
    - **Direct communication** with your elected officials (phone calls are generally more effective than form emails)
    - **Social media engagement** with the policy debate, including sharing analysis from credible sources
    - **Community forums and discussion groups** -- many libraries, universities, and civic organizations host foreign policy discussion series

### Educate Your Community

- Organize or participate in community discussions about U.S. alliances and America's role in the world. The **Foreign Policy Association's Great Decisions** program provides discussion materials and curricula for citizen groups across the country.
- If your community hosts a military installation with allied forces or participation in combined exercises, engage with opportunities to learn about the alliance firsthand. Installations like Fort Liberty (NC), Joint Base Lewis-McChord (WA), and bases in Hawaii regularly host allied forces for training.
- Support civic education initiatives that include international affairs and alliance policy in secondary and post-secondary curricula. An informed citizenry is the ultimate guarantee that alliance policy will serve the national interest.
