# Alliances and Partnerships: Reform Roadmap

Modernizing the U.S. alliance system requires a phased approach that builds consensus, demonstrates value, and systematically adapts old structures to new challenges. The phasing below reflects the reality that some reforms can be accomplished through executive action and existing institutional mechanisms, while others require legislative action, treaty negotiation, and multi-year capability development.

---

## Phase 1: Building Consensus and Addressing Urgent Gaps (Years 1-2)

This phase focuses on securing allied buy-in for a new strategic direction, addressing the most immediate capability shortfalls, and taking executive and legislative actions that can be implemented rapidly. These are primarily "low-hanging fruit" reforms that build momentum for more ambitious changes.

| Priority | Action | Lead Actor | Key Performance Indicator (KPI) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Launch "Responsibility-Sharing" Initiative** | At a NATO summit, the U.S. formally proposes a shift from the 2% GDP target to a capabilities-based "responsibility-sharing" model. Commission a comprehensive NATO capabilities review to identify the top 20 shortfalls. | President, Secretary of State, NATO Secretary General | NATO agrees to conduct a new capabilities review and develop a new force generation model. Allies commit to publishing their individual capability pledges. |
| **2. Clear the FMS Backlog** | The President uses executive authority to create an FMS "fast track" for Taiwan and key frontline NATO allies (Poland, Romania, Baltics). Establish a dedicated Taiwan Expedited Delivery Office within DSCA. Redirect existing DoD production capacity to prioritize ally deliveries. | DoD, State Dept., DSCA | The FMS delivery backlog for key systems (e.g., HIMARS, Harpoon, Stinger, Javelin) to frontline allies is reduced by 50%. Taiwan backlog ($19+ billion) reduced by at least 25%. |
| **3. Establish Atlantic-Pacific Dialogue** | The U.S. hosts the first ministerial-level meeting of the "Atlantic-Pacific Council" with NATO leadership and Indo-Pacific partners (Japan, South Korea, Australia, New Zealand). Establish permanent working groups on technology controls, maritime security, and sanctions coordination. | State Dept., NSC | A joint statement is issued identifying shared challenges and establishing working groups. Regular staff-level meetings scheduled quarterly. |
| **4. Integrate Cyber Defense** | NATO formally adopts a policy that a major cyber attack on a member state's critical infrastructure could trigger an Article 5 response. Define threshold criteria. Conduct first combined cyber-kinetic exercise scenario at Locked Shields. | NATO HQ, U.S. Cyber Command | Article 5 cyber doctrine formally adopted by North Atlantic Council. First integrated cyber-kinetic exercise completed with participation from at least 20 allies. |
| **5. Pass NATO Commitment Act** | Working with bipartisan Senate sponsors, codify the principle that the President cannot withdraw from NATO (or other mutual defense treaties) without Senate consent or an Act of Congress. Extend PDI with multi-year funding authorization. | Senate Foreign Relations Committee, Senate Armed Services Committee | NATO Commitment Act signed into law. PDI authorized at $10+ billion for 5 years. |
| **6. Accelerate AUKUS ITAR Reforms** | Implement the AUKUS-specific ITAR exemptions authorized in the FY2024 NDAA. Begin extending similar exemptions to Japan and South Korea for specific technology categories. | State Dept. (DDTC), DoD | AUKUS partners report a measurable reduction in license processing times. First Japan-specific ITAR exemptions issued. |

**Goal of Phase 1**: Secure political agreement for a new alliance framework, plug the most dangerous deterrence gaps in Europe and Asia, and establish the legislative and institutional foundations for deeper reforms.

## Phase 2: Implementation and Integration (Years 3-5)

This phase focuses on turning the new strategic concepts into concrete capabilities, institutional structures, and integrated operational plans. This is where the hard work of implementation begins.

| Priority | Action | Lead Actor | Key Performance Indicator (KPI) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Implement NATO Responsibility-Sharing** | Based on the Phase 1 capabilities review, the first set of allies formally commit to providing specific capabilities under the new model. Integrate these commitments into NATO's regional defense plans (which are being developed for the first time since the Cold War). | NATO Allied Command Operations, individual allied defense ministries | At least 10 major allies have their new capability commitments integrated into NATO's operational plans. Key gaps (air and missile defense, heavy armor, logistics) show measurable improvement. |
| **2. Expand AUKUS Pillar II** | Invite Japan to participate in specific AUKUS Pillar II projects (initially AI, undersea autonomous systems, and electronic warfare). Begin joint R&D programs with combined funding. Explore South Korean participation in munitions co-production. | DoD, AUKUS Coordination Office | Formal Japan participation in at least 2 AUKUS Pillar II technology areas. First joint R&D project produces a demonstrable prototype. |
| **3. Establish Common Munitions Fund** | NATO and Indo-Pacific allies agree on a GDP-weighted assessment to fund common munitions stockpiles. Establish forward-positioned stockpile sites in Poland, Romania, Japan, and Guam. Begin procurement of 155mm shells, GMLRS rockets, and air defense interceptors. | NATO NSPA, bilateral logistics agreements | Common fund reaches initial operating capability with $10+ billion in procurement commitments. Forward stockpile sites operational with initial deliveries. |
| **4. Build Resilient Supply Chains** | The Atlantic-Pacific Council agrees on a framework for coordinating investment in critical supply chains for semiconductors, rare earth minerals, and pharmaceuticals. Coordinate investment screening regimes to prevent adversary acquisition of critical allied technology companies. | Atlantic-Pacific Council working group, national investment screening agencies | A joint strategy for semiconductor supply chain diversification is agreed. Allied coordination on investment screening reaches operational tempo with regular information sharing. |
| **5. Institutionalize Trilateral Partnerships** | Establish permanent secretariats for U.S.-Japan-ROK trilateral (building on Camp David commitments) and an AUKUS Coordination Office. Staff with career officials to provide continuity across political transitions. | State Dept., DoD, allied foreign/defense ministries | U.S.-Japan-ROK secretariat operational with permanent staff. AUKUS Coordination Office established with tri-national staff. |
| **6. Conduct First Cross-Theater Exercise** | The U.S. conducts its first major joint exercise that involves both NATO and Indo-Pacific allies operating together in a single integrated scenario, testing the concept of cross-theater coordination. | EUCOM, INDOPACOM, Joint Staff | Exercise "Global Defender" (or equivalent) is successfully completed with forces from NATO, Japan, Australia, and South Korea participating in a combined scenario. After-action review identifies lessons for improvement. |

**Goal of Phase 2**: Move from concepts to reality. Allies begin fielding the capabilities they promised, and the transatlantic and Indo-Pacific alliance systems start to operate as an integrated network. Institutional structures are established that can sustain cooperation across political transitions.

## Phase 3: Maturing the Global Democratic Alliance Network (Years 6-10)

This phase focuses on achieving a fully integrated, capable, and resilient network of alliances that can deter and, if necessary, defeat aggression from peer competitors in multiple theaters simultaneously.

| Priority | Action | Lead Actor | Key Performance Indicator (KPI) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Achieve NATO Capability Goals** | European allies are providing the bulk of the conventional forces required to defend Europe, with the U.S. primarily in a supporting and enabling role (nuclear deterrence, ISR, strategic airlift, long-range strike). | NATO, European allied defense ministries | European allies provide 80% of the ground forces and 60% of the air forces for NATO's eastern flank defense plan. U.S. is able to shift naval and air assets to the Indo-Pacific without degrading European deterrence. |
| **2. Field Jointly Developed Technologies** | The first weapon systems developed under AUKUS Pillar II and other joint programs achieve initial operational capability and are deployed by multiple allied forces. | AUKUS Coordination Office, bilateral joint development programs | At least one jointly developed AI-enabled system (e.g., autonomous undersea vehicle, ISR fusion platform) is operational on U.S., UK, Australian, and Japanese platforms. Allied forces demonstrate interoperable autonomous systems in exercises. |
| **3. Achieve Cross-Theater Interoperability** | A secure communications and data-sharing network links NATO and key Indo-Pacific allies, enabling a common operational picture across both theaters. Allied forces can seamlessly share targeting data, ISR products, and command information. | Joint Staff, NATO ACT, allied C4ISR agencies | A common, secure, allied network is used routinely in exercises and achieves initial operational capability. Allied forces demonstrate the ability to share targeting data across theater boundaries in real time. |
| **4. Common Munitions Stockpiles at Wartime Levels** | Forward-positioned munitions stockpiles in both theaters reach levels sufficient to sustain 30+ days of high-intensity combat without resupply from the continental United States. | NATO NSPA, bilateral logistics commands | Independent assessment confirms stockpile levels meet 30-day high-intensity conflict requirement. Annual consumption exercises validate distribution and logistics chains. |
| **5. Allied Defense Industrial Integration** | Co-production agreements enable allies to produce critical U.S.-designed systems (munitions, missiles, electronics) in their own countries, creating a distributed, resilient defense industrial base that cannot be disrupted by the loss of any single production facility. | Defense industrial cooperation agreements | At least 5 allied nations are co-producing critical munitions. Total allied production capacity for key systems (155mm shells, GMLRS, air defense interceptors) exceeds pre-war 2022 levels by 300%. |

**Goal of Phase 3**: The U.S. alliance system has transformed from a set of regional, U.S.-led pacts into a global, integrated network of capable democracies. European allies can defend Europe with limited U.S. conventional support, freeing U.S. forces for the Indo-Pacific. Cross-theater coordination is institutionalized and exercised. The alliance system is resilient against both military aggression and hybrid threats.

## Risk Factors and Contingencies

| Risk | Probability | Impact | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **U.S. domestic political shift against alliances** | Medium-High | Very High | Codify commitments in law (NATO Commitment Act); diversify minilateral partnerships so no single disruption collapses the system |
| **Allied domestic political changes** (e.g., ROK progressive government cooling on trilateral) | Medium | Medium | Institutionalize commitments in permanent secretariats and binding agreements; build personal relationships across political parties |
| **Major conflict erupts before reforms mature** | Medium | Very High | Prioritize Phase 1 actions that directly improve deterrence (FMS backlog, cyber defense, munitions); maintain current force posture while reforms develop |
| **China-Russia alignment deepens into formal alliance** | Medium | High | Accelerate cross-theater integration (Atlantic-Pacific Council); expand the network to include non-traditional partners (India, Vietnam, Indonesia) |
| **AUKUS submarine program delays** | High | Medium | Develop contingency options (Virginia-class sales acceleration; conventional submarine alternatives); ensure Pillar II technology cooperation proceeds independently of Pillar I |
