# Alliances and Partnerships: Opposition to Reform

The U.S. alliance system, while enjoying broad bipartisan support in its general contours, faces persistent criticism from various ideological perspectives. These arguments challenge the cost, risk, and fundamental purpose of America's global security commitments. Understanding the opposition landscape is essential because these critiques increasingly shape domestic political debates and, in turn, allied perceptions of U.S. reliability.

---

## The "Restraint" or "Non-Interventionist" Opposition

This is the most fundamental opposition, arguing against the very concept of permanent, entangling alliances. The restraint school draws on both libertarian skepticism of government power projection and realist arguments about the limits of U.S. interests.

### Core Arguments

| Argument | Proponents' Claim | Rebuttal / Counterpoint |
|---|---|---|
| **Alliances are Entangling and Risk War** | Formal defense treaties like NATO's Article 5 are a tripwire that could automatically drag the U.S. into a major war over a distant conflict that is not a vital U.S. interest (e.g., a border dispute involving a Baltic state). The U.S. has no vital interest in defending Estonia that justifies risking nuclear war with Russia. | Alliances are a tool of deterrence, not entanglement. Their purpose is to prevent war by making the cost of aggression clear to an adversary *before* they act. The risk of being dragged into a war is lower than the risk of a major war erupting in a world without U.S. alliances. The withdrawal of security guarantees would invite the kind of aggression that alliances are designed to prevent, as the pre-World War II period demonstrated. |
| **Alliances Enable Free-Riding** | U.S. security guarantees create a moral hazard, allowing wealthy allies in Europe and Asia to underinvest in their own defense and spend the money on social programs, knowing the U.S. will bear the cost of protecting them. Europe spends its "peace dividend" on welfare states while Americans pay higher taxes for defense. | While burden-sharing is a real problem, the benefits of alliances -- forward basing, intelligence sharing, diplomatic support, and legitimacy -- far outweigh the costs of free-riding. The U.S. underwrites the system because it is in its own interest to do so. The alternative -- a world without alliances, where the U.S. must project power across oceans without bases, allies, or partners -- would be far more expensive. |
| **Alliances are Obsolete** | NATO was created to counter the Soviet Union, which no longer exists. Maintaining and expanding it only provokes Russia and is a solution in search of a problem. In Asia, bilateral alliances lock the U.S. into a confrontation with China that could be managed through diplomacy and accommodation. | The 2022 invasion of Ukraine demonstrated that the threat of Russian aggression is real and that NATO is more relevant than ever. The alliance provides a proven framework for collective defense that would be impossible to replicate from scratch. In Asia, China's military buildup, territorial claims, and coercion of neighbors demonstrate the continued need for alliance-based deterrence. |
| **Alliances Provoke Adversaries** | NATO enlargement provoked Russia by expanding a hostile military alliance to its borders, contributing to the conditions that led to the Ukraine war. Similarly, U.S. alliance-building in the Indo-Pacific is perceived by Beijing as encirclement and accelerates the security dilemma. | This argument gives adversaries a veto over the sovereign decisions of democratic nations to join defensive alliances. Nations join NATO because they fear Russia, not the reverse. Russia's behavior toward non-NATO neighbors (Georgia 2008, Ukraine 2014 and 2022) validates rather than undermines the decision to join. |

### Key Proponents and Organizations

- **Cato Institute**: The Defense and Foreign Policy Studies program has consistently argued for a strategy of restraint, advocating U.S. withdrawal from NATO and an end to security guarantees in Asia. Key scholars include Ted Galen Carpenter and Christopher Preble.
- **Quincy Institute for Responsible Statecraft**: Founded in 2019 with funding from both Charles Koch and George Soros, the institute advocates for diplomatic engagement over military alliances. Key figures include Andrew Bacevich and Trita Parsi.
- **Realist academics**: Scholars such as John Mearsheimer (University of Chicago), Stephen Walt (Harvard), and Barry Posen (MIT) argue from different theoretical frameworks for a strategy of "offshore balancing," in which the U.S. maintains the capability to intervene in Eurasia but does not station permanent forces or maintain binding alliance commitments. Mearsheimer's argument that NATO expansion provoked Russia's invasion of Ukraine has been widely debated and criticized.
- **"America First" political movement**: Former President Trump's skepticism of NATO and bilateral alliances reflected a populist-nationalist critique that allied governments do not pay their "fair share." This view resonated with a significant portion of the Republican base, and the 2024 Republican platform included language questioning open-ended alliance commitments.

## The Progressive Opposition

This opposition is less focused on the existence of alliances and more on their perceived role in promoting U.S. militarism, sustaining an unsustainable defense budget, and providing cover for questionable military interventions.

### Core Arguments

| Argument | Proponents' Claim | Rebuttal / Counterpoint |
|---|---|---|
| **Alliances Justify a Bloated Defense Budget** | The need to maintain a global presence and support dozens of allies is used as a primary justification for a defense budget that exceeds $850 billion annually, crowding out domestic priorities like healthcare, climate action, education, and infrastructure. The Pentagon budget is larger than the next 10 countries combined; alliances provide the rationale for this excess. | The U.S. defense budget is driven by the threats the nation faces, not by its alliances per se. In fact, by pooling resources and sharing burdens, alliances allow for a more efficient defense than if the U.S. had to provide for global security alone. Withdrawing from alliances would not necessarily reduce the defense budget if the U.S. still needed to maintain the capability to project power globally. |
| **Alliances Provide Cover for U.S. Interventionism** | The U.S. uses alliances like NATO to provide a veneer of multilateral legitimacy for its own military interventions. The Kosovo intervention (1999) and Libya intervention (2011) used NATO as a mechanism for U.S.-led regime change without UN authorization. | Most major alliance operations (e.g., the Balkans, Afghanistan) have been the result of broad consensus and addressed genuine humanitarian or security crises. When the U.S. has acted unilaterally (e.g., Iraq 2003), it has often been in the face of opposition from key allies, demonstrating the limits of U.S. influence over the alliance. NATO membership actually constrains U.S. action more than it enables it, because achieving consensus among 32 nations is difficult. |
| **Arms Sales and Militarization** | Alliance commitments drive billions in arms sales to allied nations, enriching U.S. defense contractors and contributing to the militarization of global politics. The pressure on allies to spend 2% of GDP on defense funnels resources into military procurement that could be better spent on development, climate, and diplomacy. | Arms sales to allies strengthen their deterrence capabilities, reduce the burden on U.S. forces, and improve interoperability. An under-armed ally is more likely to require direct U.S. military intervention in a crisis. Alliances also provide the framework for arms control agreements and confidence-building measures that reduce the risk of conflict. |
| **Nuclear Risk** | Extended nuclear deterrence -- the U.S. commitment to use nuclear weapons on behalf of allies -- creates catastrophic risks. The deployment of U.S. nuclear weapons in Europe (B61 gravity bombs in Belgium, Germany, Italy, Netherlands, and Turkey under NATO's nuclear sharing program) puts allied populations at risk and normalizes nuclear planning in alliance deliberations. | Extended deterrence has prevented nuclear proliferation by reducing allies' incentives to develop their own weapons. Without the U.S. nuclear umbrella, Japan, South Korea, Germany, and potentially others might pursue independent nuclear programs, making the world more dangerous. The alternative to extended deterrence is not a nuclear-free world but a world with more nuclear-armed states. |

### Key Proponents and Organizations

- **Progressive members of Congress**: Representatives such as Barbara Lee (D-CA), Ro Khanna (D-CA), and Ilhan Omar (D-MN) have questioned alliance spending and military commitments, particularly in the context of broader arguments for reducing the defense budget.
- **Anti-war organizations**: CodePink, Win Without War, and the American Friends Service Committee advocate for reduced military spending and a transition from military alliances to diplomatic engagement.
- **Arms control organizations**: The Arms Control Association, Ploughshares Fund, and International Campaign to Abolish Nuclear Weapons (ICAN) argue against nuclear sharing and extended deterrence practices within NATO.

## Opposition to Specific Alliance Policies

### Opposition to NATO Enlargement

Some critics, even those who support NATO's existence, argue that expanding the alliance to include countries like Ukraine or Georgia needlessly provokes Russia and extends U.S. security guarantees to nations that NATO cannot realistically defend. The debate over Ukraine's membership has been particularly intense since the 2008 Bucharest Summit, where NATO declared that Ukraine and Georgia "will become members" but without providing a Membership Action Plan. Critics argue this was the worst of both worlds: provoking Russia while providing Ukraine with neither membership protections nor a credible path to them.

Key voices in this camp include former U.S. Ambassador to Russia William Burns (now CIA Director), who warned in a 2008 cable that NATO enlargement to Ukraine was "the brightest of all red lines" for Russia, and former Secretary of State Henry Kissinger, who advocated for Ukrainian neutrality.

### Opposition to AUKUS

Critics of AUKUS span several dimensions:

- **Non-proliferation concerns**: The Arms Control Association and some former officials argue that sharing nuclear submarine technology with Australia undermines the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty (NPT) regime by creating a precedent for transferring weapons-grade highly enriched uranium (HEU) to a non-nuclear-weapons state, even for propulsion purposes. If Australia can receive HEU, other nations may demand the same treatment.
- **Alliance management concerns**: France's Naval Group and French strategic analysts argue that AUKUS was handled in a way that needlessly alienated a key ally, and that France's significant military presence in the Indo-Pacific (8,000 permanently stationed troops, a nuclear aircraft carrier, overseas territories) makes its exclusion from a Pacific security pact strategically counterproductive.
- **Cost and feasibility concerns**: Australian strategic analysts have questioned whether the A$368 billion, 30-year program will actually deliver submarines on schedule, given the chronic production problems at the two U.S. shipyards (General Dynamics Electric Boat and Huntington Ingalls Newport News) that are already struggling to meet the U.S. Navy's own submarine construction targets.
- **Sovereignty concerns**: Some Australian commentators have argued that AUKUS binds Australia too tightly to U.S. strategic decisions, potentially dragging Australia into a conflict with China over Taiwan that may not serve Australian interests.

### Opposition to Japan's Remilitarization

Within Japan, Article 9 of the Constitution -- which renounces war and the maintenance of "war potential" -- has long constrained Japanese defense policy. The constitutional revision debate pits the ruling Liberal Democratic Party (which favors amending Article 9) against opposition parties and civil society groups who view the pacifist constitution as a fundamental part of Japan's postwar identity. Internationally, China and both Koreas have expressed concern about Japanese rearmament, citing historical grievances from World War II.

### Opposition to the Quad

- **India's non-alignment tradition**: A significant faction within India's strategic establishment, including some in the Ministry of External Affairs, resists deepening security ties with the U.S., viewing the Quad as incompatible with India's tradition of strategic autonomy and its membership in groupings like BRICS and the Shanghai Cooperation Organisation. India's continued purchase of Russian S-400 air defense systems and oil reflects this hedging strategy.
- **Chinese opposition**: Beijing has vigorously opposed the Quad, labeling it an "Asian NATO" designed to contain China. China has used diplomatic and economic pressure to discourage Indo-Pacific nations from participating in Quad-adjacent activities.

## The Adversary Perspective

Understanding how U.S. adversaries view the alliance system is essential to assessing its deterrent value:

- **Russia**: Views NATO as an existential threat to its security and sphere of influence. Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine was motivated in part by a desire to prevent Ukraine from joining NATO. Russia has attempted to weaken NATO through information operations, cyber attacks, energy coercion, and support for Eurosceptic political parties in allied nations.
- **China**: Views the U.S. alliance system in the Indo-Pacific as a containment strategy reminiscent of the Cold War. China has responded by deepening its strategic partnership with Russia, expanding its military capabilities, and using economic leverage to pressure U.S. allies. China's "wolf warrior" diplomacy has, paradoxically, pushed many regional states closer to the U.S.
- **North Korea**: Views the U.S.-ROK alliance as the primary obstacle to its strategic objectives on the Korean Peninsula and has used its nuclear and missile programs as an asymmetric deterrent against the alliance.
