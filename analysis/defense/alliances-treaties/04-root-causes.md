# Alliances and Partnerships: Root Causes

## Systemic Analysis

The challenges facing the U.S. alliance system -- particularly the persistent burden-sharing debate, the difficulty of adapting to new threats, and the growing tension between alliance cohesion and strategic agility -- are not new. They stem from the fundamental structure of the alliances themselves and the rational calculations of the nations within them. These root causes interact with each other, creating self-reinforcing dynamics that have proven resistant to decades of reform efforts. Understanding these structural drivers is essential to designing interventions that can actually change allied behavior rather than merely generating communique language.

## Root Cause 1: The Free-Rider Problem in Collective Defense

The core reason many allies have historically underinvested in defense is the "free-rider problem," a classic concept in collective action theory first articulated in the alliance context by economist Mancur Olson in his 1966 work *The Logic of Collective Action* and applied specifically to NATO by Olson and Richard Zeckhauser in "An Economic Theory of Alliances" (*Review of Economics and Statistics*, 1966).

### The Logic of Free-Riding

- **Collective Good**: The security and stability provided by the U.S. military presence is a "collective good." All allies benefit from it (e.g., the U.S. nuclear umbrella and control of sea lanes protect Germany as much as they protect the U.S.), regardless of their individual contributions.
- **Non-Excludability**: The U.S. cannot easily "exclude" a non-paying ally from the security benefits. The defense of NATO's eastern flank, for example, inherently protects all of Western Europe. A nuclear deterrent deters attacks on all allies, not just those who contribute to it.
- **Rational Incentive**: For a smaller, wealthy ally, the most economically rational choice is to underinvest in its own defense and rely on the security provided by the much larger hegemon (the United States). The money saved can be spent on domestic priorities like healthcare, education, or infrastructure, which are more politically popular with voters.
- **Asymmetric Benefits**: The United States derives unique benefits from the alliance system -- forward basing, intelligence sharing, overflight rights, diplomatic support, and legitimacy for military operations -- that make the cost of maintaining the alliance worthwhile even if allies underinvest. This means the U.S. has limited credibility when threatening to withdraw over burden-sharing disputes, because withdrawal would harm U.S. interests as well.
- **U.S. Calculation**: For the U.S., the cost of an ally free-riding is less than the cost of the alliance collapsing and the U.S. having to face a major threat alone or from a more distant position. Therefore, the U.S. has historically tolerated a degree of free-riding to maintain the alliance system, which it views as essential to its own security.

**Result**: This dynamic creates a permanent tension where the U.S. consistently pushes for allies to spend more, and allies consistently do just enough to keep the U.S. engaged, but not so much that it requires major domestic political sacrifices. The surge in European spending after 2022 shows that this calculation can change, but only in the face of a direct, existential threat -- and the durability of the spending increase once the immediate crisis recedes remains uncertain. Historical precedent is not encouraging: European defense spending also surged briefly after Russia's 2014 annexation of Crimea before plateauing well below the 2% target for most allies.

### Empirical Evidence

| Period | Catalyst | European NATO Defense Spending Trend | Durability |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1949-1989 | Cold War Soviet threat | High (3-5% of GDP common) | Sustained by permanent threat |
| 1990-2013 | "Peace dividend" era | Steady decline (many below 1.5%) | Durable complacency |
| 2014-2021 | Crimea annexation | Modest increase (few new members hit 2%) | Limited; most failed targets |
| 2022-present | Full-scale Ukraine invasion | Sharp increase (23 of 32 meet 2%) | Uncertain; depends on threat perception |

## Root Cause 2: Divergent Threat Perceptions

### Geography Is Destiny

An ally's perception of threats is heavily influenced by its geography, history, and economic relationships. This creates fundamentally different strategic priorities within the same alliance:

- **Poland and the Baltic States** see Russia as an immediate, existential threat. Poland has lived experience of Russian/Soviet occupation (1795-1918, 1939-1989). Its 4%+ defense spending reflects genuine threat perception, not U.S. pressure.
- **Spain, Italy, and Greece** are more concerned with instability and migration from North Africa and the Middle East. For these countries, the primary security challenge is not a Russian armored offensive but migrant flows, terrorism, and state failure in the Sahel and Libya.
- **Germany and France** occupy a middle position: aware of the Russian threat but also deeply invested in trade with China and historically oriented toward diplomacy and engagement rather than military confrontation. Germany's decades-long dependence on Russian natural gas (terminated only under duress after 2022) illustrates the tension between economic interests and security imperatives.
- **Japan and South Korea** are focused on China and North Korea respectively, with limited interest in European security. South Korea, despite being a NATO "partner" since 2022, has resisted U.S. pressure to provide ammunition directly to Ukraine, citing concerns about North Korean retaliation.
- **The United States** is unique in trying to be a global power focused on all these threats simultaneously, creating permanent resource allocation dilemmas.

- **Impact**: This divergence makes it difficult to achieve consensus on strategic priorities, resource allocation, and the nature of the primary threat. Many European allies are reluctant to commit resources to the Indo-Pacific, viewing China primarily as an economic partner, not a military threat. Meanwhile, Indo-Pacific allies see European security as a European problem. The result is that the alliance system can agree on the *existence* of multiple threats but struggles to agree on *prioritization* and *resource allocation* across theaters.

## Root Cause 3: Economic Interdependence with Adversaries

- **Allies' Economic Ties to Adversaries**: Many key U.S. allies have deep and lucrative economic relationships with U.S. rivals. For years, Germany was dependent on Russian natural gas (at its peak, Russia supplied approximately 55% of Germany's gas). Germany remains China's largest European trading partner, with bilateral trade exceeding EUR 250 billion annually. South Korea's largest export destination is China. Australia's economy depends heavily on iron ore and LNG exports to China.
- **The Economic Interdependence Trap**: These economic ties create a structural conflict of interest. It is difficult for an ally to take a hard-line security stance against a country that is also its largest trading partner or energy supplier. China has demonstrated willingness to weaponize economic interdependence, as when it imposed unofficial trade sanctions on Australia in 2020-21 after Canberra called for an independent investigation into COVID-19's origins, and when it restricted rare earth exports to Japan during a 2010 territorial dispute.
- **Coercion Vulnerability**: Allies with high economic dependence on adversaries are vulnerable to economic coercion, which limits their willingness to join U.S.-led security initiatives. This creates a vicious cycle: allies' economic vulnerability reduces their willingness to act, which reduces deterrence, which increases the adversary's incentive to use coercion.

## Root Cause 4: Institutional Inertia and Consensus Decision-Making

### Cold War Structures in a Post-Cold War World

- **Legacy Architecture**: NATO's command structures, decision-making processes, and procurement systems were designed for the slow, deliberate pace of Cold War deterrence -- standing guard against a single, known adversary across a well-defined geographic boundary (the Fulda Gap in Central Germany). These structures are poorly suited for the fast-moving, multi-domain, multi-theater challenges of the 21st century.
- **Consensus-Based Decisions**: NATO operates by consensus, meaning all 32 members must agree on major decisions. This ensures solidarity but can be incredibly slow and cumbersome. Turkey exploited this by blocking Finland and Sweden's accession for over a year, extracting concessions on Kurdish political organizations. Hungary has repeatedly blocked EU defense initiatives and slowed NATO statements on Ukraine. Any single member can effectively veto alliance action.
- **The Lowest Common Denominator Problem**: Consensus decision-making tends to produce outcomes that reflect the preferences of the least willing member, not the most. This means that NATO strategies and communiques are frequently watered down to accommodate outliers, reducing their strategic clarity and deterrent value.

### Nationalist Procurement and Industrial Fragmentation

- Despite calls for interoperability, most allied nations still prefer to buy military equipment from their own national defense industries to protect jobs, sustain industrial capacity, and maintain strategic autonomy. France procures the Rafale fighter rather than the F-35; Germany and France are jointly developing the Future Combat Air System (FCAS) rather than joining the F-35 consortium; South Korea has invested heavily in the indigenous KF-21 fighter.
- This produces a patchwork of different systems that cannot easily operate together, complicating logistics and reducing the alliance's combat effectiveness in a coalition fight. The European defense market is fragmented among dozens of national champions, producing quantities too small for economies of scale but large enough to preclude standardization.

## Root Cause 5: The "Indispensable Nation" Dilemma

- **Path Dependence**: Having led the global security system for over 70 years, the U.S. has created a powerful expectation among allies that it will always be there to take the lead and fill capability gaps. Allies have rationally adapted their force structures to complement U.S. capabilities rather than replicate them, creating deep dependencies. European allies, for example, have minimal strategic airlift, limited aerial refueling, and almost no autonomous ISR capability -- all domains where they rely on the U.S.
- **The Leadership Trap**: If the U.S. steps back to force allies to do more, it risks the alliance weakening or collapsing, which could create a power vacuum that adversaries exploit. If the U.S. leads too strongly, it reinforces the free-rider problem and allies' dependency. This "damned if you do, damned if you don't" dynamic is at the heart of the burden-sharing debate.
- **Credibility Paradox**: U.S. threats to reduce its commitment if allies do not spend more are only effective if allies believe the threats are credible. But carrying through on such threats (e.g., withdrawing forces from Europe) would damage U.S. strategic interests, making the threats inherently incredible. Allies understand this dynamic and calibrate their spending accordingly.

## Root Cause 6: Domestic Political Volatility in the United States

- **Bipartisan Consensus Under Strain**: The traditional bipartisan consensus supporting U.S. alliances has eroded. On the right, "America First" nationalism questions whether alliances serve U.S. interests or simply subsidize wealthy free-riders. On the left, progressive anti-interventionism questions whether the alliance system perpetuates militarism and an unsustainable global posture. The result is growing allied uncertainty about whether U.S. commitments will survive the next election cycle.
- **The Four-Year Cycle Problem**: Allies must make long-term defense investments (submarine programs take 20+ years; fighter aircraft last 30+ years) based on assumptions about U.S. reliability that extend far beyond any single presidential administration. Sharp swings in U.S. alliance policy between administrations -- from Obama's "pivot to Asia" to Trump's "America First" to Biden's "America is back" -- undermine allies' confidence in making those long-term bets.
- **Impact on Allied Hedging**: U.S. domestic political volatility is driving some allies to hedge their bets. France has accelerated its push for European strategic autonomy. Japan has expanded security partnerships with Australia, the UK, and India. South Korea has invested in indigenous nuclear-capable delivery systems. These hedging strategies are rational responses to uncertainty but can themselves weaken alliance cohesion.

## Interaction Effects

These root causes do not operate in isolation. They interact in ways that make the system's dysfunctions self-reinforcing:

- **Free-riding** is enabled by the **indispensable nation dilemma**, because allies know the U.S. cannot credibly threaten withdrawal.
- **Divergent threat perceptions** are amplified by **economic interdependence**, because allies whose economies depend on China are reluctant to prioritize the China threat.
- **Institutional inertia** prevents the **consensus-based system** from adapting to new threats quickly enough, while **nationalist procurement** undermines the interoperability that would make the alliance more effective.
- **U.S. domestic political volatility** exacerbates the **free-rider problem** by creating uncertainty about whether increased allied spending will be "enough" to satisfy the next administration, while simultaneously motivating **allied hedging** that weakens cohesion.

The result is a system that has proven remarkably durable in the face of existential threats (Soviet expansion, Russia's invasion of Ukraine) but struggles to adapt to slower-moving, more ambiguous challenges (China's gray-zone coercion, cyber threats, technology competition) that do not trigger the same galvanizing effect.
