# Alliances and Partnerships: Resources

This section provides a curated list of foundational treaties, official sources, academic literature, and expert analysis on the U.S. alliance system.

---

## Foundational Treaties and Documents

- **The North Atlantic Treaty (1949)**
    - The founding document of NATO. Article 5, the collective defense clause, is the heart of the treaty. Article 13 provides for withdrawal with one year's notice.
    - [https://www.nato.int/cps/en/natohq/official_texts_17120.htm](https://www.nato.int/cps/en/natohq/official_texts_17120.htm)

- **U.S.-Japan Treaty of Mutual Cooperation and Security (1960)**
    - The cornerstone of the U.S.-Japan alliance. Article V commits each party to "act to meet the common danger" in the event of an armed attack on either party in territories under Japan's administration.
    - [https://www.mofa.go.jp/region/n-america/us/q&a/ref/1.html](https://www.mofa.go.jp/region/n-america/us/q&a/ref/1.html)

- **U.S.-Republic of Korea Mutual Defense Treaty (1953)**
    - Commits the U.S. and South Korea to mutual defense. Article III states that each party recognizes that an armed attack on either in the Pacific area would be dangerous to its own peace and safety.
    - Available through the Avalon Project at Yale Law School

- **ANZUS Treaty (1951)**
    - The Security Treaty Between Australia, New Zealand, and the United States. New Zealand was effectively suspended from ANZUS obligations in 1986 after implementing its anti-nuclear policy.
    - Available through the Avalon Project at Yale Law School

- **U.S.-Philippines Mutual Defense Treaty (1951)**
    - Commits both nations to mutual defense. The treaty's applicability to disputes in the South China Sea has been a subject of ongoing diplomatic interpretation.
    - Available through the State Department Treaty Affairs office

- **AUKUS Joint Leaders' Statement (2021)**
    - The document announcing the creation of the AUKUS partnership, including Pillar I (nuclear submarine cooperation) and Pillar II (advanced technology collaboration).
    - [https://www.whitehouse.gov/briefing-room/statements-releases/2021/09/15/joint-leaders-statement-on-aukus/](https://www.whitehouse.gov/briefing-room/statements-releases/2021/09/15/joint-leaders-statement-on-aukus/)

- **NATO 2022 Strategic Concept**
    - Adopted at the Madrid Summit, this document defines NATO's strategic direction, identifies Russia as the "most significant and direct threat," and addresses China for the first time.
    - [https://www.nato.int/cps/en/natohq/topics_210907.htm](https://www.nato.int/cps/en/natohq/topics_210907.htm)

- **Washington Declaration (2023)**
    - U.S.-ROK joint statement establishing the Nuclear Consultative Group and strengthening extended deterrence commitments on the Korean Peninsula.
    - Available through the White House briefing room

- **Camp David Principles (2023)**
    - U.S.-Japan-ROK trilateral statement establishing the framework for annual leader-level meetings, real-time missile warning data sharing, and trilateral exercises.
    - Available through the White House briefing room

## Primary Government Sources

- **NATO Public Diplomacy Division**
    - Publishes annual reports with detailed data on member defense spending (by country, year, and category), as well as official communiques from summits and ministerial meetings.
    - [https://www.nato.int/](https://www.nato.int/)
    - Key annual publication: *Defence Expenditure of NATO Countries*

- **U.S. Department of State, Bureau of Political-Military Affairs**
    - Manages U.S. security agreements, status of forces agreements, and foreign military sales.
    - [https://www.state.gov/bureaus-offices/under-secretary-for-arms-control-and-international-security-affairs/bureau-of-political-military-affairs/](https://www.state.gov/bureaus-offices/under-secretary-for-arms-control-and-international-security-affairs/bureau-of-political-military-affairs/)

- **Defense Security Cooperation Agency (DSCA)**
    - Manages the Foreign Military Sales (FMS) program and publishes data on arms sales to allies and partners.
    - [https://www.dsca.mil/](https://www.dsca.mil/)

- **Congressional Research Service (CRS)**
    - Provides numerous, detailed, and non-partisan reports on NATO, burden-sharing, AUKUS, and U.S. strategy in Asia. Key reports include:
    - *NATO: Key Issues for the 118th Congress* (updated regularly)
    - *U.S.-Japan Alliance* (updated regularly)
    - *AUKUS and Indo-Pacific Security* (updated regularly)
    - *Status of Forces Agreements: What They Are, and Issues for Congress*
    - *Taiwan: Defense and Military Issues*

- **Government Accountability Office (GAO)**
    - Publishes assessments of alliance-related programs, including FMS delivery timelines, AUKUS implementation, and NATO burden-sharing.

- **Department of Defense**
    - *National Defense Strategy* (most recent: 2022)
    - *Indo-Pacific Strategy* (2022)
    - Annual *Report to Congress on Allied Contributions to the Common Defense* (Section 1003 report)

## Think Tanks and Expert Analysis

- **Center for Strategic and International Studies (CSIS)**: The Europe, Russia, and Eurasia Program (directed by Max Bergmann) and the Asia Program (directed by Victor Cha) provide extensive analysis on U.S. alliances. CSIS produces the annual *Global Forecast* and numerous policy briefs on alliance modernization.
    - [https://www.csis.org/](https://www.csis.org/)

- **Center for a New American Security (CNAS)**: Focuses on defense and security policy with strong coverage of Indo-Pacific alliances, technology competition, and defense strategy. Notable scholars include Stacie Pettyjohn, Lisa Curtis, and Andrea Kendall-Taylor.
    - [https://www.cnas.org/](https://www.cnas.org/)

- **Council on Foreign Relations (CFR)**: Publishes backgrounders, task force reports, and *Foreign Affairs* articles on NATO, the Indo-Pacific, and U.S. security commitments. CFR's Contingency Planning Memoranda on Taiwan and the Korean Peninsula are particularly relevant.
    - [https://www.cfr.org/](https://www.cfr.org/)

- **International Institute for Strategic Studies (IISS)**: Publishes *The Military Balance* (the definitive annual reference on the armed forces and defense spending of every country), *Survival* journal, and the annual *Strategic Survey*. Essential for comparative alliance capability data.
    - [https://www.iiss.org/](https://www.iiss.org/)

- **RAND Corporation**: Produces detailed, often classified studies on alliance war planning, force posture, and burden-sharing, funded by the DoD and allied governments. Publicly available reports include analyses of NATO's eastern flank defense, Taiwan contingency planning, and AUKUS implementation.
    - [https://www.rand.org/](https://www.rand.org/)

- **Atlantic Council**: Focuses heavily on transatlantic relations and NATO policy. The Scowcroft Center for Strategy and Security produces regular analyses of NATO posture, European defense, and transatlantic burden-sharing.
    - [https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/)

- **Brookings Institution**: The Center on the United States and Europe and the East Asia Policy Studies program provide analysis bridging the transatlantic and Indo-Pacific alliance systems. Key scholars include Michael O'Hanlon, Thomas Wright, and Mireya Solis.
    - [https://www.brookings.edu/](https://www.brookings.edu/)

- **Quincy Institute for Responsible Statecraft**: Provides the leading restraint-oriented perspective on U.S. alliances, arguing for reduced military commitments and greater diplomatic engagement.
    - [https://quincyinst.org/](https://quincyinst.org/)

- **Carnegie Endowment for International Peace**: Provides analysis on alliance dynamics with particular strength in U.S.-India relations, nuclear policy, and great power competition.
    - [https://carnegieendowment.org/](https://carnegieendowment.org/)

## Academic Literature

- Olson, Mancur, and Richard Zeckhauser. "An Economic Theory of Alliances." *Review of Economics and Statistics* 48, no. 3 (1966): 266-279. *The foundational economic analysis of burden-sharing in alliances.*
- Snyder, Glenn H. *Alliance Politics*. Ithaca: Cornell University Press, 1997. *The classic theoretical treatment of alliance dynamics, including the "abandonment-entrapment" dilemma.*
- Walt, Stephen M. *The Origins of Alliances*. Ithaca: Cornell University Press, 1987. *Argues that states form alliances to balance against threats, not simply against power.*
- Beckley, Michael. "The Myth of Entangling Alliances." *International Security* 39, no. 4 (2015): 7-48. *Challenges the claim that alliances drag the U.S. into unnecessary wars.*
- Brooks, Stephen G., and William C. Wohlforth. *America Abroad: The United States' Global Role in the 21st Century*. Oxford: Oxford University Press, 2016. *Defends the U.S. alliance system as essential to American security and prosperity.*
- Posen, Barry R. *Restraint: A New Foundation for U.S. Grand Strategy*. Ithaca: Cornell University Press, 2014. *The most comprehensive academic case for reducing U.S. alliance commitments.*
- Samuels, Richard J. *Securing Japan: Tokyo's Grand Strategy and the Future of East Asia*. Ithaca: Cornell University Press, 2007. *Analyzes Japan's strategic evolution and the U.S.-Japan alliance.*
- Sarotte, Mary Elise. *Not One Inch: America, Russia, and the Making of Post-Cold War Stalemate*. New Haven: Yale University Press, 2021. *Definitive history of NATO enlargement and the promises (or lack thereof) made to Russia.*
- Shifrinson, Joshua R. Itzkowitz. *Rising Titans, Falling Giants: How Great Powers Exploit Power Shifts*. Ithaca: Cornell University Press, 2018. *Analyzes how great powers manage alliance transitions during power shifts.*

## Data Sources

- **NATO Defence Expenditure Data**: Annual datasets published by NATO's Public Diplomacy Division, providing defense spending by country as a percentage of GDP and in absolute terms. Available at nato.int.
- **IISS Military Balance**: Annual publication with detailed data on force structure, equipment inventories, and defense budgets for every country. The definitive reference for comparing allied military capabilities.
- **Stockholm International Peace Research Institute (SIPRI)**: Publishes data on global military expenditure, arms transfers, and arms production. The SIPRI Arms Transfers Database tracks weapons exports and imports among allied nations.
    - [https://www.sipri.org/](https://www.sipri.org/)
- **World Bank Development Indicators**: GDP data used to calculate defense spending as a percentage of GDP for burden-sharing analysis.
