# Alliances and Partnerships: Overview

## Overview

The United States' global network of alliances is its most significant asymmetric advantage in an era of renewed great power competition. Unlike rivals such as China and Russia, the U.S. is bound by formal treaties and informal partnerships to dozens of nations, creating a collective security architecture that has underpinned global stability for over 70 years. The 2022 National Defense Strategy identifies strengthening these alliances as a central line of effort, essential for deterring aggression and prevailing in conflict.

This alliance system is historically unprecedented. Never before has a single power maintained mutual defense commitments spanning two oceans and four continents simultaneously. The network encompasses NATO's 32-member collective defense pact in Europe, five bilateral mutual defense treaties in the Indo-Pacific, and a growing constellation of minilateral partnerships -- AUKUS, the Quad, I2U2, and trilateral arrangements -- designed to address specific strategic challenges. Together, the United States and its formal treaty allies account for roughly 60% of global GDP and over 60% of global military spending (IISS, *The Military Balance*, 2024). No rival power commands anything comparable: China's formal alliance extends only to North Korea under the 1961 Sino-North Korean Treaty of Friendship, while Russia's Collective Security Treaty Organization (CSTO) has proven largely dysfunctional, as demonstrated by Armenia's effective withdrawal in 2023.

However, this system faces profound challenges. Persistent "burden-sharing" debates, particularly within NATO, raise questions about equity and commitment. Alliances forged during the Cold War must adapt to modern threats that transcend geography, such as cyber attacks, space warfare, and technological competition in AI and autonomy. Furthermore, the U.S. and its allies face the strategic dilemma of confronting an acute threat from Russia in Europe while simultaneously preparing for the long-term pacing challenge from China in the Indo-Pacific, straining resources and strategic focus. Domestic political volatility in the United States -- exemplified by sharp swings in alliance rhetoric between the Obama, Trump, and Biden administrations -- has introduced a new variable: allied uncertainty about the reliability of the American security guarantee itself.

## Scope

This analysis examines the primary components of the U.S. alliance system:

- **Transatlantic Alliance**: The North Atlantic Treaty Organization (NATO) and its role in European security and collective defense against Russia, including its 2022 Strategic Concept, new force model, and the implications of Finnish and Swedish accession.
- **Indo-Pacific Alliances**: The "hub-and-spokes" system of bilateral mutual defense treaties with Japan, South Korea, Australia, the Philippines, and Thailand, and the ongoing effort to weave these bilateral ties into a more networked architecture.
- **Minilateral Security Partnerships**: Modern, flexible groupings focused on specific challenges, including AUKUS (technology sharing and nuclear submarine cooperation), the Quadrilateral Security Dialogue (the Quad), the U.S.-Japan-ROK trilateral, and I2U2.
- **Burden-Sharing**: The debate over equitable contributions to collective defense, centered on NATO's 2% of GDP defense spending guideline but extending to capability commitments, host-nation support, and non-military contributions.
- **Alliance Modernization**: Efforts to adapt alliances to new domains like cyber and space, new challenges like supply chain security and technological competition, and the integration of European and Indo-Pacific alliance networks.
- **Treaty Mechanisms**: The constitutional and legal processes governing alliance commitments, including Senate advice and consent under Article II, status of forces agreements (SOFAs), treaty withdrawal procedures, and the role of executive agreements.

## Core Tensions and Tradeoffs

- **Burden-Sharing vs. U.S. Influence**: Pressuring allies to spend more on defense can increase their capability but may also lead them to demand more say in strategy, potentially constraining U.S. action. France's pursuit of "strategic autonomy" for Europe illustrates this dynamic.
- **Europe vs. Asia Focus**: Allocating U.S. military assets (e.g., naval ships, ISR platforms) to deter Russia in Europe directly reduces their availability to deter China in the Indo-Pacific. The Navy's 2024 force structure assessment identified a shortfall of approximately 60 ships relative to the fleet needed for simultaneous deterrence in both theaters.
- **Alliance Cohesion vs. Strategic Clarity**: Maintaining consensus among 32 NATO allies can lead to slower decision-making and watered-down strategies compared to more agile, unilateral action. Turkey's blocking of Finland and Sweden's NATO accession for over a year demonstrated this friction.
- **Enlargement vs. Deepening**: Should alliances like NATO focus on adding new members (Ukraine, Georgia), or on improving the capabilities and interoperability of existing members? The debate over Ukraine's NATO membership pathway encapsulates this tension.
- **Technology Sharing vs. Security**: Sharing advanced technology (like nuclear submarine propulsion under AUKUS Pillar I) strengthens allies but increases the risk of technology transfer to adversaries through espionage or leaks. The International Traffic in Arms Regulations (ITAR) regime reflects this tension.
- **Extended Deterrence Credibility**: Would the United States truly risk a nuclear exchange with Russia or China to defend an ally? The credibility of the U.S. nuclear umbrella is foundational to the alliance system, yet it remains an inherently untestable proposition. The 2023 Washington Declaration with South Korea was designed in part to address Seoul's doubts on this question.

## Key Questions

1. Is the 2% of GDP spending target the right metric for measuring allied contributions, or should the focus be on specific capabilities, readiness levels, and non-military contributions?
2. How can the U.S. and its allies deter both Russia and China simultaneously without overstretching their forces?
3. Are Cold War-era alliance structures, designed for a single threat, adaptable enough for the complex, multi-domain challenges of the 21st century?
4. How can the U.S. better integrate its European and Indo-Pacific alliance systems to address the global challenge posed by China?
5. What institutional reforms are needed to accelerate technology sharing and joint development among allies while protecting sensitive capabilities?
6. How should the alliance system account for the growing risk that U.S. domestic political volatility could undermine allied confidence in American commitments?

## Vision of Success

A successful alliance strategy would feature a global network of capable, interoperable, and resilient allies who:

- **Take primary responsibility** for their own regional defense, supported by unique U.S. enablers such as nuclear deterrence, intelligence, long-range strike, and space-based capabilities.
- **Actively contribute** to addressing global security challenges, from maritime security and cyber defense to counter-proliferation and climate-related security threats.
- **Seamlessly integrate** their military forces and defense industrial bases with the United States through common standards, shared platforms, interoperable communications, and joint development programs.
- **Present a united front** to deter aggression from autocratic rivals, backed by credible force posture, rapid decision-making, and demonstrated political will.
- **Build resilience** against hybrid threats, including economic coercion, supply chain disruption, disinformation, and critical infrastructure attacks, by coordinating national resilience policies across the alliance.
