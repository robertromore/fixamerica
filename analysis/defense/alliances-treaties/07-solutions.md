# Alliances and Partnerships: Solutions

To adapt the U.S. alliance system for an era of great power competition, reforms must focus on improving allied contributions, adapting to new threats, better integrating the transatlantic and Indo-Pacific security architectures, and building institutional resilience against the domestic political volatility that increasingly threatens alliance credibility.

---

## 1. Reform Burden-Sharing

### A. Shift from Burden-Sharing to "Responsibility-Sharing"

- **Description**: The 2% of GDP defense spending target is a blunt and often misleading metric. It measures inputs (money spent) rather than outputs (capability delivered). A country can meet 2% while spending inefficiently on bloated personnel costs or domestic pork-barrel programs. The focus should shift to a more sophisticated model where allies commit to providing specific, high-end capabilities that the alliance needs, rather than just hitting an arbitrary spending number.
- **Implementation**: NATO should conduct a comprehensive capabilities review to identify critical shortfalls, building on the NATO Defence Planning Process (NDPP). The review would identify the top 20 capability gaps (e.g., integrated air and missile defense, long-range precision fires, strategic airlift, munitions stockpiles, logistics and sustainment) and assign each ally responsibility for filling specific gaps based on their economic capacity and comparative advantages. Allies would then publicly commit to filling these gaps as their primary contribution, a model sometimes called "NATO Force Goals 2.0." For example:
    - **Germany**: Provide three fully equipped heavy armored divisions capable of deploying to the eastern flank within 30 days
    - **Italy**: Provide integrated air and missile defense for the southern flank and Mediterranean maritime patrol
    - **Canada**: Provide Arctic surveillance, domain awareness, and a deployable brigade for NATO's northern flank
    - **Japan** (as a NATO partner): Provide ISR and anti-submarine warfare capabilities in the Western Pacific that complement U.S. forces
- **Precedent**: The NATO Force Model agreed at the 2022 Madrid Summit moved in this direction by specifying force readiness tiers (300,000 at high readiness in 30 days; 200,000 within 30-180 days; 500,000+ within 180 days). The responsibility-sharing model would extend this approach to specific capability types.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Aligns allied defense investments with actual strategic needs. Creates more accountability than a simple spending target. Allows allies to specialize in areas where they have a comparative advantage, improving overall alliance efficiency.

### B. Link U.S. Posture to Allied Contributions

- **Description**: Make the size and nature of the U.S. military presence in a region explicitly conditional on the defense efforts of the allies in that region, using positive incentives rather than threats of withdrawal.
- **Implementation**: In Europe, the U.S. could announce that for every new, fully equipped heavy brigade that European allies field, the U.S. will match it with a unique enabling capability (e.g., a squadron of F-35s, a strategic ISR platform, a THAAD battery). This creates a "matching" dynamic: allies invest in conventional mass, and the U.S. provides the high-end enablers that multiply the value of allied forces. Conversely, if allies fail to meet their capability commitments, the U.S. would shift enabling assets to theaters where allies are contributing more.
- **Indo-Pacific Application**: Japan's doubling of defense spending could be "matched" with deeper access to U.S. ISR data, accelerated F-35 deliveries, and expanded joint command arrangements. South Korea's investment in indigenous capabilities could be rewarded with enhanced nuclear consultation through the Nuclear Consultative Group.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Creates a direct, transparent link between allied effort and U.S. commitment, moving beyond threats of withdrawal to a more constructive dynamic that rewards investment.

### C. Establish a Common Allied Munitions Fund

- **Description**: The Ukraine conflict revealed that NATO allies collectively lack the munitions stockpiles needed for a sustained high-intensity conflict. Create a pooled fund to procure, store, and distribute critical munitions across the alliance.
- **Implementation**: NATO members would contribute to a common fund (assessed on a GDP-weighted basis) to establish forward-positioned stockpiles of key munitions: 155mm artillery shells, GMLRS rockets, air-to-air missiles, precision-guided bombs, and anti-ship missiles. The stockpiles would be held at NATO logistics bases in Poland, Romania, Japan, and Guam and would be available to any contributing ally in a crisis.
- **Precedent**: NATO already operates the NATO Support and Procurement Agency (NSPA), which manages common procurement for some items. The common munitions fund would expand this model dramatically.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Addresses one of the most critical gaps exposed by the Ukraine war. Creates immediate, tangible military capability. Demonstrates allied resolve to potential adversaries by showing that the alliance has the staying power for a sustained conflict.

## 2. Adapt Alliances for 21st Century Threats

### A. Integrate Cyber and Space as Core Alliance Missions

- **Description**: Fully integrate the cyber and space domains into alliance planning, exercises, and collective defense commitments. Currently, allied cyber and space activities are largely national, with limited coordination.
- **Implementation**:
    - **NATO**: Adopt a formal policy that a major cyber attack on a member state's critical infrastructure (energy grids, financial systems, communications networks) could trigger an Article 5 collective defense response. Define thresholds based on the scale of damage and attribution confidence. Establish a standing NATO Cyber Operations Centre at SHAPE with authority to coordinate offensive and defensive cyber operations. Conduct annual exercises (building on "Cyber Coalition" and "Locked Shields," hosted by the NATO Cooperative Cyber Defence Centre of Excellence in Tallinn) that simulate combined cyber-kinetic scenarios.
    - **Space**: Establish a shared space situational awareness network among NATO and Indo-Pacific allies, building on the U.S. Space Surveillance Network. Pool allied satellite imagery, communications, and PNT (positioning, navigation, timing) capabilities to reduce dependence on a small number of U.S. satellites that are vulnerable to Chinese and Russian anti-satellite weapons.
    - **AUKUS**: Fully fund and accelerate AUKUS Pillar II, focusing on joint development of AI, quantum computing, and cyber capabilities. Use this as a model for technology sharing with other trusted allies, potentially expanding Pillar II collaboration to Japan and South Korea.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Raises the cost of aggression in non-physical domains. Pools allied resources to compete more effectively with China and Russia in emerging technologies. Closes a critical gap in the collective defense framework.

### B. Build a Network of Allied Resilience

- **Description**: Expand the focus of alliances beyond traditional military threats to include "hybrid" threats like supply chain disruption, critical infrastructure vulnerabilities, economic coercion, and disinformation.
- **Implementation**: Create a "NATO Resilience Council" or equivalent body with the following mandates:
    - **Supply Chain Security**: Map critical supply chain dependencies on adversary nations (rare earths, pharmaceuticals, semiconductors) and coordinate allied investment to diversify sources. Build on the EU's Critical Raw Materials Act and the U.S. CHIPS and Science Act.
    - **Undersea Infrastructure Protection**: Coordinate allied naval and intelligence assets to protect the 400+ undersea cables that carry 97% of intercontinental data traffic, following the suspected sabotage of the Nord Stream pipelines (September 2022) and Baltic cable damage (2023).
    - **Counter-Disinformation**: Establish an allied center for countering foreign information manipulation, building on NATO's StratCom Centre of Excellence in Riga and the EU's East StratCom Task Force. Share best practices for identifying and countering state-sponsored disinformation campaigns.
    - **Energy Security**: Coordinate allied strategies to reduce energy dependence on adversary nations, building on the painful lessons of Europe's Russian gas dependency.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Makes allied societies less vulnerable to the coercive, non-military tactics favored by Russia and China. Addresses the "gray zone" between peace and war where adversaries currently operate with near-impunity.

### C. Streamline Alliance Decision-Making

- **Description**: NATO's consensus-based decision-making, while politically important for legitimacy, is too slow for the speed of modern crises. Reform the process to enable faster action without abandoning the consensus principle for fundamental decisions.
- **Implementation**:
    - **Pre-authorized Response Plans**: For specific, pre-identified scenarios (e.g., a cyber attack on Baltic critical infrastructure, a Chinese blockade of a Philippine-occupied feature in the South China Sea), pre-negotiate and pre-authorize military response options that SACEUR or INDOPACOM can execute immediately without waiting for a full North Atlantic Council vote.
    - **"Coalition of the Willing" within NATO**: For non-Article 5 operations, allow subsets of allies to act under the NATO flag without requiring full consensus, similar to the "enhanced cooperation" mechanism in EU law.
    - **Qualified Majority Voting for Administrative Decisions**: Reserve consensus for fundamental decisions (Article 5 invocation, new members, strategic concepts) but allow qualified majority voting for administrative, budgetary, and procedural matters.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Enables the alliance to respond to fast-moving crises without the delays imposed by consensus decision-making among 32 members. Reduces the ability of a single member to block alliance action.

## 3. Integrate Transatlantic and Indo-Pacific Alliances

### A. Create an "Atlantic-Pacific Council"

- **Description**: Establish a formal dialogue between NATO and key U.S. allies in the Indo-Pacific (Japan, South Korea, Australia, New Zealand), breaking down the artificial separation between the two alliance systems.
- **Implementation**: The council would meet annually at the foreign and defense minister level (a "2+2+2" format) to coordinate policy on shared challenges, particularly those posed by China's global activities. Working groups would focus on:
    - Technology export controls and investment screening (building on the G7 framework)
    - Maritime security and freedom of navigation operations
    - Sanctions coordination and economic statecraft
    - Defense industrial cooperation and co-production
- **Precedent**: NATO has already begun this process informally by inviting the leaders of Japan, South Korea, Australia, and New Zealand to NATO summits since 2022 (the "AP4" format). The Atlantic-Pacific Council would formalize and deepen this engagement.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Breaks down the artificial divide between the European and Asian theaters. Creates a united front of democracies to counter global pressure from autocratic states. Enables coordinated action on cross-theater challenges like China's global infrastructure lending or Russian energy coercion.

### B. Reform and Streamline Foreign Military Sales (FMS)

- **Description**: The current FMS process for selling U.S. military equipment to allies is notoriously slow and bureaucratic. Cases can take years to process, with layers of approval from the State Department, Defense Security Cooperation Agency (DSCA), and Congress (via the Foreign Military Sales Notification process). This delays the delivery of critical capabilities to allies precisely when deterrence requires speed.
- **Implementation**:
    - **Create a "Tier 1" Ally Expedited Process**: Designate a group of trusted allies (e.g., UK, Australia, Japan, South Korea, key NATO members) eligible for an expedited FMS process with presumptive approval for most defensive systems and reduced congressional notification timelines.
    - **Reform ITAR for Allies**: Expand the ITAR exemptions available to AUKUS nations to a broader set of trusted allies, reducing the bureaucratic barriers to technology sharing. The 2023 NDAA included AUKUS-specific ITAR reforms; extend these to the broader allied network.
    - **Co-Production and Licensing**: Encourage allies to produce U.S.-designed weapons systems domestically under license, reducing the burden on U.S. production lines. Japan's licensed production of F-35 components and South Korea's production of 155mm artillery ammunition for potential U.S. procurement illustrate this model.
    - **Prioritize Taiwan**: Taiwan's FMS backlog (estimated at over $19 billion in approved but undelivered arms as of 2024) represents a critical deterrence gap. Create a dedicated Taiwan Expedited Delivery Office within DSCA to accelerate deliveries.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Strengthens allied deterrence by reducing the timeline for acquiring key capabilities. Reduces the burden on U.S. forces by accelerating allied self-defense capability. Creates stronger defense industrial ties that reinforce political alliance commitments.

### C. Institutionalize Trilateral and Minilateral Cooperation

- **Description**: Formalize the growing web of trilateral and minilateral partnerships to make them more resilient to political changes in any single member state.
- **Implementation**:
    - **U.S.-Japan-ROK**: Establish a permanent trilateral secretariat to institutionalize the Camp David commitments, ensuring continuity beyond any single administration in Washington, Tokyo, or Seoul. Include a standing military coordination mechanism for missile defense and ISR sharing.
    - **AUKUS**: Establish an AUKUS Coordination Office with permanent staff from all three nations, similar to NATO's International Staff. This would manage the complex Pillar I submarine program and coordinate Pillar II technology development.
    - **Quad**: Move beyond leader-level summitry to establish working-level coordination mechanisms on maritime domain awareness, technology standards, and supply chain resilience. Expand the IPMDA initiative to include more Indo-Pacific partners.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Makes minilateral partnerships more durable and less vulnerable to political disruption. Creates institutional inertia that sustains cooperation even when political leaders change.

## 4. Insulate Alliances from Domestic Political Volatility

### A. Codify Alliance Commitments in Law

- **Description**: Reduce the vulnerability of alliance commitments to shifts in presidential rhetoric by strengthening the legislative foundation of key alliances.
- **Implementation**:
    - **NATO Commitment Act**: Building on the bipartisan NATO Support Act passed by the Senate in 2024 (which declared that the President cannot unilaterally withdraw from NATO without Senate consent or an Act of Congress), codify this principle in permanent law and extend it to other treaty alliances.
    - **Pacific Deterrence Initiative (PDI) Expansion**: Increase and multi-year fund the PDI to provide predictable, long-term resourcing for Indo-Pacific alliance infrastructure, reducing vulnerability to annual appropriations politics.
    - **Mandate Allied Consultation**: Require the executive branch to formally consult with treaty allies before making major changes to U.S. force posture in their region, institutionalizing a practice that has been inconsistently followed.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Provides legal guardrails against unilateral presidential decisions to weaken or abandon alliances. Reassures allies that U.S. commitments have a durable legislative foundation, not just executive rhetoric.
