# Arms Sales: Root Causes

## Systemic Analysis

The United States' position as the world's leading arms exporter is not accidental. It is the result of a powerful confluence of strategic, economic, and political drivers that create a self-reinforcing system with immense institutional momentum. Each actor within the system -- the State Department, the Pentagon, Congress, defense contractors, and foreign customers -- behaves rationally within the incentive structure they face, yet the collective outcome is a system that tends to favor the volume and speed of sales over careful assessment of their consequences.

Understanding these root causes is essential to designing reforms that can change outcomes rather than merely rearranging bureaucratic processes.

---

## Root Cause 1: Strategic Drivers -- Arms Sales as Statecraft

From the U.S. government's perspective, arms sales are a powerful and flexible tool of foreign policy that serves multiple strategic objectives simultaneously.

### 1a. Building Partner Capacity

- **The Core Rationale**: The primary strategic goal is to enable allies and partners to provide for their own defense. By equipping partners, the U.S. reduces the likelihood that it will have to intervene directly with its own forces in a crisis. This is the logic of "helping partners defend themselves" and has been a core principle of U.S. security cooperation since the Nixon Doctrine.

- **Deterrence**: Arming frontline states like Taiwan or Poland with "asymmetric" capabilities (e.g., anti-ship and anti-air missiles, HIMARS, Javelin ATGMs) is intended to raise the cost of aggression for potential adversaries like China and Russia, thereby strengthening deterrence without requiring permanent forward deployment of large U.S. forces.

- **Burden-Sharing**: Arms sales serve the domestic political goal of demonstrating that allies are investing in their own defense, addressing perennial complaints about "free riding." When Japan purchases $23 billion in F-35s and Aegis systems, it reduces pressure on the U.S. to defend Japan unilaterally.

- **Shaping the Operational Environment**: By pre-positioning interoperable equipment in partner nations, the U.S. creates a more favorable operational environment for any future contingency. Allies operating U.S. systems can share ammunition, spare parts, and intelligence feeds from the first day of a crisis.

### 1b. Ensuring Interoperability

- **Fighting Together**: When allies operate the same equipment as U.S. forces (e.g., F-35 fighters, Aegis missile defense, Link 16 data networks), it allows for seamless integration in joint operations. They can share data, munitions, and spare parts, acting as a true force multiplier.

- **NATO Standards**: NATO's interoperability standards are built largely around U.S. equipment and communications protocols. Countries that operate non-U.S. systems (e.g., Turkey's S-400) face integration challenges that reduce their value as alliance partners.

- **Locking in Relationships**: Selling a major weapon system is not a one-time transaction. It is the start of a multi-decade relationship involving training, maintenance, upgrades, spare parts, and eventual replacement.
    - An F-35 sale commits the buyer to 30-40 years of engagement with U.S. industry and military
    - This "lock-in" effect binds the partner nation to the U.S. security ecosystem
    - It makes it operationally difficult and prohibitively expensive to pivot to a rival supplier
    - Lockheed Martin estimates that each F-35 customer creates $10+ billion in follow-on support revenue over the aircraft's lifecycle

- **The "Ecosystem" Effect**: As more allies adopt U.S. systems, the value of the U.S. defense ecosystem increases for every member. A country that operates the F-35 gains access to:
    - The global F-35 logistics network
    - Shared threat libraries and electronic warfare data
    - Software upgrades funded collectively by all partner nations
    - This network effect creates a powerful incentive for new customers to choose U.S. systems

### 1c. Gaining Foreign Policy Influence

- **Access and Leverage**: A major arms sale relationship provides U.S. officials with regular access to a partner nation's senior military and political leadership. This access can be used to influence the partner's policy on a wide range of issues beyond defense, from trade to human rights to voting at the United Nations.

- **Signaling Commitment**: A decision to sell a sensitive weapon system is a powerful signal of U.S. commitment to a partner's security, reassuring allies and warning adversaries.
    - The sale of F-16V Block 70 fighters to Taiwan signals U.S. commitment to Taiwan's defense
    - The sale of THAAD to Saudi Arabia signals commitment to Gulf security
    - Conversely, the decision to withhold weapons (as with the 2016 PGM suspension to Saudi Arabia) signals displeasure

- **Conditionality as Leverage**: The implicit or explicit threat of withholding arms provides the U.S. with leverage over partner behavior.
    - The Obama administration's 2016 suspension of precision-guided munition sales to Saudi Arabia was intended to pressure Riyadh to reduce civilian casualties in Yemen
    - The Trump administration reversed the suspension within months, illustrating the fragility of conditionality

## Root Cause 2: Economic Drivers -- The Business of Arms

The economic incentives for arms exports are just as powerful as the strategic ones, and in practice the two are deeply intertwined.

### 2a. Sustaining the Defense Industrial Base

- **Economies of Scale**: The U.S. military procures weapon systems in quantities that are often too small to be cost-efficient. Foreign sales extend production runs, lowering the per-unit cost for the DoD.
    - The F-35 program illustrates this dynamic: international orders for over 900 F-35s from 18 partner and customer nations have been essential to driving down the per-unit flyaway cost from over $100 million to approximately $80 million
    - Each additional export order reduces the cost for the U.S. military, creating a powerful incentive for the Pentagon to support foreign sales

- **Production Line Continuity**: For some systems, foreign orders are the only thing keeping the production line open.
    - The F-15 line at Boeing's St. Louis facility was sustained primarily by Saudi and Qatari orders after the U.S. Air Force stopped purchasing new F-15s in 2001 (before the F-15EX revival)
    - Without these exports, the production line would have closed and the skilled workforce would have dispersed
    - The M1 Abrams tank production line in Lima, Ohio has similarly benefited from international orders

- **Funding R&D**: A portion of the revenue from foreign sales is often reinvested by defense companies into R&D for the next generation of weapons.
    - International cooperative development programs, like the F-35 partnership with eight original development partners, share R&D costs across multiple nations
    - This model is being extended through AUKUS, where Australia is expected to contribute significantly to AI and quantum research that benefits the U.S. defense technology base

### 2b. Domestic Economic and Political Benefits

- **Jobs**: The defense industry is a major employer of high-skilled, high-wage labor.
    - Arms exports are estimated to support over a million U.S. jobs across manufacturing, engineering, and services
    - Defense export jobs are concentrated in politically important states: Texas, California, Connecticut, Virginia, Missouri, and Alabama
    - The geographic distribution of export-related jobs creates a political constituency in favor of sales in nearly every congressional district

- **Trade Balance**: Arms exports are a significant contributor to the U.S. balance of trade.
    - In an era of persistent trade deficits, defense exports represent one of the few sectors where the U.S. consistently runs a large surplus
    - The Commerce Department and U.S. Trade Representative actively promote defense exports as part of broader trade policy

- **Congressional Incentives**: Because defense export contracts support jobs in specific districts, members of Congress have strong incentives to advocate for sales regardless of their foreign policy implications.
    - A member whose district hosts an F-35 supplier has a personal political interest in maximizing F-35 exports
    - This dynamic reinforces the "Iron Triangle" of defense contractors, DoD, and Congress

### 2c. The "Offset" Dynamic

Many foreign customers require "offsets" as a condition of major arms purchases:

- Offsets are agreements by the U.S. contractor to invest in the buyer's economy, transfer technology, or create local production capacity
- Common offset arrangements include technology licensing, co-production agreements, and mandatory subcontracting to local firms
- While offsets can build partner capacity, they also transfer manufacturing know-how to potential future competitors
- South Korea's purchase of F-35s included offsets that supported Korean aerospace industry development, which now competes with U.S. firms in some export markets
- The UAE and Saudi Arabia have used offset requirements to build domestic defense industries (EDGE Group in the UAE, SAMI in Saudi Arabia) that aim to reduce dependence on imports

## Root Cause 3: The Presumption of Approval

The arms sales process has a built-in structural bias toward approving sales, driven by the convergence of strategic, economic, and political interests.

### The "Iron Triangle" of Exports

Similar to domestic procurement, arms exports are driven by a powerful triangle of interests:

1. **Defense contractors** want to sell products to keep production lines open and increase revenue
2. **The DoD** wants allies to have interoperable equipment and wants to lower its own unit costs through longer production runs
3. **Congress** wants to support high-wage jobs in their districts and states

When all three legs of the triangle support a sale, the burden of proof falls on those who oppose it. The default position is approval; opponents must demonstrate compelling reasons to deny.

### Human Rights as an Afterthought

Within the interagency process, the Bureau of Democracy, Human Rights, and Labor (DRL) has the weakest institutional position:

- DRL can raise objections, but it lacks the authority to block a sale
- Strategic and economic advocates within the State Department, the Pentagon, and the National Security Council can overrule human rights objections -- and they routinely do
- A former DRL official described the dynamic: "The question is never 'should we sell?' It's always 'how can we structure the sale to manage the risk?'"
- DRL has only a handful of personnel assigned to arms sales review, compared to dozens at PM and DSCA

### The "If Not Us, Then Them" Argument

Perhaps the most powerful argument in favor of any given sale:

- If the U.S. declines, a rival supplier (Russia, China, or France) will fill the order instead, with fewer conditions and less oversight
- This argument, while often valid, can become a self-fulfilling prophecy that justifies virtually any sale
- It is deployed reflexively by industry and DoD even in cases where the alternative supplier is unlikely or where the recipient would accept conditions
- The argument effectively shifts the burden of proof: opponents must demonstrate not only that the sale is harmful, but that refusing it would produce a better outcome than selling with conditions

### Bureaucratic Momentum

Once a potential sale enters the FMS pipeline, it acquires institutional momentum:

- DSCA case officers, military department program managers, and industry contractors all invest time and resources in developing the case
- This creates a constituency for its approval within the bureaucracy
- Cases are rarely killed once they reach the formal congressional notification stage
- The informal pre-notification process with Congress is the most likely point of intervention, but it operates behind closed doors

## Root Cause 4: The Proliferation Dilemma -- New Technologies

The traditional model of arms control is based on tracking physical objects. The rise of software-defined warfare breaks this model.

### The Nature of Software

- Unlike a tank, a piece of software can be copied perfectly and infinitely at near-zero cost
- An AI targeting algorithm, once transferred, can proliferate uncontrollably
- The marginal cost of proliferation is essentially zero, eliminating the economic barriers that naturally limit hardware proliferation
- A single USB drive or encrypted email can transfer capabilities that would otherwise require entire factories to produce

### Dual-Use Technology

- The most advanced AI and cyber technologies are often developed in the commercial sector by companies like Google, Microsoft, Amazon, and Palantir
- This blurs the line between military and civilian technology
- A cloud computing platform that processes commercial satellite imagery can be repurposed for military intelligence analysis with minimal modification
- Applying traditional arms export controls (ITAR) to dual-use AI is both technically difficult and politically contentious

### The Repurposing Risk

- A defensive AI algorithm for drone navigation could potentially be retrained by the recipient for offensive purposes
- A cyber forensics tool sold to a partner could be repurposed to spy on political dissidents
- The "end use" of software is fluid and almost impossible to monitor after transfer
- The NSO Group's Pegasus spyware -- sold as a counter-terrorism tool but widely used against journalists, human rights activists, and political opponents -- illustrates this risk vividly
- Once transferred, the recipient has both the capability and the incentive to adapt the software for purposes the exporter never intended

### Speed of Obsolescence

- Unlike hardware that may remain in service for decades, software can become obsolete or require updates within months
- This creates pressure for continuous technology transfer and ongoing access to U.S. development teams
- One-time export control decisions are inadequate for technology that requires regular updating
- The subscription/licensing model (standard in commercial software) has not been adopted for military AI exports

### The Fundamental Dilemma

The U.S. faces a structural tension: to ensure interoperability, it must share its most advanced AI and networking technology with allies. But in doing so, it risks losing control of that technology in a way that was never possible with hardware, potentially fueling a new and dangerous form of proliferation.

## Root Cause 5: Congressional Oversight Dysfunction

The congressional oversight mechanism for arms sales is structurally weak and has never functioned as a meaningful check on the executive branch.

### The Resolution of Disapproval

- Under AECA Section 36, Congress can block a sale by passing a joint resolution of disapproval within the notification period
- However, this requires passing both chambers and surviving a presidential veto
- No major arms sale has ever been blocked through this mechanism
- Even when bipartisan majorities in both chambers voted to block sales to Saudi Arabia in 2019, President Trump vetoed the resolutions
- The veto threshold makes the disapproval mechanism effectively unusable against a determined president

### The Informal Pre-Notification

- The real leverage point is the informal pre-notification process, where the administration briefs committee staff before formal notification
- This gives a few senior committee members (SFRC chair and ranking member, HFAC chair and ranking member) effective veto power
- But it operates behind closed doors with no public accountability
- The public learns about "holds" only through media leaks, not official process

### Lobbying Pressure

- Defense contractors actively lobby committee members to support sales
- They use the same tools (campaign contributions, job creation, industry access) deployed for domestic procurement
- Members whose districts benefit from export contracts have personal incentives aligned with approval
- The defense sector spent approximately $140 million on lobbying in 2024, with export policy as a significant focus

### Information Asymmetry

- The executive branch controls the intelligence assessments, human rights evaluations, and strategic analyses that inform arms sales decisions
- Congress receives summaries, not the underlying data
- This limits Congress's ability to conduct independent assessment
- Classified briefings further restrict the number of staff who can analyze proposed sales

## Root Cause 6: Weak International Norms

The international framework governing arms transfers is fragmented and largely unenforceable.

### The Arms Trade Treaty (ATT)

- Adopted by the UN General Assembly in 2013
- Establishes baseline standards for international arms transfers, including requirements to assess the risk of serious IHL violations
- The U.S. signed in 2013 but never ratified
- The Trump administration "unsigned" it in 2019
- Major arms exporters Russia and China have not joined
- Even among signatories, compliance and reporting are inconsistent

### The Wassenaar Arrangement

- Provides a framework for coordinating export controls among 42 participating states
- Decisions are non-binding and implementation varies widely
- Consensus decision-making allows any member to block new controls
- Russia is a member, limiting the arrangement's utility for denying Russian access to Western technology

### MTCR Limitations

- Effective at limiting the spread of ballistic missiles
- Has struggled to adapt to armed drones and cruise missiles
- China (not a member) has become a major drone exporter, exploiting the gap
- The 2020 U.S. reinterpretation weakened the drone restriction, but not enough to match Chinese competitiveness

### No AI/Cyber Norms

- No international framework specifically governs the export of military AI or offensive cyber capabilities
- The U.S. has begun discussions with allies (AUKUS, NATO AI strategy, Political Declaration on Responsible Military Use of AI)
- No binding norms exist
- The speed of AI development outpaces the speed of diplomatic negotiation

## Interaction Effects

These root causes do not operate in isolation. They interact in ways that make the system highly resistant to reform:

- **Strategic rationale** provides cover for **economically motivated** sales, allowing contractors to argue that every sale serves national security
- **The presumption of approval** ensures that **human rights concerns** are overridden by the combined weight of strategic and economic arguments
- **Congressional oversight dysfunction** means there is no effective check on the executive branch when strategic, economic, and industry interests align
- **Weak international norms** mean the U.S. cannot point to binding multilateral commitments as a reason to deny sales that would otherwise proceed
- **The technology dilemma** adds a new dimension of risk that the existing institutional framework is not designed to address, but the urgency of great power competition creates pressure to transfer technology faster, not slower

The result is a system with powerful institutional momentum toward selling more weapons to more countries with fewer restrictions -- precisely the opposite of what careful management of proliferation risks, human rights, and strategic stability would require.
