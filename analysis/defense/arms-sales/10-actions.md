# Arms Sales: What You Can Do

## Overview

While arms sales policy is set at the federal level, informed citizens can play a significant role in demanding transparency and accountability in the process. The arms sales system operates with less public scrutiny than most areas of federal policy, in part because its complexity and the "national security" framing discourage citizen engagement. But the decisions about which countries receive U.S. weapons -- and under what conditions -- are among the most consequential that the U.S. government makes. Citizens who understand the system and engage effectively can influence these decisions, particularly through Congress, which has the constitutional authority to oversee and block arms transfers.

---

## Individual Actions

### 1. Get Informed

Understanding the arms sales system is the essential first step. The following resources are publicly available and provide the foundation for effective advocacy:

- **Track the Sales**: Use public resources to see what weapons the U.S. is selling and to whom:
    - **DSCA Major Arms Sales notifications** (<https://www.dsca.mil/press-media/major-arms-sales>): Every major FMS case notified to Congress is published here, including the recipient country, the items, and the value. Set up alerts for new notifications.
    - **Security Assistance Monitor** (<https://securityassistance.org>): The most comprehensive public database of U.S. security assistance, maintained by the Center for International Policy. Allows searching by country, program, and year.
    - **SIPRI Arms Transfers Database** (<https://www.sipri.org/databases/armstransfers>): Tracks global arms transfers by supplier and recipient, allowing comparison of U.S. sales with those of Russia, China, and other exporters.
- **Understand the Law**: Read about the Arms Export Control Act and the Leahy Laws. Understand the legal framework that is supposed to govern these sales, including the congressional notification process and the 30-day review window.
    - The Congressional Research Service publishes regularly updated, readable summaries of the arms sales process and pending major sales. These are available at <https://crsreports.congress.gov>.
- **Follow the Debate**: Read analysis from organizations across the political spectrum:
    - **Reform perspective**: Arms Control Association, Center for International Policy, Quincy Institute
    - **Establishment perspective**: CSIS, CNAS, Atlantic Council
    - **Industry perspective**: Defense News, Breaking Defense
    - **Human rights perspective**: Amnesty International, Human Rights Watch
    - **Libertarian perspective**: Cato Institute

### 2. Engage with Congress

Congress has the power to block a major arms sale by passing a joint resolution of disapproval within a 30-day review window. While this power has never been successfully exercised to block a sale, congressional pressure has delayed, modified, and conditioned numerous sales. The informal pre-notification process gives key committee members even more leverage. This makes Congress the most direct point of citizen influence on arms sales.

**When to Engage**:

- When DSCA publishes a notification of a major arms sale (these are public and available on the DSCA website)
- When a controversial sale is reported in the media (often before formal notification)
- When Congress is debating the annual National Defense Authorization Act (NDAA) or foreign operations appropriations, which set security assistance policy and funding levels
- When a humanitarian crisis involving U.S. weapons is in the news (e.g., civilian casualties in a conflict zone involving a U.S. arms customer)

**How to Engage**:

- **Contact Your Representatives**: Call, write, or email your Representative and Senators. Tell them whether you support or oppose a specific sale and why. Be specific about the sale and reference the DSCA notification number.
- **Prioritize Key Committees**: If your Representative sits on the House Foreign Affairs Committee or your Senator sits on the Senate Foreign Relations Committee, their office has direct influence over the arms sales notification process. Contacts with these offices carry particular weight.
- **Ask Specific Questions at Town Halls**: Public questions at town halls are especially effective because they create accountability. Examples:
    - "The U.S. recently approved a $[X] billion sale of [weapons] to [Country]. Given reports of civilian casualties using U.S. weapons in [conflict], what assurances has the administration provided that these weapons will be used in compliance with international humanitarian law?"
    - "I support our alliance with [Country], but I am concerned about their human rights record. How do you weigh those competing interests when considering arms sales?"
    - "What is Congress doing to ensure that U.S.-made weapons are not being used against civilians in the [Y conflict]?"
    - "Do you believe our current laws are adequate to control the export of AI and cyber weapons to foreign governments?"
    - "Will you support legislation requiring greater transparency for Direct Commercial Sales, which account for over $150 billion in annual arms authorizations?"

### 3. Advocate for Transparency and Reform

- **Support Watchdog Groups**: Financially support and amplify the work of non-partisan organizations that track arms sales and advocate for transparency and accountability:
    - **Center for International Policy / Security Assistance Monitor**: The primary public tracker of U.S. arms sales data
    - **Arms Control Association**: Expert analysis and policy advocacy
    - **Forum on the Arms Trade**: Academic and policy research network
    - **Project on Government Oversight (POGO)**: Defense spending accountability
- **Demand DCS Transparency**: Advocate for legislation requiring public disclosure of Direct Commercial Sales licenses. Currently, DCS accounts for the majority of U.S. arms transfer value (~$157 billion in FY2023) but has the least public oversight. This is a concrete, achievable reform that would significantly improve democratic accountability.
- **Support Civilian Harm Accountability**: Advocate for legislation mandating post-sale civilian harm assessments for countries involved in active conflicts. The precedent set by NSM-20 should be codified in law to make it durable across administrations.
- **Promote a Modern Framework**: Engage in the debate about the need for new arms control frameworks for AI and autonomous systems. This is a new and critical area where public awareness can help drive policy change before the technology outpaces regulation.

### 4. Support Investigative Journalism

Independent journalism plays a critical role in arms sales accountability, often revealing information that the government does not voluntarily disclose:

- Subscribe to outlets that cover arms sales and defense policy: *Defense One*, *Defense News*, *Breaking Defense*, *The War Zone*
- Share and amplify investigative reports on arms sales controversies, particularly those documenting civilian harm or arms diversion
- Support nonprofit investigative organizations like *The Intercept*, *Bellingcat*, and *ProPublica* that investigate arms flows and their consequences
- Engage with open-source intelligence (OSINT) communities that track weapons in conflict zones by documenting serial numbers, satellite imagery, and battlefield footage

### 5. Track the Budget Process

Arms sales policy is shaped not only by individual transfer decisions but also by the annual budget and authorization process:

- **Foreign Military Financing (FMF)**: Monitor congressional appropriations for FMF, which funds grants that countries like Israel ($3.8 billion annually), Egypt (~$1.3 billion), and Jordan (~$500 million) use to purchase U.S. weapons. Advocate for conditions on FMF that require compliance with human rights standards.
- **NDAA Security Cooperation Provisions**: The annual National Defense Authorization Act contains provisions governing security cooperation authorities (10 U.S.C. 333), end-use monitoring, and Leahy Law implementation. Track these provisions and submit public comments during the legislative process.
- **State Department Foreign Operations Appropriations**: This annual bill funds the State Department's arms sales oversight capacity, including DRL staffing and end-use monitoring programs. Advocate for adequate funding for oversight functions.

### Build Coalitions

Arms sales reform is a rare area where left-right coalitions are possible:

- **Fiscal conservatives** concerned about taxpayer subsidies for defense contractor exports
- **Progressive foreign policy advocates** concerned about human rights and civilian harm
- **Libertarians** opposed to arms sales as foreign entanglements and corporate welfare
- **Faith-based communities** motivated by moral concerns about weapons proliferation and civilian harm
- **Veterans' organizations** whose members have seen firsthand the consequences of arming unstable partners

### Engage at the State and Local Level

While arms sales are a federal policy matter, state and local actions can contribute to accountability:

- **State pension fund activism**: Advocate for state pension funds to adopt responsible investment policies regarding defense companies involved in controversial arms sales. Several European pension funds have divested from companies supplying weapons to conflict zones.
- **Local government resolutions**: City councils and county boards can pass resolutions calling on Congress to strengthen arms sales oversight, creating grassroots political pressure.
- **University engagement**: Advocate for transparent disclosure of defense industry funding at universities and research institutions, and encourage academic research on arms transfer policy.
- **Community education**: Host community forums on arms sales policy featuring speakers from reform organizations, veterans' groups, and human rights organizations.

## Electoral Actions

### Candidate Evaluation

When evaluating candidates for Congress, consider:

- Do they support strengthening congressional oversight of arms sales, including reforming the joint resolution of disapproval process?
- Have they voted to block or condition arms sales to countries with poor human rights records?
- Do they support transparency for Direct Commercial Sales?
- Do they receive significant campaign contributions from defense contractors involved in arms exports? (Check OpenSecrets.org)
- Are they willing to distinguish between strategic arms sales that serve national security and commercially motivated sales driven by contractor interests?

### Campaign Finance

- Support public financing of congressional elections, which would reduce the influence of defense industry campaign contributions on arms sales votes
- Advocate for stronger disclosure requirements for defense industry political contributions
- Research and publicize the connection between defense contractor contributions and arms sales votes in your state and district

## Effectiveness Tips

- **Be specific**: Reference specific sales by DSCA notification number, recipient country, and weapon type. General complaints about "arms sales" are less effective than targeted advocacy on a specific pending sale.
- **Be timely**: The 30-day congressional notification window is the critical period. Engage when a sale is announced, not months later.
- **Be nonpartisan**: Arms sales reform does not map neatly onto partisan lines. Frame advocacy in terms that resonate across the spectrum: accountability, transparency, strategic prudence, and fiscal responsibility.
- **Use data**: Reference DSCA notifications, Security Assistance Monitor data, SIPRI transfer data, and GAO reports in communications with elected officials. Evidence-based advocacy is more credible and harder to dismiss.
- **Be persistent**: The arms sales lobby operates continuously. Citizen advocacy must be sustained across multiple sales and multiple congressional cycles to build institutional pressure for reform.
- **Connect to broader concerns**: Link arms sales to issues your representatives care about -- fiscal responsibility, human rights, regional stability, or strategic competition -- to make your advocacy relevant to their priorities.
