# Arms Sales: Reform Roadmap

## Overview

Reforming the U.S. arms sales process requires a phased approach that prioritizes immediate transparency and process improvements while developing the more complex frameworks needed for emerging technologies. The roadmap below sequences reforms to build political momentum, establish institutional capacity, and overcome entrenched opposition. Each phase builds on the previous one, making subsequent reforms both more feasible and more resistant to rollback.

---

## Phase 1: Foundational Reforms and Transparency (Years 1-2)

### Objective

Build public awareness, improve process speed for trusted allies, establish transparency infrastructure, and begin tackling the AI challenge through immediate legislative and administrative actions.

### Actions

| Priority | Action | Mechanism | Lead Actor | KPI |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **1** | **Pass the "Arms Export Transparency Act"** | Federal legislation | Congress (SFRC/HFAC) | DCS database is live and publicly accessible within 12 months of enactment. |
| **2** | **Implement Tiered FMS Processing** | State Dept/DoD joint guidance | State PM / DSCA | Average FMS case processing time for Tier 1 allies reduced by 30%. At least 10 countries designated as Tier 1. |
| **3** | **Charter AI Export Control Commission** | Congressional commission statute | Congress | Commission fully staffed with technical, legal, and ethical experts. First public hearing held within 6 months. |
| **4** | **Strengthen Human Rights Vetting** | State Dept policy directive | Secretary of State | DRL concurrence authority formalized. First formal non-concurrence and Secretarial waiver documented. |
| **5** | **Establish Civilian Harm Tracking Unit** | Administrative action | State Dept / DoD | Unit operational with staff and budget. First quarterly report on civilian harm incidents involving U.S.-origin weapons published. |
| **6** | **Mandate DCS Quarterly Reporting** | Executive Order or legislation | White House / Congress | First quarterly DCS transparency report published, covering all approved licenses above $1 million. |
| **7** | **Launch FMS Digital Case Management** | DoD IT initiative | DSCA | Digital case management system operational, providing real-time status tracking accessible to interagency stakeholders and congressional staff. |
| **8** | **Issue Updated Leahy Law Guidance** | Policy directive | State Dept / DoD | Updated guidance issued clarifying "systemic abuse" applicability and remediation standards. |

### Political Strategy

Phase 1 reforms are designed to be achievable through a combination of executive action and bipartisan legislation. Transparency measures are difficult for opponents to argue against publicly, even if they privately resist. Tiered FMS processing can be framed as "strengthening alliances" and "competing more effectively with China" -- messaging that appeals to both parties. The AI commission defers the hardest decisions while demonstrating action, buying time to build the technical foundation for Phase 2.

**Coalition targets**:

- **Hawks**: Attracted by faster processing for allies and competition with China/Russia
- **Reformers**: Attracted by transparency, human rights vetting, and civilian harm tracking
- **Industry**: Supportive of faster FMS processing (offsets opposition to transparency measures)
- **Allies**: Supportive of tiered processing and clearer technology release pathways

### Risk Assessment

| Risk | Likelihood | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| Industry lobbies against DCS transparency | High | Frame as competitiveness measure; exempt commercially sensitive details |
| Allies resist enhanced EUM for Tier 3 sales | Medium | Make EUM a condition of access to advanced systems |
| Commission appointment becomes politicized | Medium | Require bipartisan membership with technical qualifications |

**Goal of Phase 1**: Increase transparency across the system, make the U.S. a more agile partner for its closest allies, begin building institutional capacity for digital export controls, and lay the groundwork for Phase 2 legislation.

## Phase 2: Building the New Digital Framework and Structural Reform (Years 3-5)

### Objective

Implement the recommendations of the AI commission, enact structural reforms to the oversight process, and build the technical tools for digital arms control.

### Actions

| Priority | Action | Mechanism | Lead Actor | KPI |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **1** | **Enact the "Digital Arms Control Act"** | Federal legislation | Congress | Act signed into law. State Department begins rulemaking for new USML categories covering AI, autonomous systems, and cyber tools. |
| **2** | **Launch Technical Digital EUM Pilots** | DoD/Industry partnership | DSCA / defense industry | At least three pilot programs operational, testing "heartbeat" monitoring and digital watermarking on software exports to Tier 1 allies. |
| **3** | **Reform Congressional Notification Process** | AECA amendment | Congress | Simple-majority disapproval mechanism enacted. Mandatory floor votes for sales exceeding $500 million. |
| **4** | **Establish Arms Transfer Review Board** | Federal legislation | Congress / White House | Board members confirmed. First annual public report published. |
| **5** | **Streamline Interagency Review** | State/DoD directive | State PM / DSCA | Binding timeline requirements implemented. Average interagency staffing time reduced by 25%. |
| **6** | **Expand Leahy Law Framework** | Legislation | Congress | "Systemic abuse" trigger codified. Annual Leahy vetting report published. |
| **7** | **Create Allied Technology Export Compact** | Multilateral diplomacy | State Dept / NSC | Framework agreement negotiated with at least 10 allied nations establishing common AI/cyber export principles. |
| **8** | **Develop Exportable Drone Platform** | DoD acquisition program | DoD / industry | Program of record established for competitively priced, export-designed armed drone with built-in technology controls. |

### Political Strategy

Phase 2 requires building a legislative coalition around the principle that "responsible arms sales are smart arms sales." The key insight is that the same reforms that strengthen human rights oversight (civilian harm tracking, DRL empowerment, Leahy expansion) also improve strategic decision-making by ensuring that sales serve long-term interests rather than short-term commercial pressures.

**Key messaging**:

- "We can be fast AND responsible" -- tiered processing proves the concept
- "The AI challenge requires new rules" -- commission report provides the evidence base
- "Congress should have a real voice" -- notification reform empowers oversight
- "Our allies need a reliable, modern partner" -- digital framework enables sharing with controls

### Risk Assessment

| Risk | Likelihood | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| Digital Arms Control Act faces industry opposition | High | Involve industry in commission; design framework that enables controlled sharing rather than restricting it |
| Congressional notification reform triggers executive branch resistance | Medium | Frame as strengthening democratic legitimacy, not weakening executive authority |
| Technical EUM pilots reveal feasibility challenges | Medium | Start with Tier 1 allies who are cooperative; iterate based on lessons learned |
| Multilateral compact negotiations stall | Medium | Pursue bilateral agreements with key allies in parallel |

**Goal of Phase 2**: Move from an industrial-age to a digital-age arms control paradigm by creating and testing the new rules and technologies needed to manage software-defined weapons, while strengthening congressional oversight and human rights accountability.

## Phase 3: Full Implementation and International Norm-Setting (Years 6-10)

### Objective

Fully operationalize the new frameworks, build international norms, and institutionalize reforms so they are resistant to rollback.

### Actions

| Priority | Action | Mechanism | Lead Actor | KPI |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **1** | **Full Operational Capability of Digital Controls** | Implementation | State / DSCA | 90% of relevant digital exports processed under the new licensing and monitoring framework. |
| **2** | **Establish International AI Export Norms** | Multilateral diplomacy | State / NSC | Formal "Code of Conduct for Military AI Exports" signed by the U.S. and at least 15 allied nations. |
| **3** | **Institutionalize Tiered FMS System** | Legislation codifying tiered approach | Congress | Tier system codified in law with clear criteria for tier assignment and regular review. |
| **4** | **Achieve Comprehensive Transparency** | Full implementation | State / DSCA | Unified public database covering both FMS and DCS, updated quarterly, with searchable data on all transfers above $1 million. |
| **5** | **Counter-China Drone Initiative Fully Operational** | Procurement and security assistance | DoD / State | Exportable drone platform fielded by at least 10 partner nations, replacing or competing with Chinese alternatives. |
| **6** | **Arms Transfer Review Board Mature Operations** | Ongoing oversight | ATRB | Third annual report published. Board recommendations result in documented policy changes. |
| **7** | **Post-Sale Assessment Cycle Institutionalized** | Ongoing operations | State / DoD | Biennial post-sale impact assessments completed for all Tier 3 countries and all countries involved in active conflicts. |

### Political Strategy

Phase 3 requires sustained commitment across administrations. The key to durability is building institutional capacity that becomes self-reinforcing:

- The Arms Transfer Review Board generates public reports that create accountability
- The digital EUM system generates data that informs policy decisions
- The tiered FMS system creates bureaucratic efficiency that allies and industry both support
- International norms create multilateral commitments that are costly to abandon

**Goal of Phase 3**: The U.S. arms transfer system is agile, transparent, responsible, and fully equipped to handle the challenges of 21st-century warfare. The system is recognized internationally as the gold standard for combining strategic effectiveness with human rights accountability.

---

## Sequencing Rationale

The three-phase approach reflects political reality:

1. **Phase 1 (transparency and speed)** builds the evidence base and political coalition needed for Phase 2 legislation. By delivering tangible benefits to allies (faster processing) and reformers (transparency) simultaneously, it demonstrates that reform need not be a zero-sum game.
2. **Phase 2 (structural reform)** creates the legal framework and institutional capacity needed for Phase 3. The AI commission's recommendations provide political cover for the Digital Arms Control Act. Congressional notification reform builds on the transparency established in Phase 1.
3. **Phase 3 (institutionalization)** locks in reforms through legislation, international agreements, and institutional capacity that is resistant to erosion by future lobbying pressure or changes in administration.

Each phase makes the next phase more politically feasible and more resistant to rollback, following the principle that transparency measures and institutional capacity, once established, are difficult for opponents to dismantle without appearing to favor corruption or strategic negligence.
