# Arms Sales: Stakeholders

## Overview

The U.S. arms sales process involves a complex interplay between government agencies, Congress, private industry, foreign customers, and civil society organizations, each with its own set of interests, leverage, and influence. Understanding these stakeholders -- their motivations, their power, and their interactions -- is essential to comprehending why the system functions as it does and where reform efforts are most likely to succeed or fail.

## U.S. Government Stakeholders

### Department of State (Bureau of Political-Military Affairs)

**Role**: The statutory lead agency for arms transfer policy. The Bureau of Political-Military Affairs (PM) has overall responsibility for authorizing and overseeing both FMS and DCS transfers.

**Key Sub-Offices**:

- **Directorate of Defense Trade Controls (DDTC)**: Licenses Direct Commercial Sales and maintains the U.S. Munitions List (USML)
- **Office of Security Assistance**: Manages FMS policy and interagency coordination
- **Office of Weapons Removal and Abatement**: Handles conventional weapons destruction and post-conflict cleanup

**Primary Interest**: Ensuring sales align with U.S. foreign policy goals, strengthen alliances, and respect human rights obligations. The State Department views arms sales as a diplomatic tool and seeks to maintain its gatekeeper role against Pentagon efforts to expedite sales outside traditional channels.

**Leverage**: Statutory authority under the AECA to approve or deny all arms transfers. Can delay or condition sales through the interagency review process.

**Internal Tension**: PM's interest in facilitating sales to advance strategic relationships often conflicts with the Bureau of Democracy, Human Rights, and Labor (DRL), which advocates for greater human rights scrutiny. Regional bureaus (e.g., Bureau of Near Eastern Affairs) may advocate for sales to maintain relationships with countries in their portfolio, even when DRL raises objections.

### Department of Defense (Defense Security Cooperation Agency -- DSCA)

**Role**: The implementing agency for the FMS program. DSCA manages approximately 14,000 active FMS cases with over 170 countries, representing the operational backbone of U.S. arms transfers.

**Key Personnel**:

- **Director, DSCA**: A lieutenant general or vice admiral who reports to the Under Secretary of Defense for Policy
- **Military department program managers**: Manage individual FMS cases for the Army, Navy, and Air Force
- **Security Cooperation Officers (SCOs)**: Stationed at U.S. embassies worldwide, SCOs are the front-line interface with foreign customers

**Primary Interest**: Enhancing partner military capability, promoting interoperability with U.S. forces, and meeting U.S. security objectives. The DoD also has a strong interest in using foreign sales to sustain the defense industrial base and lower per-unit costs for its own procurement.

**Leverage**: Controls the technical details of FMS cases, including pricing, delivery schedules, and technology release decisions. The DoD's Technology Security and Foreign Disclosure (TSFD) process determines what level of technology can be shared with each country.

### Congress (Senate Foreign Relations and House Foreign Affairs Committees)

**Role**: Constitutional authority to oversee arms transfers, receive notifications of major sales, and potentially block sales through joint resolutions of disapproval.

**Key Committees and Members**:

- **Senate Foreign Relations Committee (SFRC)**: Primary Senate committee for arms sales oversight. The chair and ranking member receive pre-notification briefings on major sales.
- **House Foreign Affairs Committee (HFAC)**: Counterpart House committee with parallel notification rights.
- **Senate Armed Services Committee (SASC) / House Armed Services Committee (HASC)**: Exercise indirect influence through their authority over defense authorization and the defense industrial base.
- **Appropriations Committees**: Control Foreign Military Financing (FMF) funding levels, which determine how much grant military aid the U.S. provides to countries like Israel, Egypt, and Jordan.

**Primary Interest**: Dual and often conflicting interests: (1) ensuring sales serve U.S. national interests and do not enable human rights abuses; (2) supporting defense industry jobs and contracts in their districts and states.

**Leverage**: The power to delay sales through the informal pre-notification hold process. The power to pass joint resolutions of disapproval (never successfully used to date). The power to set FMF funding levels and impose conditions on security assistance through annual appropriations.

**Key Tension**: Members whose districts host major defense export facilities have personal political incentives to support sales that may conflict with broader foreign policy concerns. The same member who publicly criticizes Saudi Arabia's human rights record may privately support Saudi arms sales that sustain jobs in their district.

### Department of Commerce (Bureau of Industry and Security)

**Role**: Regulates the export of "dual-use" items -- commercial goods and technologies that can also have military applications -- through the Export Administration Regulations (EAR) and the Commerce Control List (CCL).

**Primary Interest**: Balancing export control objectives with the economic interests of U.S. commercial technology companies. BIS has historically favored less restrictive controls than the State Department, reflecting its dual mandate.

**Leverage**: Controls a separate export control system that sometimes overlaps or conflicts with the State Department's ITAR system. The ongoing USML-to-CCL reform process, which moves certain items from stringent ITAR control to less restrictive EAR control, gives BIS growing authority over defense-adjacent technologies.

**Key Tension**: For emerging technologies like AI, quantum computing, and advanced semiconductors, the boundary between BIS (Commerce) and DDTC (State) jurisdiction is contested, creating regulatory gaps and confusion for exporters.

### Intelligence Community

**Role**: Provides classified assessments that inform arms sales decisions, including evaluations of recipient country stability, end-use risks, technology compromise risks, and the strategic implications of proposed sales.

**Key Actors**: The CIA, the Defense Intelligence Agency (DIA), and the National Security Agency (NSA) all contribute to the intelligence assessment of proposed arms transfers.

**Leverage**: Intelligence assessments can support or undermine a proposed sale. A classified assessment that a recipient country is likely to misuse weapons or allow technology compromise can significantly complicate the approval process.

**Key Tension**: Intelligence assessments are not shared fully with Congress, giving the executive branch an information advantage in arms sales debates.

## Private Sector Stakeholders

### Prime Defense Contractors

**Key Actors**:

| Contractor | Key Export Programs | Estimated Export Revenue |
|---|---|---|
| **Lockheed Martin** | F-35, F-16V, THAAD, PAC-3, C-130J, Sikorsky helicopters | ~$15-20 billion/year |
| **RTX (Raytheon)** | Patriot, AMRAAM, Javelin, Stinger, Pratt & Whitney engines | ~$10-15 billion/year |
| **Boeing** | F-15EX, F/A-18E/F, AH-64 Apache, KC-46, P-8A Poseidon | ~$8-12 billion/year |
| **General Dynamics** | M1A2 Abrams, Stryker, Gulfstream (modified), munitions | ~$5-8 billion/year |
| **Northrop Grumman** | Global Hawk, E-2D Hawkeye, LITENING, ammunition | ~$4-6 billion/year |

**Primary Interest**: Revenue and profit. Foreign sales extend production runs, maintain workforce skills, and contribute to shareholder value. For some programs, export orders are essential to keeping the production line open.

**Leverage**: Immense lobbying power in Congress (the defense sector spent approximately $140 million on lobbying in 2024); control of high-skilled jobs in key districts; deep technical expertise that makes them essential partners for the government; strategic distribution of subcontracts across congressional districts; and the revolving door pipeline that gives them access to former government decision-makers.

**How They Influence the Process**:

- **Direct lobbying**: Contractor lobbyists work Congress and the executive branch to support specific sales
- **Marketing missions**: Companies send representatives to foreign air shows and defense exhibitions (IDEX, DSEI, Paris Air Show) with active U.S. government support
- **Foreign Military Sales as a service**: Companies assign dedicated FMS program offices that work closely with DSCA to develop cases
- **Offsets and industrial cooperation**: Companies negotiate offset agreements with foreign customers, creating local constituencies for the sale

### Subcontractors and Supply Chain

**Role**: Receive subcontracts from prime contractors that flow from major foreign sales. A single FMS case can generate subcontracts to hundreds of suppliers across dozens of states.

**Primary Interest**: Stable orders and revenue. For many small and mid-tier defense firms, export-related subcontracts represent a significant share of their business.

**Leverage**: Collectively represent a vast number of jobs spread across almost every congressional district. Lockheed Martin's F-35 program, for example, involves over 1,700 suppliers in 45+ states, and foreign orders for the F-35 sustain a significant portion of these suppliers' workload.

### Defense Industry Trade Associations

- **Aerospace Industries Association (AIA)**: Advocates for the aerospace and defense sector on export policy, ITAR reform, and international competitiveness. A consistent voice for relaxing export restrictions.
- **National Defense Industrial Association (NDIA)**: Represents the broader defense industrial base, including many export-dependent firms. Publishes analyses framing export restrictions as threats to industrial base health.
- **Defense Security Cooperation University (DSCU)**: While a government institution, it provides training to the security cooperation workforce and helps socialize industry and government personnel into the arms sales process.

## External Stakeholders

### Foreign Governments (Customers)

**Primary Interest**: Acquiring military capabilities for defense, deterrence, prestige, or internal security. Each customer has unique motivations:

| Customer Type | Examples | Primary Motivation |
|---|---|---|
| **Frontline deterrence states** | Taiwan, Poland, Baltic states | Deterring specific adversaries (China, Russia) |
| **Gulf security partners** | Saudi Arabia, UAE, Qatar | Regional power projection, Iran deterrence |
| **Major allies** | Japan, Australia, South Korea, UK | Interoperability with U.S. forces, alliance deepening |
| **Emerging partners** | India, Indonesia, Vietnam | Diversifying away from Russian equipment, hedging against China |
| **Status-oriented buyers** | Various | National prestige, signaling sovereignty |

**Leverage**: The power to choose which country to buy from. When the U.S. process is too slow or too conditional, customers can -- and do -- turn to France, Russia, or China. Turkey's purchase of the Russian S-400 air defense system after being denied Patriot on acceptable terms is the most prominent example of this leverage in action.

### Human Rights Organizations

**Key Actors**:

- **Amnesty International**: Documents arms flows to conflict zones and advocates for stricter transfer controls. Published extensive reports on U.S.-made weapons used in the Yemen conflict.
- **Human Rights Watch**: Investigates specific incidents of civilian harm involving U.S.-supplied weapons and advocates for congressional action.
- **Physicians for Human Rights**: Documents medical facility strikes using U.S.-origin munitions.
- **Reprieve**: Tracks drone strikes and their impact on civilian populations.
- **Mwatana for Human Rights**: Yemen-based organization that documents civilian casualties from coalition strikes using U.S.-supplied weapons.

**Primary Interest**: Preventing U.S. weapons from being used to commit human rights abuses and international humanitarian law violations.

**Leverage**: Moral authority; ability to generate negative media attention; capacity to influence congressional votes through targeted advocacy campaigns; standing to bring legal challenges in some jurisdictions.

### Arms Control and Foreign Policy Organizations

**Key Actors**:

- **Arms Control Association (ACA)**: Nonpartisan organization providing expert analysis on arms transfers, treaties, and proliferation risks.
- **Center for International Policy (CIP)**: Hosts the Security Assistance Monitor database, the most comprehensive public tracker of U.S. arms sales and security assistance.
- **Stimson Center**: Conducts research on conventional arms transfers and arms trade treaty implementation.
- **Quincy Institute for Responsible Statecraft**: Advocates for restraint in arms transfers, particularly to authoritarian regimes and conflict zones.
- **Forum on the Arms Trade**: Academic and policy network connecting researchers working on arms transfer issues.

**Primary Interest**: Preventing proliferation and regional arms races, strengthening international arms control norms, and increasing transparency in the arms transfer process.

**Leverage**: Policy expertise; influence in academic and policy debates; ability to shape media narratives through research and commentary; connections to sympathetic congressional offices.

### International Organizations

- **United Nations**: The UN Arms Trade Treaty (ATT) Secretariat monitors treaty implementation. UN panels of experts investigate arms embargo violations and document arms flows to conflict zones.
- **Stockholm International Peace Research Institute (SIPRI)**: The authoritative independent source for global arms transfer data, providing the trend-indicator values used worldwide to track arms flows.
- **International Committee of the Red Cross (ICRC)**: Advocates for arms transfer controls as part of its mandate to promote international humanitarian law.

## Power Analysis

| Stakeholder | Political Power | Economic Resources | Information Access | Organizational Capacity |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Prime defense contractors | Very High | Very High | High | Very High |
| Pentagon (DSCA) | High | N/A | Very High | Very High |
| State Dept (PM/DDTC) | High | N/A | High | High |
| Congress (key committees) | High | N/A | Medium | High |
| Foreign governments (major buyers) | Medium-High | High | Medium | Medium |
| Defense trade associations | Medium | Medium | Medium | High |
| Arms control organizations | Low-Medium | Low | Medium | Medium |
| Human rights organizations | Low-Medium | Low | Medium | Medium |
| General public | Low (diffuse) | N/A | Low | Very Low |

**The Fundamental Imbalance**: The actors who benefit from the current system -- contractors, the Pentagon, key committee members, and foreign customers -- are highly organized, well-resourced, and concentrated. The actors who bear the costs -- civilians harmed by U.S.-exported weapons, taxpayers who subsidize the process, and the public interest in strategic stability -- are diffuse and poorly organized. This asymmetry is the defining feature of the arms sales political landscape and the primary obstacle to reform.
