# Arms Sales: Resources

This section provides a curated list of key government sources, data providers, academic research, expert analysis, and advocacy organizations on U.S. arms sales and security cooperation.

---

## Primary Government Sources

### Executive Branch

- **U.S. Department of State, Bureau of Political-Military Affairs (PM)**
    - The lead bureau for arms transfer policy. Its website provides official reports, fact sheets, the annual Section 655 report on military assistance, and policy announcements.
    - URL: <https://www.state.gov/bureaus-offices/under-secretary-for-arms-control-and-international-security-affairs/bureau-of-political-military-affairs/>

- **U.S. Department of State, Directorate of Defense Trade Controls (DDTC)**
    - Administers ITAR, the U.S. Munitions List, and the DCS licensing process. Provides guidance on export control compliance.
    - URL: <https://www.pmddtc.state.gov/>

- **Defense Security Cooperation Agency (DSCA)**
    - The DoD agency that manages the Foreign Military Sales (FMS) program. Its website includes a database of all major FMS sales notifications to Congress, the Historical Facts Book, and policy guidance.
    - URL: <https://www.dsca.mil/>

- **Department of Commerce, Bureau of Industry and Security (BIS)**
    - Administers the Export Administration Regulations (EAR) and the Commerce Control List (CCL) for dual-use items.
    - URL: <https://www.bis.gov/>

### Legislative Branch

- **Congressional Research Service (CRS)**
    - Produces comprehensive, nonpartisan reports on arms sales policy, including regularly updated reports on major pending sales, the FMS process, and export control reform.
    - Key Reports:
        - Javits, Paul K., and Bolkcom, Christopher. *Arms Sales: Congressional Review Process*. CRS Report RL31675.
        - Kerr, Paul K., and Theohary, Catherine A. *Conventional Arms Transfers to Developing Nations*. CRS Report R44716.
        - Grimmett, Richard F. (various years). *Conventional Arms Transfers to the Third World*. CRS Reports (historical series).
    - URL: <https://crsreports.congress.gov>

- **Government Accountability Office (GAO)**
    - Conducts audits and evaluations of arms sales programs, end-use monitoring, and export control implementation.
    - Key Reports:
        - GAO. *Foreign Military Sales: DOD Needs to Improve Its Processes for Tracking and Reporting Delivery Timelines*. GAO-22-105389. 2022.
        - GAO. *End Use Monitoring: Certain Improvements in Monitoring of Defense Articles Would Strengthen Controls*. GAO-09-888. 2009.
    - URL: <https://www.gao.gov>

- **Congressional Budget Office (CBO)**
    - Provides cost analysis of security assistance programs and foreign military financing.
    - URL: <https://www.cbo.gov>

## Key Legal Authorities

- **Arms Export Control Act (AECA)**, 22 U.S.C. 2751-2799aa-4
    - The foundational U.S. law governing arms transfers. Establishes presidential authority, congressional notification requirements, and enforcement provisions.

- **International Traffic in Arms Regulations (ITAR)**, 22 CFR Parts 120-130
    - The detailed regulations implementing the AECA, including the U.S. Munitions List (USML) and licensing procedures for defense articles and services.

- **Foreign Assistance Act of 1961**, 22 U.S.C. 2151 et seq.
    - Authorizes security assistance programs. Section 502B addresses human rights considerations. Section 620M contains the State Department Leahy Law.

- **Leahy Laws**
    - State Department: 22 U.S.C. 2378d
    - Department of Defense: 10 U.S.C. 362

- **Export Control Reform Act (ECRA)**, 50 U.S.C. 4801-4852
    - Governs dual-use exports through the Commerce Department's Bureau of Industry and Security.

- **National Security Memorandum 20 (NSM-20)**, February 2024
    - Requires countries receiving U.S. arms to provide written assurances of compliance with international humanitarian law and to facilitate humanitarian aid delivery.

## Key Data Sources

- **Stockholm International Peace Research Institute (SIPRI) Arms Transfers Database**
    - The most comprehensive, independent public database on global arms transfers. Essential for tracking trends and comparing suppliers and recipients. Uses a "trend-indicator value" methodology rather than financial values.
    - URL: <https://www.sipri.org/databases/armstransfers>

- **SIPRI Arms Industry Database**
    - Tracks the revenues of the world's 100 largest arms-producing companies.
    - URL: <https://www.sipri.org/databases/armsindustry>

- **Security Assistance Monitor**
    - A project by the Center for International Policy that tracks and analyzes all forms of U.S. security assistance, including arms sales (FMS and DCS), military aid, and training programs, in a comprehensive, user-friendly database. The single most useful tool for tracking U.S. arms transfers at the country and program level.
    - URL: <https://securityassistance.org/>

- **DSCA Major Arms Sales Notifications**
    - Official notifications to Congress of major FMS cases, including descriptions of the items, quantities, and values.
    - URL: <https://www.dsca.mil/press-media/major-arms-sales>

- **UN Register of Conventional Arms**
    - Voluntary reporting mechanism where states report arms imports and exports. Coverage is incomplete but provides useful supplementary data.
    - URL: <https://www.unroca.org/>

- **The World Military Expenditures and Arms Transfers (WMEAT)**
    - Published by the State Department, provides comprehensive data on military expenditures and arms transfers worldwide.

## Books

- Hartung, William D. *Prophets of War: Lockheed Martin and the Making of the Military-Industrial Complex*. New York: Bold Type Books, 2011.
    - Comprehensive history of the largest defense contractor, including its role in arms exports.

- Stohl, Rachel, and Suzette Grillot. *The International Arms Trade*. Cambridge: Polity Press, 2009.
    - Academic overview of the global arms trade, including U.S. policy, multilateral controls, and the role of non-state actors.

- Phythian, Mark. *The Politics of British Arms Sales Since 1964*. Manchester: Manchester University Press, 2000.
    - Comparative analysis useful for understanding how other democracies manage the arms sales dilemma.

- Brzoska, Michael, and Frederic S. Pearson. *Arms and Warfare: Escalation, De-escalation, and Negotiation*. Columbia: University of South Carolina Press, 1994.
    - Examines the relationship between arms transfers and conflict dynamics.

- Grimmett, Richard F. *Conventional Arms Transfers to Developing Nations*. Various years. Congressional Research Service.
    - The definitive longitudinal dataset on global arms transfers, published annually by CRS for decades.

- Pierre, Andrew J. *The Global Politics of Arms Sales*. Princeton: Princeton University Press, 1982.
    - Classic analysis of the strategic and political dimensions of the international arms trade.

## Academic Research

- Boutton, Andrew. "US Foreign Aid, Interstate Rivalry, and Incentives for Counterterrorism Cooperation." *Journal of Peace Research* 51, no. 6 (2014): 741-754.
    - Examines how security assistance, including arms sales, affects partner-nation counter-terrorism behavior.

- Pamp, Oliver, et al. "The Build-Up of Coercive Capacities: Arms Imports and the Outbreak of Violent Intrastate Conflict." *Journal of Peace Research* 55, no. 4 (2018): 430-444.
    - Finds that arms imports increase the risk of intrastate conflict, challenging the "stability through strength" rationale.

- Caverley, Jonathan D. "United States Hegemony and the New Economics of Defense." *Security Studies* 16, no. 4 (2007): 598-614.
    - Analyzes how U.S. defense market dominance shapes the economics of arms transfers.

- Erickson, Jennifer L. *Dangerous Trade: Arms Exports, Human Rights, and International Reputation*. New York: Columbia University Press, 2015.
    - Examines how arms exports affect the exporting country's international reputation and human rights commitments.
