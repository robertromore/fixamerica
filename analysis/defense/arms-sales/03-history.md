# Arms Sales: History

## Historical Development

U.S. arms sales policy has evolved dramatically since World War II, shifting from a tool for arming allies against the Soviet Union to a complex instrument of global influence, counter-terrorism, and great power competition. Each era has produced a distinctive approach to arms transfers, and the accumulated legacy of these approaches -- including their failures -- shapes the system that exists today.

---

## World War II and the Lend-Lease Precedent (1941-1945)

Before the Cold War, the United States did not maintain a permanent arms export system. The **Lend-Lease Act of 1941** established the first large-scale program for transferring U.S. military equipment to foreign governments, providing approximately $50 billion (over $700 billion in 2024 dollars) in military aid to Allied nations including the United Kingdom, the Soviet Union, China, and France.

Lend-Lease demonstrated that arms transfers could serve as a powerful tool of foreign policy -- Winston Churchill called it "the most unsordid act in the history of any nation" -- but it was understood as a wartime emergency measure, not a permanent program.

### Key Lend-Lease Statistics

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Total Lend-Lease value | ~$50 billion ($700+ billion in 2024 dollars) |
| Recipient countries | 38 |
| Largest recipient | United Kingdom (~$31 billion) |
| Soviet Union share | ~$11 billion |
| Program duration | 1941-1945 |

The end of World War II initially brought expectations of demobilization, but the emerging Cold War would transform arms transfers from a temporary expedient into a permanent feature of American foreign policy.

## The Cold War (1949-1991): A Tool of Containment

### Context and Strategy

The primary goal of Cold War arms transfers was to contain the Soviet Union by arming and strengthening anti-communist allies around the world. Arms sales became one of the "three pillars" of containment alongside diplomatic alliances (NATO, SEATO, CENTO) and economic assistance (the Marshall Plan and its successors).

### Key Legislation and Policy Frameworks

- **The Mutual Security Act (1951)**: Established the framework for U.S. military assistance programs, replacing ad hoc arrangements with a systematic approach to arming allies. The Act created the Military Assistance Program (MAP), which provided grants of weapons and equipment to countries considered essential to containing communism.

- **The Foreign Military Sales Act (1968)**: Created the formal FMS system, shifting the primary mechanism for arms transfers from grants (MAP) to sales. This reflected both fiscal pressures and the desire to treat allies as partners rather than dependents.

- **The Arms Export Control Act (1976)**: Passed in the wake of Vietnam-era controversies, the AECA consolidated and strengthened the legal framework for arms transfers. Key provisions included:
    - The congressional notification process for major sales
    - Required presidential reports to Congress on arms transfer decisions
    - Codified the principle that arms transfers should serve foreign policy and national security objectives
    - Established the "U.S. Munitions List" as the controlling document for defense articles
    - Senator Gaylord Nelson and other reformers pushed the legislation as a response to concerns about uncontrolled weapons proliferation

- **The Nixon Doctrine (1969)**: President Nixon explicitly stated that the U.S. would "furnish military and economic assistance" to allies so they could provide for their own defense, reducing the need for direct American military intervention. This marked a pivotal shift from grants of military aid to sales of military equipment, transforming allies into customers and creating the commercial dynamic that drives the system today.

### Landmark Sales and Cautionary Tales

**Iran under the Shah (1950s-1979)**:

Iran became a pillar of U.S. policy in the Persian Gulf and one of the largest purchasers of advanced U.S. military technology. Key facts:

- The Shah purchased F-14 Tomcat fighters, F-4 Phantoms, AH-1 Cobra attack helicopters, and advanced naval vessels
- Between 1972 and 1977, Iran placed approximately $20 billion in arms orders from the United States
- The 1972 Nixon-Kissinger "blank check" policy allowed the Shah to purchase virtually any non-nuclear weapons system in the U.S. inventory
- This ended abruptly with the 1979 Islamic Revolution, which left sensitive U.S. technology -- including the F-14 and its Phoenix missile system -- in the hands of a hostile regime
- Iran's F-14s remained in service for decades, and Iran attempted to acquire spare parts through black market channels
- The Iranian experience remains the most powerful cautionary tale in arms sales history, demonstrating the risk of selling advanced weapons to unstable authoritarian allies

**Saudi Arabia -- The AWACS Controversy (1981)**:

The Reagan administration's proposed sale of five E-3A AWACS surveillance aircraft and F-15 enhancement packages to Saudi Arabia triggered one of the fiercest congressional battles in arms sales history:

- The sale passed the Senate by a narrow vote of 52-48 only after intense White House lobbying
- Opponents, led by the American Israel Public Affairs Committee (AIPAC), argued the sale threatened Israeli security and the regional balance of power
- Supporters argued the sale was essential to countering the Soviet-backed threat from Iraq and Iran
- The AWACS sale cemented the Saudi-American arms relationship and established the pattern of contentious congressional battles over Gulf state sales that continues today

**Israel**:

The U.S.-Israel arms relationship deepened throughout the Cold War:

- The U.S. provided increasingly advanced systems including F-15 and F-16 fighters, beginning in the 1970s
- The 1981 Israeli bombing of Iraq's Osirak nuclear reactor using U.S.-supplied F-16s raised early questions about end-use constraints and the distinction between "defensive" and "offensive" uses of transferred weapons
- The Camp David Accords (1978) established the framework for U.S. military aid to both Israel and Egypt that persists today, with Israel receiving approximately $3 billion annually (later increased to $3.8 billion under the 2016 MOU)
- Israel's "Qualitative Military Edge" (QME) -- the U.S. commitment to ensure Israel maintains military superiority over any combination of regional adversaries -- became a statutory requirement under the 2008 Naval Vessel Transfer Act

**The Iran-Contra Affair (1985-1987)**:

The secret sale of U.S. weapons to Iran in exchange for hostages, with profits diverted to fund Nicaraguan Contra rebels, exposed the dark side of arms transfers conducted outside legal channels:

- Senior National Security Council officials, including Oliver North, orchestrated the sales in violation of both the arms embargo on Iran and congressional restrictions on aid to the Contras
- The scandal led to congressional hearings, criminal prosecutions, and lasting distrust of executive branch management of arms sales
- It strengthened the argument for robust congressional oversight and contributed to subsequent AECA amendments

### Cold War Legacy

This era established arms sales as a key tool of U.S. foreign policy, but also highlighted the risks of:

- Betting on the stability of authoritarian partners (Iran)
- Consequences of inadequate congressional oversight (Iran-Contra)
- The tension between arming one partner and threatening another (AWACS and Israel)
- The long-term maintenance burden created by decades of arms sales to dozens of countries

## The Post-Cold War (1991-2001): Interoperability, Market Share, and Human Rights

### Context

With the Soviet threat gone, the rationale for arms sales shifted from containment to alliance management, interoperability, and economic competitiveness. Defense budgets shrank, and the defense industry faced an existential crisis.

### New Goals

**Interoperability**:

The First Gulf War (1991) demonstrated the value of allied forces operating U.S.-compatible equipment:

- Coalition partners using U.S.-made M1 Abrams tanks, F-15 fighters, and Patriot missile batteries integrated seamlessly with American forces
- Saudi Arabia's AWACS fleet (the same aircraft that had been so controversial a decade earlier) provided critical airborne surveillance for the coalition
- This success drove further sales of U.S. systems to Gulf states and NATO allies, establishing interoperability as a primary justification for arms transfers

**Economic Competitiveness**:

With domestic defense budgets shrinking during the post-Cold War drawdown:

- U.S. companies aggressively pursued foreign sales to keep production lines open and maintain market share
- Growing European competition from firms like Dassault (Rafale), the Eurofighter consortium, and Saab (Gripen) created competitive pressure
- The Clinton administration's 1995 "Conventional Arms Transfer Policy" explicitly recognized the economic benefits of arms exports
- The post-Cold War consolidation that reduced the domestic defense industrial base (see [Military-Industrial Complex: History](../military-industrial-complex/03-history.md)) intensified the economic pressure to export, as fewer, larger firms became more dependent on foreign sales to sustain production

### Human Rights Developments

**The Leahy Laws**:

This era saw the codification of the first systematic human rights conditions on arms transfers:

- First enacted as a provision in the 1997 Foreign Operations Appropriations Act, sponsored by Senator Patrick Leahy of Vermont
- The laws prohibit assistance to foreign military units that have committed gross violations of human rights
- They represented the first systematic attempt to condition arms transfers on human rights performance
- A parallel DoD provision was added in subsequent years, creating dual Leahy Laws administered by both State and Defense

**The Wassenaar Arrangement (1996)**:

- Established as a successor to the Cold War-era Coordinating Committee for Multilateral Export Controls (COCOM)
- Created a framework for multilateral coordination of arms export controls among 42 participating states
- Unlike COCOM (which targeted the Soviet bloc), Wassenaar was not directed at a specific adversary
- However, its non-binding nature and consensus decision-making limited its effectiveness

**Advocacy for International Norms**:

- Amnesty International and the Nobel Peace Laureates Initiative began pushing for a binding international code of conduct governing arms transfers
- This planted the seeds for the Arms Trade Treaty negotiations that would follow in the next decade
- The European Union adopted its own Code of Conduct on Arms Exports in 1998, providing a regional model

### Key Sales

- The sale of 150 F-16s to Taiwan in 1992, approved by President George H.W. Bush in a reversal of prior policy, demonstrated that arms sales to Taiwan would remain a flashpoint in U.S.-China relations
- Sales of Patriot missile batteries to Israel, Saudi Arabia, and Kuwait surged after the Gulf War demonstrated the system's capabilities (and limitations)
- The F-16 became the world's most widely exported fighter, with sales to over 25 countries solidifying its position as the backbone of allied air forces

## The Post-9/11 Era (2001-2017): The War on Terror

### Context and Strategic Shift

The September 11, 2001 attacks fundamentally reoriented U.S. security cooperation. Arms sales were reoriented to equip partner nations to fight terrorist groups in their own territories, reducing the burden on U.S. forces.

### Counter-Terrorism Arms Transfers

This era involved providing less advanced, "CT-appropriate" equipment:

- Light attack aircraft (A-29 Super Tucano for Afghanistan, Lebanon)
- Helicopters (Black Hawks, Apaches for Afghanistan, Iraq, Saudi Arabia)
- Surveillance drones (ScanEagle, MQ-1 Predator variants)
- Armored vehicles (MRAPs, Humvees)
- Small arms, body armor, and communications equipment

### The Rise of Drones

The U.S. became the world leader in unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs), but was highly restrictive in exporting armed drones:

- The Missile Technology Control Regime (MTCR) treats armed UAVs with range exceeding 300 km and payload exceeding 500 kg as Category I items, subject to a "strong presumption of denial"
- Multiple allies and partners -- including the UAE, Saudi Arabia, Jordan, and Italy -- sought to purchase armed MQ-9 Reapers but faced extended delays or denials
- This created a market opening that China eagerly filled
- By 2017, Chinese-made armed drones (Wing Loong, CH-4, CH-5) were operating in Iraq, Saudi Arabia, Egypt, Pakistan, Nigeria, and others
- China's willingness to sell armed drones without political conditions or extensive end-use monitoring made it the supplier of choice for countries frustrated by U.S. restrictions

### "Train and Equip" Programs

The U.S. launched massive train-and-equip programs in Iraq and Afghanistan:

- Transferred huge quantities of small arms, vehicles, body armor, and communications equipment to nascent security forces
- A 2016 DoD Inspector General report found that the U.S. could not account for approximately $1 billion in weapons and equipment transferred to Iraq
- Some unaccounted weapons were captured by ISIS during its 2014 offensive, including U.S.-origin Humvees, M16 rifles, and M1 Abrams tanks
- In Afghanistan, the 2021 Taliban takeover resulted in the capture of an estimated $7 billion in U.S.-provided military equipment, including Black Hawk helicopters, armored vehicles, and night-vision goggles
- The Special Inspector General for Afghanistan Reconstruction (SIGAR) documented extensive waste and diversion throughout the 20-year program

### The "Section 1206" Authority

- Congress created new authorities (later consolidated as 10 U.S.C. 333, the "Building Partner Capacity" authority) allowing the DoD to directly train and equip foreign military forces
- This bypassed the slower State Department-led FMS process
- Gave the Pentagon greater control over security cooperation but reduced the State Department's traditional gatekeeper role
- Raised concerns about the "militarization" of foreign policy and the erosion of civilian oversight

### Saudi Arabia and the Yemen Crisis

Beginning in 2015, the Saudi-led coalition intervened militarily in Yemen's civil war:

- Used large quantities of U.S.-supplied weapons, including precision-guided munitions (Paveway bombs, JDAM kits), F-15 fighter jets, and AH-64 Apache attack helicopters
- Documented strikes on hospitals, schools, funerals, and civilian gatherings using U.S.-manufactured bombs generated intense controversy
- A 2018 CNN investigation traced serial numbers on bomb fragments at the site of a school bus strike that killed 40 children to a Lockheed Martin MK-82 bomb sold through FMS
- Led to the first serious congressional efforts to block arms sales to Saudi Arabia under AECA Section 36
- The Obama administration suspended sales of precision-guided munitions to Saudi Arabia in late 2016 due to civilian casualty concerns
- The Yemen conflict became the defining test case for U.S. arms sales accountability and remains so today

## The Great Power Competition Era (2018-Present)

### Context

The 2018 National Defense Strategy pivoted U.S. focus to competition with China and Russia, fundamentally reshaping the logic of arms transfers.

### Integrated Deterrence Through Arms Sales

Arms sales are now seen as a primary tool for "integrated deterrence":

- The goal is to arm allies on the front lines -- Taiwan, Japan, Poland, the Baltic states, and Australia -- with advanced, asymmetric capabilities
- Key capability areas: anti-ship missiles (Harpoon, LRASM), air and missile defense (Patriot, THAAD, NASAMS), long-range fires (HIMARS, ATACMS), and advanced fighter aircraft (F-35)
- The logic is to raise the cost of aggression so high that adversaries are deterred from acting

### Strategic Technology Transfer: AUKUS

The **AUKUS** agreement (announced September 2021) represents the most ambitious technology transfer arrangement in recent history:

- Involves sharing nuclear submarine technology with Australia -- only the second time the U.S. has shared this technology (after the UK in 1958)
- Goes beyond submarines to include cooperation on AI, quantum computing, hypersonics, and cyber capabilities ("Pillar II")
- Represents a new model of "deep integration" with the most trusted allies
- Raises questions about whether this level of sharing can be extended to other partners, and on what terms

### Ukraine as Inflection Point

Russia's February 2022 invasion of Ukraine triggered the most rapid and extensive U.S. arms transfer operation since Lend-Lease:

- Through Presidential Drawdown Authority (PDA) and the Ukraine Security Assistance Initiative (USAI), the U.S. provided over $50 billion in security assistance in the first two years
- Key systems transferred: Javelin ATGMs, Stinger MANPADS, HIMARS, Patriot air defense, M1 Abrams tanks, Bradley IFVs, and millions of rounds of ammunition
- This experience exposed both strengths (speed of PDA drawdowns, allied coordination) and weaknesses (industrial base capacity constraints, ammunition shortages, political sustainability) of the U.S. arms transfer system
- The "arsenal of democracy" challenge -- can the U.S. industrial base produce enough munitions to sustain Ukraine while maintaining readiness for other contingencies? -- became a central defense policy question

### The Evolving Policy Framework

- **2018 CAT Policy (Trump)**: Prioritized economic benefits, reduced barriers to exports, relaxed armed drone restrictions through MTCR reinterpretation
- **2023 CAT Policy (Biden)**: Greater emphasis on human rights and civilian harm, but still sought to streamline processes for strategic partners
- **NSM-20 (February 2024)**: Required countries receiving U.S. arms to provide written assurances of compliance with international humanitarian law
- **The Abraham Accords Effect (2020)**: Normalization of relations between Israel and several Arab states unlocked new arms sales dynamics, including the UAE's request for F-35 fighters -- a request that remains pending amid technology security and regional balance concerns
- Neither administration has created a comprehensive framework for controlling AI and cyber exports

## Recurring Themes Across Eras

| Theme | Examples | Lesson |
|---|---|---|
| **Regime change risk** | Iran (1979), Iraq (2003), Afghanistan (2021) | Arms sold to allies can end up in adversary or non-state actor hands |
| **Congressional-executive tension** | AWACS (1981), Saudi Arabia/Yemen (2019) | Congress asserts oversight but rarely blocks sales |
| **Human rights vs. strategy** | Saudi Arabia, Egypt, Bahrain | Strategic interests consistently override human rights concerns |
| **Technology competition** | Chinese drones, Russian S-400 | Restrictive U.S. policies can cede market share to rivals |
| **Industrial base dependency** | F-15 line, F-35 production | Export orders become essential to sustaining domestic production |
