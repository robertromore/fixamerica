# Arms Sales: Opposition

## Overview

U.S. arms sales, particularly to non-democratic countries or those involved in active conflicts, face strong and persistent opposition from a range of human rights, arms control, and anti-interventionist groups. However, there is also significant opposition to *reforming* arms sales -- that is, powerful actors who oppose any effort to impose greater restrictions, increase transparency, or strengthen human rights conditions on the transfer of weapons.

This section examines both the critique of current arms sales practices and the opposition to reform efforts, identifying the key actors, their arguments, and the rhetorical strategies they deploy.

---

## The Human Rights Critique

This is the most prominent and politically potent line of opposition to current arms sales practices.

### The Yemen Case Study

The Saudi-led coalition's intervention in Yemen beginning in 2015 became the defining test case for U.S. arms sales accountability:

- U.S.-manufactured precision-guided munitions (including Raytheon-made Paveway bombs and Lockheed Martin JDAM kits) were used in documented strikes on hospitals, schools, funerals, and wedding parties
- A 2018 CNN investigation traced the serial numbers on bomb fragments at the site of a school bus strike that killed 40 children to a Lockheed Martin MK-82 bomb sold to Saudi Arabia through the FMS process
- The UN Panel of Experts on Yemen documented numerous coalition strikes that may constitute violations of international humanitarian law, many involving U.S.-origin weapons
- Mwatana for Human Rights, a Yemeni organization, documented over 380 coalition airstrikes that hit civilian targets between 2015 and 2021
- Despite these findings, U.S. arms transfers to Saudi Arabia continued, with only brief pauses:
    - The Obama administration's 2016 PGM suspension
    - The Biden administration's 2021 temporary halt on offensive arms (later partially reversed)

### The Gaza Case Study (2023-Present)

The October 7, 2023 Hamas attack and subsequent Israeli military operations in Gaza created a second major test case:

- The U.S. authorized emergency transfers of precision-guided munitions and other weapons to Israel during active combat operations
- Documented strikes on densely populated civilian areas, hospitals, and UN facilities raised questions about compliance with international humanitarian law
- NSM-20 (February 2024) required recipient countries to provide written assurances of IHL compliance, but critics argued the mechanism lacked enforcement teeth
- Congressional efforts to condition arms sales to Israel faced bipartisan opposition, illustrating the political constraints on human rights accountability

### Key Arguments

| Argument | Proponents' Claim | Rebuttal / Counterpoint |
|---|---|---|
| **Complicity in Abuses** | Selling weapons to regimes with poor human rights records makes the U.S. complicit in the abuses they commit. U.S.-made bombs used against civilians are a direct stain on American values and international standing. | The U.S. provides training on the laws of armed conflict and can use its leverage as a supplier to encourage better behavior. If we don't sell, rivals will fill the order with no conditions at all. |
| **Empowering Autocrats** | Arms sales strengthen the security forces of authoritarian regimes, enabling them to suppress their own people and resist democratic change. Bahrain used U.S.-supplied armored vehicles during the 2011 crackdown. Egypt uses U.S. equipment to maintain an authoritarian system. | Security and stability are often prerequisites for democratic reform. In some cases, a strong military is necessary to counter external threats, creating the space for political development. |
| **Leahy Law is Insufficient** | The Leahy Law vets specific foreign units, not entire militaries or governments that are systemically abusive. Unit designations can be manipulated through reorganization. | Broader restrictions would be a blunt instrument, punishing entire nations and undermining U.S. security interests. Unit-level vetting is targeted and effective. |
| **Civilian Harm Ignored** | Post-sale civilian harm assessments are rare, incomplete, and have no binding consequences. The U.S. sells weapons, documents deaths, and continues selling. | The 2023 CHIRG and NSM-20 represent meaningful progress. The U.S. has the most rigorous arms sales process of any major exporter. |
| **International Law Violations** | Continued sales to countries credibly accused of IHL violations may itself violate U.S. obligations under international law, including the Genocide Convention and Common Article 1 of the Geneva Conventions. | The U.S. conducts its own independent legal analysis and does not accept external determinations of IHL violations as binding on transfer decisions. |

**Key Opposing Stakeholders**: Amnesty International, Human Rights Watch, Mwatana for Human Rights, Physicians for Human Rights, Win Without War, Center for Civilians in Conflict (CIVIC), and progressive foreign policy organizations.

## The Strategic Stability Critique

This critique focuses on the risk that arms sales can destabilize regions and increase the likelihood of war.

### Case Studies in Destabilization

**The Middle East Arms Spiral**:

- U.S. sales of F-35s and advanced air defense to Israel, alongside massive arms packages to Saudi Arabia, the UAE, and Qatar, have contributed to a regional arms race
- Each country seeks to match or exceed its neighbors' capabilities
- The Abraham Accords opened new arms sales channels but did not resolve underlying security competition
- The cumulative effect is a heavily armed region with multiple potential flashpoints and declining conflict resolution mechanisms

**The South China Sea and Taiwan Strait**:

- U.S. arms sales to Taiwan, while intended to deter Chinese aggression, are viewed by Beijing as provocative
- China has cited Taiwan arms sales as justification for its own military buildup and aggressive posture
- Each new Taiwan arms package triggers diplomatic crises and potential escalation risks
- The question of whether arming Taiwan deters or provokes Chinese action remains genuinely contested among strategists

**Arms Diversion and Capture**:

- Weapons transferred to Iraq were captured by ISIS during its 2014 offensive, including U.S.-origin Humvees, M16 rifles, and M1 Abrams tanks
- U.S.-origin weapons have been documented in the hands of non-state actors in Libya, Syria, and sub-Saharan Africa
- The 2021 Taliban capture of an estimated $7 billion in U.S.-provided equipment in Afghanistan represented the largest single diversion event in U.S. arms transfer history
- Bellingcat and Conflict Armament Research have traced U.S.-origin weapons to unauthorized end users in multiple conflict zones

### Key Arguments

| Argument | Proponents' Claim | Rebuttal / Counterpoint |
|---|---|---|
| **Fueling Arms Races** | Selling advanced weapons to one country in a tense region inevitably prompts rivals to seek similar or superior weapons, sparking costly and dangerous arms races. | The U.S. carefully reviews the regional military balance before any major sale. The goal is legitimate self-defense and stable deterrence, not overwhelming superiority. |
| **Moral Hazard** | Providing a partner with advanced offensive weapons can embolden reckless behavior. Saudi Arabia's intervention in Yemen was enabled by decades of U.S. arms sales. | The deep relationship from a major arms sale gives the U.S. more leverage to restrain a partner, not less. |
| **Proliferation and Diversion** | Weapons can be lost, stolen, captured, or intentionally diverted. The ISIS and Taliban captures demonstrate the scale of this risk. | The U.S. has robust EUM programs. The risk of diversion is lower than the risk of an ally collapsing due to lack of support. |
| **Technology Compromise** | Every sale of an advanced system increases the risk of adversaries gaining access through espionage, reverse engineering, or partner security failures. Turkey's purchase of the Russian S-400 while operating F-35s illustrated this risk. | The U.S. uses tiered technology release and export-variant configurations. Turkey was removed from the F-35 program as a consequence. |

**Key Opposing Stakeholders**: Arms Control Association, Quincy Institute for Responsible Statecraft, Cato Institute, Stimson Center, non-proliferation experts, and advocates for foreign policy restraint.

## The Economic and Governance Critique

### Key Arguments

| Argument | Proponents' Claim | Rebuttal / Counterpoint |
|---|---|---|
| **Corporate Welfare** | The arms sales process functions as a taxpayer-subsidized marketing and delivery service for defense contractors. The government pays for DSCA case officers, SCOs at embassies, and State licensing staff to facilitate private sales. | Arms sales lower unit costs for the U.S. military, saving taxpayer money. They support over a million jobs and contribute to the trade balance. Administrative costs are modest relative to benefits. |
| **Undermining Democracy** | The "Iron Triangle" creates a system biased toward approving sales, overriding human rights or strategic concerns in favor of economic interests. | The U.S. has the most rigorous arms sales oversight of any major exporter, with multiple checks and balances. |
| **Opacity of DCS** | DCS accounts for ~$157 billion in annual authorizations but operates with minimal transparency. Individual licenses are not publicly disclosed. | Full DCS disclosure would compromise commercial confidentiality and could provide adversaries with intelligence on allied capabilities. |
| **Offsets Undermine Industrial Base** | Offset requirements transfer technology and manufacturing know-how to foreign competitors. South Korea, Turkey, and the UAE have leveraged offsets to build competing domestic industries. | Offsets are a commercial reality. Without them, the U.S. would lose sales to competitors offering more generous industrial cooperation. |
| **Regulatory Capture** | The arms sales process is captured by industry interests through lobbying, the revolving door, and campaign contributions. The "presumption of approval" reflects this capture. | The interagency process includes multiple independent voices. Not every sale serves industry at the expense of the public interest. |

**Key Opposing Stakeholders**: Project on Government Oversight (POGO), Center for International Policy, Taxpayers for Common Sense, and government watchdog organizations.

## Opposition to Reform: The Pro-Sales Coalition

Efforts to impose greater restrictions, transparency, or human rights conditions on arms sales face formidable opposition from a coalition of economic and strategic interests.

### Defense Contractors

Defense firms oppose reforms that would slow, restrict, or add conditions to export sales:

- **Direct lobbying**: The defense sector spent approximately $140 million on lobbying in 2024, with arms export policy as a significant focus
- **Campaign contributions**: Strategically directed to members of the SFRC, HFAC, and defense appropriations subcommittees
- **Industry-funded policy analysis**: Defense firms fund think tanks (CSIS, CNAS, Atlantic Council) that produce analyses arguing for faster, less restrictive export processes
- **Jobs messaging**: Contractor communications emphasize the employment impact of export sales in specific congressional districts
- **Trade shows and marketing**: Companies invest heavily in international defense exhibitions (IDEX, DSEI, Paris Air Show) with active U.S. government support through the advocacy program

### The Pentagon

The DoD consistently advocates for faster, less restrictive arms sales processes:

- "Our allies need these capabilities now, not in five years"
- "Every month of delay pushes partners toward Russian or Chinese alternatives"
- "The FMS process is a strategic liability in the era of great power competition"
- "Interoperability with allies is essential to integrated deterrence"

The Pentagon's reform proposals typically focus on streamlining the process and reducing restrictions, not on strengthening human rights oversight or transparency.

### Hawkish Foreign Policy Think Tanks

Organizations aligned with assertive U.S. foreign policy oppose arms sales restrictions:

| Organization | Key Argument |
|---|---|
| **Foundation for Defense of Democracies (FDD)** | Arms sales to Middle East partners are essential to anti-Iran strategy |
| **American Enterprise Institute (AEI)** | Human rights conditions are naive and counterproductive |
| **Hudson Institute** | Arms sales should be used aggressively as tools of great power competition |
| **Heritage Foundation** | Human rights conditions undermine alliance relationships |
| **CSIS** | Process reform should focus on speed and competitiveness, not additional oversight |

### Key Opposition Arguments Against Reform

| Argument | Claim |
|---|---|
| **"If not us, then them"** | Any U.S. restraint will push customers to Russia or China, who sell without conditions, losing the U.S. both influence and revenue. |
| **"Reform means weakness"** | Adding conditions or transparency signals unreliability and makes the U.S. a less attractive partner. |
| **"Speed is survival"** | In great power competition, the slow U.S. process is a strategic liability that reforms would worsen. |
| **"Jobs and industrial base"** | Restrictions threaten the defense industrial base, already strained by supply chain challenges. |
| **"Allies need capabilities, not lectures"** | Conditioning sales on human rights is paternalistic and undermines sovereign partners. |
| **"The process already works"** | The U.S. has the most rigorous arms sales oversight of any major exporter; additional reforms are unnecessary. |

### Bipartisan Nature of Pro-Sales Opposition

Unlike many policy areas, opposition to arms sales reform is genuinely bipartisan:

- Republican hawks support sales as tools of alliance management and power projection
- Many Democrats support sales for economic reasons (jobs) and strategic reasons (deterring China/Russia)
- The foreign policy establishment in both parties broadly supports the current system
- Disputes are typically limited to specific sales (e.g., Saudi Arabia) rather than structural reform
- The Armed Services Committees and Foreign Affairs/Relations Committees in both chambers are populated by members with strong pro-sales incentives
- This bipartisan alignment makes structural reform particularly difficult because it cannot be framed as a partisan issue
