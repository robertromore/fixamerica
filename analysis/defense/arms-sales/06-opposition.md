# Arms Sales: Opposition

U.S. arms sales, particularly to non-democratic countries or those involved in active conflicts, face strong and persistent opposition from a range of human rights, arms control, and anti-interventionist groups.

---

## The Human Rights Critique

This is the most prominent and politically potent line of opposition.

| Argument | Proponents' Claim | Rebuttal / Counterpoint |
|---|---|---|
| **Complicity in Abuses** | Selling weapons to regimes with poor human rights records makes the U.S. complicit in the abuses they commit. U.S.-made bombs used against civilians in conflicts like the war in Yemen are a direct stain on American values. | The U.S. provides training on the laws of armed conflict and can use its leverage as a supplier to encourage better behavior. If we don't sell to them, our rivals (China, Russia) will, with no human rights conditions at all. |
| **Empowering Autocrats** | Arms sales strengthen the security forces of authoritarian regimes, enabling them to suppress their own people and resist democratic change. | Security and stability are often prerequisites for democratic reform. In some cases, a strong military is necessary to counter external threats or internal extremists, creating the space for political development. |
| **Leahy Law is Insufficient** | The Leahy Law, which vets specific foreign units for human rights violations, is too narrow. It doesn't prevent sales to an entire military or government that is systemically abusive. | Broader restrictions would be a blunt instrument, punishing entire nations and potentially undermining U.S. security interests. Vetting specific units is a more targeted and effective approach. |

**Key Opposing Stakeholders**: Amnesty International, Human Rights Watch, and many progressive foreign policy organizations.

---

## The Strategic Stability Critique

This critique focuses on the risk that arms sales can destabilize regions and increase the likelihood of war.

| Argument | Proponents' Claim | Rebuttal / Counterpoint |
|---|---|---|
| **Fueling Arms Races** | Selling advanced weapons to one country in a tense region (e.g., the Middle East) inevitably prompts its rivals to acquire similar or better weapons, sparking a costly and dangerous arms race. | The U.S. carefully reviews the regional military balance before any major sale. The goal is to provide for an ally's legitimate self-defense and maintain a stable deterrent balance, not to give any one country overwhelming superiority. |
| **Moral Hazard** | Providing a partner with advanced offensive weapons can embolden them to take risks or initiate conflicts they otherwise would have avoided, knowing they have a powerful U.S.-supplied military. | The U.S. focuses on selling defensive systems. The deep relationship that comes with a major arms sale gives the U.S. *more* leverage to restrain a partner from reckless behavior, not less. |
| **Proliferation and Diversion** | Weapons sold to a partner can be lost, stolen, or captured on the battlefield (as seen in Iraq and Afghanistan), or intentionally transferred to third parties, ending up in the hands of terrorists or adversaries. | The U.S. has robust end-use monitoring programs to track sensitive equipment. While no system is perfect, the risk of diversion is managed and is often lower than the risk of an ally collapsing due to lack of support. |

**Key Opposing Stakeholders**: Arms control associations, non-proliferation experts, and advocates for foreign policy restraint.

---

## The Economic and Governance Critique

| Argument | Proponents' Claim | Rebuttal / Counterpoint |
|---|---|---|
| **Corporate Welfare** | The arms sales process is a form of corporate welfare, where U.S. taxpayer money is used to subsidize the marketing and sale of weapons for the profit of a few large defense contractors. | Arms sales lower the unit cost of weapons for the U.S. military, saving taxpayer money. They also support over a million high-paying American jobs and contribute positively to the U.S. balance of trade. |
| **Undermining Democracy** | The powerful "Iron Triangle" of industry, DoD, and Congress creates a system with a built-in bias toward approving sales, often overriding human rights or strategic concerns in favor of economic interests. | The process includes multiple checks and balances, including mandatory review by the State Department's human rights officers and a 30-day window for Congressional disapproval. |

**Key Opposing Stakeholders**: Government watchdog groups (e.g., Project on Government Oversight) and progressive economic policy groups.
