# Arms Sales: Overview

## Overview

United States arms sales are a primary instrument of foreign policy, a cornerstone of the defense industrial base, and a subject of intense domestic and international debate. As the world's largest arms exporter, the U.S. transfers tens of billions of dollars in military equipment and services annually to allies and partners. These sales are intended to build partner capacity, enhance interoperability with U.S. forces, and strengthen diplomatic relationships. The system is bifurcated into government-to-government Foreign Military Sales (FMS) and licensed Direct Commercial Sales (DCS), both overseen by the State Department and Department of Defense.

The scale of U.S. arms transfers is staggering. In FY2023 alone, the State Department authorized approximately $238 billion in combined FMS and DCS sales, cementing America's position as the supplier of choice for allied nations from the Indo-Pacific to the Middle East to Europe. According to the Stockholm International Peace Research Institute (SIPRI), the United States accounted for approximately 42% of all global arms exports between 2019 and 2023, more than the next four largest exporters -- France, Russia, China, and Germany -- combined. This dominance is not accidental: it reflects decades of deliberate policy linking arms transfers to alliance management, regional deterrence, and industrial base preservation.

This system faces a dual crisis. First, its bureaucratic slowness is a strategic liability, as partners in need of capabilities quickly can turn to rivals like China or Russia. The average FMS case takes 2-5 years from initial request to delivery, and cases involving the most advanced systems can take far longer. Poland's urgent request for HIMARS systems following Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine, for example, highlighted the gap between the pace of geopolitical change and the speed of the U.S. sales process. Second, and more profoundly, the rise of software-defined warfare -- particularly artificial intelligence (AI), autonomy, and cyber capabilities -- threatens to make the entire industrial-age control regime obsolete. Unlike a fighter jet, an AI algorithm can be copied and repurposed, and a cyber weapon can proliferate globally in an instant. This creates unprecedented risks of unintended escalation and loss of technological advantage, demanding a fundamental rethinking of how America controls its most advanced weapons.

The human cost of arms transfers also demands attention. U.S.-manufactured munitions have been documented in strikes on civilian infrastructure in Yemen, where the Saudi-led coalition used American-made precision-guided bombs in a campaign that the United Nations described as producing the world's worst humanitarian crisis. These cases raise fundamental questions about complicity, end-use accountability, and the gap between stated policy and actual practice.

## Scope

This analysis covers the key components of U.S. arms sales and security cooperation policy:

- **Regulatory Framework**: The Arms Export Control Act (AECA), the International Traffic in Arms Regulations (ITAR), the Conventional Arms Transfer (CAT) policy, and the Missile Technology Control Regime (MTCR).
- **Sales Channels**: Foreign Military Sales (FMS), Direct Commercial Sales (DCS), and related security assistance programs including Excess Defense Articles (EDA) and the Building Partner Capacity (BPC) authorities.
- **Key Actors**: The roles of the State Department (Bureau of Political-Military Affairs), Department of Defense (DSCA), Congress (Senate Foreign Relations and House Foreign Affairs Committees), the intelligence community, and the defense industry.
- **Strategic Rationale**: Building partner capacity, alliance interoperability, integrated deterrence, and foreign policy influence.
- **Economic Impact**: The effect of arms sales on the U.S. defense industrial base, jobs, production line sustainability, and the balance of trade.
- **Major Recipients**: The dynamics of arms relationships with Saudi Arabia, Israel, Taiwan, the UAE, Japan, Australia, and European NATO allies.
- **Human Rights and Accountability**: The Leahy Laws, end-use monitoring programs, civilian harm assessments, and the tension between strategic interests and human rights obligations.
- **Emerging Technologies**: The unique challenges of controlling the export of AI, autonomous systems, cyber capabilities, and hypersonic technologies.
- **Great Power Competition**: Competition with Russia and China in the global arms market and the strategic implications of losing market share.

## Core Tensions and Tradeoffs

- **Economic Benefits vs. Human Rights**: The economic imperative to export arms and support U.S. jobs often conflicts with foreign policy goals related to promoting human rights and democracy.
    - The $110 billion Saudi arms package announced in 2017 was simultaneously celebrated as a jobs program and condemned for fueling the war in Yemen.
    - This tension is not theoretical -- it plays out in every interagency review of a sale to a country with a questionable rights record.

- **Strengthening Allies vs. Fueling Arms Races**: Providing arms to one partner can be seen as a threat by its neighbors, potentially sparking regional instability and arms races.
    - U.S. sales to Taiwan, while intended to deter Chinese aggression, are viewed by Beijing as a provocation and a violation of the "One China" framework.
    - Gulf state arms sales contribute to a regional arms spiral that increases the risk of miscalculation.

- **Interoperability vs. Technology Proliferation**: Selling advanced systems to allies allows them to fight alongside U.S. forces, but increases the risk that sensitive technology could be compromised or transferred to adversaries.
    - The compromise of F-35 technology through espionage or partner-nation security failures is a persistent concern.
    - Turkey's purchase of the Russian S-400 while operating F-35s forced the U.S. to remove Turkey from the F-35 program, demonstrating the real-world consequences of technology compromise risk.

- **Control vs. Speed**: The rigorous, multi-year review process designed to control arms transfers makes the U.S. a slow and frustrating partner compared to rivals who sell systems with fewer restrictions.
    - Russia's willingness to deliver Su-35 fighters within months, rather than years, has cost the U.S. market share in countries like Egypt and Indonesia.
    - China's no-conditions drone exports have captured markets the U.S. declined to serve.

- **Hardware vs. Software**: The current control regime is designed to track physical objects (hardware). It is fundamentally unequipped to control software, AI algorithms, and cyber tools that can be replicated and modified after transfer.
    - ITAR was written for an era of steel and semiconductors, not algorithms and cloud computing.
    - No effective framework exists for monitoring the end-use of transferred software.

- **Bipartisan Support vs. Democratic Accountability**: Arms sales enjoy broad bipartisan support in Congress, creating a political consensus that can insulate the system from scrutiny.
    - Congress has never successfully blocked a major arms sale through a joint resolution of disapproval, raising questions about the effectiveness of legislative oversight.
    - The informal pre-notification process gives key committee members de facto veto power but operates without transparency.

## Key Questions

1. How can the U.S. reform its arms sales process to be faster and more responsive without sacrificing oversight?
2. What new legal and technical frameworks are needed to control the export and use of military AI and cyber capabilities?
3. Should human rights considerations be given more weight in arms sales decisions, even if it means ceding market share to rivals?
4. How can the U.S. balance the economic need to export with the strategic risk of proliferating its most advanced technologies?
5. What role should Congress play in arms sales oversight, and is the current notification-and-disapproval framework adequate?
6. How should the U.S. respond to Russia's and China's growing share of the global arms market, particularly in regions where American influence is waning?

## Vision of Success

A successful arms sales policy would be:

- **Strategic**: Sales are driven by clear, long-term foreign policy goals, not short-term commercial interests.
    - Every major sale is tied to a documented strategic rationale reviewed at the interagency level.
    - The portfolio of sales across regions and partners reflects coherent strategic priorities, not ad hoc deal-making.

- **Agile**: The process is fast and efficient enough to be the preferred choice for U.S. partners.
    - Trusted allies can acquire standard capabilities within months, not years.
    - A tiered system ensures that the most trusted partners face the fewest bureaucratic barriers.

- **Responsible**: A rigorous and transparent process ensures that sales are consistent with U.S. values and international humanitarian law.
    - Robust monitoring of how weapons are used after delivery, with enforceable consequences for misuse.
    - Sales to countries with poor human rights records trigger enhanced scrutiny and enforceable end-use conditions.
    - Post-sale impact assessments are conducted regularly and inform future transfer decisions.

- **Modern**: A new control regime is in place to manage the unique risks of exporting software, AI, and cyber weapons.
    - Technical safeguards, licensing models, and digital end-use monitoring are standard features of all digital exports.
    - The U.S. leads international norm-setting for military AI transfers.

- **Accountable**: Congress exercises meaningful oversight, and the public has access to comprehensive data on what is being sold and to whom.
    - Both FMS and DCS data are publicly accessible in a unified database.
    - There are real consequences -- including suspension or termination -- when arms are misused.
