# Arms Sales: Solutions

## Overview

Reforming U.S. arms sales policy requires a dual approach: streamlining the process to make it more agile for trusted partners, while simultaneously strengthening oversight and creating a new framework for emerging technologies. The solutions below are designed to address the root causes identified in this analysis and are organized around four objectives: process reform, digital-age export controls, human rights accountability, and transparency. Each solution includes specific implementation mechanisms, evidence supporting its feasibility, and anticipated counterarguments.

---

## 1. Reform the Arms Transfer Process

### A. Create a Tiered, "Risk-Based" FMS Process

**Problem Addressed**: The one-size-fits-all FMS process treats sales to the United Kingdom the same as sales to countries with poor human rights records, making the U.S. slow for its closest allies and insufficiently rigorous for its most problematic customers (Root Causes 3 and 5).

**Proposal**: Replace the uniform FMS process with a tiered system that fast-tracks sales to close, trusted allies while applying greater scrutiny to others.

**Implementation**:

- **Tier 1 (Trusted Allies)**: Create a "Tier 1" list of allies based on existing technology-sharing agreements and intelligence partnerships (e.g., Five Eyes nations, Japan, key NATO members). Sales to these nations would have:
    - A presumption of approval for items below MTCR Category I thresholds
    - A 15-day Congressional notification period (reduced from 30 days)
    - Expedited interagency review (target: 90 days from LOR to LOA for standard items)
    - Pre-authorized technology release for specified categories of equipment
    - The AUKUS technology sharing framework provides a model for this approach
- **Tier 2 (Strategic Partners)**: The standard 30-day notification and review process would apply. This tier would include countries like South Korea, Poland, Israel, Singapore, and India -- nations with strong security relationships but where full Tier 1 trust has not been established.
- **Tier 3 (High-Risk Countries)**: Sales to countries with documented human rights concerns, involvement in active conflicts, or significant diversion risks would require:
    - A 45-day notification period (extended from 30 days)
    - A mandatory public human rights impact assessment prepared by DRL
    - Enhanced end-use monitoring agreements before the sale proceeds
    - Mandatory 2-year post-sale impact assessments
    - Automatic suspension triggers tied to documented civilian harm incidents

**Evidence Base**: The existing NATO+ tiered notification process (15 days vs. 30 days) already recognizes the principle that different partners warrant different levels of scrutiny. Australia's AUKUS-related reforms demonstrate the feasibility of creating expedited pathways for the most trusted allies. The UK's Consolidated Criteria for arms exports provide a model for explicit risk assessment.

**Anticipated Impact**: Dramatically accelerates sales to key allies, strengthening alliances and making the U.S. a more responsive partner, while increasing oversight where it is most needed.

### B. Increase Transparency of Direct Commercial Sales (DCS)

**Problem Addressed**: The DCS process, which accounts for the majority of U.S. arms transfer value (~$157 billion in FY2023), is far more opaque than FMS. Individual license approvals are not publicly disclosed, limiting congressional and public oversight (Root Causes 3 and 5).

**Proposal**: Mandate greater transparency for licensed commercial sales.

**Implementation**:

- Require the State Department to publish a public, searchable database of all approved DCS licenses above $1 million, including the recipient country, the general category of item (referencing the USML category), and the authorized value, within 90 days of approval
- Aggregate DCS data by country and USML category in quarterly public reports
- Require annual reporting to Congress on DCS denials, including the reason for denial and the country involved
- Harmonize DCS reporting with FMS reporting to create a comprehensive picture of total U.S. arms transfers

**Evidence Base**: The EU's Annual Report on Arms Exports provides aggregated data on all member state exports by destination and category, demonstrating that transparency is achievable without compromising commercial confidentiality. The Security Assistance Monitor (Center for International Policy) already compiles available DCS data, demonstrating public demand for this information.

**Anticipated Impact**: Allows for greater public and congressional oversight of the vast majority of U.S. arms exports, enabling more informed debate and closing a significant transparency gap.

### C. Streamline Interagency Coordination

**Problem Addressed**: The interagency review process involves multiple agencies (State, DoD, Commerce, intelligence community) with overlapping authorities and no clear timeline requirements, creating delays that can stretch for months or years (Root Cause 3).

**Proposal**:

- Establish binding timeline requirements for each stage of the interagency review: 30 days for initial assessment, 60 days for full interagency staffing, 90 days for final approval decision
- Create a digital case management system that provides real-time status tracking for all pending sales, accessible to all relevant agencies and congressional oversight committees
- Designate a single "case champion" within DSCA for each major sale, responsible for driving the case through the interagency process
- Establish an interagency escalation mechanism: if a case is not resolved within the prescribed timeline, it automatically escalates to the Deputies Committee of the National Security Council

**Evidence Base**: GAO has repeatedly found that the lack of defined timelines and clear process ownership contributes to FMS delays. The DoD's own internal reviews have identified interagency coordination as the primary bottleneck.

## 2. Create a New Framework for Digital and AI Exports

### A. Establish a "Digital Arms Control" Regime

**Problem Addressed**: The current ITAR framework is designed for hardware and is inadequate for software, AI, and cyber capabilities. There is no coherent legal or regulatory framework for controlling the export of digital weapons (Root Cause 4).

**Proposal**: Create a new, purpose-built regulatory framework for military AI and cyber exports.

**Implementation**:

- **Create a New USML Category**: Add a new category to the U.S. Munitions List specifically for "AI-enabled and autonomous military systems and related software," with subcategories for:
    - Autonomous targeting systems
    - AI-enabled intelligence, surveillance, and reconnaissance (ISR) processing
    - Autonomous unmanned systems (aerial, maritime, ground)
    - Offensive cyber tools and exploits
    - AI training data specifically curated for military applications
- **License- and Subscription-Based Transfers**: Shift the model from "selling" a copy of software to "licensing" its use. Transfers could be time-limited, requiring regular re-authorization (e.g., annual license renewal). This gives the U.S. government ongoing leverage over how the technology is used.
- **Technical "Guardrails"**: Mandate that any exported military AI system must include technical safeguards to prevent misuse or unauthorized modification:
    - Hardware security modules ("kill switches") that can remotely disable the system
    - Geofencing restrictions that limit operational areas
    - Tamper-evident logging that records all use
    - Ethical constraints (e.g., human-in-the-loop requirements for lethal applications) that cannot be removed without rendering the system inoperable
- **Classification of AI Training Data**: Establish export control categories for curated military AI training datasets, recognizing that the data used to train an AI system is often as sensitive as the algorithm itself

**Evidence Base**: The software licensing model is standard in the commercial technology industry and has been adapted for some defense applications (e.g., satellite imagery services). The AUKUS AI collaboration framework provides a multilateral model for sharing advanced AI with trusted allies under controlled conditions. Israel's experience with exporting cyber tools (including the NSO Group Pegasus scandal) illustrates the risks of uncontrolled digital arms transfers.

**Anticipated Impact**: Creates a regulatory framework that recognizes the unique nature of software and AI, giving the U.S. government greater long-term control over how its technology is used while enabling sharing with trusted allies.

### B. Develop Technical End-Use Monitoring for Digital Exports

**Problem Addressed**: Existing end-use monitoring (Golden Sentry, Blue Lantern) is designed for physical objects and cannot track the use of software or AI after transfer (Root Cause 4).

**Proposal**: Create new technical methods to monitor the use of exported software and AI.

**Implementation**:

- Require that licensed military software include a secure "heartbeat" function that regularly reports to a U.S. government or third-party server to confirm its location, operational status, and usage patterns. Tampering with this function would render the software inoperable.
- Develop "digital watermarking" techniques for AI models that allow post-deployment identification of U.S.-origin algorithms even if they have been modified
- Create a Digital End-Use Monitoring Center within DSCA, staffed with cybersecurity and AI expertise, to analyze heartbeat data and investigate anomalies
- Pilot the system with Tier 1 allies before expanding to broader deployment

**Evidence Base**: Commercial software licensing already uses similar "phone home" verification systems (e.g., Adobe Creative Cloud, Microsoft Office 365). The defense sector uses comparable techniques for some classified communication systems. Machine learning watermarking is an active area of academic research with promising results.

**Anticipated Impact**: Provides a technical means of end-use monitoring for non-physical arms, addressing a critical gap in the current system.

## 3. Strengthen Human Rights and Accountability

### A. Empower the State Department's Human Rights Vetting

**Problem Addressed**: The Bureau of Democracy, Human Rights, and Labor (DRL) is institutionally weak within the interagency process and can be overridden by strategic and economic advocates without formal accountability (Root Cause 3).

**Proposal**: Give DRL a formal veto-like authority over sales to the highest-risk recipients.

**Implementation**:

- Require the head of DRL to formally concur on any proposed sale to a country that the State Department's own annual Human Rights Reports classifies as having "significant human rights concerns" or that is involved in an active armed conflict
- If DRL does not concur, the Secretary of State must issue a formal written waiver with a detailed justification, which must be transmitted to Congress within 48 hours
- The waiver and justification must be unclassified to the maximum extent possible, enabling public accountability
- Establish a dedicated DRL arms transfer review unit with sufficient staffing (currently, DRL has only a handful of personnel assigned to arms sales review)

**Evidence Base**: The Leahy Law vetting process demonstrates that human rights review can be integrated into security assistance decisions. The Conventional Arms Transfer policy already lists human rights as a factor, but without binding procedural mechanisms, it functions as an aspiration rather than a constraint.

**Anticipated Impact**: Elevates human rights concerns within the interagency process, forcing a more explicit and accountable tradeoff between strategic goals and values.

### B. Mandate Post-Sale Civilian Harm Assessments

**Problem Addressed**: Currently, assessments of arms sales impact happen primarily before a sale. There is no systematic review of how U.S. arms are actually being used after delivery, particularly in conflict zones (Root Cause 3).

**Proposal**: Mandate regular, public assessments of how U.S.-origin weapons are used in active conflicts.

**Implementation**:

- Require the State Department and DoD to conduct and publish a joint assessment every two years on the use of U.S.-origin weapons by countries involved in active armed conflicts
- The assessment must include:
    - Documented incidents of civilian harm involving U.S.-origin weapons
    - Evaluation of the recipient's compliance with end-use agreements
    - Recommendations for changes to future sales, including suspension or additional conditions
    - Input from the intelligence community, international organizations, and credible NGO reports
- Establish automatic suspension triggers: if an assessment documents a pattern of serious civilian harm, new sales of offensive weapons to that country are automatically suspended pending a Secretarial review
- Create a dedicated civilian harm tracking unit that monitors open-source reporting and partner-provided data on incidents involving U.S.-origin weapons

**Evidence Base**: The 2023 Civilian Harm Incident Response Guidance (CHIRG) and National Security Memorandum 20 (NSM-20) requiring assessments of Israeli and other countries' use of U.S. weapons represent steps in this direction, but they are policy documents that can be rescinded by a future administration. Codifying these requirements in law would make them durable.

**Anticipated Impact**: Creates a feedback loop that can inform future sales decisions and increases accountability for how U.S. weapons are used.

### C. Strengthen and Expand the Leahy Laws

**Problem Addressed**: The Leahy Laws apply only to specific *units*, not to entire militaries or governments that systematically commit abuses. Units can be reorganized to evade Leahy restrictions (Root Cause 3).

**Proposal**:

- Expand the Leahy Law framework to include a "systemic abuse" trigger: when credible evidence indicates that an entire military or security force engages in a pattern of gross human rights violations, the prohibition extends to the entire force, not just identified units
- Require annual publication of the Leahy Law vetting database (with appropriate redactions for sources and methods), enabling public accountability
- Mandate that the "remediation" pathway requires independently verified reforms, not merely statements of intent from the recipient government
- Apply Leahy principles explicitly to DCS transactions, closing a gap in the current framework

## 4. Enhance Congressional Oversight

### A. Reform the Notification and Disapproval Process

**Problem Addressed**: The current process has never resulted in a successful congressional block of a major arms sale. The joint resolution of disapproval is subject to presidential veto, and the informal pre-notification process operates without transparency or public accountability (Root Cause 5).

**Proposal**:

- **Lower the veto threshold**: Amend the AECA to allow Congress to block a sale with a simple majority in both chambers, not subject to presidential veto (similar to the Congressional Review Act mechanism for regulations)
- **Mandatory floor votes**: Require that all congressional notifications for sales exceeding $500 million receive a recorded floor vote in both chambers, not just committee consideration
- **Public pre-notification**: Require that the substance of pre-notification briefings be summarized in a public document within 14 days, enabling public input before formal notification
- **Expand notification requirements**: Lower the DCS notification threshold to match FMS thresholds, ensuring that major commercial sales receive the same congressional scrutiny

### B. Create an Arms Transfer Oversight Board

**Problem Addressed**: No single entity within the government is responsible for comprehensive evaluation of U.S. arms transfer policy outcomes.

**Proposal**: Establish a statutory Arms Transfer Review Board, modeled on the Privacy and Civil Liberties Oversight Board (PCLOB), with the following mandate:

- Conduct annual reviews of U.S. arms transfer policy implementation
- Evaluate the effectiveness of end-use monitoring programs
- Assess the long-term strategic and humanitarian consequences of major arms sales
- Publish annual public reports with recommendations for policy changes
- Membership: 5 members appointed by the President and confirmed by the Senate, serving staggered 6-year terms, with no more than 3 from the same political party

## 5. Address Great Power Competition Dynamics

### A. Multilateral Export Control Modernization

**Problem Addressed**: Existing multilateral frameworks (Wassenaar Arrangement, MTCR) were designed for Cold War-era technologies and are inadequate for AI, cyber, and autonomous systems (Root Cause 6).

**Proposal**:

- Lead the creation of a new multilateral agreement -- an "Allied Technology Export Compact" -- among like-minded democracies (drawing from AUKUS, Five Eyes, and key NATO/Indo-Pacific partners) that establishes common standards for AI and cyber exports
- Develop shared "denied entity" lists and coordinated technology denial strategies targeting China and Russia
- Create joint allied alternatives to Chinese drone and surveillance technology exports, reducing the market opening that U.S. restrictions have created
- Invest in allied defense industrial capacity to provide customers with alternatives when U.S. systems are unavailable or when delivery timelines are unacceptable

### B. Counter Chinese Arms Market Expansion

**Problem Addressed**: U.S. restrictions on armed drone exports, combined with slow FMS processing, have ceded significant market share to China, particularly in Africa and the Middle East (Root Cause 4).

**Proposal**:

- Develop and offer a competitively priced, exportable armed drone platform designed specifically for the partner market, with appropriate technology controls built in from the design stage
- Create an "Allied Drone Initiative" that provides financing and training packages to countries currently operating or considering Chinese drones
- Offer trade-in programs for countries willing to retire Chinese-made systems in favor of U.S. or allied alternatives
