# Arms Sales: Current State

## Sales Volume and Key Customers

The United States is the world's dominant arms exporter. Sales are conducted through two primary channels: Foreign Military Sales (FMS), which are government-to-government agreements, and Direct Commercial Sales (DCS), which are licensed by the State Department directly between U.S. companies and foreign buyers.

### U.S. Arms Sales Data (FY2023)

| Category | Value | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **Foreign Military Sales (FMS)** | $80.9 billion | Government-to-government sales managed by DSCA. |
| **Direct Commercial Sales (DCS)** | $157.5 billion | Commercial sales authorized by the State Department. |
| **Total Authorized Sales** | **$238.4 billion** | |

*Source: U.S. Department of State, Bureau of Political-Military Affairs, 2024*

The FY2023 figures represent a significant increase over recent years. For comparison, combined FMS and DCS authorizations averaged approximately $160-180 billion annually between FY2018 and FY2022. The spike reflects surging demand driven by Russia's invasion of Ukraine, increased Indo-Pacific tensions, and ongoing Middle East security concerns.

## The Regulatory Process

U.S. arms sales are governed by a complex legal and regulatory framework designed to balance national security, foreign policy, and economic interests.

### Key Laws and Regulations

| Law/Regulation | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Arms Export Control Act (AECA)**, 22 U.S.C. 2751-2799 | The foundational law authorizing the President to control the import and export of defense articles and services. Establishes the congressional notification process for major sales. |
| **International Traffic in Arms Regulations (ITAR)**, 22 CFR 120-130 | The detailed regulations, administered by the State Department's Directorate of Defense Trade Controls (DDTC), that implement the AECA. Includes the U.S. Munitions List (USML), which specifies what items are controlled. |
| **Foreign Assistance Act**, 22 U.S.C. 2151 et seq. | Authorizes security assistance programs and contains key human rights provisions, including Section 502B (prohibiting security assistance to gross human rights violators) and the Leahy Law. |
| **Export Control Reform Act (ECRA)**, 50 U.S.C. 4801 et seq. | Governs "dual-use" items (those with both commercial and military applications) through the Commerce Department's Bureau of Industry and Security (BIS) and the Export Administration Regulations (EAR). |
| **Conventional Arms Transfer (CAT) Policy** | Presidential policy guidance (most recently updated in 2023) that establishes the criteria and priorities for arms transfer decisions. Not statutory but binding on the executive branch. |

### The Foreign Military Sales (FMS) Process

1. **Letter of Request (LOR)**: A partner nation submits a formal request to the U.S. government for a specific item or capability. LORs are typically developed in consultation with the U.S. Security Cooperation Organization (SCO) embedded in the U.S. embassy in the requesting country.
2. **Case Development**: The relevant U.S. military department and the Defense Security Cooperation Agency (DSCA) develop a case, including pricing, availability, delivery timeline, and associated training and support packages.
3. **State Department Review**: The Department of State reviews the proposed sale for foreign policy implications, including human rights considerations, regional stability, and consistency with U.S. treaty obligations. The Bureau of Political-Military Affairs leads this review, with input from the Bureau of Democracy, Human Rights, and Labor (DRL) and relevant regional bureaus.
4. **Congressional Notification**: For major sales exceeding statutory thresholds, the State Department must formally notify Congress. The AECA Section 36(b) thresholds are:
    - **$25 million** for major defense equipment to NATO+ countries (NATO members, Japan, Australia, South Korea, Israel, New Zealand)
    - **$100 million** for other defense articles/services to NATO+ countries
    - **$14 million** for major defense equipment to all other countries
    - **$50 million** for other defense articles/services to all other countries
    - Congress has **30 calendar days** (15 days for NATO+ allies) to pass a joint resolution of disapproval to block the sale.
5. **Letter of Offer and Acceptance (LOA)**: If Congress does not block the sale, the U.S. government sends a formal LOA to the partner nation specifying terms, prices, and conditions.
6. **Implementation**: Once the partner signs the LOA and provides payment (or financing arrangements are in place), the DoD procures the item from U.S. industry and manages delivery, training, and logistics support.

This process is notoriously slow, often taking 2-5 years from initial request to final delivery. For the most complex systems, such as fighter aircraft or integrated air defense, the timeline can extend to 7-10 years.

### Direct Commercial Sales (DCS)

DCS transactions are licensed by the State Department's Directorate of Defense Trade Controls (DDTC) but executed directly between U.S. defense companies and foreign buyers. Key characteristics:

- **Volume**: DCS authorizations consistently exceed FMS in total dollar value ($157.5 billion vs. $80.9 billion in FY2023), though FMS accounts for the larger share of major weapons platforms.
- **Scope**: DCS covers a wide range of items from major weapons to components, spare parts, technical data, and defense services.
- **Transparency**: DCS is significantly less transparent than FMS. Unlike FMS, where DSCA publishes notices of major sales, DCS license approvals are not routinely made public. The aggregate data reported by the State Department provides limited detail on individual transactions.
- **Congressional Oversight**: DCS is subject to congressional notification for major sales, but the notification thresholds are higher and the process provides less information than FMS notifications.

### The "Informal" Pre-Notification Process

In practice, the formal congressional notification is preceded by an informal pre-notification process in which the State Department briefs key committee staff on upcoming sales. If significant opposition emerges during this informal period, the administration often delays or modifies the sale to avoid a formal confrontation. This process gives a small number of senior committee members and staff effective veto power over arms sales, though it operates largely outside public view.

## End-Use Monitoring (EUM)

The U.S. government has programs to ensure that U.S.-origin defense articles are used only for their intended purposes and are not transferred to third parties without authorization.

### Golden Sentry Program

- **Run by**: DSCA
- **Focus**: Monitors sensitive defense articles transferred through FMS
- **Methods**: Routine inventories, physical inspections, and serial number verification of sensitive U.S. equipment in foreign countries
- **Coverage**: Approximately 100 countries with active monitoring agreements
- **Staffing**: Relies heavily on Security Cooperation Officers (SCOs) at U.S. embassies, who often have competing duties

### Blue Lantern Program

- **Run by**: State Department (DDTC)
- **Focus**: Pre-license and post-shipment checks on Direct Commercial Sales
- **Methods**: Verifies the bona fides of foreign parties, confirms receipt of items, checks for diversion
- **Volume**: Approximately 600-800 end-use checks per year
- **Results**: Identifies approximately 10-15% of checked transactions as "unfavorable," meaning evidence of diversion or other violations

### Enhanced End-Use Monitoring (EEUM)

For the most sensitive items -- such as advanced missiles, night-vision devices, and encryption equipment -- the U.S. requires Enhanced End-Use Monitoring agreements that impose additional inspection rights, storage requirements, and usage restrictions on the recipient country.

### Limitations of Current EUM

- **Resource constraints**: The Golden Sentry and Blue Lantern programs cover only a fraction of total transfers. GAO has repeatedly found that EUM is under-resourced relative to the volume of arms being transferred.
- **Access challenges**: Some recipient countries restrict or delay U.S. access for monitoring inspections, particularly in sensitive operational areas.
- **Conflict zone blindness**: During active conflicts (e.g., Yemen, Syria), end-use monitoring becomes practically impossible, precisely when the risk of misuse is highest.
- **Software gap**: EUM is designed for physical hardware -- a tank, a missile, a rifle. There is currently no effective technical or policy framework for "Digital End-Use Monitoring" of software, AI algorithms, or cyber tools once they have been transferred.
- **Accountability gap**: Even when violations are documented, consequences for recipient countries are rare. Suspension or termination of sales is politically costly and therefore seldom invoked.

## The Leahy Laws

The Leahy Laws (named for Senator Patrick Leahy) are the primary legal mechanism for incorporating human rights considerations into arms sales decisions.

- **State Department Leahy Law** (22 U.S.C. 2378d): Prohibits the State Department from furnishing assistance to any unit of foreign security forces if the Secretary of State has credible information that the unit has committed a gross violation of human rights (GVHR).
- **DoD Leahy Law** (10 U.S.C. 362): Imposes a parallel prohibition on DoD-funded training and equipment.
- **Remediation**: Both laws include a remediation clause allowing assistance to resume if the recipient government takes "effective steps" to bring the responsible members to justice.

**Limitations**: The Leahy Laws apply to specific *units*, not to entire militaries or governments. A military that systematically commits human rights abuses may continue to receive U.S. arms as long as the specific units involved are identified and excluded -- a process that critics argue is both narrow and easily circumvented through unit reorganization.

## Global Arms Market Competition

### U.S. Market Share vs. Competitors (2019-2023)

| Exporter | Global Market Share | Key Customers | Competitive Advantage |
|---|---|---|---|
| **United States** | ~42% | Saudi Arabia, Japan, Australia, NATO | Most advanced systems, interoperability, long-term support |
| **France** | ~11% | India, Qatar, UAE, Egypt | Rafale fighter, no ITAR restrictions, fewer political conditions |
| **Russia** | ~11% | India, China, Egypt, Algeria | Low cost, no human rights conditions, rapid delivery |
| **China** | ~5.8% | Pakistan, Bangladesh, Myanmar, Sub-Saharan Africa | Low cost, no political conditions, drone exports |
| **Germany** | ~4.2% | South Korea, Greece, Hungary, Egypt | Submarines, armored vehicles |

*Source: SIPRI, 2024.*

Russia's invasion of Ukraine has significantly damaged its arms export position, as sanctions have disrupted its supply chains and its military equipment has performed poorly in combat, damaging its brand. China, meanwhile, has been aggressively expanding its arms exports, particularly in armed drones -- a market the U.S. largely ceded due to restrictive MTCR policies. Chinese-made Wing Loong and CH-series drones now operate in over 15 countries.

## Emerging Technology Sales

The sale of AI-enabled systems, autonomous drones, and cyber tools is a growing but poorly tracked part of U.S. arms transfers.

### Key Categories

- **AI-Enabled ISR**: Systems that use AI to automatically detect and identify targets from drone or satellite footage (e.g., Project Maven derivatives). Multiple allies have expressed interest in acquiring these capabilities.
- **Autonomous Systems**: Unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) and unmanned maritime vessels with high degrees of autonomy. The U.S. has relaxed restrictions on armed drone exports under the 2020 reinterpretation of the MTCR, but remains more restrictive than China.
- **Cyber Defense Tools**: Software and hardware for network defense, intrusion detection, and cyber forensics. The U.S. sells cyber tools through both FMS and DCS, though the scale is not publicly reported.
- **Space-Based Systems**: Satellite imagery, space domain awareness tools, and missile warning data shared with allies through a growing network of bilateral agreements.

### The Regulatory Gap

- **ITAR's Limitations**: ITAR was designed to control hardware. It is unclear how it applies to a line of code or a trained AI model. An algorithm can be transferred electronically in seconds and is difficult to track.
- **Dual-Use Problem**: Many AI and cyber technologies have both commercial and military applications, blurring the line between the Commerce Department's less restrictive Export Administration Regulations (EAR) and the State Department's more stringent ITAR controls. The ongoing USML-to-CCL transfer process has moved some defense items to the less restrictive Commerce Control List, but for AI the categorization remains contested.
- **Intangible Transfers**: The "transfer" can occur simply by training a foreign partner or allowing them to view the source code, making traditional export controls based on physical shipments irrelevant.
- **Wassenaar Arrangement Gaps**: The multilateral Wassenaar Arrangement on Export Controls for Conventional Arms and Dual-Use Goods and Technologies provides some framework for coordinating controls among 42 participating states, but it is non-binding and lacks enforcement mechanisms for emerging technologies.

## The Israel Special Relationship

Israel occupies a unique position in U.S. arms sales. Under a series of Memoranda of Understanding (MOUs), the United States provides Israel with approximately $3.8 billion annually in Foreign Military Financing (FMF) -- grants that Israel uses to purchase U.S. defense equipment. Israel is the only country permitted to spend a portion of its FMF (historically ~26%, now being phased out) on domestically produced defense items rather than U.S. products. Israel also receives access to U.S. technology at levels exceeding those of most NATO allies, including the F-35I variant with unique Israeli modifications.

The October 7, 2023 Hamas attack and subsequent Israeli military operations in Gaza have reignited intense debate over U.S. arms transfers to Israel, including emergency transfers of precision-guided munitions, the applicability of the Leahy Laws, and the adequacy of end-use monitoring in an active conflict zone.

## Current Policy Framework: The 2023 CAT Policy

The Biden administration issued a revised Conventional Arms Transfer (CAT) policy in February 2023 that updated the framework for arms sales decisions. Key features:

- **Human rights emphasis**: Elevated civilian harm and human rights considerations as factors in transfer decisions
- **Climate considerations**: For the first time, included climate change as a factor in security cooperation planning
- **Strategic competition**: Maintained emphasis on arms sales as a tool of great power competition
- **Process reform**: Directed interagency efforts to streamline the FMS process for priority partners
- **No structural overhaul**: Did not create new authorities for AI/cyber exports or fundamentally restructure the oversight process
