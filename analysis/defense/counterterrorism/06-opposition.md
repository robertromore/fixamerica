# Counterterrorism: Opposition

U.S. counterterrorism policy, particularly its reliance on targeted killing, broad legal authorities, mass surveillance, and indefinite detention, faces sustained opposition from a diverse coalition of civil liberties advocates, human rights organizations, proponents of foreign policy restraint, and increasingly from within the national security establishment itself. This opposition spans the political spectrum and targets distinct aspects of CT policy, making it both fragmented and persistent.

---

## The Legal and Constitutional Critique

This opposition focuses on the legal foundation for the "war on terror," primarily the 2001 Authorization for Use of Military Force (AUMF) and the broader framework of executive war powers.

| Argument | Proponents' Claim | Rebuttal / Counterpoint |
|---|---|---|
| **The "Blank Check" Argument** | The 2001 AUMF, intended for the perpetrators of 9/11, has been stretched beyond recognition to justify war against groups that didn't exist on 9/11, in countries far from Afghanistan. It has become a "blank check" for endless, global war with no congressional oversight. By 2025, the AUMF will have been in force for nearly a quarter century -- longer than any previous U.S. war authorization. | The terrorist threat has evolved, and the AUMF must be interpreted with flexibility to allow the President to pursue new "associated forces" as they emerge. Repealing it without a replacement would tie the President's hands and endanger national security. The "associated forces" doctrine has been upheld by federal courts in habeas proceedings. |
| **Violation of Separation of Powers** | By relying on a 20+ year-old authorization, the executive branch has usurped Congress's constitutional power to declare war. The "war" is now whatever the President says it is. Four successive administrations (Bush, Obama, Trump, Biden) have used the AUMF to justify operations in at least 22 countries. | Congress has had over 20 years to repeal or amend the AUMF. By continuing to fund operations under the AUMF, Congress has given its implicit consent. The Supreme Court in *Hamdi v. Rumsfeld* (2004) recognized the AUMF as authorizing the use of military force. |
| **Section 702 FISA Surveillance** | Section 702 of the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act, reauthorized in 2024, allows warrantless surveillance of foreign targets' communications, but inevitably sweeps up vast quantities of Americans' communications ("incidental collection"). FBI queries of the Section 702 database for information about U.S. persons -- "backdoor searches" -- circumvent the Fourth Amendment's warrant requirement. | Section 702 is essential to national security and has contributed to disrupting numerous terrorist plots. The FISA Court provides judicial oversight, and minimization procedures protect Americans' privacy. The 2024 reauthorization added new safeguards including warrant requirements for certain FBI queries. |

**Key Opposing Stakeholders**: American Civil Liberties Union (ACLU), Brennan Center for Justice, the Liberty and National Security Program at the Brennan Center, the Constitution Project, and a bipartisan coalition of members of Congress (including Senators Tim Kaine, Todd Young, Rand Paul, and Chris Murphy) who advocate for repealing and replacing the AUMF.

**Key Legal Cases**:

- *Hamdi v. Rumsfeld*, 542 U.S. 507 (2004): Established that U.S. citizens detained as enemy combatants retain the right to challenge their detention.
- *Boumediene v. Bush*, 553 U.S. 723 (2008): Extended habeas corpus rights to Guantanamo detainees.
- *Al-Aulaqi v. Obama* (D.D.C. 2010): Dismissed a challenge to the targeted killing of Anwar al-Awlaki on political question grounds.
- *Ziglar v. Abbasi*, 582 U.S. __ (2017): Barred claims by post-9/11 detainees against government officials, citing national security.

## The Human Rights and Ethical Critique

This critique focuses on the methods of modern CT, especially drone warfare, detention, and the use of AI in targeting.

| Argument | Proponents' Claim | Rebuttal / Counterpoint |
|---|---|---|
| **Civilian Casualties ("Bug Splat")** | Drone strikes inevitably kill innocent civilians. The U.S. government systematically undercounts these casualties and fails to provide accountability or compensation. Airwars estimates that U.S.-led coalition strikes in Iraq and Syria alone killed between 8,000 and 13,000 civilians. The August 2021 Kabul strike that killed 10 civilians (including 7 children) while targeting zero terrorists epitomizes the problem. The Pentagon initially called it a "righteous strike" and no one was disciplined. | Drones are the most precise weapon in history. While tragic, civilian casualties are far lower than they would be with conventional bombing or ground combat. The U.S. takes extraordinary measures to avoid civilian harm, including the Presidential Policy Guidance "near certainty" standard and the development of low-collateral-damage munitions like the R9X. |
| **Targeted Killing is Extrajudicial Execution** | The targeted killing of individuals, including U.S. citizens like Anwar al-Awlaki, outside of a recognized battlefield and without due process is a form of extrajudicial execution that violates both U.S. and international law. The government's own "disposition matrix" -- a classified kill list -- operates with no judicial review and minimal congressional oversight. The OLC's legal memos justifying the killing of U.S. citizens were classified for years. | The U.S. is in a state of armed conflict with terrorist groups like al-Qaeda and ISIS. Under the laws of war, enemy combatants are legitimate targets wherever they are found. Due process does not mean a criminal trial for an enemy combatant planning attacks. The Supreme Court in *Hamdi* acknowledged the government's authority to detain (and by extension, target) enemy combatants. |
| **Guantanamo and Indefinite Detention** | Holding individuals for 20+ years without trial violates fundamental due process rights and international humanitarian law. The military commission process has been a failure -- no major 9/11 trial has been completed despite decades of proceedings. Guantanamo has become a global symbol of American hypocrisy on human rights and a propaganda asset for terrorist organizations. The facility costs over $500 million per year for approximately 30 detainees. | Some Guantanamo detainees are too dangerous to release and cannot be tried in civilian courts due to the tainted evidence (obtained through "enhanced interrogation") and the inability to present classified sources in open proceedings. The detainees are held under the law of war, which permits detention for the duration of hostilities. |
| **AI Dehumanizes Killing** | Using AI to help identify targets ("algorithmic warfare") removes human judgment and moral reasoning from the decision to use lethal force. It creates a risk of automation bias and catastrophic errors. Reports about Israel's AI targeting system "Lavender" in Gaza -- alleged to generate targeting recommendations with only seconds of human review -- illustrate the danger. | AI is a tool to assist human analysts, not replace them. It helps find needles in a haystack of data. A human operator always makes the final decision to fire, consistent with DoD policy on "meaningful human control" (DoDD 3000.09). AI-assisted targeting can actually reduce civilian casualties by improving the accuracy of intelligence. |

**Key Opposing Stakeholders**: Amnesty International, Human Rights Watch, Reprieve (which represents Guantanamo detainees), the Center for Constitutional Rights, the Campaign to Stop Killer Robots (now "Stop Killer Robots"), and investigative journalism outlets like the Bureau of Investigative Journalism, Airwars, *The Intercept* (whose "Drone Papers" series published leaked targeting documents), and *+972 Magazine*.

## The Strategic "Restraint" Critique

This critique argues that the entire CT enterprise is a strategic overreach that harms U.S. interests, and that the threat has been systematically inflated to justify a permanent wartime footing.

| Argument | Proponents' Claim | Rebuttal / Counterpoint |
|---|---|---|
| **CT is a Strategic Distraction** | The obsessive focus on a low-level threat from terrorist groups distracts attention and resources from the far more significant challenge of great power competition with China and Russia. Terrorism has killed approximately 100 Americans per year since 9/11 (including domestic terrorism), while the U.S. has spent trillions of dollars and deployed its best forces on CT missions. The risk-reward calculation is wildly disproportionate. | The OTH strategy is specifically designed to *prevent* distraction. It is an economy-of-force measure that manages the CT threat with minimal resources, allowing the rest of the DoD to focus on GPC. Moreover, another 9/11-scale attack would be catastrophic not just in lives lost but in its political and economic consequences. |
| **"Mowing the Grass" is Endless** | The current strategy of killing individual terrorists is like "mowing the grass." It deals with the symptom but not the cause. It creates a self-perpetuating cycle of strikes and radicalization without ever ending the threat. The U.S. has killed or captured dozens of top-tier terrorist leaders since 2001, yet the threat has metastasized from one group in one country to dozens of groups across multiple continents. | The goal is not to eliminate terrorism entirely, which is impossible. The goal is to keep constant pressure on terrorist groups to prevent them from developing the capability to launch another 9/11-style attack on the U.S. homeland. "Mowing the grass" is not a failure if it keeps the grass short enough to manage. |
| **Blowback** | U.S. drone strikes and support for partner forces in places like Somalia, Syria, and Yemen are seen as foreign intervention, which fuels anti-American sentiment and acts as a recruiting tool for the very groups the U.S. is trying to fight. The concept of "blowback" -- the unintended consequences of covert operations -- has been documented by the CIA itself. The Iraq War is the most catastrophic example: the U.S. invasion destabilized the region and directly led to the creation of ISIS. | The alternative -- doing nothing and allowing terrorist groups to establish safe havens -- is far more dangerous. The risk of "blowback" from targeted action is less than the risk of a catastrophic attack planned from an uncontested sanctuary. And the "blowback" argument can be used to oppose any military action, which would effectively cede the initiative to terrorists. |
| **Threat Inflation** | The CT establishment has a structural interest in inflating the terrorist threat because higher threat perceptions justify larger budgets, broader authorities, and continued institutional relevance. The "terrorism-industrial complex" -- including CT agencies, defense contractors, and think tanks -- collectively benefits from maintaining a state of permanent emergency. | The threat is real, as demonstrated by ongoing attacks and disrupted plots. Dismissing the threat as "inflated" is easy when attacks are being prevented by the very CT apparatus that critics want to reduce. Intelligence professionals risk their lives to protect the country and should not be accused of bad faith. |

**Key Opposing Stakeholders**: Quincy Institute for Responsible Statecraft, Cato Institute, the Stimson Center, academic proponents of "restraint" foreign policy (including Stephen Walt, John Mearsheimer, and Andrew Bacevich), and non-interventionist voices in both the libertarian right and progressive left.

## The Domestic CT and Civil Liberties Critique

A distinct body of opposition focuses specifically on the domestic counterterrorism and surveillance apparatus:

- **Muslim Community Groups**: Organizations like the Council on American-Islamic Relations (CAIR), the Muslim Public Affairs Council (MPAC), and the American-Arab Anti-Discrimination Committee (ADC) argue that CT policies, particularly CVE programs and FBI investigations, disproportionately target Muslim Americans. They cite the FBI's use of informants in mosque surveillance, "sting" operations that critics describe as entrapment, and the broad Section 702 collection that sweeps up Muslim Americans' communications.
- **Privacy Advocates**: The Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF), Electronic Privacy Information Center (EPIC), and the ACLU's National Security Project challenge the mass surveillance apparatus built after 9/11, including bulk metadata collection (revealed by Snowden in 2013), Section 702 collection, and the TSA's Quiet Skies surveillance program.
- **Political Activists**: Across the political spectrum, there are concerns that the domestic CT apparatus could be turned against political movements. Left-leaning groups point to FBI surveillance of Black Lives Matter activists; right-leaning groups argue that the post-January 6 investigations represent political targeting. Both concerns reflect the historical reality that government surveillance powers, once created for CT purposes, tend to expand into political monitoring.

## The Bipartisan and Cross-Spectrum Nature of Opposition

Unlike many policy areas, opposition to CT policy spans the political spectrum:

- **The libertarian right** (Rand Paul, Thomas Massie, Cato Institute) opposes the AUMF, mass surveillance, and executive war powers on constitutional and individual liberty grounds.
- **The progressive left** (Barbara Lee, Ilhan Omar, Quincy Institute) opposes drone strikes, Guantanamo, and the militarization of CT on human rights and anti-war grounds.
- **National security realists** (restraint school) argue on strategic grounds that CT overreach undermines great power competition.
- **Muslim and civil liberties communities** oppose the domestic surveillance and CVE infrastructure on grounds of religious freedom, racial profiling, and Fourth Amendment rights.

This cross-spectrum opposition creates potential for unusual coalitions on specific reforms (such as AUMF repeal or surveillance reform) but also means that the opposition is fragmented, with different groups prioritizing different issues and sometimes working at cross-purposes.
