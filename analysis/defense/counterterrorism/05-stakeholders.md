# Counterterrorism: Stakeholders

The U.S. counterterrorism enterprise is a vast and complex network of government agencies, military commands, intelligence organizations, and private sector partners. It is one of the largest national security enterprises ever created, consuming hundreds of billions of dollars annually and employing hundreds of thousands of people across government, the military, and the private sector. Understanding the stakeholders -- their interests, their leverage, and their conflicts -- is essential to evaluating CT policy and designing viable reforms.

## U.S. Government Stakeholders

### Executive Branch Leadership

| Stakeholder | Primary Interest | Role in the CT Enterprise |
|---|---|---|
| **The President & National Security Council (NSC)** | Preventing attacks on the U.S. homeland; setting the legal and policy framework for CT operations; managing the political risks of CT decisions. | **Decision-Maker.** Makes final decisions on high-value targets through the "disposition matrix" process and approves CT policy. The NSC's CT Directorate coordinates interagency CT policy. |
| **The National Security Advisor** | Coordinating CT policy across agencies; managing the intelligence-to-action pipeline. | **Coordinator.** Chairs Deputies Committee and Principals Committee meetings on CT issues. Key gatekeeper for CT decisions reaching the President. |

### Department of Defense

| Stakeholder | Primary Interest | Role in the CT Enterprise |
|---|---|---|
| **Secretary of Defense** | Executing the military aspects of CT while balancing CT demands against GPC priorities. | **Policy Authority.** Approves deployment orders, strike authorities, and rules of engagement for CT operations. |
| **U.S. Special Operations Command (SOCOM)** | Leading the direct action, unconventional warfare, and partner force training components of CT. Annual budget exceeds $14 billion. | **Primary Military Actor.** Plans and conducts special operations missions globally. Its sub-component, the Joint Special Operations Command (JSOC), based at Fort Liberty (formerly Fort Bragg), North Carolina, is responsible for the most sensitive counterterrorism missions, including the raids that killed bin Laden, al-Baghdadi, and other high-value targets. |
| **U.S. Central Command (CENTCOM)** | Overseeing military operations in the Middle East and Central Asia, including CT in Iraq, Syria, and Afghanistan. | **Theater Commander.** CENTCOM has responsibility for the geographic area containing the densest concentration of CT operations. The CENTCOM commander approves most strike decisions in the region. |
| **U.S. Africa Command (AFRICOM)** | Overseeing CT operations across Africa, particularly in Somalia and the Sahel. | **Theater Commander.** Manages the growing CT mission in Africa, including operations against al-Shabaab and ISIS affiliates. Operates from a headquarters in Stuttgart, Germany, with forward-deployed assets at Camp Lemonnier, Djibouti. |
| **U.S. Cyber Command** | Conducting offensive and defensive cyber operations against terrorist organizations. | **Cyber Operator.** Conducts operations to disrupt terrorist communications, propaganda, and financing networks. Joint Task Force ARES was specifically created for anti-ISIS cyber operations. |

### The Intelligence Community

| Stakeholder | Primary Interest | Role in the CT Enterprise |
|---|---|---|
| **National Counterterrorism Center (NCTC)** | Integrating all-source intelligence on terrorism; maintaining the terrorist watchlist and the TIDE database (1.6+ million records). Created by the Intelligence Reform and Terrorism Prevention Act of 2004. | **Integrator.** The NCTC is the central node for CT intelligence analysis. It produces strategic threat assessments and maintains the Terrorist Screening Database used for watchlisting and travel screening. |
| **Central Intelligence Agency (CIA)** | Collecting human intelligence on terrorist organizations; conducting covert action, including targeted killing under Title 50 authorities. | **Collector and Covert Actor.** The CIA's Counterterrorism Center (CTC) has been the lead agency for many of the most significant CT operations since 9/11, including the drone programs in Pakistan and Yemen and the intelligence operation that located bin Laden. The CIA's paramilitary Special Activities Center (SAC) conducts direct action missions. |
| **National Security Agency (NSA)** | Collecting signals intelligence on terrorist communications worldwide. | **Signals Intelligence Provider.** The NSA's global SIGINT capabilities are the foundation of intelligence-driven CT targeting. Programs like PRISM and upstream collection under Section 702 of FISA provide access to communications data that is essential for finding and tracking terrorist operatives. |
| **National Geospatial-Intelligence Agency (NGA)** | Providing geospatial intelligence and imagery analysis for CT targeting. | **Geospatial Intelligence Provider.** NGA analysts provide the imagery analysis that identifies targets, maps terrorist infrastructure, and supports strike assessment. |
| **Defense Intelligence Agency (DIA)** | Providing military intelligence on terrorist organizations and their capabilities. | **Military Intelligence Provider.** DIA analysts support SOCOM and combatant commands with intelligence on terrorist order of battle, capabilities, and intentions. |

### Law Enforcement and Homeland Security

| Stakeholder | Primary Interest | Role in the CT Enterprise |
|---|---|---|
| **Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI)** | Investigating and prosecuting terrorism cases within the U.S. and against U.S. persons abroad; preventing domestic terrorist attacks. | **Lead Domestic CT Agency.** The FBI's Counterterrorism Division, Joint Terrorism Task Forces (JTTFs, operating in over 200 cities), and the Terrorist Screening Center are the backbone of domestic CT. The FBI has over 2,700 open domestic terrorism investigations. |
| **Department of Homeland Security (DHS)** | Protecting the homeland from terrorist attacks through border security, aviation security, and intelligence sharing. | **Homeland Protector.** DHS components include the Transportation Security Administration (TSA), Customs and Border Protection (CBP), the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA), and the Center for Prevention Programs and Partnerships (CP3). |
| **Department of Justice (DOJ)** | Prosecuting terrorism cases in federal court; providing legal opinions on CT authorities. | **Prosecutor and Legal Advisor.** The DOJ's National Security Division prosecutes international and domestic terrorism cases. The Office of Legal Counsel (OLC) provides classified legal opinions on the scope of presidential CT authority. |

### Diplomatic and Financial

| Stakeholder | Primary Interest | Role in the CT Enterprise |
|---|---|---|
| **Department of State** | Leading the diplomatic and foreign policy aspects of CT; designating Foreign Terrorist Organizations (FTOs); building international CT coalitions. | **Diplomat.** The Bureau of Counterterrorism manages CT diplomacy, the Global Coalition to Defeat ISIS (85+ member nations), and Countering Violent Extremism (CVE) programs abroad. The State Department's Rewards for Justice program offers bounties of up to $25 million for information on terrorist leaders. |
| **Department of the Treasury** | Disrupting terrorist financing through sanctions, designations, and financial intelligence. | **Financial Warfare Operator.** The Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC) and the Office of Terrorism and Financial Intelligence (TFI) impose sanctions on terrorist groups and their financiers. Treasury's Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN) tracks suspicious financial transactions. |

### Congressional Oversight

| Stakeholder | Primary Interest | Role in the CT Enterprise |
|---|---|---|
| **Senate and House Intelligence Committees** | Overseeing the intelligence community's CT activities, including covert action programs. | **Intelligence Overseer.** These committees receive classified briefings on CT operations, approve covert action findings, and control the intelligence community's budget. |
| **Senate and House Armed Services Committees** | Authorizing military CT operations and SOCOM funding. | **Military Overseer.** The NDAA and its provisions shape the legal authorities, force structure, and funding for military CT. |
| **Senate Foreign Relations / House Foreign Affairs Committees** | Overseeing the diplomatic and foreign policy dimensions of CT, including AUMFs. | **War Powers Overseer.** These committees have jurisdiction over Authorizations for Use of Military Force, making them central to the AUMF reform debate. |

## Internal Conflicts and Turf Wars

- **DoD vs. CIA (Title 10 vs. Title 50)**: A long-standing rivalry over control of lethal CT operations, particularly drone strike programs. The DoD operates under the laws of war (Title 10 of the U.S. Code), while the CIA operates under intelligence authorities (Title 50). Title 10 operations are subject to the laws of armed conflict and Congressional Armed Services Committee oversight; Title 50 covert actions are reported to the Intelligence Committees. The Obama administration shifted more strike authority from CIA to DoD for greater transparency; the Trump administration reversed some of these changes.
- **Executive Branch vs. Congress**: A perpetual struggle over war powers. The executive branch has consistently used a broad interpretation of the 2001 AUMF to maximize its operational flexibility, while many in Congress argue this has sidelined their constitutional duty to declare war. Congressional efforts to repeal or replace the AUMF have repeatedly failed due to a combination of executive resistance, congressional reluctance to take politically risky votes, and disagreement over what a replacement should look like.
- **SOCOM vs. Conventional Forces ("Big Army")**: A budgetary and cultural tension. SOCOM's budget grew from approximately $3 billion in 2001 to over $14 billion by 2024. As the DoD pivots to GPC, there is pressure to shift funds from SOF to conventional forces and platforms (ships, aircraft, tanks) optimized for great power conflict. Conventional force advocates argue that the SOF-centric CT model has come at the expense of readiness for large-scale conventional warfare.
- **FBI vs. DHS (Domestic CT)**: Jurisdictional tension over domestic CT responsibilities. The FBI has traditionally led domestic CT investigations through its JTTFs, but DHS has expanded its CT role through the creation of the Intelligence and Analysis office and CP3. The two agencies have different institutional cultures: the FBI is investigative and law enforcement-oriented; DHS is more focused on prevention and protection.
- **NCTC vs. CIA CTC**: The NCTC was created to be the central integrator of CT intelligence, but the CIA's Counterterrorism Center retains its own analytical and operational capabilities. Turf battles over analytical primacy and information sharing persist.

## External Stakeholders

| Stakeholder | Primary Interest | Source of Power |
|---|---|---|
| **Foreign Partner Forces** (e.g., Syrian Democratic Forces, Somali Danab Brigade, Iraqi ICTS, Kurdish Peshmerga) | Receiving U.S. training, equipment, and air support to fight terrorist groups in their own countries. Their interests do not always align with U.S. CT objectives -- the SDF, for example, is primarily focused on Kurdish autonomy. | Provide the ground forces that the U.S. is no longer willing to commit. Without these partners, the OTH model collapses. |
| **Allied Intelligence Services** (UK MI6/GCHQ, Australian ASD, Canadian CSE, French DGSE, Israeli Mossad) | Intelligence sharing, joint operations, and maintaining the international CT coalition. The "Five Eyes" intelligence alliance (US, UK, Australia, Canada, New Zealand) is the backbone of CT intelligence sharing. | Provide intelligence access, basing, and capabilities that the U.S. cannot replicate unilaterally. |
| **Private Technology Companies** (Palantir, Anduril, L3Harris, Northrop Grumman, General Atomics) | Securing lucrative DoD and IC contracts for AI, cloud computing, drone platforms, and data analysis services. | Ownership of the core technologies (AI models, cloud infrastructure, drone platforms) that modern CT depends on. General Atomics is the manufacturer of the MQ-9 Reaper. |
| **Social Media Platforms** (Meta, Google/YouTube, X, Telegram, TikTok) | Balancing content moderation (removing terrorist content) with user engagement, free speech principles, and global legal compliance. | Control the digital infrastructure through which radicalization and recruitment occur. Their moderation policies and algorithm designs shape the information environment. |
| **Human Rights and Civil Liberties Groups** (ACLU, Amnesty International, Human Rights Watch, Reprieve, Center for Constitutional Rights) | Protecting civil liberties; ensuring U.S. CT operations comply with international law; transparency regarding civilian casualties; challenging Guantanamo detention and military commissions. | Moral authority; ability to conduct investigations, file lawsuits (e.g., ACLU's challenges to the drone program and NSA surveillance), generate media attention, and influence public opinion. |
| **The American Public** | Security from terrorist attacks without sacrificing core values or becoming entangled in "endless wars." | Public opinion, which ultimately shapes the political will for CT operations. Polling consistently shows that Americans want to be safe but oppose large-scale military deployments and indefinite wars. |
| **Terrorist Groups** (ISIS, al-Qaeda, affiliates) | Survival, recruitment, territorial control, and carrying out attacks to advance their ideological goals. | Ability to adapt tactics, exploit grievances, use the internet for propaganda and recruitment, and regenerate after leadership losses. Their resilience is the fundamental driver of continued CT operations. |

## Power Analysis

| Stakeholder | Political Power | Economic Resources | Information Access | Organizational Capacity |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| President / NSC | Very High | Very High (budget authority) | Very High | Very High |
| Intelligence Community (CIA, NSA, NCTC) | Very High | High ($90B+ IC budget) | Very High | Very High |
| DoD / SOCOM | Very High | Very High ($14B+ SOCOM budget) | Very High | Very High |
| FBI (domestic CT) | High | Medium ($10B+ FBI budget) | High | High |
| Congress (Intelligence/Armed Services) | High | N/A | High (classified access) | Medium |
| Defense/tech contractors | Medium-High | High | Medium-High | High |
| Foreign partner forces | Medium | Low | Medium | Medium (varies) |
| Civil liberties / human rights groups | Low-Medium | Low | Medium | Medium |
| American public | Low (diffuse) | N/A | Low | Very Low |

The fundamental characteristic of the CT stakeholder landscape is the enormous concentration of power in the executive branch -- the President, NSC, intelligence community, and military -- with relatively weak congressional oversight and minimal public transparency. This imbalance is a root cause of the accountability deficits in CT policy.
