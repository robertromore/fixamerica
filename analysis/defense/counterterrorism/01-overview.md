# Counterterrorism: Overview

## Overview

More than two decades after the September 11th attacks, counterterrorism (CT) remains an enduring national security priority for the United States. However, the strategy has shifted dramatically. The era of large-scale, nation-building ground wars in the Middle East has given way to an "over-the-horizon" approach that relies on a smaller footprint, intelligence-driven targeting, and advanced technology. This new model seeks to manage a persistent, dispersed threat from groups like ISIS and al-Qaeda without detracting from the primary strategic focus on great power competition with China and Russia.

The scale of the post-9/11 CT enterprise has been staggering. The Costs of War Project at Brown University estimates that the United States has spent over $8 trillion on post-9/11 wars and related homeland security measures, with over 7,000 U.S. service members killed in Iraq and Afghanistan and an estimated 900,000 total deaths across all conflict zones. These wars reshaped the federal budget, the intelligence community, the military's force structure, and the relationship between executive power and congressional oversight.

This shift is enabled and complicated by emerging technologies. Artificial intelligence (AI) is used to sift through vast amounts of surveillance data to find threats, while autonomous systems (drones) provide persistent surveillance and precision strike capabilities. Cyber operations are used to disrupt terrorist financing and propaganda. This technology-centric approach is more sustainable and less costly in blood and treasure, but it raises profound legal and ethical questions. The entire U.S. CT enterprise continues to operate largely under the legal authority of the 2001 Authorization for Use of Military Force (AUMF), a law passed in the immediate aftermath of 9/11 that critics argue is dangerously outdated and provides a blank check for endless war.

Meanwhile, the domestic terrorism threat has evolved substantially. The January 6, 2021, attack on the U.S. Capitol, the 2019 El Paso Walmart shooting, the 2018 Pittsburgh Tree of Life synagogue massacre, and the 2017 Charlottesville car attack underscore that ideologically motivated violent extremists -- particularly those driven by white supremacist and anti-government ideologies -- now represent, according to the FBI and DHS, the most lethal terrorist threat to the U.S. homeland. This domestic dimension complicates CT policy because the tools designed for foreign adversaries (surveillance, military force, intelligence collection) cannot be applied to U.S. citizens without raising fundamental First and Fourth Amendment concerns.

## Scope

This analysis covers the key components of modern U.S. counterterrorism policy:

- **Threat Landscape**: The nature of current threats from Salafi-jihadist groups (ISIS, al-Qaeda), Iran-backed proxies, and ideologically motivated violent extremists (IMVEs), including racially and ethnically motivated violent extremists (REMVEs).
- **Strategic Approach**: The "over-the-horizon" strategy and its reliance on special operations forces (SOF), intelligence, and partner forces, as well as the evolution from the Global War on Terror (GWOT) to the current "enduring mission" framework.
- **Technological Tools**: The role of AI, autonomous systems (drones), and cyber capabilities in CT operations, including programs like Project Maven and the Replicator initiative.
- **Legal Framework**: The 2001 AUMF, the 2002 Iraq AUMF (repealed 2023), the domestic CT legal architecture under the FBI, and the legal and constitutional debates surrounding their use.
- **Ethical Challenges**: The issues of civilian casualties, targeted killing, the use of AI in the "kill chain," Guantanamo Bay detention, and military commissions.
- **Domestic CT**: The FBI-led approach to domestic terrorism, Countering Violent Extremism (CVE) programs, and the challenge of balancing security with civil liberties and free speech.
- **Resource Allocation**: The balance between funding CT operations and investing in capabilities for great power competition.

## Core Tensions and Tradeoffs

- **Counterterrorism vs. Great Power Competition (GPC)**: Every dollar, intelligence asset, and special operator focused on CT is one not focused on deterring China or Russia. The 2022 National Defense Strategy designates China as the "pacing challenge," yet CT demands continue to consume significant ISR assets, SOF deployments, and intelligence bandwidth. How does the U.S. balance managing a persistent CT threat without being strategically distracted?
- **Security vs. Civil Liberties**: The tools used to track foreign terrorists -- such as surveillance of digital communications under Section 702 of the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act (FISA) -- can easily sweep up the data of U.S. citizens, creating a fundamental tension with privacy and civil liberties. The domestic CT mission, particularly regarding ideologically motivated extremism, raises even sharper concerns about government monitoring of protected speech and political activity.
- **Effectiveness vs. Legitimacy**: Drone strikes may be effective at eliminating high-value targets, but if they cause significant civilian casualties, they can undermine the legitimacy of the U.S. and serve as a recruiting tool for terrorists. The 2021 Kabul airport drone strike that killed 10 Afghan civilians, including 7 children, while targeting zero ISIS-K operatives, exemplified this risk.
- **Speed vs. Human Control**: AI can identify targets much faster than human analysts, but relying on it for targeting decisions creates risks of error and removes human moral judgment from the decision to use lethal force. The "kill chain" is compressing, raising questions about whether "meaningful human control" can be maintained.
- **Legal Overreach vs. Operational Flexibility**: The broad interpretation of the 2001 AUMF gives the executive branch maximum flexibility to pursue threats, but critics argue it is an unconstitutional blank check for war that bypasses Congressional oversight. The AUMF has been invoked to justify operations against groups and in countries that Congress never contemplated in September 2001.
- **Foreign vs. Domestic Terrorism**: The U.S. has no domestic terrorism statute equivalent to the foreign terrorism laws under 18 U.S.C. 2339, creating an asymmetry in how foreign-inspired and domestically motivated terrorism are investigated and prosecuted. Proposals for a domestic terrorism law raise concerns about potential political abuse and chilling effects on constitutionally protected speech.

## Key Questions

1. Is the 2001 AUMF still a legitimate legal basis for current CT operations, or does it need to be repealed and replaced with a narrowly tailored authorization?
2. What ethical and legal "guardrails" are needed for the use of AI and autonomous systems in targeted killing?
3. How can the U.S. effectively counter terrorist propaganda and recruitment online without infringing on free speech?
4. Is the "over-the-horizon" strategy a sustainable and effective model for preventing another major terrorist attack on the U.S. homeland, given the intelligence gaps created by the Afghanistan withdrawal?
5. How should the U.S. structure its approach to domestic terrorism threats without creating authorities that could be used to suppress legitimate political dissent?
6. What is the appropriate balance between military, intelligence, diplomatic, and development tools in a comprehensive CT strategy?

## Vision of Success

A successful 21st-century counterterrorism policy would:

- **Be Legally Sound**: Operations are conducted under a clear, narrow, and updated legal authorization from Congress, with meaningful sunset clauses that force regular democratic debate about the scope and duration of military action.
- **Be Sustainable**: The CT mission is resourced at a level that effectively manages risk without derailing the larger strategic focus on GPC. It does not consume a disproportionate share of ISR, SOF, or intelligence resources.
- **Be Technologically and Ethically Advanced**: The U.S. leverages the best technology for intelligence and targeting while adhering to a strict ethical framework that minimizes civilian harm, maintains meaningful human control over lethal decisions, and subjects AI-enabled targeting to independent oversight.
- **Be Integrated**: CT is not a separate "war" but is integrated into broader foreign policy, using diplomatic and economic tools to address the root causes of extremism alongside security measures.
- **Be Accountable**: Transparent reporting on operations, civilian casualties, and detainee treatment is the norm, not the exception. Independent oversight bodies have the access and authority to investigate and report on CT activities.
- **Protect Civil Liberties**: Domestic CT efforts are conducted within a framework that respects the First and Fourth Amendments, with robust judicial oversight of surveillance and clear boundaries between monitoring threats and suppressing dissent.
