# Counterterrorism: What You Can Do

While counterterrorism policy is often shrouded in secrecy and conducted far from public view, citizens have a vital role to play in demanding that these operations are conducted legally, ethically, and with democratic oversight. The post-9/11 era demonstrated the dangers of unchecked executive power in the CT domain -- from the invasion of Iraq to warrantless surveillance to "enhanced interrogation." Sustained citizen engagement is the most durable safeguard against the erosion of democratic governance in the name of security.

---

## 1. Advocate for Legal and Constitutional Clarity

The most significant action citizens can take is to push for a reform of the legal framework governing the "war on terror." The 2001 AUMF has been in force for nearly a quarter-century without meaningful revision -- longer than any previous U.S. war authorization. This is a failure of democratic governance that citizens can directly address.

### Contact Your Elected Officials

- **Demand a Vote on the AUMF**: Contact your Representative and Senators and urge them to support a repeal and replacement of the 2001 Authorization for Use of Military Force. Argue that any U.S. military action should be conducted under a specific, narrow, and time-limited authorization that is regularly debated and voted on by Congress.
    - **Key legislation to reference**: The bipartisan Kaine-Young AUMF replacement bill (introduced in multiple sessions); the 2023 repeal of the 2002 Iraq AUMF as a precedent.
    - **Key argument**: Members of Congress should not be able to delegate their constitutional war powers indefinitely. Regular votes on the AUMF are how a democracy conducts military operations.
- **Support War Powers Reform**: Urge support for legislation that strengthens the War Powers Resolution and creates practical mechanisms for congressional-executive consultation before military action in new theaters.

### Ask Specific Questions

At town halls, constituent meetings, or in written correspondence, ask your elected officials:

- "Do you believe the 2001 AUMF provides a legitimate legal basis for military strikes in countries like Somalia, Syria, or across Africa today -- over 20 years after 9/11?"
- "When was the last time you voted to authorize the use of military force? Do you believe Congress should regularly debate and vote on which wars we are fighting?"
- "What is your position on repealing the 2001 AUMF and forcing a new debate on war powers?"
- "Have you reviewed the classified War Powers Report that the President submits to Congress? Can you tell your constituents where the U.S. is currently engaged in combat operations?"

### Support Key Organizations

- **Brennan Center for Justice**: Publishes research and advocacy materials on AUMF reform and war powers. Their "War Powers" project provides accessible analysis of the legal issues.
- **ACLU National Security Project**: Advocates for legislative reform of CT authorities and litigates cases challenging executive overreach.

## 2. Demand Transparency and Accountability

The CT enterprise operates with minimal public transparency. Citizens should push back against excessive secrecy, particularly regarding civilian casualties, drone strikes, and the use of AI in targeting.

### Advocate for Transparency on Civilian Harm

- Support legislation that mandates public reporting on U.S. drone strikes and credible assessments of civilian casualties. Reference the *New York Times*' "Civilian Casualty Files" investigation (2021) and the Kabul drone strike (August 2021) as evidence that the government has systematically underreported civilian harm.
- Support the work of independent monitoring organizations:
    - **Airwars**: The leading independent tracker of civilian harm from airstrikes. Their data is essential for holding the government accountable.
    - **Center for Civilians in Conflict (CIVIC)**: Advocates for better civilian protection policies and condolence payments to families harmed by U.S. operations.
    - **Bureau of Investigative Journalism**: Long-running investigations into the covert drone war.

### Call for Ethical AI Oversight

- Engage in the public debate about the use of AI in warfare. Urge your representatives to support the creation of independent, civilian-led oversight bodies (like the proposed National Commission on AI and Security) to ensure that autonomous systems are used ethically and responsibly.
- Reference the DoD's own Ethical Principles for AI (adopted 2020) and ask how those principles are being implemented and verified in practice. Ask whether any independent body outside the DoD has reviewed the AI systems used in CT targeting.

### Push for Guantanamo Resolution

- Contact your members of Congress and urge them to lift the ban on transferring Guantanamo detainees to the U.S. mainland for prosecution in federal courts.
- Argue that 23+ years of detention without trial is incompatible with American values, that federal courts have successfully convicted over 600 terrorists while military commissions have produced only 8 convictions (several overturned), and that the facility costs over $500 million per year for approximately 30 detainees.

## 3. Understand the Threat and the Tradeoffs

Informed citizens make better advocates. Understanding the CT landscape -- both the genuine threats and the costs of the response -- is essential for meaningful engagement.

### Get Informed

- Read beyond government press releases. Follow the work of organizations listed in the [Resources](09-resources.md) section to get a more complete picture of the terrorist threat and the impact of U.S. CT operations.
- **Key starting points**:
    - The DNI's Annual Threat Assessment (unclassified, published each spring) for the intelligence community's view of the threat.
    - The Costs of War Project at Brown University for the comprehensive costs of the post-9/11 wars.
    - The CSIS Transnational Threats Project for data-driven analysis of the global jihadist threat.
    - *Just Security* and *Lawfare* for accessible expert analysis of the legal and policy debates.
    - The 9/11 Commission Report for the foundational account of the intelligence failures that led to 9/11.

### Recognize the Tradeoffs

- Understand the core tensions of CT policy. Acknowledge that there is often a difficult balance between security and liberty, between effectiveness and legitimacy, and between counterterrorism and great power competition. Promote a nuanced public conversation that avoids simplistic solutions on either side.
- Be wary of both threat inflation (exaggerating the danger to justify unchecked authority) and threat dismissal (downplaying genuine risks to score political points). The truth is that the terrorist threat is real but manageable, and that the response should be proportionate, legal, and subject to democratic oversight.

### Understand the Domestic Dimension

- The domestic terrorism threat is real and growing. Educate yourself about the range of threats -- from white supremacist violence to anti-government extremism to jihadist-inspired attacks -- and resist efforts to politicize the threat assessment.
- Support evidence-based prevention programs while remaining vigilant about civil liberties. Prevention programs should be community-led, transparent, and subject to independent evaluation -- not used as vehicles for government surveillance of political or religious communities.

## 4. Support Non-Military Solutions

- **Advocate for a "Whole-of-Government" Approach**: Argue for increased funding for diplomacy, development aid, and programs that counter violent extremism by addressing its root causes -- poor governance, lack of education, economic exclusion, and the exploitation of grievances. A purely military approach is a strategy of "mowing the grass" that can manage symptoms but never achieves a lasting solution.
- **Support International CT Cooperation**: Advocate for U.S. leadership in multilateral CT frameworks, including the Global Coalition to Defeat ISIS, the Global Counterterrorism Forum, and UN-led efforts. Unilateral military action is less effective and less legitimate than coordinated international approaches.
- **Encourage Tech Accountability**: Advocate for social media platforms and technology companies to invest in transparent, effective content moderation that removes terrorist propaganda and recruitment content while respecting free expression. Support research into algorithmic deradicalization and counter-narrative strategies.

## 5. Engage in the Democratic Process

### Vote and Organize

- Evaluate candidates based on their positions on CT policy, war powers, civil liberties, and government transparency. Few voters consider these issues in elections, which allows members of Congress to avoid accountability for their CT votes (or their failure to vote at all on matters of war and peace).
- Organize with others in your community who share concerns about CT policy. Join or create local chapters of national reform organizations. Attend town halls and make CT policy a visible issue for your elected officials.

### Participate in the Policy Process

- Submit public comments on CT-related regulatory actions and proposed rules (e.g., surveillance authorities, AI governance).
- Attend or stream congressional hearings on CT policy (available via committee websites and C-SPAN). The Senate and House Intelligence, Armed Services, Foreign Relations, and Judiciary Committees all hold hearings relevant to CT.
- Track the annual NDAA process, which is the primary legislative vehicle for CT authorities and funding. The NDAA typically passes in the fall and is one of the few "must-pass" bills, making it a key target for reform amendments.

### Support Investigative Journalism

- Subscribe to and share the work of journalists and outlets that cover CT policy in depth: *The Intercept*, *The New York Times* national security desk, *The Washington Post* national security desk, *ProPublica*, *Foreign Policy*, and the *Bureau of Investigative Journalism*.
- Investigative reporting has been responsible for some of the most significant revelations about CT policy, from the Abu Ghraib photos to the Snowden disclosures to the Civilian Casualty Files. Supporting this journalism is supporting accountability.

## Effectiveness Tips

- **Be specific**: General statements about "ending forever wars" are less effective than specific requests for a vote on the AUMF, support for a civilian harm transparency bill, or action on Guantanamo.
- **Be nonpartisan**: CT reform draws support from across the political spectrum. Frame your advocacy in terms of constitutional principles, democratic governance, and effective security rather than partisan politics.
- **Use data**: Reference specific reports, investigations, and data points in your communications. The Costs of War Project, Airwars, and GAO reports provide authoritative data that is difficult for officials to dismiss.
- **Be persistent**: The national security establishment has structural advantages in secrecy, expertise, and institutional continuity. Citizen engagement must be sustained, not episodic, to counter these advantages.
- **Connect to values**: Frame CT reform in terms of the values that Americans share: that we are a nation of laws, that the government should be accountable to the people, that we do not wage war without democratic consent, and that we do not abandon our principles in the face of threats.
