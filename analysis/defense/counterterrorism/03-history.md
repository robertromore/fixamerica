# Counterterrorism: History

## Historical Development

U.S. counterterrorism policy has evolved through distinct phases, from a pre-9/11 era when terrorism was treated primarily as a law enforcement problem, through the massive militarization of the Global War on Terror, to the current "over-the-horizon" model. Understanding this evolution is essential to assessing the strengths and pathologies of the current approach.

---

## Pre-9/11: Terrorism as a Law Enforcement Problem (1970s-2001)

### Early CT Architecture

Before September 11, 2001, the United States treated terrorism primarily as a criminal matter. The FBI, not the military, was the lead agency for CT investigations. Key developments in this era included:

- **1972 Munich Olympics Massacre**: The killing of 11 Israeli athletes by the Palestinian group Black September prompted the creation of the first dedicated U.S. counterterrorism units, including the Army's Delta Force (1977) and the FBI's Hostage Rescue Team (1982).
- **1979 Iran Hostage Crisis**: The failed Operation Eagle Claw rescue mission exposed catastrophic coordination failures among the military services, contributing to the creation of the Joint Special Operations Command (JSOC) in 1980 and the passage of the Goldwater-Nichols Act in 1986.
- **1983 Beirut Barracks Bombing**: A Hezbollah truck bomb killed 241 U.S. Marines in Lebanon, the deadliest terrorist attack against Americans until 9/11.
- **1993 World Trade Center Bombing**: A truck bomb detonated in the parking garage of the World Trade Center, killing 6 and injuring over 1,000. The perpetrators were prosecuted in federal criminal court -- the standard approach of the era.
- **1995 Oklahoma City Bombing**: Timothy McVeigh's truck bomb attack on the Alfred P. Murrah Federal Building killed 168 people, demonstrating the lethality of domestic terrorism.
- **1998 U.S. Embassy Bombings**: Al-Qaeda attacked U.S. embassies in Kenya and Tanzania, killing 224 people. The U.S. responded with cruise missile strikes on suspected al-Qaeda sites in Afghanistan and Sudan -- a harbinger of the remote-strike approach that would dominate the post-9/11 era.
- **2000 USS Cole Attack**: Al-Qaeda operatives attacked the destroyer USS Cole in Aden, Yemen, killing 17 sailors.

### The Intelligence Failure

The 9/11 Commission identified systemic failures that allowed the September 11 attacks to succeed:

- **"The Wall"**: Legal and bureaucratic barriers prevented effective information sharing between intelligence agencies (CIA) and law enforcement (FBI). The Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act (FISA) was interpreted to require strict separation.
- **Failure of Imagination**: Intelligence agencies failed to anticipate the possibility of hijacked aircraft being used as weapons, despite warnings from counterterrorism officials like Richard Clarke and the August 6, 2001 Presidential Daily Brief titled "Bin Ladin Determined to Strike in US."
- **Fragmented Authority**: No single agency or official had responsibility for integrating all intelligence on terrorist threats. The CIA tracked al-Qaeda abroad; the FBI investigated domestic threats; and neither effectively shared information with the other.

## Phase 1: The "Global War on Terror" (2001-2008)

### The Immediate Response

- **September 14, 2001**: Congress passes the Authorization for Use of Military Force (AUMF) with only one dissenting vote (Rep. Barbara Lee, D-CA). President Bush signs it on September 18.
- **September 20, 2001**: President Bush addresses a joint session of Congress and declares: "Our war on terror begins with al-Qaeda, but it does not end there. It will not end until every terrorist group of global reach has been found, stopped, and defeated."
- **October 7, 2001**: Operation Enduring Freedom begins with U.S. airstrikes and special operations forces entering Afghanistan.
- **October 26, 2001**: The USA PATRIOT Act is signed into law, dramatically expanding surveillance authorities, including roving wiretaps, access to business records under Section 215, and the ability to share foreign intelligence information with law enforcement.

### Strategy and Doctrine

The Bush administration articulated a CT strategy built on several pillars:

- **Preemption Doctrine**: The September 2002 National Security Strategy asserted the right to use preemptive military force against terrorist threats before they materialized. This "Bush Doctrine" represented a fundamental break from the deterrence and containment strategies of the Cold War.
- **"Freedom Agenda"**: The administration argued that promoting democracy in the Middle East would drain the "swamp" of extremism by giving people an alternative to radical ideology.
- **"With Us or Against Us"**: A framework that demanded foreign governments choose sides in the war on terror, with U.S. military action as the consequence of non-cooperation.

### Key Operations and Programs

- **Operation Enduring Freedom (Afghanistan, 2001-2014)**: The invasion toppled the Taliban regime within weeks using a combination of CIA paramilitary teams, Army Special Forces, U.S. airpower, and Afghan Northern Alliance fighters. What followed became the longest war in American history.
- **Operation Iraqi Freedom (Iraq, 2003-2011)**: Justified in part by erroneous claims of Iraqi links to al-Qaeda and weapons of mass destruction, the Iraq invasion ultimately consumed the majority of U.S. military resources for a decade.
- **Creation of the National Counterterrorism Center (NCTC, 2004)**: Established by the Intelligence Reform and Terrorism Prevention Act of 2004 (in response to 9/11 Commission recommendations), the NCTC was designed to be the central hub for integrating all-source intelligence on terrorism.
- **Creation of the Department of Homeland Security (2002)**: The largest reorganization of the federal government since the National Security Act of 1947, combining 22 agencies with approximately 180,000 employees.
- **Enhanced Interrogation and Black Sites**: The CIA established a network of secret detention facilities ("black sites") in countries including Thailand, Poland, Romania, and Lithuania. Detainees were subjected to "enhanced interrogation techniques" including waterboarding, sleep deprivation, and stress positions. The Senate Intelligence Committee's 2014 report (the "Torture Report") concluded that these techniques were ineffective and produced unreliable intelligence.
- **Guantanamo Bay**: The detention facility at U.S. Naval Base Guantanamo Bay, Cuba, began receiving detainees in January 2002. At its peak, it held 780 individuals classified as "enemy combatants" -- a legal category created to deny them both prisoner-of-war protections under the Geneva Conventions and the constitutional rights of criminal defendants.
- **NSA Warrantless Surveillance (Stellar Wind)**: Authorized by President Bush, the NSA conducted warrantless surveillance of domestic communications -- revealed by the *New York Times* in December 2005 and later by Edward Snowden's disclosures in 2013.

### Consequences

- **Iraq Diverted Resources from Afghanistan**: The invasion of Iraq drew critical military, intelligence, and financial resources away from Afghanistan, allowing al-Qaeda and the Taliban to reconstitute.
- **Abu Ghraib (2004)**: Photographs of U.S. soldiers abusing detainees at Abu Ghraib prison in Iraq caused a global scandal that severely damaged U.S. credibility and served as a powerful recruiting tool for terrorist organizations.
- **The Rise of al-Qaeda in Iraq (AQI)**: The chaos and sectarian conflict in post-invasion Iraq gave rise to Abu Musab al-Zarqawi's al-Qaeda in Iraq, the direct predecessor of ISIS.

## Phase 2: The "Light Footprint" and Drone War Era (2009-2017)

### The Obama Administration's Approach

President Obama inherited two active wars and a public weary of large-scale military commitments. His approach sought to wind down the ground wars while maintaining pressure on terrorist groups through targeted operations.

- **Key Strategic Shift**: From "Global War on Terror" (a term the Obama administration retired) to a more targeted, intelligence-driven approach focused on "disrupting, dismantling, and defeating al-Qaeda."
- **The Drone Campaign Escalation**: The Obama administration dramatically expanded the use of armed drones for targeted killing. CIA drone strikes in Pakistan increased from an estimated 36 under Bush to over 370 under Obama (Bureau of Investigative Journalism data). Strikes were also conducted in Yemen, Somalia, and Libya.
- **The Bin Laden Raid (May 2, 2011)**: A JSOC-led operation (Neptune Spear) in Abbottabad, Pakistan killed Osama bin Laden. The operation -- a helicopter-borne assault by Navy SEAL Team Six -- represented the pinnacle of the intelligence-driven, SOF-centric approach.
- **The al-Awlaki Strike (September 30, 2011)**: A drone strike in Yemen killed Anwar al-Awlaki, a U.S. citizen and al-Qaeda propagandist. Two weeks later, a separate strike killed his 16-year-old son, Abdulrahman, also a U.S. citizen. The killing of U.S. citizens without trial remains one of the most controversial CT actions of the post-9/11 era.
- **Presidential Policy Guidance (PPG, May 2013)**: The Obama administration issued guidelines for drone strikes outside active hostilities, requiring "near certainty" that no civilians would be harmed and that the target posed a "continuing and imminent threat" to U.S. persons. However, the PPG was classified and its application was not subject to independent review.

### The Rise of ISIS and Operation Inherent Resolve (2014-present)

- **The ISIS Caliphate**: The collapse of order in Iraq and Syria following the U.S. withdrawal from Iraq (2011) and the Syrian civil war (2011-present) enabled ISIS to seize territory across both countries, declaring a "caliphate" in June 2014 at its peak controlling territory the size of the United Kingdom.
- **Operation Inherent Resolve**: The U.S. responded with an air campaign and a "by, with, and through" strategy of supporting local partner forces -- the Iraqi Security Forces, the Kurdish Peshmerga, and the Syrian Democratic Forces (SDF). U.S. SOF conducted direct action raids, including the October 2019 raid that killed ISIS leader Abu Bakr al-Baghdadi.
- **Territorial Defeat**: By March 2019, the ISIS territorial caliphate was eliminated. However, the group transitioned to a global insurgency, with affiliates expanding across Africa, Central Asia, and Southeast Asia.

### Domestic Terrorism Escalation

This period saw a marked increase in domestic terrorism in the United States:

- **2013 Boston Marathon Bombing**: Tsarnaev brothers, inspired by al-Qaeda propaganda, killed 3 and injured over 260.
- **2015 San Bernardino Shooting**: A married couple inspired by ISIS killed 14.
- **2016 Orlando Pulse Nightclub Shooting**: A lone actor pledging allegiance to ISIS killed 49.
- **2017 Charlottesville Attack**: A white supremacist drove a car into counter-protesters, killing Heather Heyer. This period marked a visible escalation of white nationalist violence.

## Phase 3: The "Over-the-Horizon" and GPC Era (2018-Present)

### The Strategic Pivot

- **2018 National Defense Strategy**: Formally declared that great power competition with China and Russia, not terrorism, was the primary national security challenge. CT was reframed as an "enduring" but secondary mission.
- **Withdrawal from Afghanistan (August 2021)**: The chaotic U.S. withdrawal and the Taliban's rapid seizure of power ended the 20-year American military presence. The August 26 ISIS-K bombing at Abbey Gate killed 13 U.S. service members and approximately 170 Afghan civilians.
- **The August 29, 2021 Kabul Drone Strike**: In the final days of the withdrawal, a U.S. drone strike intended to target an ISIS-K vehicle-borne IED instead killed 10 Afghan civilians, including 7 children. A Pentagon investigation initially called the strike "righteous" before acknowledging the error. No service members were disciplined. The incident became a symbol of the risks inherent in the OTH model.

### Key Operations in the Current Phase

- **The Zawahiri Strike (July 31, 2022)**: A CIA drone strike in Kabul killed al-Qaeda leader Ayman al-Zawahiri on his apartment balcony, using two R9X "flying Ginsu" missiles (kinetic projectiles with extending blades, designed to minimize collateral damage). Presented as proof of OTH viability.
- **The Qurayshi Raid (February 3, 2022)**: A JSOC raid in Idlib, Syria targeted ISIS leader Abu Ibrahim al-Hashimi al-Qurayshi, who detonated a suicide vest killing himself and family members during the operation.
- **Continued Operations in Syria, Iraq, and Somalia**: Small numbers of U.S. SOF (approximately 900 in Syria, 2,500 in Iraq, and several hundred in Somalia) continue to partner with local forces to conduct CT operations. In Somalia, U.S. forces resumed airstrikes in 2022 after a pause under the Trump administration's late-term troop withdrawal order.
- **Sahel Setbacks**: The withdrawal of French forces and military coups in Mali, Burkina Faso, and Niger (2020-2023) have disrupted Western CT partnerships across West Africa. The U.S. withdrew forces from Niger in 2024 after a military coup ended the security cooperation agreement.

### Characteristics of the Current Posture

- **Zero Footprint Aspiration (Partial Reality)**: The OTH model aims to conduct CT from bases and assets located outside the target country, but in practice the U.S. maintains small contingents in Syria, Iraq, Somalia, and various African nations.
- **AI-Enabled**: Increasingly reliant on AI (like Project Maven) to process vast amounts of ISR data to find and track targets across multiple theaters simultaneously.
- **Legal Status Quo**: Continues to rely on the 2001 AUMF as the primary legal basis for global operations, despite the changing nature of the threat and multiple failed congressional efforts to pass a replacement.
- **Domestic Focus Intensifying**: The FBI, DHS, and DOJ are devoting increasing resources to domestic terrorism and targeted violence, a shift accelerated by the January 6 Capitol attack.

## Historical Pattern

This evolution shows a clear trend: from massive, manpower-intensive operations to a smaller, more sustainable, and technology-centric approach. Each phase has reduced the cost in U.S. lives and dollars but has also made the operations more remote, more reliant on technology, and arguably less transparent to the public and Congress. The cycle also reveals a recurring pattern: each CT approach generates its own unintended consequences -- the Iraq War spawned ISIS, the drone campaign fueled radicalization narratives, and the Afghanistan withdrawal created intelligence gaps -- that require subsequent adaptation.

| Phase | Period | Troop Commitment | Primary Tool | Legal Basis | Key Weakness |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Pre-9/11 | 1970s-2001 | Minimal | Criminal prosecution | Federal criminal law | Intelligence fragmentation |
| GWOT | 2001-2008 | 200,000+ (peak) | Ground forces, COIN | 2001 AUMF | Unsustainable cost, strategic distraction |
| Light Footprint | 2009-2017 | 10,000-50,000 | Drones, SOF, partner forces | 2001 AUMF (expanded) | Civilian casualties, blowback |
| OTH/GPC | 2018-present | ~5,000 | AI, remote ISR, cyber | 2001 AUMF (further stretched) | Intelligence gaps, legal atrophy |
