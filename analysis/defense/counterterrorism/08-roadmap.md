# Counterterrorism: Reform Roadmap

Reforming U.S. counterterrorism policy requires a deliberate, phased approach that begins with re-establishing a firm legal foundation, then builds new oversight mechanisms for modern technologies, and ultimately institutionalizes a sustainable CT posture that balances effectiveness, accountability, and civil liberties. The roadmap below sequences reforms to build political momentum and ensure each phase creates the conditions for the next.

---

## Phase 1: Reclaiming War Powers and Increasing Transparency (Years 1-2)

This phase focuses on passing landmark legislation to reform the legal basis of the war on terror and increase accountability. These reforms exploit existing bipartisan momentum and are designed to be difficult for opponents to argue against publicly.

### Objective

End the "forever war" legal framework, establish new standards for transparency, and create the institutional infrastructure for deeper reforms.

| Priority | Action | Mechanism | Key Performance Indicator (KPI) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Repeal and Replace the 2001 AUMF** | Congress passes a new, narrowly tailored Authorization for Use of Military Force that names specific groups and regions, includes a 3-year sunset clause, and defines "associated forces." The 2001 AUMF is repealed with a 180-day transition period. | Federal legislation (building on the Kaine-Young bipartisan framework) | New AUMF is signed into law. |
| **2. Mandate Transparency for Lethal Strikes** | Congress passes a "Targeted Operations and Civilian Protection Act" requiring annual public reports on drone strikes and civilian casualties outside active hostilities. | NDAA provision or standalone legislation | First annual unclassified report is published by the Director of National Intelligence. |
| **3. Charter AI Oversight Commission** | Congress passes legislation to charter the independent "National Commission on AI and Security" with cleared experts empowered to review classified targeting algorithms. | Federal legislation | Commission members are appointed; first classified briefing is conducted. |
| **4. Publish CT Resource Allocation Data** | DoD publishes first annual report on resource allocation between CT and GPC missions, as mandated by NDAA provision. | NDAA reporting requirement | Report is delivered to Armed Services Committees, identifying ISR, SOF, and intelligence resource allocation. |
| **5. Establish Civilian Harm Assessment Office** | DoD establishes a permanent, independent office to investigate credible claims of civilian harm from CT operations, with authority to recommend condolence payments. | Executive order + NDAA provision | Office is established, staffed, and conducts first investigations. First condolence payments are issued. |

### Political Strategy

Phase 1 reforms are designed to exploit bipartisan consensus on AUMF reform (which has co-sponsors from both parties), war weariness among the public, and the growing consensus that AI oversight is necessary. Transparency measures are politically difficult to oppose -- few members of Congress want to be seen defending a lack of accountability for civilian casualties.

**Key coalitions**:

- Bipartisan AUMF reformers (Kaine-Young coalition in the Senate; similar bipartisan groupings in the House)
- Fiscal conservatives concerned about open-ended military commitments
- Progressive members who have long advocated for ending "forever wars"
- Technology policy advocates who support AI governance

**Goal of Phase 1**: End the "forever war" legal framework and establish new standards for transparency and accountability in counterterrorism operations.

## Phase 2: Building New Oversight and Rebalancing Resources (Years 3-5)

This phase focuses on operationalizing the new oversight bodies established in Phase 1, rebalancing resources between CT and GPC, and beginning to address the domestic CT challenge.

### Objective

Normalize new oversight mechanisms. Shift resources from military-led CT to a more holistic, whole-of-government approach. Strengthen the domestic CT framework.

| Priority | Action | Mechanism | Key Performance Indicator (KPI) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Operationalize AI Oversight** | The National Commission on AI and Security conducts its first classified audit of a DoD AI targeting program (e.g., Project Maven or its successor) and delivers findings to Congress. | Commission authority from Phase 1 legislation | First classified audit report is delivered to the Intelligence Committees; first public annual report is published. |
| **2. First AUMF Sunset Debate** | Congress holds its first debate on re-authorizing the new AUMF as it approaches its 3-year sunset. | Statutory sunset clause from Phase 1 | The AUMF is either re-authorized (with any necessary modifications) or allowed to expire, forcing a democratic debate on the future of CT operations. |
| **3. Shift Funding to Non-Kinetic CT** | Congress increases the budget for the State Department's Bureau of Counterterrorism and USAID programs that address root causes of extremism by 25% over 3 years. | Appropriations legislation | New CVE/TVTP programs are funded and operational in at least 10 priority countries. |
| **4. Reform Section 702 Surveillance** | At the next FISA Section 702 reauthorization, Congress enacts warrant requirements for U.S. person queries and establishes a standing FISA Court amicus panel. | FISA reauthorization legislation | Warrant requirement is in force; amicus panel is appointed and participating in FISA Court proceedings. |
| **5. Address Guantanamo** | Administration negotiates transfer agreements for approved-for-release detainees; Congress considers lifting the ban on mainland transfers for those who can be prosecuted in federal courts. | Executive action (transfers) + legislation (ban lift) | Detainee population reduced to fewer than 15; at least one military commission case transferred to federal court. |
| **6. Strengthen Domestic CT Framework** | Congress passes a targeted domestic terrorism statute with robust First Amendment protections, sunset provisions, and mandatory reporting requirements. | Federal legislation | Statute enacted; FBI publishes first annual domestic terrorism threat report under the new mandate. |
| **7. CT vs. GPC Rebalancing** | Based on the net assessment from Phase 1, DoD begins redeploying CT-dedicated ISR assets and SOF units to Indo-Pacific and European theaters, while maintaining a sustainable CT footprint. | DoD force posture decisions informed by net assessment | At least 20% of CT-dedicated ISR assets are reassigned to GPC theaters. |

### Political Strategy

Phase 2 requires sustained political attention and the willingness to make difficult tradeoffs. The AUMF sunset debate will be the most politically significant event -- it forces every member of Congress to take a public position on the future of the war on terror. The key framing is that regular democratic debate over war is a feature, not a bug, of constitutional governance.

**Goal of Phase 2**: Establish and normalize the new oversight mechanisms. Begin the process of rebalancing resources from military-led CT to a more holistic, whole-of-government approach. Address the most significant domestic CT and civil liberties issues.

## Phase 3: Institutionalizing a Modern CT Posture (Years 6-10)

This phase focuses on ensuring the reforms are durable and that the U.S. has a sustainable, effective, and legally sound CT posture for the long term.

### Objective

Make reforms irreversible by embedding them in institutional practice and legal structure. Achieve a CT posture that is effective, sustainable, and consistent with democratic values.

| Priority | Action | Mechanism | Key Performance Indicator (KPI) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. A Sustainable CT Footprint** | The DoD's resource allocation reflects the NDS priority of GPC, with CT managed as a sustainable, "economy of force" mission with clearly defined resource ceilings. | NDS implementation | ISR assets and SOF deployments previously focused on CT are successfully re-postured to the Indo-Pacific, while CT effectiveness metrics remain stable. |
| **2. Ethical AI as a Standard** | Recommendations from the AI Commission are integrated into DoD doctrine, acquisition requirements, and international norms for all autonomous systems used in CT. | DoD directive, acquisition policy, NATO/international standards | All new autonomous systems used in CT operations are required to pass an "ethical and safety" review before fielding. U.S. leads international negotiations on AI in armed conflict. |
| **3. A New Legal Consensus** | The process of debating and re-authorizing the AUMF every few years becomes a normal and accepted part of Congress's national security responsibilities. | Institutional practice | The AUMF is re-authorized for a third time, cementing the new legal and political consensus that war powers are shared, not delegated. |
| **4. Guantanamo Closed** | The Guantanamo detention facility is closed, with remaining detainees transferred to federal custody, repatriated, or held under a transparent statutory framework. | Executive action + legislation | Facility is closed; annual savings of $500+ million are redirected to other CT or national security priorities. |
| **5. Prevention as a Primary Strategy** | Non-military CT tools -- diplomacy, development, CVE, governance assistance -- receive sustained funding at levels commensurate with their importance. | Appropriations, State/USAID programming | State Department/USAID CT and prevention spending reaches at least 10% of total USG CT expenditure (up from current ~2-3%). |
| **6. Domestic CT in Balance** | The domestic CT apparatus operates within a clear legal and oversight framework that protects civil liberties while effectively addressing the threat from violent extremism across all ideological categories. | Statutory framework, oversight bodies | Annual reports show domestic terrorism investigations distributed proportionally to actual threat assessments across ideology categories. No significant civil liberties violations identified by oversight bodies. |

### Political Strategy

Phase 3 requires sustained political commitment over multiple administrations and Congresses. The key to durability is building institutional capacity (AI oversight, civilian harm assessment, AUMF sunset processes) that becomes self-reinforcing and resistant to rollback. Once members of Congress have voted to re-authorize the AUMF multiple times, the precedent becomes established. Once the AI Commission is publishing annual reports, rolling it back becomes politically costly.

## Risk Assessment

| Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Major terrorist attack during AUMF transition period | Low-Medium | Very High | 180-day transition period; new AUMF drafted to cover all current operations; emergency provisions for rapid amendment |
| Congressional failure to re-authorize at sunset | Medium | High | Automatic 90-day extension provision if Congress is actively debating; public pressure for action |
| AI Commission captured by industry or IC interests | Medium | Medium | Strict conflict-of-interest provisions; balance of technical, ethical, and legal expertise; transparent appointment process |
| International crisis diverts attention from reform | Medium | Medium | Frame CT reform as strengthening readiness for all contingencies; automate oversight mechanisms |
| Domestic terrorism statute used for political targeting | Low | Very High | Robust First Amendment protections; sunset clause; mandatory AG certification of non-political motivation; judicial review |
| Post-Afghanistan intelligence gap allows major attack | Medium | High | Invest in next-gen ISR; strengthen Central Asian partnerships; expand OSINT capabilities |

---

## Sequencing Rationale

The three-phase approach reflects the political reality that comprehensive reform cannot be achieved in a single legislative session:

1. **Phase 1 (legal foundation and transparency)** creates the evidence base, democratic legitimacy, and institutional infrastructure needed to support Phase 2 reforms.
2. **Phase 2 (rebalancing and oversight)** implements the structural changes and addresses the most significant policy gaps, building on the political momentum from Phase 1.
3. **Phase 3 (institutionalization)** ensures that reforms are durable, embedded in practice, and resistant to rollback by future administrations.

Each phase makes the next more politically feasible and more resistant to opposition.
