# Counterterrorism: Solutions

Reforming U.S. counterterrorism policy for the modern era requires updating its legal foundations, strengthening its ethical guardrails, rebalancing its strategic priorities, and building institutions capable of overseeing an increasingly technology-driven enterprise. The solutions below address the root causes identified in this analysis and are designed to be mutually reinforcing. They range from achievable legislative reforms to structural changes requiring sustained political effort.

---

## 1. Reform the Legal Framework

### A. Repeal and Replace the 2001 AUMF

- **Problem Addressed**: The outdated and overly broad 2001 AUMF serves as a legal "blank check" for global, indefinite military operations, bypassing Congress's constitutional war powers authority. It has been in force for nearly a quarter-century and invoked to justify operations in at least 22 countries against groups that did not exist on September 11, 2001.
- **Description**: The cornerstone of CT reform is to replace the 2001 AUMF with a new statute that is narrowly tailored to the current threat.
- **Implementation**: Congress should pass a new AUMF that:
    - **Names Specific Adversaries**: Explicitly names the specific terrorist groups the U.S. is authorized to use military force against (e.g., ISIS and its designated affiliates, al-Qaeda and its designated affiliates, al-Shabaab). New groups may be added only through a congressional notification process with a 30-day review period.
    - **Includes Geographic Limits**: Defines the specific countries or regions where military force is authorized, with a process for the President to notify Congress and seek approval before expanding to new theaters.
    - **Contains a Sunset Clause**: Requires the authorization to be debated and re-approved by Congress every 3 years, forcing a regular review of the war's progress and necessity. If Congress fails to re-authorize, the AUMF expires.
    - **Repeals the 2001 AUMF**: Explicitly repeals the 2001 authorization (the 2002 Iraq AUMF was already repealed in 2023) to eliminate legal ambiguity. The repeal should take effect 180 days after the new AUMF is enacted to ensure no gap in authority.
    - **Defines "Associated Forces"**: Provides a statutory definition of "associated forces" with clear criteria, rather than leaving this determination entirely to executive discretion.
- **Precedent**: Senators Tim Kaine (D-VA) and Todd Young (R-IN) have introduced bipartisan AUMF replacement legislation in multiple sessions of Congress. The 2023 repeal of the 2002 Iraq AUMF demonstrates that reform is achievable.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Restores Congress's constitutional war powers. Provides a clear and legitimate legal basis for current CT operations. Ends the "forever war" legal theory while maintaining operational flexibility within defined boundaries.

### B. Reform War Powers Consultation

- **Problem Addressed**: The War Powers Resolution of 1973 (50 U.S.C. 1541-1548) has been routinely circumvented or ignored by the executive branch.
- **Implementation**: Pass a War Powers Consultation Act (based on proposals by the bipartisan Miller Center Commission) that:
    - Requires the President to consult with a permanent consultative group of congressional leaders before initiating CT operations in new theaters.
    - Establishes expedited procedures for congressional votes on new military actions.
    - Creates a Congressional Office of War Powers Legal Counsel to provide independent legal analysis on the scope of existing authorizations.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Establishes a practical mechanism for congressional-executive consultation that is more workable than the current War Powers Resolution.

## 2. Strengthen Ethical and Technical Guardrails

### A. Legislate a Framework for Targeted Killing

- **Problem Addressed**: The U.S. drone program operates under classified executive guidelines (Presidential Policy Guidance / Principles, Standards, and Procedures) that have been modified by each administration without public or congressional input. There is no statutory framework governing targeted killing outside active hostilities.
- **Implementation**: Pass a "Targeted Operations and Civilian Protection Act" that would:
    - **Codify Standards**: Legislate the "near certainty" standard for civilian harm in strikes outside active hostilities. Require positive identification of a specific, named individual on a vetted target list (no "signature strikes" based on pattern-of-life analysis alone).
    - **Mandate Transparency**: Require the executive branch to publish an annual, unclassified report detailing:
        - The number of strikes conducted outside active hostilities
        - The countries where they occurred
        - An assessment of combatant and non-combatant casualties (using both government and credible external estimates)
        - Actions taken in response to credible claims of civilian harm
    - **Create an Independent Review Mechanism**: Establish a civilian harm assessment office with authority to investigate all credible claims of civilian casualties from U.S. CT operations, with subpoena power and the ability to recommend corrective action and condolence payments.
    - **Mandate Post-Strike Review**: Require post-strike assessment of every lethal action outside active hostilities, with results reported to the congressional intelligence and armed services committees.
- **Evidence Base**: The Biden administration's 2022 Civilian Harm Mitigation and Response Action Plan (CHMR-AP) acknowledged systemic failures in tracking and responding to civilian harm. The DoD's own Rand study found that the military lacked the institutional processes to learn from civilian casualty incidents. Legislating these requirements would make them durable across administrations.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Increases transparency and accountability for the drone program. Provides a stronger basis for legitimacy both at home and abroad. Creates institutional learning processes that reduce civilian casualties over time.

### B. Establish an Oversight Body for Algorithmic Warfare

- **Problem Addressed**: AI is increasingly central to CT operations -- from intelligence analysis (Project Maven) to target identification to autonomous surveillance -- but there is no independent, technically proficient body overseeing its use.
- **Implementation**: Charter a "National Commission on AI and Security" composed of civilian AI experts, ethicists, legal scholars, and national security professionals. This commission would be:
    - Granted security clearances and access to review the algorithms, training data, and performance metrics of classified DoD and IC AI systems used in the CT "kill chain."
    - Empowered to audit AI targeting systems for bias, accuracy, error rates, and compliance with ethical principles and international humanitarian law.
    - Required to publish an annual unclassified report on the state of AI in CT operations, with a classified annex provided to the intelligence committees.
    - Authorized to issue binding recommendations on AI system performance thresholds that must be met before deployment.
- **International Context**: The European Union's AI Act (2024) provides a framework for categorizing AI systems by risk level, with "high-risk" systems (including those used by law enforcement) subject to mandatory conformity assessments. NATO has adopted principles for the responsible use of AI that include human oversight, accountability, and explainability.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Provides meaningful civilian oversight of a highly technical and secret process. Helps ensure that AI is used as a tool to support, not supplant, human judgment and moral responsibility.

### C. Close Guantanamo Bay Detention Facility

- **Problem Addressed**: Guantanamo has been operating for over 23 years, holds approximately 30 detainees at a cost of over $500 million annually, and its military commission process has failed to deliver justice for the 9/11 attacks. The facility is a symbol of legal limbo that damages U.S. credibility on human rights.
- **Implementation**:
    - Transfer detainees who have been approved for release to host countries with appropriate security guarantees (as has been done for hundreds of former detainees).
    - For detainees who can be prosecuted, transfer them to the federal court system, which has a strong track record of convicting terrorists (over 600 terrorism-related convictions since 9/11, compared to 8 convictions by military commissions, several of which have been overturned).
    - For the small number of detainees who are deemed too dangerous to release but cannot be tried (due to tainted evidence from "enhanced interrogation"), establish a transparent, statutory framework for continued law-of-war detention with periodic review, or negotiate plea agreements.
    - Repeal the congressional ban on transferring detainees to the U.S. mainland.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Removes a major source of international criticism and terrorist propaganda. Saves hundreds of millions of dollars annually. Demonstrates that the U.S. can handle terrorism within its existing legal framework.

## 3. Rebalance Strategic Priorities and Resources

### A. Mandate a Net Assessment of CT vs. GPC

- **Problem Addressed**: There is no transparent, data-driven process for determining how much of the U.S. military's resources should be devoted to CT versus great power competition. Decisions are driven by inertia, institutional politics, and crisis response rather than strategic analysis.
- **Implementation**: The annual National Defense Authorization Act (NDAA) should require a report from the DoD that details:
    - The percentage of the budget, intelligence assets (especially ISR platforms), SOF personnel, and cyber capabilities dedicated to CT missions versus GPC-related missions.
    - An assessment of the strategic risk being accepted in the GPC portfolio due to the demands of the CT mission.
    - An assessment of the risk being accepted in CT by the redeployment of assets to GPC theaters.
    - A comparison of the resource allocation with the stated priorities of the National Defense Strategy.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Forces an explicit and data-driven conversation about the strategic tradeoffs being made. Creates pressure to ensure the CT mission is right-sized and not consuming a disproportionate share of resources needed for the pacing challenge of China.

### B. Invest in Non-Military CT Tools

- **Problem Addressed**: The U.S. CT strategy is overwhelmingly military and intelligence-led. Diplomatic, economic, and development tools -- which address the root causes of terrorism -- receive a fraction of the resources devoted to kinetic operations.
- **Implementation**:
    - **Increase Funding for State Department and USAID CT Programs**: The State Department's Bureau of Counterterrorism and USAID programs that address governance, education, and economic development in fragile states should receive a significant funding increase (at least 25% over five years). Current spending on these programs is a rounding error compared to military CT spending.
    - **Strengthen the Global Coalition to Defeat ISIS**: Expand the 85-member coalition's focus from military operations to stabilization, governance, and preventing the conditions that allow ISIS to regenerate in liberated territories.
    - **Reform CVE/TVTP Programs**: Restructure domestic Countering Violent Extremism (now Targeted Violence and Terrorism Prevention) programs to:
        - Fund community-led initiatives rather than top-down government programs
        - Include independent evaluation of program effectiveness
        - Address all forms of violent extremism (not just Islamist-inspired)
        - Incorporate civil liberties safeguards and community advisory boards
    - **International Prevention Models**: Invest in adapting proven international prevention models, such as Denmark's "Aarhus Model" (community-based reintegration of returning foreign fighters) and Germany's "Hayat" program (family counseling for radicalized individuals), for the U.S. context.
- **Anticipated Impact**: A more balanced "whole-of-government" approach that focuses on prevention rather than just kinetic strikes. Potentially more cost-effective in the long run than a purely military-led strategy. Addresses the root causes that generate new recruits faster than military operations can eliminate existing ones.

### C. Address the Post-Afghanistan Intelligence Gap

- **Problem Addressed**: The withdrawal from Afghanistan created significant intelligence gaps that degrade the OTH model's effectiveness.
- **Implementation**:
    - Invest in next-generation, long-endurance ISR platforms (including high-altitude autonomous systems) that can maintain persistent surveillance over denied areas like Afghanistan from bases in the Persian Gulf or Central Asia.
    - Strengthen partnerships with Central Asian states (Uzbekistan, Tajikistan) for intelligence cooperation and potential forward basing.
    - Expand the use of open-source intelligence (OSINT) and commercial satellite imagery to supplement classified collection.
    - Develop human intelligence networks inside Afghanistan that do not depend on a permanent military presence.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Reduces the intelligence deficit created by the Afghanistan withdrawal and strengthens the OTH model's viability.

## 4. Reform Domestic CT Institutions

### A. Strengthen Domestic CT Legal Framework

- **Problem Addressed**: The lack of a comprehensive federal domestic terrorism statute creates investigative and prosecutorial asymmetries.
- **Implementation**:
    - Pass a targeted federal domestic terrorism statute that criminalizes acts of terrorism committed by U.S. persons in the U.S. with domestic motivations, using the same penalty enhancements available for international terrorism. The statute should:
        - Apply to completed attacks and material support
        - Include robust First Amendment protections (no prosecution for speech, association, or belief alone)
        - Require the same evidentiary standards as existing criminal law
        - Include sunset and review provisions
    - Mandate that the FBI and DHS publish annual public reports on domestic terrorism threats, investigations, and disruptions, broken down by ideology category.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Closes the prosecutorial gap between domestic and international terrorism cases. Provides better data on the domestic threat landscape. Must be carefully designed to avoid chilling effects on protected speech.

### B. Reform Surveillance Authorities

- **Problem Addressed**: The post-9/11 surveillance architecture, particularly Section 702 of FISA, sweeps up enormous quantities of Americans' communications data and is subject to insufficient judicial and congressional oversight.
- **Implementation**:
    - Require a warrant for FBI queries of the Section 702 database using U.S. person identifiers (the "backdoor search" problem).
    - Expand the FISA Court's adversarial process by creating a standing panel of cleared privacy advocates (amici curiae) with automatic standing to challenge government surveillance applications.
    - Mandate regular, independent audits of intelligence community compliance with minimization procedures and civil liberties protections.
    - Require declassification of FISA Court opinions addressing significant legal interpretations of surveillance authority.
- **Anticipated Impact**: Preserves the intelligence value of Section 702 while strengthening the civil liberties protections that maintain public trust in the CT enterprise.
