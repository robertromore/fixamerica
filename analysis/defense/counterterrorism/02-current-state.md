# Counterterrorism: Current State

## The Threat Landscape (2025)

The terrorist threat has become more ideologically diverse and geographically diffuse than it was on 9/11. The primary threats emanate from Salafi-jihadist groups and, increasingly, from ideologically motivated violent extremists (IMVEs). The 2024 Annual Threat Assessment from the Director of National Intelligence identifies both categories as significant, persistent dangers to the United States.

### Key Foreign Terrorist Organizations (FTOs)

| Group | Region of Operation | Estimated Strength | Status / Key Threat |
|---|---|---|---|
| **ISIS-Core** | Syria, Iraq | 3,000 - 5,000 | Degraded but resilient. Operates as a low-level insurgency with capacity for spectacular attacks, as demonstrated by the January 2024 Iran bombings and the March 2024 Crocus City Hall attack in Moscow claimed by ISIS-K. |
| **ISIS-Khorasan (ISIS-K)** | Afghanistan, Pakistan, Central Asia | 2,000 - 4,000 | Most capable external operations affiliate. Demonstrated intent and capability to strike Western targets. Responsible for the August 2021 Abbey Gate bombing at Kabul airport (13 U.S. service members killed). |
| **Al-Shabaab** | Somalia, East Africa | 7,000 - 12,000 | Al-Qaeda's largest and wealthiest affiliate. Controls significant territory in southern Somalia. Revenue estimated at $100-150 million annually from extortion and taxation. Attacked a U.S. military base at Manda Bay, Kenya, in January 2020. |
| **Al-Qaeda in the Arabian Peninsula (AQAP)** | Yemen | ~1,000 | Degraded by sustained U.S. and Saudi operations but retains aspirations for external attacks. Historically the al-Qaeda affiliate most focused on attacking the U.S. homeland (2009 Christmas Day underwear bomb, 2010 cargo plane plot). |
| **Hayat Tahrir al-Sham (HTS)** | Northwest Syria | 10,000 - 15,000 | Formerly Jabhat al-Nusra (al-Qaeda affiliate). Now governing Idlib province. Has distanced itself from al-Qaeda but remains a designated FTO. Relationship with external CT threat is evolving. |
| **Hezbollah** | Lebanon, regional | Tens of thousands | Iran-backed proxy. Possesses a massive arsenal of rockets and advanced weapons. While primarily a state-like actor, retains global operational capability. Linked to the 1994 AMIA bombing in Argentina and plots in Europe and Southeast Asia. |
| **Jama'at Nasr al-Islam wal Muslimin (JNIM)** | Sahel (Mali, Burkina Faso, Niger) | 2,000 - 3,000 | Al-Qaeda-linked coalition expanding rapidly across West Africa as French and Western forces withdraw. The Sahel has become one of the world's most active conflict zones. |

*Source: National Counterterrorism Center (NCTC), UN Security Council reports, CSIS Transnational Threats Project, 2024-2025.*

### The Evolving ISIS Threat

Despite the destruction of its territorial "caliphate" in 2019, ISIS has proven remarkably resilient as a transnational network:

- **Decentralized Command**: ISIS has shifted from a centralized territorial organization to a network of semi-autonomous "provinces" (wilayats) across Africa, Central Asia, and Southeast Asia. The most dangerous of these is ISIS-Khorasan, which has conducted attacks in Iran, Russia, and Afghanistan.
- **Prison Break Risk**: Approximately 10,000 ISIS fighters are held in makeshift prisons in northeastern Syria, guarded by the Syrian Democratic Forces (SDF) with U.S. support. The NCTC has assessed that a mass breakout would represent a "generational threat." A February 2022 breakout attempt at al-Hasakah prison required 10 days of fighting to contain.
- **African Expansion**: ISIS affiliates in the Sahel (Islamic State in the Greater Sahara), Mozambique (ISIS-Mozambique), and the Democratic Republic of the Congo (Islamic State Central Africa Province) are expanding, exploiting governance vacuums left by the withdrawal of Western forces.

### Ideologically Motivated Violent Extremists (IMVEs) and Domestic Terrorism

- **Definition**: Individuals or groups who commit violence in support of a political or social ideology, often without direct connection to a formal FTO. This includes racially or ethnically motivated violent extremists (REMVEs), anti-government/anti-authority violent extremists, and involuntary celibate (incel)-motivated violence.
- **Threat Assessment**: The Director of National Intelligence, the FBI Director, and the DHS Secretary have all assessed that domestic violent extremists, particularly white supremacists and anti-government militia adherents, pose the most lethal and persistent terrorist threat to the U.S. homeland.
- **Scale**: The FBI has over 2,700 open domestic terrorism investigations as of 2023, roughly double the number from 2020. The number of domestic terrorism incidents has risen sharply since 2015.
- **Key Incidents (2017-2024)**:
    - 2017 Charlottesville car attack (1 killed, 35 injured)
    - 2018 Pittsburgh Tree of Life synagogue shooting (11 killed)
    - 2019 El Paso Walmart shooting (23 killed, targeting Hispanic community)
    - 2021 January 6 Capitol attack (multiple deaths, 140+ officers injured)
    - 2022 Buffalo supermarket shooting (10 killed, racially motivated)
- **Characteristics**: Leaderless, decentralized, and radicalized online. The "lone wolf" or "lone actor" model makes these threats particularly difficult for law enforcement to detect and prevent.

## U.S. Counterterrorism Posture

The U.S. has shifted from a large-footprint, "nation-building" approach to a small-footprint, "over-the-horizon" (OTH) strategy. This shift was codified in the 2018 National Defense Strategy and accelerated by the August 2021 withdrawal from Afghanistan.

### Military Components

- **Special Operations Forces (SOF)**: Small teams of elite operators from Joint Special Operations Command (JSOC), Army Special Forces (Green Berets), Navy SEALs, Marine Raiders, and Air Force Special Tactics advise and assist partner forces and conduct direct action missions. Approximately 5,000 SOF personnel are deployed across more than 80 countries on any given day, though actual numbers are classified. SOCOM's annual budget exceeds $14 billion.
- **Intelligence-Driven Targeting**: Relies on a vast network of signals intelligence (SIGINT) collected by the National Security Agency (NSA), human intelligence (HUMINT) from the CIA's Directorate of Operations, geospatial intelligence (GEOINT) from the National Geospatial-Intelligence Agency (NGA), and aerial surveillance (ISR) to find and fix targets. The "F3EAD" cycle (Find, Fix, Finish, Exploit, Analyze, Disseminate) is the operational backbone of the CT targeting process.
- **Partner Forces**: The U.S. works "by, with, and through" local partner forces who bear the brunt of ground combat:
    - Syrian Democratic Forces (SDF) in northeastern Syria (~100,000 fighters, Kurdish-led)
    - Somali National Army and Danab Brigade in Somalia
    - Iraqi Counter-Terrorism Service (ICTS) in Iraq
    - Various African partner forces across the Sahel and East Africa
- **Remote Strikes**: The primary tool for lethal action is the use of armed drones (UAVs) and manned aircraft, often launched from bases hundreds or thousands of miles away. Key basing locations include Al Udeid Air Base (Qatar), Camp Lemonnier (Djibouti), and various undisclosed locations in the Middle East and Africa.

### The Post-Afghanistan Intelligence Gap

The withdrawal from Afghanistan in August 2021 created significant CT intelligence challenges:

- **Loss of In-Country Assets**: The U.S. lost its extensive network of military bases, intelligence stations, and human sources within Afghanistan. The CIA's Kabul station was shuttered.
- **OTH Limitations**: The OTH model requires ISR assets to fly long distances from bases outside Afghanistan, reducing the time they can spend over target areas. A round-trip from the Persian Gulf to targets in Afghanistan can take 6-8 hours, dramatically reducing the "dwell time" available for surveillance.
- **The Zawahiri Strike as Test Case**: The July 2022 drone strike that killed al-Qaeda leader Ayman al-Zawahiri on a balcony in Kabul was presented by the Biden administration as proof that OTH CT was viable. Critics noted that the strike relied on months of intelligence preparation and that the target was a high-profile, sedentary individual -- far different from the dynamic, mobile targets that characterize most CT operations.

## The Role of Technology

### AI and Intelligence Analysis

- **Project Maven**: The DoD's flagship AI program, which uses machine learning algorithms to automatically detect, classify, and track objects and individuals in full-motion video from drones and other ISR platforms. Originally launched in 2017 by the DoD's Algorithmic Warfare Cross-Functional Team, Maven generated controversy when Google employees protested the company's involvement, leading Google to withdraw from the project. The contract subsequently moved to other firms including Palantir and Anduril.
- **Data Fusion**: AI tools are increasingly used to fuse disparate data sources -- SIGINT, GEOINT, social media, financial transactions, and travel data -- to identify terrorist networks and predict potential threats. The National Counterterrorism Center's Terrorist Identities Datamart Environment (TIDE) database contains over 1.6 million identity records.
- **Predictive Analytics**: Experimental programs use machine learning to predict potential radicalization pathways and identify individuals at risk of engaging in terrorism, though these programs face significant criticism regarding accuracy, bias, and civil liberties implications.
- **Challenge**: The use of "black box" AI algorithms for intelligence analysis raises questions about bias, accuracy, and the ability of human analysts to understand and verify the AI's conclusions. An April 2024 investigation by *+972 Magazine* alleged that the Israeli military's AI targeting system "Lavender" in Gaza generated targeting recommendations with minimal human review, sparking international debate about AI in the kill chain.

### Autonomous Systems and Drones

- **Workhorse Platform**: The **MQ-9 Reaper** is the primary platform for OTH surveillance and strikes. Its long endurance (up to 27 hours) allows for persistent "eyes on" a target area. The U.S. Air Force operates approximately 300 MQ-9s, with plans to transition to the MQ-Next generation platform.
- **Emerging Capabilities**: The DoD's Replicator initiative aims to field thousands of small, cheap, autonomous drones within 18-24 months for various missions. These "attritable" systems could revolutionize CT by providing persistent, low-cost surveillance in areas currently covered only intermittently.
- **Non-State Actor Drones**: Terrorist groups, particularly Hezbollah and the Houthis, now employ commercial and military-grade drones for surveillance and attack, creating a counter-UAS challenge for U.S. forces. The October 2023 drone and rocket attacks on U.S. bases in Iraq and Syria by Iran-backed militias demonstrated this threat.
- **Ethical Debate**: The key debate is over "Lethal Autonomous Weapon Systems" (LAWS) -- weapons that can independently select and engage targets without direct human control. Current U.S. policy (DoDD 3000.09, updated 2023) requires "appropriate levels of human judgment" for any lethal decision. The international Campaign to Stop Killer Robots has called for a binding treaty to ban fully autonomous weapons.

### Cyber Operations

- **Disrupting Financing**: U.S. Cyber Command and the Treasury Department's Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC) work to disrupt terrorist financing by targeting digital currency exchanges, hawala networks, and online fundraising platforms. The Treasury Department has designated numerous cryptocurrency wallets linked to ISIS and al-Qaeda fundraising.
- **Countering Propaganda**: Cyber Command's Joint Task Force ARES conducted operations to take down ISIS's media operation, including the Amaq News Agency, online magazines (*Dabiq*, *Rumiyah*), and social media accounts. Major tech platforms (Meta, X, YouTube, Telegram) have also removed millions of terrorist accounts, though content migrates to smaller, less moderated platforms.
- **Intelligence Gathering**: Cyber operations penetrate terrorist communications networks -- including encrypted messaging applications -- to gather intelligence for targeting. The tension between law enforcement demands for "exceptional access" to encrypted communications and the cybersecurity community's insistence on strong encryption remains unresolved.
- **Offensive Operations**: Operation Glowing Symphony (2016) was a landmark offensive cyber operation against ISIS's digital infrastructure, degrading its ability to disseminate propaganda, recruit online, and coordinate operations.

## The Legal Framework: The 2001 AUMF

The legal foundation for nearly all U.S. counterterrorism operations remains the Authorization for Use of Military Force passed by Congress three days after the 9/11 attacks. It passed the Senate 98-0 and the House 420-1 (Rep. Barbara Lee of California cast the sole dissenting vote).

- **Original Text**: Authorizes the President to use "all necessary and appropriate force" against those nations, organizations, or persons he determines "planned, authorized, committed, or aided the terrorist attacks that occurred on September 11, 2001, or harbored such organizations or persons."
- **Expansive Interpretation**: Successive administrations (both Republican and Democratic) have interpreted this authority to apply to "associated forces" of al-Qaeda -- a term that appears nowhere in the statute's text. This interpretation has been used to justify military action against groups that did not exist on 9/11, in countries far from Afghanistan.
- **Operations Conducted Under 2001 AUMF**:
    - War in Afghanistan (vs. al-Qaeda and the Taliban, 2001-2021)
    - Operations against ISIS in Iraq and Syria (2014-present)
    - Strikes against al-Shabaab in Somalia (2007-present)
    - Strikes against AQAP in Yemen (2002-present)
    - Operations in Pakistan, Libya, the Philippines, Niger, and other nations
- **2002 Iraq AUMF**: The separate 2002 authorization for the Iraq War was repealed by Congress in March 2023 (Public Law 118-1), but the 2001 AUMF remains in force.
- **The War Powers Resolution**: The 1973 War Powers Resolution (50 U.S.C. 1541-1548) requires the President to notify Congress within 48 hours of committing armed forces and limits deployments to 60 days without congressional authorization. In practice, the 2001 AUMF is cited as the standing authorization that satisfies the Resolution's requirements, effectively nullifying its intended check on executive war powers.

**The Core Controversy**: Critics argue the 2001 AUMF is now a legal "blank check" for global, endless war, with no temporal or geographic limits, and that it has been stretched far beyond Congress's original intent. As of 2025, the AUMF has been in effect for nearly a quarter-century -- longer than any previous U.S. war authorization. Proponents argue it provides necessary flexibility to pursue evolving terrorist threats and that the cost of a legislative gap (no authority) during the process of drafting a replacement would create an unacceptable security risk.

## Guantanamo Bay and Military Commissions

- **Current Status**: The Guantanamo Bay detention facility in Cuba continues to hold approximately 30 detainees as of 2025, down from a peak of 780 in 2003. The facility costs over $500 million annually to operate -- roughly $15 million per detainee per year -- making it the most expensive detention facility in the world on a per-capita basis.
- **Military Commissions**: The military commission process at Guantanamo, established under the Military Commissions Act of 2006 (amended 2009), has been plagued by decades of legal challenges, procedural delays, and controversy. The case of Khalid Sheikh Mohammed and four co-defendants for the 9/11 attacks has been pending since 2008, with no trial date as of 2025.
- **Closure Efforts**: Presidents Obama, Trump, and Biden have each taken different positions on Guantanamo's future. Obama signed an executive order to close the facility on his second day in office but was blocked by Congress, which has repeatedly enacted legislation prohibiting the transfer of detainees to the U.S. mainland. The facility's closure remains politically fraught and legally complex.

## Countering Violent Extremism (CVE) Programs

- **Federal Approach**: The U.S. government has pursued Countering Violent Extremism (CVE) programs -- now often termed "targeted violence and terrorism prevention" (TVTP) -- since the Obama administration. These programs aim to prevent radicalization before it leads to violence through community engagement, social services, and online counter-narratives.
- **DHS Center for Prevention Programs and Partnerships (CP3)**: Established in 2021, CP3 replaced the DHS Office for Targeted Violence and Terrorism Prevention. It provides grants to community organizations for prevention programming.
- **Criticisms**: CVE programs have been criticized from multiple directions: civil liberties groups argue they disproportionately target Muslim communities and amount to surveillance under the guise of social services; conservative critics argue that recent CVE efforts have been expanded to target domestic political movements; and effectiveness researchers note the lack of rigorous evidence that CVE programs actually prevent terrorism.
- **International Comparison**: The United Kingdom's "Prevent" program, Denmark's "Aarhus Model," and Germany's "Hayat" initiative offer different models for prevention, each with distinct strengths and controversies.
