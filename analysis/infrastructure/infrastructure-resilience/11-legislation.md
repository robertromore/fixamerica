# Infrastructure Resilience: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Building infrastructure resilience requires updated design standards, expanded investment programs, improved governance, and aligned incentives. This document provides draft legislation for key reforms and analysis of existing legal frameworks.

## Federal Legislation

### Infrastructure Resilience Act

**Purpose**: Establish comprehensive federal infrastructure resilience requirements and programs.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Infrastructure
Resilience Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that—
    (1) climate change and other hazards
    increasingly threaten American infrastructure;
    (2) disaster losses exceed $100 billion
    annually;
    (3) current design standards are based on
    historical conditions;
    (4) federal investment in resilience yields
    significant returns;
    (5) comprehensive reform is needed.

SEC. 3. RESILIENCE STANDARDS.

(a) FEDERAL INFRASTRUCTURE.—All federally-funded
infrastructure shall be designed for future
climate and hazard conditions using projections
for the expected lifetime of the asset.

(b) GUIDANCE.—Within 1 year, each federal
agency shall issue guidance on climate-informed
design for infrastructure within its jurisdiction.

(c) MINIMUM STANDARDS.—Standards shall address—
    (1) temperature extremes;
    (2) precipitation and flooding;
    (3) sea level rise and coastal hazards;
    (4) wildfire;
    (5) other relevant hazards.

SEC. 4. RESILIENCE GRANT PROGRAM.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—An Infrastructure Resilience
Grant Program is established.

(b) AUTHORIZATION.—$5,000,000,000 annually is
authorized.

(c) ELIGIBLE ACTIVITIES.—
    (1) Infrastructure hardening;
    (2) Climate adaptation;
    (3) Nature-based solutions;
    (4) Cybersecurity improvements;
    (5) System redundancy.

(d) FEDERAL SHARE.—Up to 75% federal share.

(e) PRIORITY.—Priority shall be given to—
    (1) critical infrastructure;
    (2) projects benefiting vulnerable communities;
    (3) cost-effective investments.

SEC. 5. VULNERABILITY ASSESSMENT.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—Within 3 years, all federal
agencies shall complete vulnerability assessments
of infrastructure under their jurisdiction.

(b) ELEMENTS.—Assessments shall address—
    (1) current condition;
    (2) climate and hazard exposure;
    (3) consequences of failure;
    (4) interdependencies;
    (5) adaptation options.

(c) REPORTING.—Assessments shall be reported
to Congress and made public.

SEC. 6. BRIC ENHANCEMENT.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.—The Building Resilient
Infrastructure and Communities program is
authorized at $2,000,000,000 annually.

(b) SET-ASIDES.—Not less than 25% shall be
available for—
    (1) small and impoverished communities;
    (2) tribal communities;
    (3) nature-based solutions.

SEC. 7. COORDINATION.

(a) INTERAGENCY COUNCIL.—An Interagency
Infrastructure Resilience Council is established.

(b) DUTIES.—The Council shall—
    (1) coordinate federal resilience activities;
    (2) develop unified standards;
    (3) share best practices;
    (4) report to Congress.

SEC. 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect upon enactment.
```

### Critical Infrastructure Cybersecurity Act

**Purpose**: Strengthen cybersecurity requirements for critical infrastructure.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Critical Infrastructure
Cybersecurity Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that—
    (1) cyberattacks on critical infrastructure
    are increasing;
    (2) recent incidents demonstrate vulnerability;
    (3) current protections are inadequate;
    (4) mandatory standards are needed in some
    sectors.

SEC. 3. CYBERSECURITY REQUIREMENTS.

(a) COVERED SECTORS.—The following critical
infrastructure sectors shall implement
cybersecurity requirements—
    (1) energy;
    (2) water and wastewater;
    (3) transportation;
    (4) communications.

(b) STANDARDS.—Sector-specific agencies shall
establish minimum cybersecurity standards based
on the NIST Cybersecurity Framework.

(c) TIMELINE.—Standards shall be issued within
2 years of enactment.

SEC. 4. INCIDENT REPORTING.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—Covered entities shall report
significant cyber incidents to CISA within
72 hours.

(b) DEFINITION.—Significant incidents include—
    (1) unauthorized access to control systems;
    (2) disruption of operations;
    (3) ransomware attacks;
    (4) other incidents designated by CISA.

(c) LIABILITY.—Good faith reporting shall
not create liability.

SEC. 5. ASSISTANCE.

(a) TECHNICAL ASSISTANCE.—CISA shall provide
technical assistance to covered entities.

(b) GRANTS.—$500,000,000 annually is authorized
for cybersecurity improvement grants.

(c) PRIORITY.—Priority for small utilities
and local governments.

SEC. 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect upon enactment.
```

### Climate-Resilient Infrastructure Act

**Purpose**: Require climate adaptation in federally-funded infrastructure.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Climate-Resilient
Infrastructure Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that—
    (1) climate change poses significant risks
    to infrastructure;
    (2) infrastructure investments have long
    lifetimes;
    (3) designing for future conditions is
    cost-effective;
    (4) federal policy should ensure resilient
    investment.

SEC. 3. CLIMATE RESILIENCE REQUIREMENT.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—All federally-funded
infrastructure projects exceeding $10,000,000
shall—
    (1) assess climate risks;
    (2) incorporate climate projections in design;
    (3) identify adaptation measures.

(b) PROJECTIONS.—Projects shall use climate
projections for the expected useful life of
the infrastructure.

(c) GUIDANCE.—Within 1 year, federal agencies
shall issue guidance on implementing this
requirement.

SEC. 4. FLOOD RISK.

(a) FLOOD STANDARD.—Federally-funded projects
in flood-prone areas shall be designed to the
500-year flood level, or 2 feet above the
100-year level, whichever is greater.

(b) SEA LEVEL RISE.—Coastal projects shall
incorporate sea level rise projections.

SEC. 5. HEAT AND FIRE.

(a) EXTREME HEAT.—Projects in heat-vulnerable
areas shall be designed for projected extreme
heat conditions.

(b) WILDFIRE.—Projects in wildfire-prone areas
shall incorporate fire-resistant design.

SEC. 6. WAIVER.

(a) AUTHORITY.—The head of an agency may waive
requirements upon finding that compliance is
not feasible or cost-effective.

(b) REPORT.—Waivers shall be reported to
Congress.

SEC. 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect upon enactment.
```

## State Model Legislation

### Model State Infrastructure Resilience Act

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Infrastructure
Resilience Act".

SECTION 2. RESILIENCE PLANNING.

(a) STATE RESILIENCE PLAN.—The [agency] shall
develop and maintain a state infrastructure
resilience plan.

(b) ELEMENTS.—The plan shall include—
    (1) vulnerability assessment;
    (2) prioritized investments;
    (3) implementation timeline;
    (4) funding strategy.

(c) UPDATE.—The plan shall be updated every
5 years.

SECTION 3. DESIGN STANDARDS.

(a) STATE PROJECTS.—State-funded infrastructure
shall be designed for future climate conditions.

(b) GUIDANCE.—The [agency] shall issue design
guidance incorporating climate projections.

SECTION 4. BUILDING CODES.

(a) ADOPTION.—The state shall adopt and enforce
the most recent International Building Code
and International Residential Code within
2 years of publication.

(b) CLIMATE PROVISIONS.—Codes shall incorporate
climate resilience provisions as appropriate.

SECTION 5. GRANT PROGRAM.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—A state infrastructure
resilience grant program is established.

(b) USES.—Grants may be used for—
    (1) infrastructure hardening;
    (2) nature-based solutions;
    (3) risk assessment;
    (4) planning.

SECTION 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect on [date].
```

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| **Waiver abuse** | Excessive exemptions | Medium |
| **Threshold avoidance** | Project splitting | Medium |
| **Grandfather clauses** | Existing infrastructure exempt | High |
| **Projection gaming** | Selecting favorable scenarios | Medium |

### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| **Funding levels** | Below need | Budget constraints |
| **Private infrastructure** | Limited coverage | Jurisdictional limits |
| **State variation** | Inconsistent protection | Federalism |
| **Existing infrastructure** | Not addressed | Retrofit costs |

### Rectification Procedures

1. **Strong waiver standards** with reporting
2. **Anti-splitting provisions** for threshold
3. **Phased existing infrastructure** requirements
4. **Standardized projections** for planning
5. **Monitoring and enforcement** mechanisms
6. **Regular updates** to standards
7. **Private sector incentives** for compliance
8. **State program certification** for federal funding
9. **Public reporting** on resilience status
10. **Accountability mechanisms** for agencies

### General Implementation Concerns

| Concern | Mitigation |
|---------|------------|
| **Technical capacity** | Guidance, assistance |
| **Cost** | Phased implementation, funding |
| **Data availability** | Federal projections |
| **Political will** | Bipartisan framing |
| **Coordination** | Interagency structures |
