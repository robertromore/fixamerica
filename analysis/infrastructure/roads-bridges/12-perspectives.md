# Roads and Bridges: Political Perspectives Analysis

## Overview

This document analyzes road and bridge infrastructure reform -- specifically sustainable funding through vehicle miles traveled (VMT) fees, fix-it-first maintenance prioritization, safety transformation through Vision Zero, climate resilience investment, and equity-focused reconnecting communities programs -- through nine distinct political perspectives. The core questions are: how should the nation fund road and bridge maintenance in an era of declining gas tax revenue, whether maintenance should be prioritized over new construction, how aggressively safety standards should be reformed to reduce 40,000+ annual traffic deaths, what role climate resilience should play in infrastructure investment, and how to address the legacy of highway construction that destroyed communities of color. Each perspective receives engagement-consistency ratings and position scores, culminating in compromise proposals that bridge multiple viewpoints.

Road and bridge infrastructure occupies unusual political territory because virtually every perspective acknowledges the existence of a problem -- deteriorating conditions, structurally deficient bridges, and a growing repair backlog. The disagreements center on funding mechanisms, the balance between new construction and maintenance, the role of federal versus state authority, and whether infrastructure investment should incorporate safety redesign, climate adaptation, and equity objectives beyond basic transportation functionality. The $786 billion road repair backlog and $125 billion bridge repair backlog create urgency that can facilitate coalition-building, but fundamental differences over taxation, government spending, regulatory mandates, and the scope of federal authority generate friction that has prevented sustainable solutions for decades. The fact that the federal gas tax has not been raised since 1993 -- despite bipartisan acknowledgment that it is inadequate -- illustrates how political dynamics can prevent action even on widely recognized problems.

### Engagement Consistency (1-5)

This metric assesses how reliably a perspective's stated positions align with its underlying principles, and how stable those positions are when concerns are addressed. Higher scores indicate more predictable negotiating partners.

| Level | Label | Indicators |
|-------|-------|------------|
| 5 | Highly Consistent | Stated positions reflect underlying principles; positions stable when concerns addressed |
| 4 | Mostly Consistent | Principles generally align with positions; minor strategic flexibility |
| 3 | Mixed Consistency | Some positions principled, others reflect coalition pressures or unstated concerns |
| 2 | Low Consistency | Positions shift frequently; stated concerns differ from underlying priorities |
| 1 | Unpredictable | Positions appear driven primarily by opposition dynamics rather than principles |

**Coalition-Building Considerations:**

- Alignment gap: When stated concerns differ from underlying priorities, addressing stated concerns may not secure support
- Position stability: Perspectives with shifting positions require ongoing negotiation
- Principle consistency: Perspectives that apply principles uniformly are more predictable partners
- Internal diversity: Some perspectives contain factions with different priorities

## Perspective Analyses

### Conservative Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 3/5 - Mixed Consistency**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Stated commitment to fiscal responsibility and fixing deteriorating infrastructure is genuine but conflicts with consistent refusal to raise the gas tax or enact sustainable replacement revenue; the stated concern about "crumbling roads and bridges" does not translate into willingness to fund repairs through user fees that conservatives otherwise endorse in principle |
| Principle consistency | "User pays" principles logically support VMT fees or gas tax increases to fund roads, yet many conservatives oppose both; "limited government" is applied selectively -- supporting highway expansion (government spending) while opposing safety mandates (government regulation); fiscal restraint applies to transit and complete streets but not to highway expansion |
| Goalpost stability | Moderate; conservatives have consistently supported infrastructure spending in principle but shifted positions on funding mechanisms; previously acceptable gas tax increases (Reagan 1982, Bush 1990) are now politically impossible within the caucus; "infrastructure week" became a recurring promise without follow-through |
| Zero-sum behavior | Some; opposition to multimodal requirements, climate provisions, and equity mandates in infrastructure bills is sometimes driven by opposition to Democratic priorities rather than principled objection to the spending itself |

**Justification:** Conservatives face a genuine tension between supporting infrastructure investment (popular with voters, essential for commerce) and opposing tax increases (core commitment). This is not simple hypocrisy -- the tension reflects a real trade-off within conservative principles. The mixed consistency score of 3 reflects the fact that fiscal conservatism should produce support for user-fee-funded infrastructure but often does not, while the desire for highway expansion contradicts limited-government rhetoric. Conservative positions on infrastructure are more stable and principled than on some other issues but are distorted by the anti-tax pledge and by the tendency to oppose provisions (climate, equity, transit) that Democratic administrations attach to infrastructure legislation.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Republican support for the 2021 IIJA was split, with 19 Senate Republicans voting yes but House Republicans largely opposing; the opposition was partly driven by association with the broader Democratic spending agenda rather than objections to road and bridge investment
- Chamber of Commerce and American Trucking Associations have supported gas tax increases that Republican legislators refuse to endorse, revealing a gap between business-conservative and political-conservative positions
- Heritage Foundation opposes gas tax increases and VMT fees as "tax increases" while acknowledging the funding gap, recommending instead that states bear more responsibility -- a position that shifts the problem rather than solving it
- Republican governors in Indiana, Georgia, and South Carolina have raised state gas taxes or fees, demonstrating that conservative principles can accommodate user-fee funding when removed from national partisan dynamics

- Index existing gas tax to inflation rather than creating new VMT fee system
- Focus federal role on Interstate system; shift other roads to states

- **Unlikely partners:** Progressive, Democratic Socialist (on climate mandates, equity requirements, complete streets)

## Compromise Proposals

### Compromise 1: Fix-It-First Maintenance and Repair Act

**Bridges:** Conservative + Liberal + Progressive + Populist + Centrist + Religious Right + Democratic Socialist

**Core Agreement:** Repairing deteriorating roads and bridges is more cost-effective than allowing continued decline, maintenance creates good jobs, and the existing repair backlog represents a failure that all perspectives acknowledge must be addressed regardless of broader disagreements about new construction, climate, or multimodal design.

**Policy Description:**

Federal legislation establishes a fix-it-first framework requiring states to meet minimum condition targets for bridges (no more than 5% structurally deficient) and pavement (no more than 10% poor on the National Highway System) before using federal funds for capacity expansion on those systems. Federal matching for maintenance and repair projects is set at 90%, while matching for capacity expansion remains at 80%, creating a financial incentive to prioritize repair. A dedicated $2 billion annual local road assistance program helps rural and small-town governments maintain local roads that fall outside state highway programs. States must implement asset management plans using lifecycle cost analysis for all federally funded projects, ensuring that investment decisions account for long-term maintenance costs rather than just initial construction. An independent condition verification system prevents gaming through selective reporting. The legislation does not impose design mandates, climate requirements, or multimodal conditions -- it focuses exclusively on maintaining existing infrastructure in a state of good repair.

|-------------|-----------------------------------|

### Compromise 2: Sustainable Transportation Funding Act

**Bridges:** Liberal + Progressive + Libertarian + Centrist + Democratic Socialist + Constitutionalist

**Core Agreement:** The gas tax is structurally obsolete because fuel efficiency improvements and electric vehicle adoption are eroding the revenue base, and a sustainable user-fee replacement is necessary to prevent continued general fund bailouts of the Highway Trust Fund. A vehicle miles traveled fee is the most economically efficient replacement because it charges all road users in proportion to their usage.

**Policy Description:**

Federal legislation establishes a phased transition from the gas tax to a VMT fee over 10 years. The initial rate is set at 2 cents per mile, indexed annually to the construction cost index. Multiple collection options are available: odometer-based reporting at annual inspection, fuel-purchase-based estimation (simplest for most drivers), on-board device reporting (voluntary, with strict privacy protections), and a flat annual fee option based on estimated average driving. States may administer collection on behalf of the federal government and may add state VMT fees. Privacy protections prohibit location data collection without owner consent, require non-tracking options, limit data use to fee collection, and impose criminal penalties for misuse. Equity provisions include a per-mile credit for households below 200% of the federal poverty level, rural area rate adjustments reflecting limited transportation alternatives, and a heavy vehicle surcharge reflecting road damage by vehicle weight. Revenue is deposited in the Highway Trust Fund with transparent annual reporting on fund status and expenditures. The gas tax is reduced proportionally as VMT fee revenue increases.

### Compromise 3: Infrastructure Safety and Accountability Act

**Bridges:** Liberal + Progressive + Centrist + Populist + Religious Right + Democratic Socialist

**Core Agreement:** Over 40,000 annual traffic deaths represent a preventable public health crisis, and data-driven safety improvements at high-risk locations can reduce fatalities significantly without requiring the most politically contentious elements of comprehensive safety reform (speed cameras, speed limit reductions, lane narrowing).

**Policy Description:**

Federal legislation establishes a national goal of 50% traffic fatality reduction by 2040 from the 2022 baseline. A $5 billion High-Risk Location Treatment Program, funded over five years, requires states to identify the top 5% of crash locations on their road systems and treat them with proven safety countermeasures (roundabouts, protected left turns, median barriers, rumble strips, improved lighting, pedestrian refuge islands) within three years. States not meeting interim safety targets must develop safety improvement plans and dedicate a minimum percentage of federal funds to safety projects. Safety performance data must be publicly reported annually, creating transparency and accountability. A complete streets provision applies only to new construction and major reconstruction on federal-aid roads in urbanized areas, allowing states flexibility on existing roads and rural facilities. The legislation creates a National Safety Research Program funding investigation of systemic crash patterns and development of evidence-based countermeasures. Pedestrian and cyclist fatality reduction targets are established separately, recognizing that these fatalities have increased while overall vehicle occupant fatalities have declined.

## Strategic Implications

### Most Viable Coalition

The most effective coalition for road and bridge reform operates on three tracks. The broadest coalition (7/9 perspectives) can advance fix-it-first maintenance prioritization because maintenance is universally recognized as necessary and can be framed as fiscal responsibility (Conservative), good jobs (Populist, Democratic Socialist), and prudent stewardship (Religious Right). A center-left coalition supplemented by Libertarian support (6/9 perspectives) can advance VMT fee funding because the economic case for replacing the gas tax transcends ideology, and Libertarian support for true user-fee pricing breaks the usual right-left divide. Safety improvements can be advanced through a center-left coalition with Populist and Religious Right support (6/9 perspectives) when framed as practical infrastructure improvements rather than regulatory mandates.

### Key Obstacles

- **Anti-tax commitment:** The single greatest obstacle to infrastructure reform is the Republican anti-tax pledge that prevents gas tax increases or VMT fee adoption, even though user-fee funding is consistent with conservative principles; this forces reliance on general fund transfers that undermine the user-pays principle conservatives claim to support
- **Climate polarization:** Incorporating climate resilience and emissions reduction into infrastructure legislation activates partisan opposition that can sink otherwise viable bills; the IIJA succeeded in part by minimizing climate provisions, but this means infrastructure investment is not aligned with climate reality
- **Construction-over-maintenance bias:** The construction industry, engineering profession, and political incentives all favor new construction over maintenance; overcoming this bias requires changing institutional incentives through fix-it-first requirements that redirect spending
- **Rural-urban divide:** VMT fees, complete streets, and transit integration are perceived as urban priorities that disadvantage rural communities, creating a geographic divide that cuts across ideological lines; rural equity provisions are essential for any viable coalition

### Low Consistency Partners

Conservative (3/5), Populist (3/5), and Religious Right (3/5) perspectives rate at mixed consistency on infrastructure, reflecting tensions between stated support for infrastructure investment and opposition to the revenue and regulatory measures required to achieve it. Conservative support is most reliable on user-fee funding framed as market-based pricing and least reliable when infrastructure legislation includes climate, equity, or multimodal mandates. Populist support is most accessible on fix-it-first maintenance and infrastructure jobs and least accessible on VMT fees (perceived as new taxes on workers) and complete streets (perceived as urban-elite priorities). Religious Right support is most accessible through rural road and bridge repair and community safety and least accessible on climate mandates and federal design standards.

### Recommended Approach

1. **Lead with fix-it-first:** The Fix-It-First Maintenance and Repair Act (Compromise 1) commands the broadest coalition (7/9 perspectives) because maintenance prioritization is universally accepted as necessary; "fix what we have" framing transcends partisan divisions and creates the institutional foundation (asset management, condition targets, performance measurement) for subsequent reforms

2. **Build the funding coalition:** The Sustainable Transportation Funding Act (Compromise 2) commands 6/9 perspective support including the unusual Libertarian-Liberal alliance on user-fee pricing; the structural inevitability of gas tax decline as EVs proliferate creates urgency that can overcome anti-tax opposition; state pilot programs provide evidence base; phased transition minimizes disruption

3. **Advance safety through infrastructure:** The Infrastructure Safety and Accountability Act (Compromise 3) commands 6/9 perspective support by focusing on proven infrastructure countermeasures at dangerous locations rather than politically contentious automated enforcement and speed management; "fixing dangerous roads" framing resonates across ideological lines

4. **Defer contentious provisions:** Climate mandates, comprehensive complete streets requirements, and highway removal programs face structural opposition from the right; advancing these as standalone programs or administrative actions rather than as conditions on broadly supported maintenance and funding legislation prevents them from blocking the broader reform package

5. **Use data and evidence strategically:** Infrastructure policy benefits from unusually strong data -- bridge condition ratings, pavement assessments, crash statistics, cost-benefit analyses -- that can persuade evidence-responsive perspectives (Centrist, Libertarian, Constitutionalist); leading with data rather than ideology expands the coalition

6. **Address rural equity explicitly:** Every major infrastructure reform proposal must include explicit rural equity provisions -- rate adjustments for VMT fees, local road assistance programs, context-sensitive design standards -- because rural opposition can block reforms that otherwise have majority support; the Populist and Religious Right perspectives that represent rural constituencies must see their communities' needs addressed

7. **Separate federal role from federal mandates:** Constitutionalist and Libertarian opposition can be reduced by distinguishing between federal funding (broadly accepted) and federal mandates (broadly opposed); offering states options rather than imposing requirements -- incentive-based matching rates rather than prohibitions, voluntary programs rather than mandates -- expands the coalition without sacrificing policy substance
