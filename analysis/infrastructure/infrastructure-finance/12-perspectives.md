# Infrastructure Finance: Political Perspectives Analysis

## Overview

This document analyzes infrastructure finance reform -- specifically restoring Highway Trust Fund solvency, transitioning from fuel taxes to vehicle miles traveled (VMT) fees, establishing a national infrastructure bank, expanding public-private partnerships, implementing value capture mechanisms, and ensuring equitable distribution of costs and benefits -- through nine distinct political perspectives. The core questions are: how should America close a $2+ trillion infrastructure investment gap over the next decade, who should bear the cost of infrastructure maintenance and expansion, what role should the federal government versus states and the private sector play in infrastructure finance, and how should the transition from fossil fuel revenues to sustainable funding mechanisms be managed? Each perspective receives engagement-consistency ratings and position scores, culminating in compromise proposals that bridge multiple viewpoints.

Infrastructure finance is a policy domain where stated ideological commitments frequently conflict with practical political behavior. Conservatives who champion fiscal responsibility have blocked revenue increases that would prevent trust fund insolvency. Liberals who advocate public investment have sometimes resisted user fees that create sustainable dedicated funding. The fundamental challenge -- that the federal gas tax has not been raised since 1993 and has lost 47% of its purchasing power to inflation -- persists because raising visible taxes is politically costly regardless of ideological orientation. The electric vehicle transition adds urgency: as EVs erode the fuel tax base, the current revenue model faces terminal decline within two decades. The $1.2 trillion Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (IIJA) of 2021 provided a one-time infusion but did not resolve the underlying structural revenue problem, meaning the financing crisis will return as IIJA funding expires.

### Engagement Consistency (1-5)

This metric assesses how reliably a perspective's stated positions align with its underlying principles, and how stable those positions are when concerns are addressed. Higher scores indicate more predictable negotiating partners.

| Level | Label | Indicators |
|-------|-------|------------|
| 5 | Highly Consistent | Stated positions reflect underlying principles; positions stable when concerns addressed |
| 4 | Mostly Consistent | Principles generally align with positions; minor strategic flexibility |
| 3 | Mixed Consistency | Some positions principled, others reflect coalition pressures or unstated concerns |
| 2 | Low Consistency | Positions shift frequently; stated concerns differ from underlying priorities |
| 1 | Unpredictable | Positions appear driven primarily by opposition dynamics rather than principles |

**Coalition-Building Considerations:**

- Alignment gap: When stated concerns differ from underlying priorities, addressing stated concerns may not secure support
- Position stability: Perspectives with shifting positions require ongoing negotiation
- Principle consistency: Perspectives that apply principles uniformly are more predictable partners
- Internal diversity: Some perspectives contain factions with different priorities

## Perspective Analyses

### Conservative Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 3/5 - Mixed Consistency**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Stated commitment to fiscal responsibility and sound infrastructure should produce support for revenue adequacy measures that prevent trust fund insolvency, but conservatives have blocked gas tax increases for over three decades while authorizing general fund transfers that increase the deficit; the gap between fiscal responsibility rhetoric and opposition to dedicated revenue reveals that anti-tax identity outweighs infrastructure funding principles |
| Principle consistency | "User pays" principles are endorsed in theory but inconsistently applied; conservatives support user fees in concept but oppose specific fee increases when they become politically visible; "limited government" is applied to oppose new revenue mechanisms while supporting private sector subsidies through P3 frameworks; "fiscal restraint" is cited against new spending but not against the deferred maintenance costs of underinvestment |
| Goalpost stability | Moderate; conservatives have maintained consistent opposition to federal tax increases but have shifted on P3s and tolling depending on political context; some movement toward accepting VMT fees as a market-based alternative to gas taxes; however, during IIJA negotiations, many conservatives who advocated for infrastructure investment voted against the bill |
| Zero-sum behavior | Some; infrastructure has traditionally been a bipartisan area, but increasing polarization has made infrastructure votes party-line; conservative opposition to IIJA despite its inclusion of traditionally conservative priorities (highways, broadband, P3s) suggests growing zero-sum dynamics |

**Justification:** Conservatives occupy a genuinely conflicted position on infrastructure finance. Market principles logically support user fees, dedicated revenue, and private sector involvement -- all elements of the proposed reforms. Yet anti-tax commitments forged in the Grover Norquist era make it politically impossible for most conservative legislators to vote for revenue increases, even when the alternative is trust fund insolvency and deferred maintenance that costs more in the long run. The score of 3 reflects this genuine internal tension: conservative principles are coherent on infrastructure finance, but coalition pressures and anti-tax pledges create inconsistency between stated fiscal values and actual legislative behavior.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- American Society of Civil Engineers (ASCE) has received support from some conservative members for infrastructure investment, but Republican caucuses have blocked revenue bills
- Reason Foundation and Cato Institute advocate user fees and P3s consistent with market principles, but Republican legislators rarely champion these positions legislatively
- The 2021 IIJA received 19 Republican Senate votes but only 13 House Republican votes, despite including P3 expansion and highway investment
- Multiple Republican governors have raised state gas taxes (including in Georgia, Indiana, and South Carolina) while federal Republican caucuses maintain anti-tax positions

- Convert grants to loans through expanded TIFIA/WIFIA
- Toll new capacity but not existing free roads

## Compromise Proposals

### Compromise 1: User Fee Modernization and Trust Fund Restoration Act

**Bridges:** Conservative + Liberal + Libertarian + Constitutionalist + Centrist

**Core Agreement:** The Highway Trust Fund must be restored to solvency through sustainable, dedicated revenue mechanisms that maintain the user-pays principle while transitioning from fuel taxes to vehicle miles traveled fees as the fleet electrifies.

**Policy Description:**

Federal legislation restores trust fund solvency through a three-track approach. Track 1 (immediate): a 10-cent-per-gallon gas tax increase indexed to construction costs, generating approximately $15 billion annually; an annual EV fee equivalent to average fuel tax paid by comparable gasoline vehicles, collected through state registration systems. Track 2 (5-year transition): expanded VMT fee pilot programs in 10+ volunteer states with federal matching funds and technical assistance; multiple privacy-protective technology options (odometer reporting, time-based permits, mileage-based insurance) with no GPS tracking requirement; state VMT fee authorization with interstate compact authority for cross-border coordination. Track 3 (10-year goal): federal VMT fee program replacing the gas tax for all vehicles with rates set to match current fuel tax revenue per mile; variable pricing authority for congestion management; spending obligation limitation aligned with projected revenue. Equity provisions include low-income rebates for the gas tax increase (funded at $2 billion annually), rural rate adjustments for VMT fees, and accessibility accommodations for persons with disabilities.

| Libertarian | VMT fees are true user fees; congestion pricing enables market signaling; gas tax is transitional with defined sunset; multiple technology options protect privacy; state authorization respects federalism |
| Constitutionalist | Federal taxing power clearly authorizes fuel taxes and VMT fees; state authorization respects federalism; interstate compacts have constitutional precedent; spending limitation reflects fiscal discipline |

| Centrist | Accept that some perspectives will not join the coalition; accept transition timeline as politically necessary |

**Viability Assessment:**

- **Coalition Size:** 5/9 perspectives (Progressive and Democratic Socialist oppose user-fee model as regressive; Populist opposes tax increases on working people; Religious Right follows conservative coalition)
- **High Consistency Ratio:** 5/5 coalition members rated 4+ consistency (Conservative at 3 is the weakest link)
- **Durability:** High; once VMT infrastructure is built, institutional momentum sustains the program; indexing prevents future political battles over rate increases; multiple state pilots create demonstration effects; trust fund solvency eliminates the perennial crisis that destabilizes transportation planning
- **Implementation Path:** Build on IIJA VMT pilot authority; bipartisan Senate sponsorship from infrastructure-committee centrists; state governor support through revenue-sharing provisions; construction industry and engineering organizations provide lobbying support; frame as "fixing the highway trust fund" rather than "raising taxes"

### Compromise 2: American Infrastructure Investment Bank Act

**Bridges:** Liberal + Progressive + Populist + Centrist + Religious Right + Democratic Socialist

**Core Agreement:** Federal investment must be leveraged to close the infrastructure gap through a merit-based lending institution that prioritizes underserved communities, creates construction jobs, and multiplies the impact of taxpayer dollars without privatizing public infrastructure.

**Policy Description:**

Federal legislation establishes the American Infrastructure Investment Bank as a government corporation capitalized at $50 billion with authority to issue up to $200 billion in bonds. The bank provides low-interest loans (Treasury rate plus administrative fee) and loan guarantees for infrastructure projects across all sectors: surface transportation, water, energy, broadband, public buildings, and resilience. Governance includes a 7-member board with the Secretaries of Transportation and Treasury, plus 5 presidentially appointed members with expertise in infrastructure, finance, regional development, labor, and community investment. Project selection criteria include: economic benefit-cost ratio, environmental and climate benefits, regional significance, service to underserved communities (rural, tribal, environmental justice), job creation with prevailing wage and apprenticeship requirements, and innovation. Geographic equity provisions require that at least 30% of lending serve rural areas and at least 20% serve environmental justice communities. All projects receiving bank financing must comply with Buy America requirements and prevailing wage standards. The bank reports annually to Congress on lending by sector, geography, community type, and employment impact. An independent Inspector General audits operations and project outcomes.

**Concern Mapping:**

| Perspective | How This Addresses Their Concerns |
|-------------|-----------------------------------|
| Liberal | Leverages federal investment; merit-based selection is superior to earmarks; equity and environmental criteria ensure broad benefit; Buy America and prevailing wage protect workers |
| Progressive | Strong labor standards; community and environmental justice criteria; public institution rather than privatization; geographic equity provisions address underserved communities |
| Populist | Creates construction jobs with good wages; funds projects in forgotten communities; Buy America requirements protect domestic industry; anti-elite governance with community representation |
| Centrist | Merit-based selection improves investment quality; leverage multiplies federal dollars; independence reduces political allocation; accountability provisions ensure transparency |
| Religious Right | Rural lending requirements serve faith communities; community infrastructure investment; public institution with accountability; job creation in underserved areas |
| Democratic Socialist | Public institution rather than privatization; labor standards and community criteria; geographic equity; democratic governance with labor representation |

**Concessions:**

| Perspective | What They Give Up |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Liberal | Accept leverage model rather than direct appropriation for full investment need; accept that the bank cannot fund all worthy projects |
| Progressive | Accept that the bank operates within market-based lending framework; accept that $50 billion capitalization is less than the full investment need |
| Populist | Accept merit-based selection rather than geographic allocation; accept that some projects will be in urban areas |
| Centrist | Accept labor and equity criteria that may increase project costs; accept that political pressures will test bank independence |
| Religious Right | Accept prevailing wage requirements that increase costs; accept environmental criteria |
| Democratic Socialist | Accept lending model rather than direct public investment; accept that bank leverages private capital alongside public |

**Viability Assessment:**

- **Coalition Size:** 6/9 perspectives (Conservative opposes new federal institution; Libertarian opposes government banking; Constitutionalist has governance concerns)
- **High Consistency Ratio:** 4/6 coalition members rated 4+ consistency (Liberal, Progressive, Centrist, Democratic Socialist)
- **Durability:** High; once established, the bank creates institutional momentum through outstanding loans and community relationships; successful projects demonstrate value; bipartisan rural-urban coalition protects against repeal; job creation creates political constituencies for continued operation
- **Implementation Path:** Build on decades of infrastructure bank proposals; bipartisan Senate sponsorship emphasizing job creation and community investment; construction industry, labor unions, and rural development organizations provide lobbying support; frame as "investing in America's forgotten communities" to bridge populist and progressive support

### Compromise 3: State Infrastructure Finance Empowerment Act

**Bridges:** Conservative + Liberal + Libertarian + Constitutionalist + Centrist + Religious Right

**Core Agreement:** States and localities are best positioned to determine their infrastructure needs and financing mechanisms, and the federal government should empower state action through authorization, technical assistance, and incentives rather than imposing uniform federal solutions.

**Policy Description:**

Federal legislation authorizes and incentivizes state infrastructure finance reform through four mechanisms. First, it authorizes states to impose VMT fees with full federal support: technical assistance for pilot programs ($100 million annually), interstate compact authority for cross-border coordination, and federal data standards ensuring interoperability. Second, it provides federal matching incentives for states that index their fuel taxes to inflation or construction costs, with a 10% bonus federal match for states that adopt indexed rates. Third, it authorizes expanded state value capture tools: model legislation for tax increment financing, special assessment districts, development charges, and air rights development, with federal tax treatment ensuring that value capture bonds receive the same favorable treatment as general obligation bonds. Fourth, it establishes a State Infrastructure Bank Enhancement Program with federal seed capital ($500 million) for new state infrastructure banks and expanded lending authority for existing ones, with technical assistance and best-practice sharing. States receiving federal infrastructure funds must adopt asset management plans, performance-based investment criteria, and public reporting on infrastructure condition and spending outcomes.

**Concern Mapping:**

| Perspective | How This Addresses Their Concerns |
|-------------|-----------------------------------|
| Conservative | State-led approach respects federalism; no new federal taxes; federal role limited to authorization and incentives; market-based tools (VMT, value capture) emphasized |
| Liberal | Federal incentives push states toward sustainable revenue; matching provisions reward good governance; performance requirements ensure accountability; asset management improves outcomes |
| Libertarian | State action respects subsidiarity; VMT fees are true user fees; value capture is market-based; no new federal institution; reduces federal role over time |
| Constitutionalist | Clearly within federal spending power as incentive; state authority is primary; interstate compact authority has constitutional precedent; accountability requirements are tied to voluntary federal funds |
| Centrist | Pragmatic approach building on proven state models; incentive structure rewards reform; best-practice sharing accelerates adoption; performance requirements ensure results |
| Religious Right | State and local action is closer to communities; voluntary participation; no mandates; rural communities can design appropriate solutions |

**Concessions:**

| Perspective | What They Give Up |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Conservative | Accept federal incentive structure that influences state decisions; accept performance requirements tied to federal funds |
| Liberal | Accept state variation rather than uniform federal standards; accept that some states will not adopt reforms |
| Libertarian | Accept federal matching incentives as government intervention; accept performance requirements |
| Constitutionalist | Accept federal incentives that influence state policy choices; accept federal data standards |
| Centrist | Accept that state variation will produce uneven results; accept slower reform than federal action would achieve |
| Religious Right | Accept performance requirements and reporting as conditions of federal funds |

**Viability Assessment:**

- **Coalition Size:** 6/9 perspectives (Progressive opposes state-led approach as insufficient; Democratic Socialist opposes devolution; Populist is ambivalent about state variation)
- **High Consistency Ratio:** 5/6 coalition members rated 4+ consistency (Liberal, Libertarian, Constitutionalist, Centrist; Conservative at 3 is the weakest link)
- **Durability:** High; state-level reforms are difficult to reverse once implemented; indexed taxes and VMT systems create institutional infrastructure; federal incentives can be adjusted without repealing the framework; state variation allows experimentation and learning; over 30 states have already adopted elements of this approach
- **Implementation Path:** Build on existing state fuel tax indexing and EV fee adoption; bipartisan Senate sponsorship from governors-turned-senators; National Governors Association and National Conference of State Legislatures provide lobbying support; frame as "empowering states to fix their own infrastructure" to attract federalism-oriented support from both parties

## Strategic Implications

### Most Viable Coalition

The most effective coalition for infrastructure finance reform operates on three complementary tracks. The broadest coalition (6/9 perspectives) supports state-level finance empowerment because it respects federalism, avoids new federal taxes, and builds on proven state models -- this is the path of least resistance. A cross-ideological coalition (5/9 perspectives) supports VMT fee transition because user-pays principles appeal to market-oriented perspectives while sustainable revenue appeals to investment-oriented perspectives -- this is the most important long-term reform. An investment-oriented coalition (6/9 perspectives) supports a national infrastructure bank because it leverages federal dollars without new taxes and creates jobs -- this addresses the scale of the investment gap. Pursuing all three tracks simultaneously maximizes coalition breadth and creates negotiating flexibility.

### Key Obstacles

- **Anti-tax rigidity:** The federal gas tax has not been increased since 1993 because any revenue increase triggers anti-tax mobilization regardless of fiscal necessity; even indexing (which merely prevents purchasing power loss) is characterized as a tax increase; this produces the absurd result that infrastructure deteriorates and general fund transfers increase the deficit because maintaining a dedicated revenue source is labeled politically impossible
- **User fee versus public good framing:** The fundamental tension between user-fee advocates (Conservative, Libertarian) and public-good advocates (Progressive, Democratic Socialist) means that no single revenue model commands majority support; user fees are rejected as regressive by the left, while progressive taxation is rejected as redistribution by the right
- **P3 polarization:** Public-private partnerships attract strong support from the right and strong opposition from the left, making them a coalition-dividing rather than coalition-building mechanism despite their practical utility for certain project types
- **Climate-infrastructure disconnect:** Carbon pricing as an infrastructure revenue source faces structural opposition from 5/9 perspectives, preventing the integration of climate policy and infrastructure finance that would address both challenges simultaneously

### Low Consistency Partners

Populist (2/5) and Religious Right (3/5) perspectives are the least reliable partners on infrastructure finance. Populist positions shift based on which party proposes the investment, and the combination of demanding infrastructure spending while opposing all revenue mechanisms creates an incoherent position that is difficult to incorporate into any coalition. Religious Right positions follow conservative coalition alignment rather than independent faith-based analysis, meaning that engaging their genuine stewardship and community values requires separating infrastructure from the broader partisan framework. Conservative (3/5) inconsistency on infrastructure -- supporting investment in principle while blocking revenue in practice -- is the most consequential barrier because conservative votes are necessary for bipartisan legislation.

### Recommended Approach

1. **Lead with state empowerment:** The State Infrastructure Finance Empowerment Act (Compromise 3) commands the broadest coalition and faces the least opposition because it avoids new federal taxes, respects federalism, and builds on proven state models; this creates momentum and demonstrates that infrastructure finance reform is achievable

2. **Build the VMT transition:** The User Fee Modernization and Trust Fund Restoration Act (Compromise 1) addresses the fundamental revenue sustainability crisis and commands support from an ideologically diverse coalition united by the user-pays principle; framing VMT fees as "the 21st-century replacement for the gas tax" rather than "a new tax" is essential

3. **Establish the infrastructure bank:** The American Infrastructure Investment Bank Act (Compromise 2) leverages federal investment to address the scale of the gap and commands a center-left-populist coalition; job creation and community investment framing bridges the populist divide

4. **Use fiscal arguments for conservative engagement:** The most effective argument for conservative infrastructure finance support is fiscal: general fund transfers to the Highway Trust Fund increase the deficit, deferred maintenance costs 2-6 times more than timely maintenance, and user fees are more fiscally responsible than deficit spending; these arguments reach fiscal conservatives who cannot be reached by public investment framing

5. **Decouple infrastructure from climate:** While the climate-infrastructure connection is real and important, carbon pricing as an infrastructure revenue source faces structural opposition from 5/9 perspectives; pursuing infrastructure finance reform independently of climate policy maximizes coalition breadth while climate-aligned investment criteria within the infrastructure bank address environmental goals without triggering anti-climate opposition

6. **Build evidence through state pilots:** State-level VMT pilots, infrastructure bank operations, and value capture programs create the evidence base for expanding reforms; demonstrating that reformed finance mechanisms work -- generating revenue, improving infrastructure condition, and maintaining equity -- undermines opposition arguments and builds political support for broader adoption

7. **Engage construction and engineering constituencies:** The construction industry, engineering organizations, and building trades unions form a powerful cross-ideological lobbying coalition for infrastructure investment; their engagement provides political cover for revenue votes and creates sustained pressure for action
