# Ports and Waterways: Political Perspectives Analysis

## Overview

This document analyzes ports and waterways infrastructure reform -- specifically increasing investment in aging inland waterway locks, expanding seaport capacity, deepening navigation channels, improving intermodal connections, strengthening climate resilience, reforming funding mechanisms, streamlining permitting, and reducing port emissions -- through nine distinct political perspectives. The core questions are: how much should the federal government invest in maritime infrastructure and through what mechanisms, what is the proper division of responsibility between federal, state, and private actors, how should environmental protection and community impacts be balanced against economic competitiveness, and how should climate resilience be incorporated into infrastructure design and funding? Each perspective receives engagement-consistency ratings and position scores, culminating in compromise proposals that bridge multiple viewpoints.

Ports and waterways infrastructure is among the least politically polarized infrastructure domains because the economic case for investment is broadly accepted -- over 99% of overseas trade moves by water, inland waterways carry agricultural exports critical to rural economies, and port congestion imposes visible costs on businesses and consumers. However, significant disagreements emerge around the role of the federal government versus private investment, the pace of environmental regulation, labor relations and automation, climate resilience mandates, and the distribution of benefits among large gateway ports and smaller regional facilities. The 2021 Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (IIJA) demonstrated that bipartisan maritime infrastructure investment is achievable, but the scale of the challenge -- $130 billion in unmet inland waterway needs alone, locks averaging 60+ years old, and channels inadequate for modern vessel sizes -- far exceeds current commitments. The relative political consensus on the problem creates opportunities for coalition-building that are rarer in more polarized policy domains.

### Engagement Consistency (1-5)

This metric assesses how reliably a perspective's stated positions align with its underlying principles, and how stable those positions are when concerns are addressed. Higher scores indicate more predictable negotiating partners.

| Level | Label | Indicators |
|-------|-------|------------|
| 5 | Highly Consistent | Stated positions reflect underlying principles; positions stable when concerns addressed |
| 4 | Mostly Consistent | Principles generally align with positions; minor strategic flexibility |
| 3 | Mixed Consistency | Some positions principled, others reflect coalition pressures or unstated concerns |
| 2 | Low Consistency | Positions shift frequently; stated concerns differ from underlying priorities |
| 1 | Unpredictable | Positions appear driven primarily by opposition dynamics rather than principles |

**Coalition-Building Considerations:**

- Alignment gap: When stated concerns differ from underlying priorities, addressing stated concerns may not secure support
- Position stability: Perspectives with shifting positions require ongoing negotiation
- Principle consistency: Perspectives that apply principles uniformly are more predictable partners
- Internal diversity: Some perspectives contain factions with different priorities

### Conservative Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 4/5 - Mostly Consistent**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Genuine commitment to economic competitiveness and trade facilitation through infrastructure investment; fiscal restraint principles are applied more consistently in this domain than in defense spending, though conservatives have supported significant maritime spending when framed as commerce and national security |
| Principle consistency | Limited government and market-solutions principles are applied reasonably consistently: prefer user-fee funding over general revenue, favor private-sector participation, resist environmental mandates, and support streamlined permitting; however, willingness to authorize large federal infrastructure programs through WRDA reveals that "limited government" is applied flexibly when infrastructure serves business interests |
| Goalpost stability | Stable; conservative support for inland waterway investment and port infrastructure has been consistent across decades, driven by agricultural export needs and commercial interests; the WRDA authorization process has historically been bipartisan |
| Zero-sum behavior | Low on core infrastructure investment; moderate on environmental and labor provisions where conservatives resist regulatory conditions attached to spending |

**Justification:** Conservatives bring a coherent framework to ports and waterways: infrastructure investment is justified by economic returns and trade competitiveness, funding should come from user fees and trust funds rather than general revenue where possible, the federal role is legitimate under the Commerce Clause but should be efficient and streamlined, and environmental and labor regulations should not impede project delivery. The slight reduction from a perfect score reflects occasional tension between fiscal restraint rhetoric and support for large federal infrastructure programs -- conservatives have consistently voted for WRDA authorizations totaling billions while simultaneously advocating spending cuts elsewhere.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Bipartisan WRDA reauthorizations have received broad conservative support across multiple Congresses
- Republican members from agricultural states consistently champion inland waterway lock replacement
- U.S. Chamber of Commerce and National Association of Manufacturers advocate for port and waterway investment with conservative support
- Heritage Foundation infrastructure analysis supports user-fee funding and streamlined permitting while questioning general fund expenditures
- Conservative support for IIJA port provisions demonstrated willingness to invest at scale when framed as economic competitiveness

#### Alternative Proposals

- Fund infrastructure primarily through user fees (HMT, IWTF fuel tax) with reformed trust fund mechanisms rather than general revenue
- Accelerate permitting through categorical exclusions for maintenance dredging, lock rehabilitation, and channel restoration
- Expand public-private partnerships for terminal investment and port operations
- Prioritize projects with highest benefit-cost ratios regardless of geographic distribution
- Reduce regulatory conditions on federal grants to accelerate project delivery
- Allow advance construction authority so non-federal sponsors can proceed without waiting for federal appropriations

#### Coalition Potential

- **Natural allies:** Centrist (on infrastructure investment and process reform), Populist (on economic nationalism and domestic industry support), Constitutionalist (on Commerce Clause authority)
- **Potential bridges:** Liberal (on core infrastructure investment levels), Religious Right (on economic development and job creation)
- **Unlikely partners:** Progressive and Democratic Socialist (on environmental mandates and labor conditions)

### Constitutionalist Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 5/5 - Highly Consistent**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Genuine commitment to constitutional text and structure applied to maritime infrastructure; the Commerce Clause and navigation servitude provide one of the strongest constitutional foundations for federal infrastructure authority, and constitutionalists acknowledge this consistently |
| Principle consistency | Constitutional analysis uniformly applied: federal navigation authority is well-grounded in the Commerce Clause and historical practice dating to the founding; the navigation servitude gives the federal government authority over navigable waters; spending power supports port grants; but Tenth Amendment limits federal authority to mandate state action |
| Goalpost stability | Highly stable; constitutional text provides consistent reference point; the federal role in navigation has been accepted since *Gibbons v. Ogden* (1824) and is not contested |
| Zero-sum behavior | None; constitutional analysis is principled rather than partisan; applies the same framework to both expanding and limiting federal authority in this domain |

**Justification:** Constitutionalists have the most straightforward analysis of ports and waterways because federal authority over navigation and interstate/foreign commerce is among the most well-established constitutional powers. The Commerce Clause, navigation servitude, and spending power provide ample authority for the proposed legislation. Unlike more contested policy domains, there is no significant constitutional objection to federal port and waterway investment. The constitutionalist perspective primarily adds value by ensuring that programs respect federalism (states cannot be compelled to participate), that spending conditions meet South Dakota v. Dole requirements, and that regulatory authority does not exceed Commerce Clause bounds.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- *Gibbons v. Ogden*, 22 U.S. 1 (1824) -- foundational Commerce Clause case establishing federal navigation authority
- Federal navigation authority and the navigation servitude have been consistently upheld by the Supreme Court
- Rivers and Harbors Acts dating to the early Republic reflect accepted constitutional authority
- Federalist Society analysis of infrastructure authority accepts Commerce Clause foundation for navigation programs
- Constitutional scholars across perspectives accept federal port and waterway authority

## Compromise Proposals

### Compromise 1: National Waterways Modernization and Competitiveness Act

**Bridges:** Conservative + Liberal + Progressive + Populist + Centrist + Constitutionalist + Religious Right + Democratic Socialist

**Core Agreement:** Aging inland waterway locks are a critical national infrastructure crisis threatening agricultural exports, domestic commerce, and economic competitiveness, requiring accelerated federal investment funded through reformed trust fund mechanisms.

**Policy Description:**

Federal legislation authorizes $15 billion over ten years for priority lock replacement and rehabilitation, with the 20 most critical locks identified by the Corps of Engineers for accelerated construction on a ten-year schedule. The federal cost share for priority locks increases to 85%, with remaining locks at 75%. The Inland Waterways Trust Fund fuel tax increases to fund the higher non-federal share, with a phased implementation schedule. Full spending of Harbor Maintenance Tax collections is mandated, with formula reform allocating 12% to small and emerging ports, 5% to environmental restoration, and 5% to climate resilience, with the remainder distributed based on commercial need. An expedited review process for lock replacement projects reduces the study-to-construction timeline to five years through concurrent environmental review, programmatic NEPA coverage for rehabilitation projects, and decision deadlines. All construction requires compliance with prevailing wage laws. A biennial report to Congress tracks project progress, cost performance, and economic outcomes.

### Compromise 2: Port Investment and Community Protection Act

**Bridges:** Liberal + Progressive + Centrist + Religious Right + Democratic Socialist + Conservative (partial)

**Core Agreement:** Port capacity expansion is economically necessary, and communities adjacent to ports deserve protection from pollution and environmental impacts, creating a framework where investment and community benefit are complementary rather than competing priorities.

**Policy Description:**

Federal legislation expands the Port Infrastructure Development Program (PIDP) to $1.5 billion annually, with eligibility for terminal investment, intermodal connections, emission reduction equipment, and climate resilience projects. Twenty percent of funding is reserved for emission reduction and community protection projects. Ports receiving PIDP grants exceeding $10 million must conduct community health impact assessments and establish community advisory committees. A phased emission reduction framework requires ports receiving federal funds to develop emission reduction plans with achievable targets based on available technology, with EPA providing technical assistance and periodic technology readiness assessments guiding timeline adjustments. Shore power infrastructure funding of $300 million annually helps ports install plug-in capacity, with ports required to offer shore power within five years of receiving installation grants. An air quality monitoring program funds real-time monitoring at the 50 highest-volume ports. Clean truck programs receive $200 million annually for zero-emission drayage truck incentives, with phase-in timelines based on vehicle availability and cost parity. All funded projects must comply with prevailing wage requirements.

### Compromise 3: Maritime Permitting Modernization Act

**Bridges:** Conservative + Libertarian + Populist + Centrist + Liberal (partial) + Constitutionalist

**Core Agreement:** The 20-year timeline from study to construction for navigation projects is unacceptable, and permitting reform can reduce timelines significantly without eliminating environmental protections, by improving process efficiency rather than reducing substantive standards.

**Policy Description:**

Federal legislation establishes a seven-year maximum timeline from study initiation to construction start for navigation projects, with concurrent rather than sequential federal agency review, a single lead agency designated for each project, and binding decision deadlines. Programmatic environmental assessments cover routine maintenance dredging, minor lock rehabilitation, and navigation aid replacement, eliminating individual project review for categories with well-understood environmental impacts. An advance construction authority allows non-federal sponsors to begin construction using their own funds before federal appropriations are available, with federal reimbursement when funds are appropriated. A dedicated Corps of Engineers navigation project delivery fund of $200 million annually provides additional staff and technology for expedited review. Environmental standards are maintained at existing levels -- the legislation reforms process, not substance. Community input is preserved through early engagement requirements and a structured public comment period within the concurrent review timeline. An annual report to Congress tracks project timelines, identifies bottlenecks, and recommends further reforms. Categorical exclusions are reviewed and updated every five years to reflect current environmental science.

|-------------|-----------------------------------|
| Conservative | Dramatic timeline reduction; concurrent review; advance construction authority; categorical exclusions for routine work; addresses the #1 conservative infrastructure concern |
| Libertarian | Reduces government process burden; advance construction allows private initiative; agency accountability through deadlines; the most acceptable reform given broader opposition to federal programs |
| Populist | American ports can be built faster; reduces bureaucratic delays that allow foreign competitors to advance; deadline accountability for federal agencies |
| Centrist | Evidence-based process reform; maintains environmental standards while improving efficiency; structured timelines with accountability; bipartisan appeal |
| Liberal | Environmental standards maintained; community input preserved; dedicated funding improves agency capacity rather than cutting corners; early engagement may improve environmental outcomes |
| Constitutionalist | Within congressional authority to set administrative procedures; preserves due process for affected parties; improves separation of powers through congressional deadline setting |

**Concessions:**

| Perspective | What They Give Up |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Conservative | Accept that environmental standards are maintained rather than reduced; accept dedicated agency funding rather than cuts |
| Libertarian | Accept continued federal project process rather than privatization; accept that reform is incremental |
| Populist | Accept that some environmental review continues; accept structured community input |
| Centrist | Accept binding deadlines that may occasionally force premature decisions |
| Liberal | Accept significantly faster timelines; accept categorical exclusions for routine activities; accept that some environmental review is consolidated |
| Constitutionalist | Accept expanded categorical exclusion authority |

**Viability Assessment:**

- **Coalition Size:** 6/9 perspectives (Progressive and Democratic Socialist oppose permitting streamlining; Religious Right is neutral)
- **High Consistency Ratio:** 5/6 coalition members rated 4+ consistency (Conservative, Liberal, Libertarian, Centrist, Constitutionalist)
- **Durability:** High; permitting reform creates institutional change that is difficult to reverse; once timelines are established, agencies adapt processes; advance construction authority creates momentum; bipartisan support from both business and labor (faster projects mean faster job creation)
- **Implementation Path:** Bipartisan Senate sponsorship building on existing permitting reform proposals (FAST Act model, One Federal Decision framework); Corps of Engineers reform has broad stakeholder support; attach to WRDA reauthorization or standalone legislation; frame as "building faster, not building dirtier"

## Strategic Implications

### Most Viable Coalition

The most effective coalition for ports and waterways reform operates on three tracks. The broadest coalition (8/9 perspectives) can advance inland waterway lock modernization -- the reform area with near-universal support because agricultural exports, domestic commerce, and rural economic development create constituencies across the political spectrum. A bipartisan center-right coalition (Conservative, Libertarian, Populist, Centrist, Constitutionalist, with Liberal partial support) can advance permitting reform through the "process not substance" framing that maintains environmental standards while reducing timelines. A center-left coalition (Liberal, Progressive, Centrist, Religious Right, Democratic Socialist) can advance port emission reduction and community protection through environmental justice and public health framing.

### Key Obstacles

- **Environmental-permitting trade-off:** The left wants strong environmental mandates but resists permitting reform; the right wants permitting reform but resists environmental mandates; this creates the primary legislative negotiation challenge
- **Clean Ports Act polarization:** The Clean Ports Act draws the sharpest divisions, with strong support from the left (Liberal 9, Progressive 10, Democratic Socialist 10) and strong opposition from the right (Conservative 3, Libertarian 1, Populist 2); unlike lock replacement, this legislation cannot be advanced through a broad coalition
- **Funding competition:** Federal infrastructure spending competes with other priorities; even with bipartisan support for investment, appropriations face annual uncertainty and competing demands
- **Industry resistance to emission mandates:** Shipping lines, terminal operators, and trucking companies resist emission requirements that increase operating costs, creating industry lobbying against environmental provisions

### Low Consistency Partners

Populist (3/5) and Religious Right (3/5) perspectives rate at mixed consistency on ports and waterways, though for different reasons. Populist positions are shaped by framing -- economic nationalism and anti-elite themes secure support, while environmental regulation themes trigger opposition, even when the same policy serves both frames. Religious Right positions are largely derivative of conservative coalition alignment rather than independent faith-based analysis, making them potentially movable through stewardship and community protection framing. Neither perspective presents the deep alignment gaps seen in more polarized policy domains.

### Recommended Approach

1. **Lead with lock modernization:** The National Waterways Modernization and Competitiveness Act (Compromise 1) commands 8/9 perspective support and addresses the most urgent infrastructure need; attach to the next WRDA reauthorization for the strongest legislative vehicle; agricultural state delegations from both parties provide reliable bipartisan support

2. **Advance permitting reform separately:** The Maritime Permitting Modernization Act (Compromise 3) commands 6/9 perspective support and addresses the conservative priority of reducing project timelines; framing as "process reform, not environmental rollback" can secure liberal partial support; advance as standalone legislation or WRDA attachment

3. **Build the environmental coalition incrementally:** The Port Investment and Community Protection Act (Compromise 2) commands 6/9 perspective support with partial conservative acceptance through technology readiness assessments and phased timelines; advancing community health assessments and air monitoring as data-gathering measures builds the evidence base for stronger action; do not link to permitting reform to avoid activating the environmental-permitting trade-off

4. **Use the WRDA cycle strategically:** Water Resources Development Acts are reauthorized regularly with strong bipartisan traditions; use this legislative vehicle for investment and process reforms while pursuing environmental measures through standalone legislation or appropriations riders

5. **Build from IIJA implementation:** The Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act provides current funding for port and waterway projects; demonstrating effective implementation and measurable results builds the case for sustained and expanded investment

6. **Frame through economic competitiveness:** The most broadly persuasive argument for maritime infrastructure investment is economic competitiveness -- aging infrastructure makes American ports less efficient than foreign competitors, supply chain disruptions impose visible economic costs, and infrastructure investment creates domestic jobs; this framing reaches Conservative, Populist, and Centrist perspectives more effectively than environmental or equity framing

7. **Engage environmental justice communities as bridge:** Port-adjacent community health impacts provide a bridge between environmental and economic arguments; air quality monitoring data and community health assessments build an evidence base that transcends ideological framing
