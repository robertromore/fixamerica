# Infrastructure Policy: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Infrastructure legislation operates across federal, state, and local levels with overlapping jurisdictions and funding mechanisms. The legislative framework includes surface transportation reauthorizations, water infrastructure funding, broadband deployment, and climate resilience requirements. This document outlines key legislative reforms needed to address America's infrastructure crisis.

## Constitutional Amendments

### Infrastructure as a National Priority Amendment

**Purpose**: Establish constitutional support for federal infrastructure investment and sustainable funding mechanisms.

**Draft Text**:

```text
Section 1. The federal government shall maintain national infrastructure
systems adequate for commerce, defense, and public welfare, including
transportation, water, communication, and energy networks.

Section 2. Congress may levy user fees, including charges based on
vehicle miles traveled or other usage metrics, to fund transportation
infrastructure, notwithstanding any state law to the contrary.

Section 3. Nothing in this article shall prevent states from imposing
additional fees for state infrastructure systems.

Section 4. Congress shall have the power to enforce this article
by appropriate legislation.

Section 5. This article shall take effect two years after ratification.
```

**Explanation**:

- Section 1 establishes affirmative infrastructure responsibility
- Section 2 provides clear constitutional authority for VMT fees
- Section 3 preserves state funding authority
- Addresses Commerce Clause questions about national VMT systems

**Potential Challenges**:

- State sovereignty concerns over federal user fees
- Resistance from anti-tax constituencies
- Questions about "adequate" standard

**Refinements**:

- Add definition of "adequate" tied to safety and condition standards
- Include state opt-out with matching requirements
- Establish infrastructure condition floor

## Federal Legislation

### Surface Transportation Reauthorization Reform Act

**Purpose**: Restructure federal highway and transit funding to prioritize maintenance, climate resilience, and sustainable revenue.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Surface Transportation Reauthorization
Reform Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that—
(1) the Highway Trust Fund faces persistent revenue shortfalls due
to declining gas tax receipts;
(2) deferred maintenance creates larger long-term costs;
(3) climate change requires infrastructure adaptation;
(4) transportation investment creates significant economic returns.

SEC. 3. FIX-IT-FIRST REQUIREMENTS.

(a) In General.—No State shall receive Federal-aid highway funds
unless it certifies that—
  (1) at least 50 percent of Federal-aid funds are used for
  maintenance and repair of existing assets;
  (2) no new capacity expansion project may proceed until the
  State's bridge and pavement conditions meet minimum thresholds;
  (3) the State has implemented a comprehensive asset management
  system meeting Federal standards.

(b) Minimum Condition Thresholds.—
  (1) BRIDGES.—No more than 5 percent of deck area may be
  structurally deficient.
  (2) PAVEMENT.—No more than 20 percent of Interstate lane-miles
  and 30 percent of non-Interstate NHS lane-miles may be in
  poor condition.

(c) Enforcement.—The Secretary shall withhold 10 percent of
Federal-aid apportionment from any State failing to meet the
requirements of this section.

SEC. 4. VEHICLE MILES TRAVELED FEE PROGRAM.

(a) National VMT Pilot.—The Secretary shall establish a national
vehicle miles traveled fee pilot program.

(b) State Participation.—
  (1) VOLUNTARY PARTICIPATION.—States may opt into the national
  VMT program.
  (2) INCENTIVES.—Participating States shall receive a 10 percent
  bonus on Federal-aid apportionments.

(c) Privacy Protections.—
  (1) LOCATION DATA.—No Federal VMT system shall track or store
  vehicle location data.
  (2) OPTIONS.—Drivers shall have multiple compliance options,
  including mileage-based reporting without GPS tracking.

(d) Equity Adjustments.—
  (1) RURAL ADJUSTMENT.—Reduced rates for rural residents with
  limited transportation alternatives.
  (2) LOW-INCOME CREDIT.—Tax credit for VMT fees paid by
  households below 200 percent of poverty level.

SEC. 5. CLIMATE RESILIENCE REQUIREMENTS.

(a) Design Standards.—All Federal-aid projects shall incorporate
climate projections based on the most recent IPCC scenarios for
the project's expected service life.

(b) Vulnerability Assessment.—Each State shall complete a
transportation system vulnerability assessment within 3 years
of enactment.

(c) Adaptation Investment.—At least 5 percent of Federal-aid
apportionments shall be dedicated to climate adaptation projects.

SEC. 6. TRANSIT PARITY.

(a) Funding Ratio.—Federal transit funding shall be maintained
at no less than 20 percent of surface transportation spending.

(b) Operating Assistance.—Federal operating assistance shall be
available for transit systems in urbanized areas under 200,000
population and for any system facing ridership declines exceeding
20 percent.

SEC. 7. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

(a) Highway Account.—There is authorized to be appropriated to
the Highway Account of the Highway Trust Fund—
  (1) $50,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2027;
  (2) $52,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2028;
  (3) $54,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2029;
  (4) $56,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2030;
  (5) $58,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2031.

(b) Transit Account.—There is authorized to be appropriated to
the Mass Transit Account—
  (1) $15,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2027;
  (2) $16,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2028;
  (3) $17,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2029;
  (4) $18,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2030;
  (5) $19,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2031.
```

**Explanation**:

- Fix-it-first requirements shift incentives toward maintenance
- VMT program addresses gas tax revenue decline with privacy protections
- Climate requirements build resilience into new investments
- Transit parity ensures multimodal investment

**Potential Challenges**:

- State resistance to maintenance mandates
- VMT privacy concerns
- Cost of equity adjustments
- Climate denial politics

**Refinements**:

- Phase in fix-it-first over 3 years
- Allow state VMT alternatives meeting federal standards
- Create rural infrastructure block grant as alternative

### National Water Infrastructure Act

**Purpose**: Accelerate water infrastructure investment with focus on lead elimination, system consolidation, and affordability.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "National Water Infrastructure Act".

SEC. 2. LEAD SERVICE LINE ELIMINATION.

(a) Deadline.—All lead service lines in the United States shall
be replaced by December 31, 2035.

(b) Federal Funding.—
  (1) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized $45,000,000,000 over
  10 years for lead service line replacement grants.
  (2) FORMULA.—Grants shall be distributed based on estimated
  lead service line counts and community poverty rates.
  (3) FULL REPLACEMENT.—Grants shall cover full replacement
  costs for households below 200 percent of poverty level.

(c) Inventory Requirements.—
  (1) DEADLINE.—All water systems shall complete service line
  material inventories within 2 years of enactment.
  (2) PUBLIC DISCLOSURE.—Inventories shall be publicly accessible.

SEC. 3. SMALL SYSTEM CAPACITY.

(a) Technical Assistance.—The Administrator shall establish
regional technical assistance centers to support systems serving
fewer than 10,000 people.

(b) Consolidation Incentives.—
  (1) GRANTS.—Grants of up to $5,000,000 for system consolidation
  feasibility studies and implementation.
  (2) REGULATORY FLEXIBILITY.—Consolidated systems shall receive
  3-year compliance extensions for inherited violations.

(c) Circuit Rider Program.—Expand the rural water circuit rider
program to serve all States with significant small system presence.

SEC. 4. WATER AFFORDABILITY.

(a) Low-Income Assistance.—
  (1) PROGRAM.—The Administrator shall establish a national
  low-income water and sewer assistance program.
  (2) ELIGIBILITY.—Households below 150 percent of poverty level
  shall be eligible.
  (3) BENEFIT.—Assistance shall ensure water and sewer bills
  do not exceed 2 percent of household income.

(b) Authorization.—There is authorized $2,000,000,000 annually
for the water affordability program.

SEC. 5. CLIMATE RESILIENCE FOR WATER SYSTEMS.

(a) Drought Planning.—All systems serving more than 3,300 people
shall develop drought contingency plans.

(b) Flood Resilience.—Systems in FEMA-designated flood zones
shall implement flood mitigation measures.

(c) Green Infrastructure.—At least 10 percent of Clean Water
State Revolving Fund allocations shall support green infrastructure.

SEC. 6. STATE REVOLVING FUND REFORMS.

(a) Disadvantaged Communities.—States shall direct at least
40 percent of SRF principal forgiveness to disadvantaged communities.

(b) Emerging Contaminants.—SRF funds may be used for PFAS and
other emerging contaminant treatment without matching requirements.
```

**Explanation**:

- Mandatory lead elimination with adequate funding
- Small system support addresses rural challenges
- Affordability program ensures access
- Climate provisions build resilience

**Potential Challenges**:

- Cost of lead elimination program
- Opposition from large utilities to consolidation
- PFAS treatment costs uncertain
- State matching capacity

**Refinements**:

- Phase lead deadline by system size
- Make consolidation voluntary with strong incentives
- Create emergency PFAS fund separate from SRF

### Universal Broadband Act

**Purpose**: Achieve universal broadband access through expanded deployment obligations, affordability programs, and municipal network authorization.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Universal Broadband Act".

SEC. 2. DEFINITIONS.

(a) Broadband.—"Broadband" means internet service providing
download speeds of at least 100 megabits per second and upload
speeds of at least 20 megabits per second.

(b) Unserved Area.—"Unserved area" means any location lacking
access to broadband service.

(c) Underserved Area.—"Underserved area" means any location
where only one provider offers broadband service.

SEC. 3. UNIVERSAL SERVICE OBLIGATION.

(a) Requirement.—By January 1, 2030, all locations in the United
States shall have access to broadband service.

(b) Provider Obligations.—
  (1) BUILDOUT.—Providers receiving Universal Service Fund support
  shall provide broadband to all locations in their service area.
  (2) TIMELINE.—Buildout shall be completed within 4 years of
  receiving USF support.
  (3) CLAWBACK.—Failure to meet buildout obligations shall result
  in return of all USF funds received plus 10 percent penalty.

SEC. 4. MUNICIPAL BROADBAND AUTHORIZATION.

(a) Preemption.—No State law shall prohibit or unreasonably
restrict a local government from providing broadband service.

(b) Conditions.—Local governments providing broadband shall—
  (1) conduct a public hearing before initiating service;
  (2) complete a feasibility study;
  (3) establish separate accounting for broadband operations.

(c) Pole Attachment.—Local government broadband providers shall
have the same pole attachment rights as private providers.

SEC. 5. AFFORDABILITY.

(a) Affordable Connectivity Program.—
  (1) PERMANENT AUTHORIZATION.—The Affordable Connectivity Program
  is permanently authorized.
  (2) BENEFIT.—Monthly benefit of $30 for eligible households,
  $75 for Tribal lands.
  (3) ELIGIBILITY.—Households at or below 200 percent of poverty
  level or receiving Federal assistance benefits.

(b) Service Obligation.—Providers receiving USF support shall
offer a service tier at no more than $30 per month after
application of ACP benefit.

SEC. 6. MAPPING AND DATA.

(a) Location Fabric.—The Commission shall maintain a complete
location fabric identifying all broadband-serviceable locations.

(b) Speed Verification.—
  (1) TESTING.—The Commission shall conduct or require speed
  testing to verify advertised speeds.
  (2) ACCURACY.—Providers failing to deliver 80 percent of
  advertised speeds shall be subject to penalties.

(c) Challenge Process.—Streamlined challenge process for
consumers and local governments to dispute coverage claims.

SEC. 7. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There is authorized to be appropriated $10,000,000,000 annually
for universal broadband deployment through fiscal year 2035.
```

**Explanation**:

- Updated speed standards reflect current technology
- Municipal authorization removes state barriers
- Permanent ACP ensures affordability
- Accountability provisions prevent waste

**Potential Challenges**:

- Telecom industry opposition to municipal networks
- Rural deployment costs
- ISP resistance to speed verification
- Spectrum constraints for wireless

**Refinements**:

- Include wireless/satellite options for remote areas
- Phase speed requirements based on technology availability
- Add digital literacy and adoption programs

## State Model Legislation

### State Infrastructure Bank Enhancement Act

**Purpose**: Strengthen state infrastructure banks with expanded capitalization and project authority.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Infrastructure Bank
Enhancement Act".

SECTION 2. CAPITALIZATION.

(a) The State Infrastructure Bank is authorized to receive—
  (1) appropriations from the General Fund;
  (2) proceeds from infrastructure-related fees and taxes;
  (3) federal capitalization grants;
  (4) private investment subject to Bank oversight.

(b) The Bank may issue bonds backed by its loan portfolio.

SECTION 3. ELIGIBLE PROJECTS.

The Bank may provide loans, loan guarantees, and credit
enhancement for—
  (1) surface transportation projects;
  (2) water and wastewater infrastructure;
  (3) broadband infrastructure;
  (4) public buildings;
  (5) energy infrastructure;
  (6) climate resilience projects.

SECTION 4. LENDING TERMS.

(a) Interest rates shall be at or below the Bank's cost of funds.
(b) Loan terms may extend up to 35 years.
(c) Subordinate loans permitted for credit enhancement.

SECTION 5. PROJECT EVALUATION.

Projects shall be evaluated based on—
  (1) economic benefit-cost ratio;
  (2) state of good repair contribution;
  (3) climate resilience;
  (4) equity impact;
  (5) job creation.
```

**Explanation**:

- Expanded authority covers all infrastructure types
- Favorable lending terms encourage investment
- Project criteria prioritize maintenance and resilience

**Adaptations**:

- States may limit eligible sectors based on existing programs
- Bond authority may require constitutional amendment in some states
- Interest rate flexibility depends on state credit rating

### State Vehicle Miles Traveled Fee Act

**Purpose**: Establish state-level VMT fee program compatible with federal standards.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Road Usage Charge Act".

SECTION 2. ROAD USAGE CHARGE PROGRAM.

(a) The Department of Transportation shall establish a voluntary
road usage charge program.

(b) Participants may pay road usage charges in lieu of state
motor fuel taxes.

SECTION 3. RATE STRUCTURE.

(a) Base rate shall be [X] cents per mile.
(b) Electric and high-efficiency vehicles shall pay the base rate.
(c) Heavy vehicles may be assessed higher rates based on weight.

SECTION 4. REPORTING OPTIONS.

Participants may choose from—
  (1) automatic reporting via on-board device;
  (2) manual reporting via periodic odometer reading;
  (3) smartphone application;
  (4) flat annual fee based on estimated mileage.

SECTION 5. PRIVACY PROTECTIONS.

(a) No location data shall be collected or stored.
(b) Mileage data shall be deleted within 30 days of processing.
(c) Data shall not be shared with law enforcement without warrant.

SECTION 6. INTEROPERABILITY.

The program shall be designed for interoperability with federal
and other state programs.
```

**Explanation**:

- Voluntary program eases transition
- Multiple reporting options address privacy concerns
- Heavy vehicle rates capture road damage costs

**Adaptations**:

- States without gas tax may implement directly
- Cross-state travel requires interstate agreements
- Revenue distribution formula varies by state

## Regulatory Framework

### DOT Fix-It-First Rule

**Existing Authority**: 23 U.S.C. § 150 (National Goals and Performance Management)

**Draft Regulation**:

```text
23 CFR Part 490 - NATIONAL PERFORMANCE MANAGEMENT MEASURES

§ 490.XXX Fix-It-First Requirements.

(a) Definitions.
  (1) "Fix-it-first" means prioritizing maintenance, preservation,
  and rehabilitation of existing infrastructure over capacity
  expansion.
  (2) "State of good repair" means asset condition meeting
  minimum performance thresholds.

(b) State Certification.
  (1) Each State shall annually certify that Federal-aid
  expenditures prioritize state of good repair.
  (2) Certification shall include asset condition data and
  expenditure breakdown by project type.

(c) Performance Thresholds.
  (1) Bridges: No more than 10% of NHS bridge deck area in
  poor condition.
  (2) Pavement: No more than 5% of Interstate in poor condition;
  no more than 10% of non-Interstate NHS in poor condition.

(d) Consequence of Non-Compliance.
  A State failing to meet thresholds for two consecutive years
  shall dedicate an additional 10% of National Highway Performance
  Program funds to state of good repair projects.
```

**Explanation**: Implements fix-it-first through performance management system already established in law.

### EPA Lead Service Line Replacement Rule

**Existing Authority**: Safe Drinking Water Act § 1417 (Lead contamination)

**Draft Regulation**:

```text
40 CFR Part 141 - NATIONAL PRIMARY DRINKING WATER REGULATIONS

§ 141.XXX Lead Service Line Replacement.

(a) Inventory Requirement.
  (1) All community water systems shall complete a service line
  material inventory by [date].
  (2) Inventories shall be submitted to the State and made
  publicly available.

(b) Replacement Deadline.
  All lead service lines shall be replaced by [date].

(c) Replacement Rate.
  Systems shall replace at least 10% of lead service lines
  annually until complete.

(d) Full Replacement.
  Partial replacement is prohibited. Both utility-owned and
  customer-owned portions shall be replaced simultaneously.

(e) Customer Notification.
  Customers with lead service lines shall receive written
  notification within 30 days of inventory completion.
```

**Explanation**: Accelerates lead elimination beyond current Lead and Copper Rule requirements.

### FCC Broadband Deployment Rule

**Existing Authority**: Communications Act § 254 (Universal Service)

**Draft Regulation**:

```text
47 CFR Part 54 - UNIVERSAL SERVICE

§ 54.XXX Broadband Deployment Accountability.

(a) Speed Standards.
  Broadband eligible for Universal Service Fund support shall
  provide minimum speeds of 100 Mbps download/20 Mbps upload.

(b) Deployment Timeline.
  Recipients shall deploy to 40% of required locations within
  2 years, 80% within 3 years, and 100% within 4 years.

(c) Verification.
  (1) The Commission shall conduct random speed testing of
  subsidized networks.
  (2) Providers failing to meet 80% of advertised speeds shall
  be subject to enforcement action.

(d) Clawback.
  Providers failing to meet deployment obligations shall return
  all USF funds received for unserved locations plus interest.
```

**Explanation**: Strengthens accountability for broadband deployment subsidies.

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

| Issue | Analysis |
|-------|----------|
| **Commerce Clause** | Federal VMT authority likely upheld under commerce power for interstate travel |
| **Spending Power** | Conditions on highway funds routinely upheld (*South Dakota v. Dole*) |
| **Takings** | Infrastructure requirements generally not takings if applied prospectively |
| **Preemption** | Federal broadband preemption of state municipal restrictions may face challenges |

### Preemption Questions

| Area | Federal-State Balance |
|------|----------------------|
| **VMT fees** | Federal floor with state supplementation |
| **Water standards** | Federal minimums, state enforcement |
| **Broadband** | Federal preemption of anti-municipal laws contested |
| **Bridge standards** | Federal standards for federal-aid system |

### Enforcement Mechanisms

| Mechanism | Application |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Fund withholding** | Primary enforcement for transportation |
| **Permit conditions** | Water system compliance |
| **Civil penalties** | Broadband provider violations |
| **Clawback** | Return of misspent funds |

### Sunset and Review Provisions

| Provision | Schedule |
|-----------|----------|
| **Reauthorization** | 5-year surface transportation cycle |
| **GAO review** | 3-year evaluation of new programs |
| **Regulatory review** | Biennial update of performance standards |
| **Technology standards** | Annual broadband speed assessment |

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| **Capacity expansion redefinition** | States may classify expansions as "maintenance" | High |
| **VMT opt-out** | Voluntary VMT allows continued gas tax erosion | Medium |
| **Small system exemptions** | Small water systems escape requirements | Medium |
| **Broadband speed gaming** | "Up to" speeds evade minimums | High |
| **Climate projection selection** | Cherry-picking favorable projections | Medium |

### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| **Funding adequacy** | Programs underfunded relative to need | Political constraints |
| **Local capacity** | Small governments can't access programs | Complexity |
| **Private sector resistance** | Industry opposition weakens implementation | Lobbying |
| **Intergovernmental coordination** | Fragmented governance persists | Structural |

### Rectification Procedures

1. **Project classification**: DOT shall publish binding guidance defining maintenance vs. expansion with specific project type categories
2. **Mandatory VMT transition**: Phase in VMT as mandatory after pilot period with federal backstop
3. **Small system support**: Dedicated technical assistance and simplified application processes
4. **Speed testing**: Third-party verification with consumer complaint triggers
5. **Climate standards**: Require use of IPCC scenarios without modification

### General Implementation Concerns

- State administrative capacity varies significantly
- Private sector compliance monitoring requires resources
- Political changes may undermine implementation
- Technology evolution may outpace regulatory updates
