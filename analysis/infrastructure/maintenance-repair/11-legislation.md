# Infrastructure Maintenance and Repair: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Addressing the infrastructure maintenance backlog requires legislative reforms at federal and state levels. Key priorities include "fix-it-first" policies, dedicated maintenance funding, asset management requirements, and accountability mechanisms. This document provides draft legislation for essential reforms.

## Federal Legislation

### Fix-It-First Act

**Purpose**: Establish federal priority for infrastructure maintenance over new capacity expansion.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Fix-It-First Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that—
    (1) America faces a $2 trillion infrastructure
    maintenance backlog;
    (2) deferred maintenance costs 5-25 times
    more than timely repair;
    (3) political incentives favor new construction
    over preservation;
    (4) current federal programs inadequately
    prioritize maintenance;
    (5) reform is needed to ensure stewardship.

SEC. 3. FIX-IT-FIRST POLICY.

(a) POLICY.—It shall be the policy of the
United States to prioritize infrastructure
maintenance and preservation over capacity
expansion.

(b) FEDERAL PROGRAMS.—Federal infrastructure
programs shall—
    (1) require maintenance of existing assets
    before funding new capacity;
    (2) establish condition thresholds below which
    new capacity funding is restricted;
    (3) reward jurisdictions meeting state of
    good repair standards.

SEC. 4. HIGHWAY PROGRAM REQUIREMENTS.

(a) CONDITION THRESHOLDS.—A State may not
obligate funds for new highway capacity until—
    (1) less than 10 percent of Interstate
    pavement is in poor condition;
    (2) less than 5 percent of NHS bridges are
    structurally deficient.

(b) MAINTENANCE SET-ASIDE.—Not less than
25 percent of a State's apportionment shall be
used for system preservation activities.

(c) EXEMPTIONS.—The Secretary may grant
exemptions for safety projects or projects
with demonstrated economic necessity.

SEC. 5. TRANSIT REQUIREMENTS.

(a) STATE OF GOOD REPAIR.—A transit agency
may not receive capital funding for system
expansion until achieving state of good repair
targets established under section 5326 of
title 49.

(b) TRANSITION.—Agencies shall have 5 years
to achieve compliance from enactment.

SEC. 6. WATER INFRASTRUCTURE.

(a) MAINTENANCE ELIGIBILITY.—The State
Revolving Fund programs under the Safe
Drinking Water Act and Clean Water Act
are amended to allow funding for system
maintenance and operations.

(b) ASSET MANAGEMENT.—Systems receiving
SRF assistance shall implement asset
management programs.

SEC. 7. FEDERAL FACILITIES.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—Federal agencies shall
eliminate deferred maintenance backlogs
within 10 years.

(b) REPORTING.—Agencies shall report
annually on backlog status and reduction
progress.

SEC. 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect upon enactment.
```

### Infrastructure Asset Management Act

**Purpose**: Strengthen asset management requirements for federally-funded infrastructure.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Infrastructure
Asset Management Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that—
    (1) asset management enables cost-effective
    infrastructure stewardship;
    (2) many jurisdictions lack adequate asset
    data and planning;
    (3) federal requirements are inconsistent
    across programs;
    (4) improved asset management will reduce
    costs and improve conditions.

SEC. 3. TRANSPORTATION ASSET MANAGEMENT.

(a) ENHANCED REQUIREMENTS.—23 U.S.C.
§ 119(e) is amended to require—
    (1) complete asset inventories including
    all state and local roads;
    (2) condition assessment using standardized
    national methods;
    (3) lifecycle cost analysis for all
    investment decisions;
    (4) risk-based prioritization of investments.

(b) LOCAL GOVERNMENT.—States shall require
local governments receiving federal-aid to
implement asset management programs.

SEC. 4. WATER INFRASTRUCTURE.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—The Safe Drinking Water
Act is amended to require asset management
programs for all community water systems.

(b) ELEMENTS.—Asset management programs
shall include—
    (1) asset inventory with age, material,
    condition;
    (2) lifecycle cost analysis;
    (3) capital improvement planning;
    (4) rate setting for full cost recovery.

(c) TECHNICAL ASSISTANCE.—EPA shall provide
technical assistance to small systems.

SEC. 5. FEDERAL FACILITIES.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—All federal agencies shall
implement asset management programs for
real property.

(b) STANDARDS.—OMB shall issue standards
for federal facility asset management.

(c) REPORTING.—Agencies shall report asset
conditions to Congress annually.

SEC. 6. DATA STANDARDS.

(a) NATIONAL STANDARDS.—Federal agencies
shall develop national standards for—
    (1) asset identification and classification;
    (2) condition assessment methods;
    (3) data collection and reporting;
    (4) performance measurement.

(b) INTEROPERABILITY.—Standards shall ensure
data interoperability across programs.

SEC. 7. CAPACITY BUILDING.

(a) TECHNICAL ASSISTANCE.—$100,000,000
annually is authorized for technical assistance
to state and local governments.

(b) TRAINING.—Federal agencies shall support
asset management training programs.

SEC. 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect upon enactment.
```

### Infrastructure Maintenance Funding Act

**Purpose**: Create dedicated funding streams for infrastructure maintenance.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Infrastructure
Maintenance Funding Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that—
    (1) maintenance funding is inadequate at
    all levels of government;
    (2) federal programs are biased toward
    capital investment;
    (3) dedicated maintenance funding would
    improve outcomes;
    (4) user fees should support maintenance.

SEC. 3. HIGHWAY MAINTENANCE PROGRAM.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—A Highway Maintenance
Program is established within the Federal-aid
highway program.

(b) AUTHORIZATION.—$10,000,000,000 annually
is authorized for highway maintenance activities.

(c) ELIGIBLE ACTIVITIES.—Funds may be used
for—
    (1) pavement preservation and repair;
    (2) bridge maintenance and rehabilitation;
    (3) drainage system maintenance;
    (4) traffic control device maintenance;
    (5) roadside maintenance.

(d) FEDERAL SHARE.—The federal share shall
be 80 percent.

SEC. 4. TRANSIT MAINTENANCE PROGRAM.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—A Transit Maintenance
Program is established.

(b) AUTHORIZATION.—$3,000,000,000 annually
is authorized.

(c) ELIGIBLE ACTIVITIES.—Funds may be used
for—
    (1) vehicle maintenance and overhaul;
    (2) facility maintenance;
    (3) track and guideway maintenance;
    (4) systems maintenance.

SEC. 5. WATER MAINTENANCE PROGRAM.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—A Water System Maintenance
Program is established within EPA.

(b) AUTHORIZATION.—$2,000,000,000 annually
is authorized.

(c) ELIGIBLE ACTIVITIES.—Funds may be used
for—
    (1) pipe repair and rehabilitation;
    (2) treatment facility maintenance;
    (3) pump and equipment maintenance;
    (4) storage facility maintenance.

SEC. 6. USER FEE INDEXING.

(a) HIGHWAY TRUST FUND.—The motor fuel
tax rates under section 4081 of the Internal
Revenue Code are amended to be indexed
to inflation beginning October 1 of the
fiscal year following enactment.

(b) AVIATION TRUST FUND.—Aviation excise
tax rates shall be similarly indexed.

SEC. 7. REPORTING.

Recipients shall report annually on—
    (1) maintenance activities funded;
    (2) condition changes;
    (3) cost efficiency.

SEC. 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect upon enactment.
```

## State Model Legislation

### Model State Fix-It-First Act

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State]
Fix-It-First Act".

SECTION 2. POLICY.

It shall be the policy of [State] to
prioritize maintenance and preservation
of existing infrastructure over construction
of new capacity.

SECTION 3. HIGHWAY REQUIREMENTS.

(a) MAINTENANCE PRIORITY.—The Department
of Transportation shall prioritize system
preservation in capital programming.

(b) CONDITION THRESHOLDS.—New capacity
projects shall not be programmed until—
    (1) state highway pavement is 80 percent
    or better in good or fair condition;
    (2) state bridges are 95 percent or better
    in good or fair condition.

(c) MAINTENANCE BUDGET.—Not less than
40 percent of transportation capital funds
shall be used for system preservation.

SECTION 4. WATER SYSTEMS.

(a) ASSET MANAGEMENT.—All public water
systems shall implement asset management
programs within 3 years.

(b) RATE ADEQUACY.—Rates shall be set
to fund necessary maintenance and
capital replacement.

SECTION 5. STATE FACILITIES.

(a) DEFERRED MAINTENANCE.—State agencies
shall report deferred maintenance annually.

(b) ELIMINATION.—Agencies shall develop
plans to eliminate deferred maintenance
within 10 years.

SECTION 6. LOCAL GOVERNMENTS.

(a) ASSET MANAGEMENT.—Local governments
receiving state aid shall implement asset
management programs.

(b) REPORTING.—Local governments shall
report infrastructure conditions annually.

SECTION 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect on [date].
```

### Model State Infrastructure Accountability Act

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State]
Infrastructure Accountability Act".

SECTION 2. ASSET INVENTORY.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—All state and local
governments shall maintain complete
inventories of infrastructure assets.

(b) ELEMENTS.—Inventories shall include—
    (1) asset identification;
    (2) location;
    (3) age and condition;
    (4) replacement cost.

SECTION 3. CONDITION REPORTING.

(a) ANNUAL REPORT.—The [agency] shall
publish an annual infrastructure condition
report.

(b) CONTENTS.—The report shall include—
    (1) condition by category;
    (2) deferred maintenance;
    (3) investment needs;
    (4) recommendations.

SECTION 4. PERFORMANCE TARGETS.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—The [agency] shall
establish condition targets for all
infrastructure categories.

(b) REPORTING.—Progress toward targets
shall be reported annually.

(c) CONSEQUENCES.—Agencies failing to
meet targets shall submit improvement
plans.

SECTION 5. LIFECYCLE COSTING.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—All infrastructure
investments exceeding $1,000,000 shall
include lifecycle cost analysis.

(b) CONSIDERATION.—Lifecycle costs
shall be considered in project selection.

SECTION 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect on [date].
```

| Basis | Application |
|-------|-------------|
| **Commerce Clause** | Interstate infrastructure |

| **Property** | Federal facilities |
| **General Welfare** | Infrastructure investment |

| **Police power** | Infrastructure regulation |
| **Property** | State facilities |

### Federal-State Balance

| Issue | Consideration |
|-------|---------------|
| **Conditions on grants** | Within spending power |
| **State sovereignty** | Voluntary programs |
| **Regulatory authority** | Limited federal reach |
| **Preemption** | Standards, not operations |

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| **Condition gaming** | Manipulating assessment data | Medium |
| **Project classification** | Labeling expansion as preservation | High |
| **Exemption abuse** | Excessive safety exemptions | Medium |
| **Local exemption** | Federal requirements don't reach local | High |
| **Data manipulation** | Poor condition data quality | Medium |

### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| **Funding adequacy** | Below actual need | Political constraints |
| **Local government** | Limited federal reach | Constitutional limits |
| **Private infrastructure** | Not covered | Jurisdictional limits |
| **Enforcement** | Limited penalties | Political resistance |
| **Implementation capacity** | Technical limits | Resource constraints |

### Rectification Procedures

1. **Standardized condition assessment** with federal oversight
2. **Clear project classification** criteria with audit
3. **Limited exemption authority** with reporting
4. **State pass-through requirements** for local compliance
5. **Independent data verification** mechanisms
6. **Adequate funding levels** based on documented need
7. **Incentives for local participation** beyond mandates
8. **Technical assistance** for capacity building
9. **Progressive enforcement** with graduated consequences
10. **Regular program review** and adjustment

### General Implementation Concerns

| Concern | Mitigation |
|---------|------------|
| **Political sustainability** | Bipartisan design, user fee link |
| **Technical capacity** | Assistance programs |
| **Data quality** | Standards, verification |
| **Cost** | Phased implementation |
| **Resistance** | Incentive alignment |
