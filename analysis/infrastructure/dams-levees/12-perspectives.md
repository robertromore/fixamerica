# Dams and Levees: Political Perspectives Analysis

## Overview

This document analyzes dam and levee infrastructure policy reform -- specifically increased federal investment, state program strengthening, private dam assistance, strategic dam removal, levee system modernization, risk-informed management, climate adaptation, and regulatory modernization -- through nine distinct political perspectives. The core questions are: how much should the federal government invest in aging water infrastructure, how should responsibility be distributed among federal, state, local, and private dam owners, whether strategic dam removal should be a core policy tool alongside rehabilitation, and how climate change projections should be incorporated into safety standards. Each perspective receives engagement-consistency ratings and position scores, culminating in compromise proposals that bridge multiple viewpoints.

Dam and levee infrastructure is less politically polarized than many policy domains because the physical reality of aging structures, the bipartisan geographic distribution of risk (dams exist in every state and nearly every congressional district), and the shared experience of catastrophic failures create conditions for cross-ideological agreement on the need for investment. However, significant disagreements emerge around the scale of federal spending, the role of regulation versus market incentives, whether dam removal should be embraced as a primary tool, and how aggressively climate change projections should reshape safety standards. The 56% private ownership share of American dams creates a distinctive tension between property rights and public safety that cross-cuts traditional ideological lines. Unlike many infrastructure debates, dams and levees involve life-safety consequences -- dam failures kill people -- which raises the political cost of inaction and creates urgency that purely economic infrastructure debates lack.

### Engagement Consistency (1-5)

This metric assesses how reliably a perspective's stated positions align with its underlying principles, and how stable those positions are when concerns are addressed. Higher scores indicate more predictable negotiating partners.

| Level | Label | Indicators |
|-------|-------|------------|
| 5 | Highly Consistent | Stated positions reflect underlying principles; positions stable when concerns addressed |
| 4 | Mostly Consistent | Principles generally align with positions; minor strategic flexibility |
| 3 | Mixed Consistency | Some positions principled, others reflect coalition pressures or unstated concerns |
| 2 | Low Consistency | Positions shift frequently; stated concerns differ from underlying priorities |
| 1 | Unpredictable | Positions appear driven primarily by opposition dynamics rather than principles |

**Coalition-Building Considerations:**

- Alignment gap: When stated concerns differ from underlying priorities, addressing stated concerns may not secure support
- Position stability: Perspectives with shifting positions require ongoing negotiation
- Principle consistency: Perspectives that apply principles uniformly are more predictable partners
- Internal diversity: Some perspectives contain factions with different priorities

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 4/5 - Mostly Consistent**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Genuine concern about fiscal responsibility and appropriate federal role; commitment to infrastructure safety is real but tempered by reluctance to expand federal spending or regulation; stated concern about private property rights aligns with underlying priorities |
| Principle consistency | Limited government principles are applied consistently: prefer state over federal authority, private solutions over public mandates, and targeted investment over broad spending programs; fiscal restraint is genuinely applied to dam spending as to other areas |
| Goalpost stability | Stable; conservatives have consistently supported infrastructure investment at moderate levels while opposing large spending packages they view as excessive; support for Water Resources Development Acts has been bipartisan for decades |
| Zero-sum behavior | Low on this issue; infrastructure safety does not trigger the identity-based dynamics that make compromise difficult in other domains; conservatives can support dam investment without cultural concessions |

**Justification:** Conservatives approach dam and levee infrastructure with genuine consistency between principles and positions. Limited government principles produce predictable preferences: federal investment in federal dams, state responsibility for state regulatory programs, limited mandates on private owners, and skepticism toward large new spending programs. The score of 4 reflects minor flexibility on the scale of federal spending -- conservatives will support larger investments after catastrophic failures while returning to fiscal restraint rhetoric during calm periods -- but overall positions are principled and predictable.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Heritage Foundation infrastructure analysis emphasizes private ownership, state authority, and targeted federal role

### Compromise 1: Bipartisan Dam and Levee Safety Investment Act

**Bridges:** Conservative + Liberal + Progressive + Constitutionalist + Populist + Centrist + Religious Right + Democratic Socialist

**Core Agreement:** Aging federal dams and levees on navigable waters are a legitimate federal responsibility, the maintenance backlog represents a public safety emergency, and increased investment in existing infrastructure is fiscally responsible because the cost of failure vastly exceeds the cost of prevention.

**Policy Description:**

Federal legislation authorizes $750 million annually for Army Corps dam and levee rehabilitation, $250 million for Bureau of Reclamation dam safety, and $100 million for state dam safety program grants. Rehabilitation projects are prioritized through a risk-based framework that focuses investment on high-hazard dams where failure would cause the greatest loss of life. State dam safety grants are available to all states that maintain a minimum program including hazard classification, regular inspection, and emergency action plan requirements. A small dam technical assistance program provides engineering guidance and low-cost inspection services to private dam owners, funded at $25 million annually. All federally funded projects require cost-benefit analysis and performance metrics. Phased investment over 10 years ensures sustained commitment without annual appropriations uncertainty. A Dam Safety Trust Fund, seeded with hydropower revenue and supplemented by general appropriations, provides a dedicated funding mechanism.

### Compromise 2: Risk-Informed Dam Removal and River Restoration Program

**Bridges:** Liberal + Progressive + Centrist + Constitutionalist + Democratic Socialist + Libertarian

**Core Agreement:** Thousands of dams no longer serve their original purpose, imposing ongoing safety risks and maintenance costs on owners and communities; removing obsolete dams eliminates these costs and risks while restoring river ecosystems, and federal assistance for voluntary removal serves both fiscal and environmental goals.

**Policy Description:**

Federal legislation establishes a voluntary dam removal assistance program funded at $150 million annually. Eligibility is limited to dams that are (1) classified as high-hazard potential or significant hazard, (2) no longer serve a significant economic purpose (no active hydropower, water supply, or essential flood control), and (3) have owner consent for removal. Federal cost-share is 75% for removal of high-hazard dams and 65% for significant-hazard dams. Permitting is streamlined through a single lead agency (Army Corps) with an 18-month timeline and categorical exclusions for routine small dam removals. Liability protection is provided for owners who remove dams in good-faith compliance with applicable requirements. An independent engineering review panel assesses each proposed removal to ensure that removal does not create downstream risks or eliminate essential flood protection. Priority is given to removals that restore fish passage on priority rivers identified by state and federal fisheries agencies. Annual program reporting documents removals completed, costs, and ecological outcomes.

- **Coalition Size:** 6/9 perspectives (Conservative and Religious Right are ambivalent; Populist opposes)
- **High Consistency Ratio:** 5/6 coalition members rated 4+ consistency (Liberal, Centrist, Constitutionalist, Libertarian, Democratic Socialist)
- **Durability:** Moderate-high; voluntary nature reduces political opposition; engineering review provides legitimacy; documented ecological benefits build public support; dam removal has growing bipartisan momentum as hundreds of dams have been successfully removed
- **Implementation Path:** Build on existing dam removal successes (1,900+ dams removed in the US); leverage fishing and recreation industry support; frame as eliminating taxpayer liability for obsolete structures; partner with state dam safety programs that recommend removal for structures that cannot be cost-effectively rehabilitated

### Compromise 3: State Dam Safety Capacity Building Act

**Bridges:** Conservative + Liberal + Constitutionalist + Populist + Centrist + Religious Right + Democratic Socialist

**Core Agreement:** State dam safety programs are the primary regulatory framework for non-federal dams, wide variation in state capacity leaves populations in some states inadequately protected, and federal grants to build state capacity without imposing federal mandates is the most politically viable approach to strengthening dam safety nationwide.

**Policy Description:**

Federal legislation authorizes $100 million annually in state dam safety grants, distributed through a formula based on number of dams, number of high-hazard dams, and state population behind dams. States receiving grants must maintain a dam safety program that includes hazard classification, periodic inspection of high-hazard dams, and emergency action plan requirements for high-hazard dams -- but specific standards, frequencies, and methods are determined by each state. A $25 million annual technical assistance fund provides engineering guidance, inspection support, and emergency action plan templates for small and private dam owners through state programs. A National Dam Safety Training Program, funded at $10 million annually, supports professional development for state dam safety engineers and inspectors. Model legislation is developed and made available to states but is not required for grant eligibility. An annual National Dam Safety Report documents state program status, dam conditions, and incidents. Grant funding is supplemented by a competitive innovation fund ($15 million annually) for state pilot programs in risk assessment, climate adaptation, or other safety improvements that can be replicated nationally.

| Constitutionalist | State programs are the constitutional default for non-federal dams; grants voluntary without federal mandates; model legislation respects state sovereignty; Spending Clause authority is clear |
| Populist | Helps rural states with limited dam safety budgets; technical assistance reaches small community dam owners; training strengthens state workforce; formula distribution is equitable |
| Centrist | Evidence-based approach to building capacity; performance metrics through annual reporting; innovation fund generates replicable best practices; practical and achievable |

- **Implementation Path:** Expand existing FEMA state dam safety program grants; model on successful state revolving fund programs; build support through Association of State Dam Safety Officials and National Governors Association; frame as empowering states rather than expanding federal authority

## Strategic Implications

### Most Viable Coalition

Dam and levee infrastructure is one of the most favorable policy domains for bipartisan action. The broadest coalition (8/9 perspectives) supports federal investment in existing federal infrastructure because maintaining government-owned assets is accepted as legitimate even by limited-government perspectives. The state capacity building coalition (7/9) is nearly as broad because federal grants to state programs respect federalism while addressing real capacity gaps. The strategic approach should sequence reforms: (1) lead with federal dam and levee investment through the bipartisan Water Resources Development Act process, (2) simultaneously advance state capacity building through expanded FEMA grants, (3) build the evidence base for dam removal through successful voluntary removals, and (4) integrate climate adaptation incrementally through updated engineering standards rather than legislative mandates. This sequencing builds coalition support from the broadest agreement outward.

### Key Obstacles

- **Fiscal competition:** Dam and levee investment competes with other infrastructure needs (highways, bridges, broadband) for limited federal funding; the relative invisibility of dams and levees compared to transportation infrastructure makes sustained political attention difficult
- **Dam removal resistance:** Populist opposition to dam removal frames it as an attack on rural communities, even when removal serves community interests; this resistance is amplified when environmental groups are prominent advocates, triggering anti-environmentalist reflexes
- **Climate adaptation resistance:** Conservative, Populist, and Religious Right skepticism toward climate science creates a 3/9 perspective block against climate-mandated infrastructure standards; this resistance means climate adaptation must be advanced through engineering standards updates rather than legislative mandates
- **Private owner challenge:** The 56% private ownership share of American dams creates a fundamental tension between public safety and private property rights that cross-cuts ideological lines and resists clean solutions

### Low Consistency Partners

Populist (3/5) and Religious Right (3/5) perspectives rate at mixed consistency on dam and levee policy, though for different reasons. Populist support is most accessible when proposals are framed as protecting rural communities and creating jobs, and least accessible when dam removal or climate adaptation are prominent. The most effective strategy is to frame dam removal as "eliminating dangerous structures that threaten your community" rather than "restoring river ecosystems." Religious Right support is most accessible through stewardship and community protection framing and least accessible when proposals are associated with environmental or climate advocacy. Both perspectives track their broader conservative coalition, so building conservative support is the most efficient pathway to engaging Populist and Religious Right perspectives.

### Recommended Approach

1. **Lead with bipartisan infrastructure investment:** The Bipartisan Dam and Levee Safety Investment Act (Compromise 1) commands 8/9 perspective support and addresses the most urgent need -- the federal maintenance backlog and state program capacity. Frame as maintaining existing assets and protecting communities, not expanding government.

2. **Build state capacity simultaneously:** The State Dam Safety Capacity Building Act (Compromise 3) commands 7/9 support and addresses the fundamental problem of uneven state regulation without triggering federalism objections. Formula-based grants and voluntary model legislation respect state authority while building nationwide capacity.

3. **Advance dam removal through evidence:** The Risk-Informed Dam Removal and River Restoration Program (Compromise 2) commands 6/9 support and addresses the estimated 18,000+ obsolete dams in the United States. Voluntary participation, engineering review, and owner consent address the strongest objections. Successful removals build the evidence base for program expansion.

4. **Integrate climate adaptation through engineering standards:** Rather than legislative climate mandates that trigger opposition from three perspectives, advance climate adaptation through updated Probable Maximum Precipitation studies, engineering design standard revisions, and risk assessment frameworks that incorporate changing hydrology. Technical standards updates are less politically visible and more likely to achieve broad implementation.

5. **Use catastrophic events strategically:** Dam and levee failures create temporary political windows for reform. Prepared legislation that can be advanced during these windows converts crisis attention into durable policy. The Oroville spillway failure (2017) and Edenville Dam failure (2020) both generated bipartisan support for investment.

6. **Frame for the broadest coalition:** Infrastructure safety investment is most effective when framed as fiscal responsibility (the cost of failure exceeds the cost of prevention), public safety (dam failures kill people), and asset management (maintaining what we already own). These frames access conservative, centrist, and populist support that environmental or climate framing does not.
