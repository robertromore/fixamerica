# Clean Energy: Political Perspectives Analysis

## Overview

This document analyzes clean energy transition policy -- specifically renewable deployment, nuclear energy, grid modernization, transmission expansion, domestic manufacturing, workforce development, and equitable access -- through nine distinct political perspectives. The core questions are: how rapidly should the United States transition away from fossil fuels, what role should government play in directing energy markets, how should the costs and benefits of the transition be distributed, and what is the proper balance between federal authority and state control over energy policy? Each perspective receives engagement-consistency ratings and position scores, culminating in compromise proposals that bridge multiple viewpoints.

Clean energy is a distinctive policy domain because it sits at the intersection of climate science, economic competitiveness, energy security, and cultural identity. While renewable energy costs have fallen dramatically and the Inflation Reduction Act has catalyzed over $300 billion in annual private investment, the policy debate remains intensely polarized. The same data points -- 3.3 million clean energy jobs, falling solar and wind costs, growing battery storage -- are read as vindication of government investment by some and as evidence that the market no longer needs subsidies by others. The entanglement of energy policy with climate change politics, fossil fuel community identity, and geopolitical competition with China makes coalition-building both essential and exceptionally difficult. Yet areas of genuine overlap exist: energy security, domestic manufacturing, permitting reform, and grid reliability transcend ideological boundaries when detached from the climate framing.

## Scoring Framework

### Position Scores (1-10)

| Score | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| 10 | Full agreement with project analysis |
| 7-9 | Substantial to strong agreement |
| 5-6 | Mixed/moderate agreement |
| 3-4 | Substantial disagreement |
| 1-2 | Strong opposition |

### Engagement Consistency (1-5)

This metric assesses how reliably a perspective's stated positions align with its underlying principles, and how stable those positions are when concerns are addressed. Higher scores indicate more predictable negotiating partners.

| Level | Label | Indicators |
|-------|-------|------------|
| 5 | Highly Consistent | Stated positions reflect underlying principles; positions stable when concerns addressed |
| 4 | Mostly Consistent | Principles generally align with positions; minor strategic flexibility |
| 3 | Mixed Consistency | Some positions principled, others reflect coalition pressures or unstated concerns |
| 2 | Low Consistency | Positions shift frequently; stated concerns differ from underlying priorities |
| 1 | Unpredictable | Positions appear driven primarily by opposition dynamics rather than principles |

**Coalition-Building Considerations:**

- Alignment gap: When stated concerns differ from underlying priorities, addressing stated concerns may not secure support
- Position stability: Perspectives with shifting positions require ongoing negotiation
- Principle consistency: Perspectives that apply principles uniformly are more predictable partners
- Internal diversity: Some perspectives contain factions with different priorities

## Perspective Analyses

### Conservative Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 2/5 - Low Consistency**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Stated concerns about energy reliability, consumer costs, and market distortion are genuine but are frequently overridden by fossil fuel industry alignment, cultural identity politics around oil and gas communities, and reflexive opposition to climate-motivated policy |
| Principle consistency | Free market principles are applied inconsistently; conservatives oppose clean energy subsidies while defending fossil fuel tax advantages (percentage depletion, intangible drilling costs) that have existed for over a century; "all of the above" rhetoric coexists with active opposition to renewable deployment |
| Goalpost stability | Low; positions have shifted from "renewables don't work" to "renewables are too expensive" to "renewables threaten reliability" to "renewables depend on China" as each prior objection was overcome; the underlying opposition persists across shifting rationales |
| Zero-sum behavior | Significant; clean energy is frequently treated as a proxy battle in the culture war rather than evaluated on energy policy merits; support for IRA repeal persists even as conservative districts benefit disproportionately from clean energy manufacturing investment |

**Justification:** Conservatives face a deep tension on clean energy. Market principles should celebrate the cost decline of renewables and the investment boom the IRA has catalyzed, yet the political alignment with fossil fuel interests and the cultural coding of energy policy as a climate-war front line override principled engagement. The score of 2 reflects the degree to which conservative positions on clean energy are driven by fossil fuel industry relationships and anti-climate-policy identity rather than consistent application of free market or fiscal principles. When energy security and manufacturing competitiveness are separated from the climate frame, conservative engagement improves markedly -- as demonstrated by Republican members quietly protecting IRA provisions that benefit their districts while publicly calling for repeal.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- 18 House Republicans wrote to Speaker Johnson in 2023 opposing full IRA repeal because clean energy manufacturing investments in their districts were creating jobs
- Heritage Foundation and American Energy Alliance consistently frame all clean energy incentives as market-distorting while defending fossil fuel tax provisions as standard business deductions
- Conservative states (Texas, Iowa, Oklahoma) lead in wind energy deployment, creating tension between state-level support and national party messaging
- Republican governors in red states have promoted clean energy manufacturing investment while their congressional delegations vote against the enabling legislation
- The "energy dominance" framing embraces natural gas exports but resists acknowledging that wind and solar are now cheaper than new gas generation in most markets

#### Alternative Proposals

- Technology-neutral permitting reform applying equally to all energy sources
- Nuclear energy expansion with streamlined NRC licensing
- Natural gas as a bridge fuel with support for LNG exports
- Repeal of clean energy tax credits with revenue-neutral tax reform
- Tariffs on Chinese clean energy products rather than domestic subsidies
- Voluntary emissions reduction through innovation, not mandates
- State-level energy policy without federal standards or targets
- Liability reform for advanced nuclear and carbon capture

#### Coalition Potential

- **Natural allies:** Libertarian (on opposing subsidies and federal mandates), Constitutionalist (on opposing federal energy authority), Religious Right (on cultural alignment)
- **Potential bridges:** Populist (on domestic manufacturing and China competition), Centrist (on permitting reform and nuclear energy)
- **Unlikely partners:** Progressive, Democratic Socialist (on virtually all dimensions), Liberal (on scale of government intervention)

### Liberal Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 4/5 - Mostly Consistent**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Genuine commitment to climate action, clean energy deployment, and equitable transition; consistent advocacy for government investment in clean energy infrastructure |
| Principle consistency | Equality and environmental protection principles consistently applied; support for both aggressive deployment targets and environmental review processes creates internal tension but reflects genuine multi-value engagement |
| Goalpost stability | Stable; has consistently supported renewable energy investment, emissions reduction, and environmental justice for decades; positions have evolved on nuclear but within a consistent framework of low-carbon energy support |
| Zero-sum behavior | Low; willing to accept permitting reform that some environmental allies oppose; willing to include nuclear alongside renewables; seeks workable coalitions across ideological lines |

**Justification:** Liberals maintain a mostly consistent position on clean energy. Their advocacy for aggressive deployment, government investment, transmission expansion, and equity aligns coherently with their broader principles of environmental responsibility and social equity. The slight reduction from a perfect score reflects tension between urgency on deployment and commitment to environmental review processes that can slow projects, and between clean energy environmental groups (who sometimes oppose specific projects) and the broader climate coalition. Some liberal positions on natural gas have also shifted under progressive pressure, creating minor inconsistency between stated "all of the above clean energy" positions and increasing resistance to gas as a bridge fuel.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Biden administration successfully passed IRA and pursued aggressive clean energy deployment while navigating NEPA review requirements
- Liberal think tanks (Center for American Progress, World Resources Institute) consistently support comprehensive clean energy investment
- Democratic state governors have implemented clean electricity standards and incentive programs
- Liberal support for offshore wind has persisted despite cost increases and project delays
- Willingness to support permitting reform (Manchin-Schumer deal elements) despite environmental community concerns

| Zero-sum behavior | Minimal; progressives view clean energy as an opportunity to simultaneously address climate, jobs, and inequality rather than requiring trade-offs between them |

**Justification:** Progressives are the most consistent perspective on clean energy because their positions flow directly from core principles of environmental justice, worker empowerment, and systemic transformation. There is no gap between stated concerns and underlying priorities. The consistency score of 5 reflects that progressive positions are entirely predictable from their principles -- if a clean energy proposal includes strong labor standards, community benefits, environmental justice provisions, and aggressive timelines, progressives will support it; if it prioritizes speed over equity or corporate profits over worker welfare, they will oppose it regardless of the climate benefit.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Green New Deal resolution (2019) established a comprehensive framework linking climate, jobs, and justice that has remained the progressive organizing blueprint
- Congressional Progressive Caucus has consistently pushed for stronger climate provisions in every legislative vehicle
- Progressive organizations (Sunrise Movement, Justice Democrats) have maintained consistent positions on climate investment, environmental justice, and labor standards
- Progressive members opposed natural gas provisions in infrastructure legislation even when it complicated broader coalition-building
- Consistent support for community solar, public power, and cooperative ownership models

### Libertarian Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 4/5 - Mostly Consistent**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Stated commitment to free markets and minimal government intervention in energy is genuine and broadly consistent; libertarians genuinely believe market competition produces better outcomes than government direction |
| Principle consistency | Free market principles are applied more consistently than by conservatives; libertarians oppose both fossil fuel and clean energy subsidies, which is intellectually coherent even if politically marginal; however, emphasis is disproportionately placed on opposing clean energy subsidies |
| Goalpost stability | Relatively stable; libertarian positions on energy have been consistent -- remove all subsidies, eliminate market-distorting regulations, let consumers choose; the consistency of the framework is a strength even when the policy implications are unpopular |
| Zero-sum behavior | Moderate; libertarians sometimes align with fossil fuel interests against clean energy not because of principled opposition to renewables but because the current political dynamic makes opposing clean energy subsidies more salient than opposing fossil fuel subsidies |

**Justification:** Libertarians are among the more principled perspectives on energy policy because their framework -- remove all subsidies and regulations, let the market decide -- is at least internally consistent. The score of 4 rather than 5 reflects the practical asymmetry in emphasis: libertarian organizations devote far more energy to opposing clean energy subsidies than fossil fuel tax advantages, suggesting that while the principle is consistent, its application is influenced by political alignment and funding sources. A perfectly consistent libertarian energy policy would advocate equally forcefully for eliminating the percentage depletion allowance and intangible drilling cost deduction as for repealing IRA clean energy credits.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Cato Institute has published analyses opposing both fossil fuel and clean energy subsidies, though the volume and emphasis skew toward opposing clean energy
- Reason Foundation has advocated for removing all energy subsidies and letting market competition determine the energy mix
- Libertarian Party platform consistently opposes government involvement in energy markets

### Constitutionalist Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 4/5 - Mostly Consistent**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Stated commitment to enumerated powers and federalism is genuine; constitutionalist concerns about federal energy authority reflect principled engagement with structural questions about government power |
| Principle consistency | Federalism principles are applied relatively consistently to energy policy; constitutionalists object to federal mandates, federal siting authority, and federal industrial policy on structural grounds, not just because they dislike clean energy; however, less vocal when federal power supports fossil fuels (Strategic Petroleum Reserve, federal oil and gas leasing) |
| Goalpost stability | Stable; constitutionalist positions on federal energy authority have been consistent -- energy regulation is not an enumerated power and should be a state function; this position predates the current clean energy debate |
| Zero-sum behavior | Low; constitutionalists engage on structural grounds and can be satisfied by structural reforms (state-level action, voluntary federal programs) regardless of the substantive policy direction |

**Justification:** Constitutionalists are relatively principled partners on clean energy because their objections are primarily structural rather than substantive. They do not inherently oppose clean energy -- they oppose the federal government directing energy markets. This makes them potentially constructive partners for state-level clean energy policy and for designing federal programs that respect federalism. The slight reduction from 5 reflects the asymmetry in applying constitutional scrutiny: constitutionalists have been less vocal about federal fossil fuel programs (oil and gas leasing on federal lands, the Strategic Petroleum Reserve, defense fuel procurement) than federal clean energy programs, suggesting some alignment with conservative coalition partners beyond pure constitutional principle.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Federalist Society analyses of federal energy authority have consistently questioned the constitutional basis for EPA climate regulation, FERC transmission siting, and federal clean energy mandates
- Constitutionalist legal challenges to EPA power plant regulations (West Virginia v. EPA) reflect genuine structural concerns about administrative overreach
- State-level constitutionalists have supported state clean energy programs that achieve similar goals through state authority
- Major questions doctrine arguments have been applied to federal energy regulation with intellectual consistency

| Equity Programs | Liberal, Progressive, Centrist, Democratic Socialist | Conservative, Religious Right | Libertarian, Populist, Constitutionalist |
| Carbon Pricing | Liberal, Libertarian, Centrist | Conservative, Progressive, Constitutionalist, Religious Right, Democratic Socialist | Populist |

**Key insight:** Clean energy policy generates a distinctive pattern where the broadest coalition forms around *removing barriers* (permitting reform, interconnection reform) rather than *adding government programs* (equity, carbon pricing). Permitting reform has the broadest support of any solution (8 of 9 perspectives at 5+) because it can be framed as deregulation by the right and deployment acceleration by the left. Domestic manufacturing commands strong support (7 perspectives at 5+) because China competition concerns bridge ideological lines. Transmission expansion and equity programs are the most divisive -- transmission because it requires federal authority that right-of-center perspectives oppose, and equity because it involves government-directed benefit allocation. Carbon pricing reveals a surprising pattern: opposition from both ends of the spectrum (populist, progressive, democratic socialist) for different reasons, with support concentrated in the center. The strategic implication is that clean energy reform should lead with permitting and interconnection reform to build broad coalitions, pair manufacturing with workforce investment for center-out support, and pursue transmission and equity through narrower but committed coalitions.

## Compromise Proposals

### Compromise 1: Energy Infrastructure Permitting and Interconnection Reform Act

**Bridges:** Conservative + Liberal + Progressive + Libertarian + Constitutionalist + Populist + Centrist + Religious Right

**Core Agreement:** Permitting and interconnection delays are unacceptable barriers to all energy development, and streamlining these processes accelerates investment, creates jobs, and reduces costs without requiring new spending or resolving climate policy disagreements.

**Policy Description:**

Federal legislation establishes maximum permitting timelines of 2 years for environmental impact statements and 1 year for environmental assessments for all energy projects (fossil fuel, renewable, nuclear, transmission, and storage). A lead agency is designated for each project to coordinate all federal authorizations under a single timeline. Programmatic environmental reviews and categorical exclusions are established for projects on previously disturbed land, within existing rights-of-way, and on designated brownfield sites. FERC interconnection reform requires cluster-based processing of generation queue applications, financial readiness requirements to eliminate speculative projects, maximum 1-year study timelines, and technology-neutral priority based on project readiness. Grid-enhancing technology deployment (dynamic line ratings, advanced power flow controllers, topology optimization) is mandated on all transmission lines within 5 years. Judicial review of energy project permits is expedited to 180 days with a requirement that courts consider project benefits alongside environmental concerns in fashioning remedies. The legislation explicitly preserves state siting authority for generation and distribution facilities, limiting the federal role to streamlining federal permits and interstate transmission corridors.

**Concern Mapping:**

| Perspective | How This Addresses Their Concerns |
|-------------|-----------------------------------|
| Conservative | Technology-neutral permitting reform benefits fossil fuel and nuclear projects alongside renewables; deregulatory framing; no new spending; preserves state authority |
| Liberal | Accelerates clean energy deployment by removing the most cited barrier; maintains environmental review (streamlined, not eliminated); FERC interconnection reform clears the 2,000+ GW queue |
| Progressive | Faster deployment of clean energy; programmatic review preserves environmental protections; grid-enhancing technology maximizes existing infrastructure before new construction |
| Libertarian | Deregulatory across all energy sources; reduces government barriers to market-driven investment; no new spending or subsidies |
| Constitutionalist | Reforms federal processes within existing jurisdiction; preserves state siting authority; does not create new federal power |
| Populist | Cuts bureaucratic red tape; benefits all energy including oil and gas; reduces project costs that are passed to consumers; creates construction jobs |
| Centrist | Evidence-based reform addressing the most widely cited deployment barrier; bipartisan by design; no new spending; technology-neutral |
| Religious Right | Regulatory streamlining is consistent with limited government principles; benefits all energy; no climate-specific framing |

**Concessions:**

| Perspective | What They Give Up |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Conservative | Accept that reform will primarily accelerate clean energy (since renewables dominate the queue); accept expedited rather than eliminated environmental review |
| Liberal | Accept technology-neutral reform that also streamlines fossil fuel projects; accept judicial review limitations that reduce litigation as a tool |
| Progressive | Accept reforms benefiting fossil fuel projects alongside clean energy; accept expedited judicial review that limits community legal challenges |
| Libertarian | Accept that reform streamlines rather than eliminates government permitting; accept continued federal role in energy project review |
| Constitutionalist | Accept FERC interconnection reform as exercise of existing interstate commerce jurisdiction |
| Populist | Accept that reform will disproportionately benefit clean energy given queue composition |
| Centrist | Accept that technology neutrality means some fossil fuel projects also benefit |
| Religious Right | Accept the legislative package without culture war framing |

**Viability Assessment:**

- **Coalition Size:** 8/9 perspectives (Democratic Socialist support is conditional on community protections; score of 5 on permitting reform reflects ambivalence, not opposition)
- **High Consistency Ratio:** 5/8 coalition members rated 4+ consistency (Liberal, Libertarian, Constitutionalist, Centrist, plus conditionally Progressive)
- **Durability:** Very high; permitting reform is the rare energy policy that genuinely benefits all sides; technology-neutral framing removes the partisan coding that makes most energy policy fragile; once timelines and processes are established, they become the new baseline that future coalitions of any composition defend
- **Implementation Path:** Bipartisan legislation with energy committee leads from both parties; builds on Manchin-Barrasso permitting reform discussions; FERC interconnection reform can proceed administratively through Commission orders if legislation stalls; grid-enhancing technology mandate has support from grid operators and utilities

### Compromise 2: American Energy Manufacturing and Workforce Investment Act

**Bridges:** Conservative + Liberal + Progressive + Populist + Centrist + Religious Right + Democratic Socialist

**Core Agreement:** Regardless of disagreements about climate policy, all perspectives can agree that the United States should manufacture its own energy equipment rather than depend on China, and that energy sector jobs should be good jobs available to American workers.

**Policy Description:**

Federal legislation extends 45X advanced manufacturing production credits through 2035 for solar modules, wind components, battery cells, inverters, transformers, and transmission equipment manufactured in the United States. Domestic content bonuses in IRA clean energy tax credits are strengthened with a phased schedule: 40% domestic content by 2027, 55% by 2030, and 70% by 2033. A $5 billion Critical Minerals Production and Processing Fund supports domestic mining (with streamlined permitting), processing, and recycling infrastructure for lithium, cobalt, nickel, rare earths, and copper, with allied nation sourcing as an alternative to Chinese supply chains. A $3 billion Clean Energy Workforce Investment Fund supports community college programs, registered apprenticeships, and career pathway development in clean energy manufacturing, installation, and maintenance, with priority funding for fossil fuel-dependent communities transitioning their economic base. Prevailing wage and apprenticeship utilization requirements apply to all projects receiving manufacturing credits, with compliance verified by the Department of Labor. A $500 million Energy Community Transition Fund provides planning grants, infrastructure investment, and economic diversification support for communities with significant fossil fuel employment. Trade enforcement provisions authorize increased tariffs on Chinese clean energy products manufactured with forced labor, below-cost government subsidies, or non-market practices.

**Concern Mapping:**

| Perspective | How This Addresses Their Concerns |
|-------------|-----------------------------------|
| Conservative | Reduces China dependence; strengthens domestic manufacturing; tariff authority addresses unfair trade; framed as economic competitiveness, not climate policy |
| Liberal | Extends critical IRA manufacturing provisions; strong workforce standards; fossil fuel community transition support; allied supply chain diversification |
| Progressive | Labor standards (prevailing wage, apprenticeship) are robust; community transition funding; trade enforcement addresses labor exploitation in Chinese supply chains |
| Populist | Made in America emphasis; anti-China trade enforcement; domestic jobs for American workers; fossil fuel community support; economic nationalism framing |
| Centrist | Evidence-based investment in manufacturing competitiveness; phased domestic content avoids supply shocks; workforce development addresses documented skills gaps |
| Religious Right | American jobs and economic self-sufficiency; anti-China framing resonates; community transition supports rural communities |
| Democratic Socialist | Strong labor provisions; community transition investment; trade enforcement against forced labor; would prefer public ownership but supports worker protections |

**Concessions:**

| Perspective | What They Give Up |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Conservative | Accept $13.5 billion in new federal spending; accept prevailing wage and apprenticeship requirements; accept extended manufacturing tax credits |
| Liberal | Accept that the legislation is framed as manufacturing competitiveness, not climate policy; accept aggressive trade provisions that may increase near-term costs |
| Progressive | Accept that manufacturing credits go to private corporations rather than public enterprises; accept phased domestic content that allows some continued imports |
| Populist | Accept government spending programs as the mechanism for achieving manufacturing goals; accept clean energy framing of manufacturing investment |
| Centrist | Accept higher tariffs that may increase equipment costs; accept spending levels that exceed their preferred fiscal footprint |
| Religious Right | Accept significant government spending on industrial policy |
| Democratic Socialist | Accept private corporate subsidy model rather than public manufacturing; accept that ownership structure is not addressed |

**Viability Assessment:**

- **Coalition Size:** 7/9 perspectives (Libertarian opposes all industrial policy; Constitutionalist questions federal manufacturing authority)
- **High Consistency Ratio:** 4/7 coalition members rated 4+ consistency (Liberal, Progressive, Centrist, Democratic Socialist)
- **Durability:** High; manufacturing investment creates constituencies (factory workers, supply chain companies, host communities) that defend the programs across political cycles; anti-China framing has bipartisan durability; the 18 House Republicans who defended IRA manufacturing provisions in 2023 demonstrate that job creation in districts creates durable support regardless of party messaging
- **Implementation Path:** Bipartisan legislation building on IRA framework; anti-China trade provisions attract conservative and populist support; manufacturing credit extension has backing from industry and organized labor; energy committee and finance committee jurisdiction; components have appeared in various forms in recent legislative proposals

### Compromise 3: State Clean Energy and Grid Reliability Framework

**Bridges:** Liberal + Progressive + Constitutionalist + Centrist + Religious Right + Democratic Socialist

**Core Agreement:** States are the appropriate level of government to set energy standards, and a framework that supports state action while investing in grid reliability can advance clean energy deployment without requiring federal mandates that trigger ideological opposition.

**Policy Description:**

Model state legislation establishes technology-inclusive clean electricity standards: 50% clean by 2030, 80% by 2035, and 100% by 2045. Clean energy is defined to include solar, wind, geothermal, hydropower, nuclear, and energy storage -- but not natural gas with carbon capture (which is addressed separately under emissions standards). Compliance pathways include direct generation, clean energy purchases, clean energy certificates, and an alternative compliance payment of $50/MWh indexed to inflation. Utilities may recover prudently incurred compliance costs. Federal companion legislation provides $5 billion annually in Clean Energy Reliability Grants to states that adopt clean electricity standards meeting minimum benchmarks, with funds directed to grid modernization, storage deployment, and transmission upgrades that enhance reliability during the clean energy transition. FERC is directed to reform regional transmission planning to incorporate state clean energy targets as a planning input, allocate transmission costs based on projected benefits from clean energy deployment, and require regional transmission organizations to study and implement interregional transfer capability enhancements. A $2 billion Grid Reliability and Resilience Fund provides emergency support for grid operators managing high-penetration renewable systems, including funding for flexible resources, improved forecasting, and weather hardening. States that choose not to adopt clean electricity standards receive no penalty -- they simply do not receive the reliability grant funding.

**Concern Mapping:**

| Perspective | How This Addresses Their Concerns |
|-------------|-----------------------------------|
| Liberal | Clean electricity standards advance decarbonization; federal reliability funding enables ambitious state targets; transmission planning reform removes a key bottleneck |
| Progressive | 100% clean targets are included; technology-inclusive approach accelerates deployment; reliability investment addresses genuine grid integration challenges |
| Constitutionalist | State-level standards respect federalism; federal funding is voluntary under Spending Clause; no federal mandate on energy mix; states that decline face no penalty |
| Centrist | Technology-inclusive standards are evidence-based; reliability investment addresses genuine integration challenges; state-level approach is politically feasible; phased timelines are achievable |
| Religious Right | State authority preserved; no federal mandate; technology inclusion means nuclear (which many in the Religious Right support) counts toward clean energy targets |
| Democratic Socialist | 100% clean targets are the right direction; reliability investment prevents rollback arguments based on grid concerns; state standards create framework that unions and advocates can build upon |

**Concessions:**

| Perspective | What They Give Up |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Liberal | Accept state-level approach without federal mandate; accept that non-adopting states will not be compelled; accept technology-inclusive definition that includes nuclear |
| Progressive | Accept 2045 timeline rather than 2030-2035 for 100%; accept that natural gas CCS is not included as clean energy (a concession they welcome) but that nuclear is; accept voluntary state adoption |
| Constitutionalist | Accept federal reliability grants that incentivize state clean energy standards; accept FERC transmission planning reform that incorporates state targets |
| Centrist | Accept that voluntary adoption will leave some states behind; accept 100% clean target that some analysts consider technically challenging |
| Religious Right | Accept that federal funding incentivizes state clean energy adoption; accept government spending on grid reliability |
| Democratic Socialist | Accept state-by-state approach rather than national mandate; accept technology-inclusive definition; accept that public ownership is not required |

**Viability Assessment:**

- **Coalition Size:** 6/9 perspectives (Conservative opposition to clean energy standards at any level; Libertarian opposition to government energy mandates; Populist opposition to clean energy targets perceived as raising costs)
- **High Consistency Ratio:** 5/6 coalition members rated 4+ consistency (Liberal, Progressive, Constitutionalist, Centrist, Democratic Socialist)
- **Durability:** Moderate to high; state-level adoption creates durable standards that survive federal political shifts; reliability investment addresses the strongest substantive objection to high clean energy penetration; the voluntary nature means no state is compelled, which removes the coercion argument; however, states with the most fossil fuel dependence are least likely to adopt, limiting the framework's reach to states already inclined toward clean energy
- **Implementation Path:** Model state legislation promoted through National Conference of State Legislatures and bipartisan governors' coalitions; federal reliability funding through energy committee legislation; FERC transmission planning reform can proceed through Commission orders with legislative support; build on existing state clean energy standards already adopted by 30+ states

## Strategic Implications

### Most Viable Coalition

The most effective coalition for clean energy reform operates on three tracks. The broadest coalition (8-9 perspectives) can advance permitting reform and interconnection reform -- deregulatory measures that remove barriers without requiring new government programs or spending. These reforms are also the most impactful: the 2,000+ GW interconnection queue and multi-year permitting timelines are the most binding constraints on clean energy deployment, and resolving them accelerates private investment already motivated by IRA incentives and falling technology costs. A center-out manufacturing coalition (7 perspectives) can advance domestic manufacturing investment and workforce development by framing clean energy through economic competitiveness and China competition rather than climate urgency. A center-left coalition (Liberal, Progressive, Centrist, Democratic Socialist) with selective Constitutionalist support can advance state-level clean energy standards and transmission expansion, accepting that the most ideologically resistant states will not participate but building an expanding network of clean energy states.

### Key Obstacles

- **Climate framing toxicity:** Clean energy policy is politically coded as climate policy, which triggers identity-based opposition from conservative and populist perspectives regardless of the economic merits; the same policy (domestic solar manufacturing) receives dramatically different support depending on whether it is framed as "fighting climate change" or "competing with China"
- **Fossil fuel incumbent power:** The fossil fuel industry wields disproportionate political influence through campaign contributions, lobbying, and economic dependence in key states and districts; this influence is particularly effective with conservative and populist perspectives, creating a gap between their principled positions (free markets, energy independence) and their operational positions (defending fossil fuel advantages)
- **Transmission as the binding constraint:** Even with permitting reform, manufacturing investment, and clean energy standards, the lack of interstate transmission capacity limits deployment of the lowest-cost clean energy resources; transmission expansion requires federal authority that right-of-center perspectives oppose on structural grounds, creating a bottleneck that cannot be resolved through deregulation alone
- **Equity vs. speed trade-off:** Progressive and democratic socialist insistence on community benefit, environmental justice, and public ownership provisions can slow deployment of projects that would accelerate decarbonization; the tension between "get it built" and "get it right" divides the left in ways that complicate coalition-building with the center and right
- **Subsidy dependence and political risk:** The IRA's tax credit structure makes clean energy investment dependent on political durability of credits that could be repealed or modified in future Congresses; this political risk discourages long-term investment and makes industry cautious despite current incentives

### Low Consistency Partners

Conservative (2/5) and Populist (2/5) perspectives rate at low consistency on clean energy, the lowest ratings in this analysis. This reflects the degree to which both perspectives' positions are driven by fossil fuel industry alignment and cultural identity rather than consistent application of stated principles. Conservative support is most reliable on permitting reform and nuclear energy -- issues where deregulatory framing aligns with stated principles -- and least reliable on clean energy investment or standards that require accepting the legitimacy of the energy transition. Populist support is most accessible when clean energy is framed as domestic manufacturing, China competition, and energy cost reduction, and least accessible when framed through climate urgency or environmental justice. The Religious Right (3/5) is the most movable partner among right-of-center perspectives, with creation care theology providing theological grounding for clean energy support that is currently suppressed by conservative coalition dynamics but could be activated through deliberate engagement with evangelical environmental organizations.

The strategic approach should: (1) frame clean energy reform as economic competitiveness and energy security to access conservative and populist support for manufacturing and permitting; (2) separate infrastructure reform (permitting, interconnection, transmission) from climate policy to maintain the broadest possible coalition; (3) invest in the creation care narrative within evangelical communities to gradually shift Religious Right engagement; and (4) accept that some reforms (transmission authority, equity provisions) require narrower but committed coalitions rather than attempting to build consensus that does not exist.

### Recommended Approach

1. **Lead with permitting and interconnection reform:** The Energy Infrastructure Permitting and Interconnection Reform Act (Compromise 1) commands 8/9 perspective support by framing barrier removal as deregulation rather than clean energy promotion; this single reform would accelerate deployment more than any new subsidy by clearing the 2,000+ GW interconnection queue and reducing multi-year permitting timelines

2. **Build the manufacturing coalition around China competition:** The American Energy Manufacturing and Workforce Investment Act (Compromise 2) commands 7/9 perspective support by framing clean energy manufacturing as national security and economic competitiveness; anti-China sentiment is the most powerful bipartisan force in contemporary American politics, and clean energy manufacturing can ride this wave to build durable domestic industrial capacity

3. **Pursue clean energy standards through states:** The State Clean Energy and Grid Reliability Framework (Compromise 3) respects federalism while creating momentum; 30+ states already have some form of clean energy standard, and federal reliability funding removes the strongest substantive objection (grid reliability) while encouraging adoption without mandating it

4. **Separate clean energy from climate:** Every effort should be made to frame clean energy reform through economic competitiveness, energy security, domestic manufacturing, and consumer cost frames rather than climate urgency; "cheaper energy" and "American-made" resonate across the political spectrum in ways that "climate emergency" does not

5. **Invest in transmission through narrower coalition:** Transmission expansion requires federal authority and cannot be achieved through deregulation alone; build the center-left coalition (Liberal, Progressive, Centrist, Democratic Socialist) needed for National Transmission Infrastructure Act while designing FERC reform to operate within existing authority where possible

6. **Protect IRA investments through constituency-building:** The most durable protection for clean energy tax credits is the factory workers, construction crews, and host communities that benefit from them; every clean energy manufacturing facility in a Republican district builds political durability; prioritize rapid deployment of IRA-funded projects in politically competitive areas

7. **Address equity through implementation, not legislation:** Environmental justice provisions face structural opposition from 3-5 perspectives; rather than making equity the legislative centerpiece (which narrows the coalition), build equity into implementation -- siting priorities, community benefit requirements, workforce diversity targets -- where administrative discretion allows progressive goals to be achieved without legislative battles
