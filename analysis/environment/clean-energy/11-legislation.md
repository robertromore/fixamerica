# Clean Energy: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Accelerating clean energy deployment requires legislation addressing permitting, transmission, market design, manufacturing, and workforce development. This document provides draft legislation for key reforms.

## Federal Legislation

### Clean Energy Permitting Reform Act

**Purpose**: Streamline permitting for clean energy projects while maintaining environmental protection.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Clean Energy Permitting Reform
Act of 2025".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that—
    (1) clean energy deployment must accelerate to meet climate
    goals;
    (2) permitting delays significantly slow deployment;
    (3) current processes can be improved while protecting
    environmental values;
    (4) transmission expansion is critical; and
    (5) federal coordination can streamline processes.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:
    (1) CLEAN ENERGY PROJECT.—The term "clean energy project"
    means a project for the generation, storage, or transmission
    of electricity from renewable energy sources, nuclear energy,
    or other zero-emission sources.

    (2) COVERED PROJECT.—The term "covered project" means a
    clean energy project requiring federal authorization.

SEC. 4. LEAD AGENCY DESIGNATION.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—For each covered project, the agency with
primary permitting responsibility shall serve as lead agency.

(b) COORDINATION.—The lead agency shall coordinate all federal
authorizations.

(c) TIMELINE.—The lead agency shall establish a permitting
timeline not to exceed—
    (1) 2 years for projects requiring an environmental
    impact statement; and
    (2) 1 year for projects requiring an environmental
    assessment.

SEC. 5. CATEGORICAL EXCLUSIONS.

(a) CLEAN ENERGY.—The Council on Environmental Quality shall
establish categorical exclusions for—
    (1) solar projects on previously disturbed land;
    (2) battery storage projects;
    (3) transmission upgrades within existing rights-of-way;
    (4) offshore wind in designated areas; and
    (5) other clean energy projects with minimal impact.

(b) TIMELINE.—Categorical exclusions shall be established
within 1 year.

SEC. 6. TRANSMISSION SITING.

(a) NATIONAL INTEREST CORRIDORS.—The Secretary of Energy
may designate national interest electric transmission corridors.

(b) FERC BACKSTOP.—If a state does not act on a permit
application for a transmission line in a designated corridor
within 1 year, FERC may issue the permit.

(c) COST ALLOCATION.—FERC shall establish rules for allocating
the costs of transmission facilities based on benefits.

(d) FEDERAL LANDS.—Federal agencies shall expedite rights-of-way
for transmission on federal lands.

SEC. 7. JUDICIAL REVIEW.

(a) TIMELINE.—Judicial review of covered projects shall be
completed within 180 days of filing.

(b) STANDING.—Standing requirements shall apply.

(c) REMEDIES.—Courts shall consider project benefits in
fashioning remedies.

SEC. 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect upon enactment.
```

### National Transmission Infrastructure Act

**Purpose**: Enable construction of a modern interstate transmission system.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "National Transmission
Infrastructure Act of 2025".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that—
    (1) transmission infrastructure is essential for clean
    energy deployment;
    (2) the current transmission system is inadequate;
    (3) interstate transmission faces siting barriers;
    (4) cost allocation disputes delay projects;
    (5) federal leadership is necessary.

SEC. 3. NATIONAL TRANSMISSION PLANNING.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—The Secretary of Energy, in coordination
with FERC, shall develop a National Transmission Plan.

(b) ELEMENTS.—The Plan shall identify—
    (1) transmission needs for achieving clean energy goals;
    (2) priority corridors;
    (3) technology requirements; and
    (4) cost estimates.

(c) UPDATE.—The Plan shall be updated every 3 years.

SEC. 4. TRANSMISSION AUTHORITIES.

(a) FERC SITING.—FERC shall have authority to site
interstate transmission lines designated in the National
Transmission Plan if—
    (1) the line is necessary to achieve national clean
    energy goals;
    (2) state processes have not resulted in approval
    within 2 years; and
    (3) the line is in the public interest.

(b) EMINENT DOMAIN.—Transmission developers may exercise
eminent domain for approved lines with just compensation.

SEC. 5. COST ALLOCATION.

(a) PRINCIPLE.—Transmission costs shall be allocated to
beneficiaries.

(b) PRESUMPTION.—For lines designated in the National Plan,
broad regional or interregional allocation is presumed
appropriate.

(c) FERC RULES.—FERC shall establish rules implementing
this section.

SEC. 6. FEDERAL LANDS AND RIGHTS-OF-WAY.

(a) CORRIDORS.—Federal agencies shall designate energy
corridors on federal lands.

(b) EXPEDITED PROCESSING.—Federal land agencies shall process
right-of-way applications within 1 year.

(c) COORDINATION.—Agencies shall coordinate with the
National Transmission Plan.

SEC. 7. FINANCING.

(a) LOAN GUARANTEES.—DOE may provide loan guarantees for
qualifying transmission projects.

(b) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized $10 billion in
loan authority.

SEC. 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect upon enactment.
```

### Clean Energy Manufacturing Act

**Purpose**: Accelerate domestic clean energy manufacturing.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Clean Energy Manufacturing
Act of 2025".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that—
    (1) clean energy manufacturing creates good jobs;
    (2) domestic manufacturing enhances energy security;
    (3) the IRA provides significant incentives;
    (4) additional support is needed for supply chains; and
    (5) workforce development is essential.

SEC. 3. MANUFACTURING INCENTIVES.

(a) EXTENSION.—The 45X advanced manufacturing production
credit is extended through 2035.

(b) EXPANSION.—The credit is expanded to include—
    (1) transmission equipment;
    (2) grid components;
    (3) heat pumps; and
    (4) other clean energy equipment as designated.

SEC. 4. SUPPLY CHAIN.

(a) CRITICAL MINERALS.—There is authorized $5 billion for
domestic critical mineral production and processing.

(b) RECYCLING.—There is authorized $2 billion for clean
energy equipment recycling infrastructure.

(c) STOCKPILE.—The Secretary shall maintain strategic
stockpiles of critical clean energy materials.

SEC. 5. WORKFORCE.

(a) TRAINING.—There is authorized $3 billion for clean
energy manufacturing workforce training.

(b) STANDARDS.—The Secretary of Labor shall establish
industry-recognized credentials.

(c) APPRENTICESHIP.—Manufacturing facilities receiving
credits shall participate in registered apprenticeship.

SEC. 6. BUY AMERICA.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—Federal clean energy procurement shall
prioritize domestic content.

(b) WAIVER.—Waivers may be granted only upon finding
domestic products unavailable.

SEC. 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect upon enactment.
```

## State Model Legislation

### Model State Clean Electricity Standard

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Clean Electricity
Standard Act".

SECTION 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSE.

The Legislature finds that clean electricity is essential for
addressing climate change, improving air quality, and creating
economic opportunity.

SECTION 3. CLEAN ELECTRICITY STANDARD.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—Each retail electricity provider shall
ensure that the following percentages of electricity sold
is from clean energy sources:
    (1) 50 percent by 2030;
    (2) 80 percent by 2035;
    (3) 100 percent by 2040.

(b) CLEAN ENERGY.—Clean energy includes renewable energy,
nuclear, and other zero-emission sources.

SECTION 4. COMPLIANCE.

Compliance may be demonstrated through:
(a) Direct generation;
(b) Purchase of clean energy;
(c) Purchase of clean energy certificates; or
(d) Alternative compliance payment.

SECTION 5. ALTERNATIVE COMPLIANCE PAYMENT.

The alternative compliance payment shall be $50/MWh, adjusted
annually for inflation.

SECTION 6. COST RECOVERY.

Utilities may recover prudently incurred costs of compliance.

SECTION 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect upon passage.
```

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| **Timeline exceptions** | Delays despite limits | Medium |
| **Cost allocation disputes** | Continued litigation | High |
| **State resistance** | Non-cooperation | Medium |
| **Categorical exclusion abuse** | Inappropriate application | Low |
| **Manufacturing credits** | Benefit foreign-owned | Low |

### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| **Political durability** | Policy reversal risk | Partisan division |
| **Implementation capacity** | Agency resources | Funding |
| **Litigation** | Delay despite reforms | Judicial review |
| **Supply chain** | Constraints continue | Global market |
| **Workforce** | Skills gaps persist | Training time |

### Rectification Procedures

1. **Strict timeline enforcement** with consequences for agencies
2. **Clear cost allocation rules** with limited litigation
3. **Strong federal backstop** for state non-action
4. **Narrow categorical exclusions** with monitoring
5. **Domestic ownership requirements** for full credits
6. **Bipartisan support** for durability
7. **Adequate agency funding** for implementation
8. **Expedited judicial review** with deference
9. **Supply chain diversification** requirements
10. **Robust workforce investment** with accountability

### General Implementation Concerns

| Concern | Mitigation |
|---------|------------|
| **Political reversal** | Bipartisan coalition |
| **Litigation** | Strong legal foundation |
| **State opposition** | Federal backstop |
| **Industry gaming** | Clear rules, enforcement |
| **Equity** | Justice provisions |
