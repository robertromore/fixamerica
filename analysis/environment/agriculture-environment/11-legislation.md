# Agriculture and Environment: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Transforming agriculture's environmental impact requires comprehensive legislation reforming farm programs, conservation incentives, pollution regulation, and market structures. This document provides draft legislation for key reforms and analysis of existing legal frameworks.

## Federal Legislation

### Sustainable Agriculture Investment Act

**Purpose**: Reform farm programs to prioritize conservation and sustainability.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Sustainable Agriculture
Investment Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that—
    (1) agricultural practices significantly impact
    environmental quality;
    (2) current farm programs inadequately address
    environmental concerns;
    (3) conservation programs are underfunded;
    (4) sustainable practices benefit farmers and
    the environment; and
    (5) comprehensive reform is necessary.

SEC. 3. CONSERVATION PROGRAM FUNDING.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.—Conservation program funding shall
be increased by $5 billion annually.

(b) ALLOCATION.—
    (1) 40% for working lands programs (EQIP, CSP).
    (2) 30% for land retirement (CRP).
    (3) 20% for wetland and easement programs.
    (4) 10% for technical assistance.

SEC. 4. OUTCOME-BASED CONSERVATION.

(a) ENVIRONMENTAL OUTCOMES.—Conservation programs shall
prioritize measurable environmental outcomes including—
    (1) water quality improvement;
    (2) soil carbon sequestration;
    (3) greenhouse gas reduction;
    (4) biodiversity enhancement; and
    (5) pollinator habitat.

(b) PAYMENT FOR OUTCOMES.—The Secretary shall develop
payment-for-outcomes options within conservation programs.

(c) MONITORING.—Environmental outcomes shall be monitored
and verified.

SEC. 5. SOIL HEALTH PROGRAM.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—There is established a National Soil
Health Program.

(b) PRACTICES.—The program shall support—
    (1) cover crops;
    (2) reduced tillage;
    (3) diverse rotations;
    (4) organic amendments;
    (5) integrated crop-livestock systems.

(c) FUNDING.—$1 billion annually is authorized for the
soil health program.

(d) TECHNICAL ASSISTANCE.—NRCS shall provide enhanced
technical assistance for soil health.

SEC. 6. CARBON SEQUESTRATION.

(a) CARBON PROGRAM.—The Secretary shall establish a
carbon sequestration payment program.

(b) MEASUREMENT.—Standardized protocols for measuring
soil carbon shall be developed.

(c) VERIFICATION.—Third-party verification shall be
required.

(d) PAYMENT.—Payments shall reflect verified carbon
sequestration.

SEC. 7. CONSERVATION COMPLIANCE.

(a) STRENGTHENING.—Conservation compliance requirements
shall be strengthened.

(b) COVER CROPS.—Cover crop requirements shall be
considered for highly erodible land.

(c) WETLANDS.—Wetland protection shall be enhanced.

SEC. 8. CROP INSURANCE REFORM.

(a) CONSERVATION INCENTIVES.—Crop insurance shall
incentivize conservation practices.

(b) COVER CROP DISCOUNT.—Premium discounts shall be
provided for cover crop adoption.

(c) SOIL HEALTH.—Soil health practices shall be
recognized in insurance products.

SEC. 9. RESEARCH.

(a) SUSTAINABLE SYSTEMS.—$500 million annually for
sustainable agriculture research.

(b) AGROECOLOGY.—Research shall include systems-based
agroecology.

(c) EXTENSION.—Extension capacity for sustainable
practices shall be expanded.

SEC. 10. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect upon enactment.
```

### Agricultural Water Quality Act

**Purpose**: Strengthen agricultural water quality protection.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Agricultural Water
Quality Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that—
    (1) agricultural nonpoint source pollution impairs
    water quality;
    (2) voluntary programs alone are insufficient;
    (3) nutrient pollution causes dead zones and
    algal blooms;
    (4) stronger measures are needed; and
    (5) farmers need support for compliance.

SEC. 3. NUTRIENT MANAGEMENT.

(a) REQUIREMENT.—Operations in priority watersheds
shall implement nutrient management plans.

(b) PRIORITY WATERSHEDS.—The Administrator shall
identify priority watersheds based on impairment.

(c) PLANS.—Nutrient management plans shall include—
    (1) soil testing;
    (2) nutrient application rates;
    (3) timing and placement;
    (4) edge-of-field practices; and
    (5) record keeping.

(d) ASSISTANCE.—NRCS shall provide technical and
financial assistance for plan development and
implementation.

SEC. 4. CONCENTRATED ANIMAL FEEDING OPERATIONS.

(a) PERMIT COVERAGE.—CAFO permit thresholds shall
be lowered to cover more operations.

(b) NUTRIENT MANAGEMENT.—Comprehensive nutrient
management shall be required.

(c) MONITORING.—Air and water monitoring shall be
enhanced.

(d) ENFORCEMENT.—Enforcement resources shall be adequate.

SEC. 5. BUFFER REQUIREMENTS.

(a) STREAMS.—Vegetated buffers shall be required
adjacent to waterways in priority watersheds.

(b) WIDTH.—Minimum buffer widths shall be established
based on conditions.

(c) MAINTENANCE.—Buffers shall be maintained.

(d) INCENTIVES.—Financial assistance shall be available
for buffer establishment.

SEC. 6. WETLAND RESTORATION.

(a) PRIORITY.—Wetland restoration shall be prioritized
in areas with water quality impairment.

(b) FUNDING.—$500 million annually for wetland
restoration.

(c) EASEMENTS.—Permanent easements shall be encouraged.

SEC. 7. WATER QUALITY TRADING.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.—The Administrator may approve water
quality trading programs.

(b) STANDARDS.—Trading programs shall meet environmental
standards.

(c) ADDITIONALITY.—Trades shall produce real reductions.

SEC. 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect upon enactment.
```

### Model Soil Health Act

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Soil Health Act".

SECTION 2. FINDINGS.

The Legislature finds that healthy soil provides
multiple benefits including water quality, carbon
sequestration, and agricultural productivity.

SECTION 3. SOIL HEALTH PROGRAM.

(a) There is established a state soil health program.

(b) The program shall provide—
    (1) technical assistance;
    (2) cost-share for practices;
    (3) demonstration projects; and
    (4) education and outreach.

SECTION 4. PRIORITY PRACTICES.

Priority practices include—
    (a) cover crops;
    (b) reduced tillage;
    (c) diverse rotations;
    (d) organic amendments; and
    (e) integrated systems.

SECTION 5. FUNDING.

[Funding mechanism for program]

SECTION 6. COORDINATION.

The [Agency] shall coordinate with federal programs
and conservation districts.

SECTION 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect on [date].
```

### Model CAFO Regulation

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Livestock
Facility Environmental Protection Act".

SECTION 2. FINDINGS.

The Legislature finds that concentrated animal
feeding operations can impact air and water quality
and comprehensive regulation is necessary.

SECTION 3. PERMIT REQUIREMENTS.

(a) Permits shall be required for operations
exceeding [threshold].

(b) Permits shall include—
    (1) nutrient management plan;
    (2) manure storage requirements;
    (3) land application limits;
    (4) monitoring requirements; and
    (5) reporting requirements.

SECTION 4. SETBACKS.

(a) New facilities shall maintain setbacks from—
    (1) residences;
    (2) schools;
    (3) waterways; and
    (4) public areas.

(b) Setback distances shall be [specified].

SECTION 5. MONITORING.

(a) Water quality monitoring shall be required.

(b) Air quality monitoring shall be required for
large facilities.

SECTION 6. ENFORCEMENT.

Violations shall be subject to penalties of up to
$[amount] per day.

SECTION 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect on [date].
```

## Constitutional Considerations

### Federal Authority

| Basis | Application |
|-------|-------------|
| **Commerce Clause** | Interstate agricultural commerce |
| **Spending Power** | Farm program conditions |
| **Clean Water Act** | Point sources (CAFOs) |

### State Authority

| Basis | Application |
|-------|-------------|
| **Police power** | Environmental regulation |
| **Agriculture** | State programs |
| **Water** | State water quality |

### Limitations

| Issue | Analysis |
|-------|----------|
| **Nonpoint source** | CWA limits federal authority |
| **Takings** | Compensation for regulation |
| **Commerce** | State vs. interstate |

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| **Voluntary programs** | No requirement to participate | High |
| **CAFO thresholds** | Many operations exempt | High |
| **Nonpoint source** | No federal regulation | High |
| **Enforcement** | Limited resources | Medium |
| **Definition gaming** | Practice definitions | Medium |

### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| **Scale** | Programs too small | Funding |
| **Voluntary nature** | Insufficient adoption | Political |
| **Monitoring** | Limited verification | Resources |
| **Enforcement** | Weak accountability | Political will |

### Rectification Procedures

1. **Mandatory compliance** in priority areas
2. **Lower CAFO thresholds** to cover more operations
3. **Nonpoint source requirements** with CWA amendments
4. **Enhanced enforcement** with adequate resources
5. **Clear definitions** preventing gaming
6. **Adequate funding** for conservation programs
7. **Outcome verification** with monitoring
8. **Technical assistance** supporting compliance
9. **Market incentives** complementing regulation
10. **Research investment** in sustainable systems

### General Implementation Concerns

| Concern | Mitigation |
|---------|------------|
| **Political opposition** | Coalition building |
| **Farmer burden** | Transition support |
| **Implementation capacity** | Phased approach |
| **Economic impacts** | Market development |
