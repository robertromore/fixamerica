# Oceans and Coasts: Political Perspectives Analysis

## Overview

This document analyzes oceans and coasts policy -- specifically marine conservation, fisheries management, coastal resilience, marine pollution reduction, ocean governance, and blue economy development -- through nine distinct political perspectives. The core questions are: how aggressively should the federal government expand marine protected areas and restrict extractive activities, what is the appropriate balance between economic use and conservation of ocean resources, who should bear the costs of coastal adaptation to sea level rise, and how should the United States engage in international ocean governance? Each perspective receives engagement-consistency ratings and position scores, culminating in compromise proposals that bridge multiple viewpoints.

Oceans and coasts policy is a distinctive domain because it sits at the intersection of climate change, economic development, property rights, international governance, and environmental conservation. Unlike many environmental issues that divide cleanly along partisan lines, ocean policy contains specific sub-issues -- such as fisheries management, coastal resilience, and offshore energy -- where traditional coalitions fracture. Coastal conservatives whose communities face flooding may support adaptation investments that inland conservatives oppose. Commercial fishing interests may align with environmentalists against industrial pollution while opposing marine protected areas. The 30x30 conservation target, $2 billion annual coastal resilience program, and extended producer responsibility for plastics proposed in the legislation represent a level of federal intervention that generates significant disagreement about scope, cost, and constitutional authority, even among perspectives that agree oceans face serious threats.

## Scoring Framework

### Position Scores (1-10)

| Score | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| 10 | Full agreement with project analysis |
| 7-9 | Substantial to strong agreement |
| 5-6 | Mixed/moderate agreement |
| 3-4 | Substantial disagreement |
| 1-2 | Strong opposition |

### Engagement Consistency (1-5)

This metric assesses how reliably a perspective's stated positions align with its underlying principles, and how stable those positions are when concerns are addressed. Higher scores indicate more predictable negotiating partners.

| Level | Label | Indicators |
|-------|-------|------------|
| 5 | Highly Consistent | Stated positions reflect underlying principles; positions stable when concerns addressed |
| 4 | Mostly Consistent | Principles generally align with positions; minor strategic flexibility |
| 3 | Mixed Consistency | Some positions principled, others reflect coalition pressures or unstated concerns |
| 2 | Low Consistency | Positions shift frequently; stated concerns differ from underlying priorities |
| 1 | Unpredictable | Positions appear driven primarily by opposition dynamics rather than principles |

**Coalition-Building Considerations:**

- Alignment gap: When stated concerns differ from underlying priorities, addressing stated concerns may not secure support
- Position stability: Perspectives with shifting positions require ongoing negotiation
- Principle consistency: Perspectives that apply principles uniformly are more predictable partners
- Internal diversity: Some perspectives contain factions with different priorities

## Perspective Analyses

### Conservative Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 3/5 - Mixed Consistency**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Stated concern for coastal communities and sustainable fisheries is genuine for coastal Republicans; inland conservatives often use cost and regulatory burden arguments to block conservation measures that do not directly affect their constituents |
| Principle consistency | Free market and property rights principles are applied selectively; conservatives support subsidized federal flood insurance (NFIP) for coastal property owners while opposing other federal interventions; support for offshore energy development contradicts deference to local control when coastal communities oppose leasing |
| Goalpost stability | Moderate; conservative positions on ocean policy have shifted from supporting marine conservation under Nixon (who signed the Coastal Zone Management Act) and George W. Bush (who expanded Papahanaumokuakea) to opposing most new federal conservation designations |
| Zero-sum behavior | Some; opposition to 30x30 is often framed in terms that reject any new conservation regardless of design, suggesting the target number itself triggers opposition rather than the specific implementation details |

**Justification:** Conservatives on ocean policy exhibit a genuine internal split between coastal and inland factions. Coastal conservatives -- including many commercial fishing interests, tourism-dependent communities, and military-adjacent districts -- are pragmatic about marine conservation and coastal resilience. Inland conservatives treat ocean policy primarily through the lens of federal overreach and energy development. The mixed score reflects this internal diversity: addressing coastal conservative concerns about economic impacts and local control can genuinely shift positions, but inland conservative opposition is often driven by broader anti-regulatory ideology rather than specific ocean policy concerns.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- George W. Bush designated the Papahanaumokuakea Marine National Monument, the largest marine protected area at the time, demonstrating that conservative presidents can support ocean conservation
- Republican coastal senators from states like Alaska, Maine, and Louisiana have supported fisheries funding and coastal resilience investments
- Heritage Foundation and Competitive Enterprise Institute publications frame 30x30 as a "land grab" regardless of how protections are structured
- Conservative support for NFIP subsidies contradicts stated opposition to market-distorting federal programs
- Republican support for offshore oil and gas leasing overrides local control objections from affected coastal communities

- Market-based fisheries management with tradeable catch shares
- Offshore energy development paired with conservation fund set-asides
- Voluntary industry partnerships for plastic reduction
- NFIP reform that phases in risk-based pricing gradually with hardship exemptions
- State-led coastal resilience rather than federal programs
- Technology investment for carbon capture rather than emissions mandates
- Bilateral fisheries and ocean agreements rather than multilateral treaties

#### Coalition Potential

- **Natural allies:** Libertarian (on opposing federal regulation), Constitutionalist (on federalism concerns), Populist (on fishing community protection)
- **Potential bridges:** Centrist (on science-based fisheries management), Religious Right (on stewardship framing)
- **Unlikely partners:** Progressive, Democratic Socialist (on scale of federal investment and regulatory authority), Liberal (on climate framing and 30x30)

**Justification:** Liberals maintain a mostly consistent ocean and coasts position rooted in environmental stewardship, scientific expertise, and government's responsibility to manage shared resources. Support for 30x30, coastal resilience investment, pollution reduction, and international cooperation follows coherently from core principles. **Justification:** Libertarians bring principled consistency to ocean policy, even though their positions are far from mainstream. **Evidence for assessment:**

### Constitutionalist Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 4/5 - Mostly Consistent**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Genuine commitment to enumerated powers and federalism; constitutional concerns about federal ocean authority are principled, not pretextual |
| Principle consistency | Federalism principles are applied relatively uniformly; oppose federal conservation designations while supporting state coastal zone management; accept federal authority over navigable waters and admiralty but question expansion beyond constitutional text |
| Goalpost stability | Stable; have consistently questioned federal authority over environmental regulation since the New Deal expansion; positions on ocean governance have not shifted for political convenience |
| Zero-sum behavior | Low; willing to support federal ocean policy that operates within what they view as constitutional bounds (admiralty, interstate commerce, treaty power) |

**Justification:** Constitutionalists bring principled constitutional analysis to ocean policy. Their distinction between federal authority over navigable waters and admiralty (accepted) versus expansive conservation mandates (questioned) is legally grounded, even if most courts have upheld federal environmental authority under the Commerce Clause. The score of 4 reflects genuine principled engagement, with the slight reduction acknowledging that constitutional analysis can sometimes be used selectively -- for example, supporting federal offshore energy leasing under the Property Clause while questioning federal conservation authority under the same clause.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Federalist Society publications on environmental law and federal authority
- Constitutional challenges to marine sanctuary designations and fishing restrictions
- Support for state coastal zone management as constitutionally appropriate
- Consistent advocacy for treaty ratification through Article II process rather than executive agreements
- Opposition to executive ocean conservation orders as exceeding presidential authority

## Compromise Proposals

### Compromise 1: Bipartisan Fisheries Modernization and Coastal Resilience Package

**Bridges:** Conservative + Liberal + Populist + Centrist + Religious Right

**Core Agreement:** All five perspectives agree that science-based fisheries management works, that fishing communities need support, and that coastal communities face real threats requiring investment. This package bundles the least controversial ocean policy elements into a single bipartisan vehicle.

**Policy Description:**

Reauthorize the Magnuson-Stevens Act with climate adaptation provisions, ecosystem-based management guidelines, and fishing community transition support. Pair with a $1 billion annual Coastal Resilience Fund emphasizing nature-based solutions, community-driven planning, and state-federal coordination. Include enhanced IUU fishing enforcement and working waterfront protection. Omit 30x30 targets, international treaty provisions, and plastic regulation to maintain bipartisan viability.

**Concern Mapping:**

| Perspective | How This Addresses Their Concerns |
|-------------|-----------------------------------|
| Conservative | Maintains existing successful framework; modest spending within fiscal constraints; state-led resilience; no new conservation mandates |
| Liberal | Updates fisheries for climate; invests in resilience; protects vulnerable communities; nature-based solutions preferred |
| Populist | Protects small-scale fishers; IUU enforcement against foreign competitors; working waterfront protection; community focus |
| Centrist | Science-based, bipartisan, evidence-driven; achievable legislative vehicle; public-private partnerships |
| Religious Right | Stewardship of fisheries; community protection from disasters; working waterfront preservation in Gulf communities |

**Concessions:**

| Perspective | What They Give Up |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Conservative | Accept climate adaptation language in fisheries management; accept $1 billion annual federal spending |
| Liberal | Defer on 30x30, UNCLOS, and comprehensive plastic regulation; accept lower spending than proposed |
| Populist | Accept some environmental regulation of fishing as necessary for sustainability |
| Centrist | Accept that some desired elements (UNCLOS, plastics) must wait for later legislation |
| Religious Right | Accept federal role in fisheries and resilience beyond current levels |

**Viability Assessment:**

- **Coalition Size:** 5/9 perspectives
- **High Consistency Ratio:** 3/5 coalition members rated 4+ consistency (Liberal, Centrist, and arguably Conservative coastal faction)
- **Durability:** High; fisheries reauthorization has historical bipartisan precedent; coastal resilience has growing support from both parties as climate impacts intensify
- **Implementation Path:** Magnuson-Stevens reauthorization is overdue and multiple draft bills exist; attaching resilience funding to the reauthorization vehicle leverages existing legislative momentum

### Compromise 2: Ocean Pollution Accountability and Prevention Initiative

**Bridges:** Liberal + Progressive + Populist + Centrist + Democratic Socialist + Religious Right

**Core Agreement:** Six perspectives agree that corporate polluters should be held accountable for ocean pollution and that plastic debris is a growing crisis. By framing plastic pollution reduction as corporate accountability rather than consumer restriction, this package bridges progressive anti-corporate framing with populist and religious stewardship concerns.

**Policy Description:**

Establish extended producer responsibility for plastic packaging with phased implementation and industry transition support. Create an Enhanced Marine Debris Program ($200 million annually) for cleanup, prevention, and research. Require corporate disclosure of ocean pollution impacts. Fund innovation in plastic alternatives and waste management infrastructure. Frame as "polluter pays" corporate accountability rather than consumer bans. Support U.S. leadership in international plastics treaty negotiations with binding corporate provisions.

**Concern Mapping:**

| Perspective | How This Addresses Their Concerns |
|-------------|-----------------------------------|
| Liberal | Establishes producer responsibility framework; funds marine debris cleanup; supports international treaty |
| Progressive | Corporate accountability and polluter pays principle; production-side intervention; international framework |
| Populist | Corporations, not consumers, bear costs; working people are not restricted; corporate accountability framing |
| Centrist | Phased implementation with industry input; evidence-based approach; achievable international engagement |
| Democratic Socialist | Corporate accountability; public investment in alternatives; challenges petrochemical industry power |
| Religious Right | Creation care stewardship; cleanup of God's creation; community-based action alongside federal programs |

**Concessions:**

| Perspective | What They Give Up |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Liberal | Accept phased implementation rather than immediate mandates |
| Progressive | Accept industry transition support rather than immediate production caps |
| Populist | Accept some federal regulatory role in pollution reduction |
| Centrist | Accept corporate accountability framing that industry partners may resist |
| Democratic Socialist | Accept market-compatible producer responsibility rather than nationalized waste management |
| Religious Right | Accept federal regulatory program rather than purely voluntary stewardship |

**Viability Assessment:**

- **Coalition Size:** 6/9 perspectives
- **High Consistency Ratio:** 5/6 coalition members rated 4+ consistency (Liberal, Progressive, Centrist, Democratic Socialist; Populist and Religious Right at 3)
- **Durability:** Moderate-to-high; plastic pollution has growing public salience; corporate accountability framing resonates across political lines; international treaty process creates momentum
- **Implementation Path:** Build on existing Break Free From Plastic Pollution Act framework; align with international plastics treaty timeline; phased implementation reduces industry opposition

### Compromise 3: State-Federal Coastal Adaptation and Insurance Reform Partnership

**Bridges:** Conservative + Liberal + Centrist + Constitutionalist + Libertarian (partial)

**Core Agreement:** Multiple perspectives agree that NFIP is broken, that coastal risk is mispriced, and that states should lead resilience planning. This package reforms flood insurance while investing in state-led adaptation, satisfying both fiscal conservatives and environmental advocates.

**Policy Description:**

Reform NFIP with gradual transition to risk-based pricing over 10 years, means-tested affordability assistance for low-income policyholders, and mandatory climate risk disclosure for coastal property transactions. Establish a Federal-State Coastal Adaptation Partnership providing matching funds for state resilience plans that adopt sea level rise projections, prioritize nature-based solutions, and include community participation requirements. Link insurance reform savings to resilience investment funding. Include voluntary buyback program for repetitive-loss properties.

**Concern Mapping:**

| Perspective | How This Addresses Their Concerns |
|-------------|-----------------------------------|
| Conservative | Moves toward market-based insurance pricing; state-led planning; reduces federal fiscal exposure; gradual transition protects current policyholders |
| Liberal | Affordability protections for vulnerable communities; nature-based solutions emphasis; climate risk disclosure; environmental justice considerations |
| Centrist | Evidence-based reform; bipartisan structure; cost-effective public-private approach; gradual implementation |
| Constitutionalist | State-led planning respects federalism; federal role limited to matching funds and insurance reform within Commerce Clause authority |
| Libertarian (partial) | Moves NFIP toward market pricing; reduces moral hazard; could be first step toward full NFIP privatization |

**Concessions:**

| Perspective | What They Give Up |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Conservative | Accept continued federal role in flood insurance (rather than elimination); accept sea level rise projection requirements |
| Liberal | Accept gradual rather than immediate reform; accept state variation in resilience approaches |
| Centrist | Accept that some perspectives will not join this coalition (Progressive, Democratic Socialist oppose insurance market framing) |
| Constitutionalist | Accept federal matching fund conditions on state planning |
| Libertarian | Accept reform rather than elimination of NFIP; accept continued government role |

**Viability Assessment:**

- **Coalition Size:** 5/9 perspectives (with Libertarian partial support)
- **High Consistency Ratio:** 4/5 coalition members rated 4+ consistency (Liberal, Centrist, Constitutionalist, Libertarian)
- **Durability:** High; NFIP reform has bipartisan support in principle; linking insurance reform to resilience investment creates a positive-sum dynamic; state-led structure reduces federal political vulnerability
- **Implementation Path:** NFIP reauthorization provides a legislative vehicle; bipartisan NFIP reform proposals already exist in Congress; state resilience matching fund can be attached to appropriations

## Strategic Implications

### Most Viable Coalition

The most realistic path forward for ocean and coasts policy is a **Liberal-Centrist-Conservative coastal core** augmented by issue-specific partners. This coalition:

- Builds on the bipartisan tradition of Magnuson-Stevens fisheries management
- Leverages growing coastal Republican support for resilience investment as climate impacts intensify
- Uses nature-based solutions as a bridge between conservation and cost-effectiveness priorities
- Avoids the most polarizing elements (30x30 targets, UNCLOS, comprehensive climate regulation) in initial legislation

The Magnuson-Stevens reauthorization is the single most achievable legislative vehicle, commanding support from all nine perspectives at some level. Coastal resilience investment is the second-strongest area for bipartisan action. Plastic pollution reduction has strong public support and can attract a surprising left-populist-religious coalition when framed as corporate accountability.

### Key Obstacles

- **Climate framing polarization:** Framing ocean issues as climate issues immediately loses Conservative, Constitutionalist, and Populist perspectives; framing them as fisheries management, pollution, or coastal safety retains broader coalitions
- **30x30 opposition:** The conservation target number itself triggers opposition from multiple perspectives regardless of implementation details; incremental, site-specific conservation designations face less resistance
- **UNCLOS ratification:** Longstanding Senate opposition crosses multiple perspectives; bilateral and regional ocean agreements are more achievable
- **Federal spending levels:** The $2+ billion annual investment proposed in the Ocean Conservation and Climate Act exceeds what Conservative and Constitutionalist perspectives will support; lower-cost initial packages with demonstrated results can build support for scaling

### Low Consistency Partners

Three perspectives rated 3/5 (Conservative, Populist, Religious Right) require special handling:

- **Conservative:** The coastal/inland split means targeting coastal Republican constituencies can yield support that broader conservative movement opposes. Legislative design should emphasize state authority, economic benefits, and fishing community protection rather than environmental goals
- **Populist:** Framing matters enormously. Corporate accountability, worker protection, and anti-foreign-competition language brings populists on board; environmental regulation language pushes them away, even when the policy substance is identical
- **Religious Right:** The creation care faction is a valuable but minority voice. Engaging evangelical environmental organizations and framing ocean stewardship through theological language can unlock support that political framing cannot

### Recommended Approach

1. **Lead with fisheries reauthorization** as the proven bipartisan vehicle with broadest support across all perspectives
2. **Attach coastal resilience funding** to fisheries legislation, keeping spending levels moderate enough for bipartisan viability ($1 billion rather than $2 billion initially)
3. **Pursue plastic pollution separately** using corporate accountability framing that bridges left-populist-religious constituencies
4. **Advance NFIP reform** through its own reauthorization process, linking insurance reform savings to resilience investment
5. **Defer 30x30 and UNCLOS** to subsequent legislative efforts after building trust and demonstrating results from initial reforms
6. **Use state-level model legislation** to build track records that make federal action politically safer
7. **Frame consistently** around fisheries sustainability, community protection, pollution accountability, and economic opportunity rather than climate change or conservation targets
