# Climate Policy: Political Perspectives Analysis

## Overview

This document analyzes U.S. climate policy -- specifically emissions reduction targets, carbon pricing mechanisms, clean electricity standards, border carbon adjustments, adaptation and resilience planning, just transition provisions, and international climate commitments -- through nine distinct political perspectives. The core questions are: how urgently must the United States reduce greenhouse gas emissions, what policy mechanisms should drive that reduction, who should bear the costs of the energy transition, and what role should the federal government play relative to states and markets? Each perspective receives engagement-consistency ratings and position scores, culminating in compromise proposals that bridge multiple viewpoints.

Climate policy is among the most politically polarized domains in American politics. Unlike many policy areas where the facts are contested at the margins, climate policy involves a substantial faction that disputes the scientific consensus on anthropogenic climate change itself, making even the problem definition contentious. The analysis presented in the source documents -- which accepts the scientific consensus, identifies fossil fuel industry influence as a root cause of policy failure, and proposes aggressive federal intervention through mandates, carbon pricing, and regulation -- reflects a center-left to progressive analytical framework. This means perspectives further to the right will score lower not necessarily because they are disengaged but because the analytical baseline itself is contested. The challenge for coalition-building is that climate policy operates on an existential timeline: the atmosphere does not negotiate, and delayed action has compounding costs. This creates asymmetric urgency that complicates the normal dynamics of political compromise.

## Scoring Framework

### Position Scores (1-10)

| Score | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| 10 | Full agreement with project analysis |
| 7-9 | Substantial to strong agreement |
| 5-6 | Mixed/moderate agreement |
| 3-4 | Substantial disagreement |
| 1-2 | Strong opposition |

### Engagement Consistency (1-5)

This metric assesses how reliably a perspective's stated positions align with its underlying principles, and how stable those positions are when concerns are addressed. Higher scores indicate more predictable negotiating partners.

| Level | Label | Indicators |
|-------|-------|------------|
| 5 | Highly Consistent | Stated positions reflect underlying principles; positions stable when concerns addressed |
| 4 | Mostly Consistent | Principles generally align with positions; minor strategic flexibility |
| 3 | Mixed Consistency | Some positions principled, others reflect coalition pressures or unstated concerns |
| 2 | Low Consistency | Positions shift frequently; stated concerns differ from underlying priorities |
| 1 | Unpredictable | Positions appear driven primarily by opposition dynamics rather than principles |

**Coalition-Building Considerations:**

- Alignment gap: When stated concerns differ from underlying priorities, addressing stated concerns may not secure support
- Position stability: Perspectives with shifting positions require ongoing negotiation
- Principle consistency: Perspectives that apply principles uniformly are more predictable partners
- Internal diversity: Some perspectives contain factions with different priorities

## Perspective Analyses

### Conservative Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 2/5 - Low Consistency**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Stated concern for economic competitiveness, energy affordability, and technological innovation is genuine but frequently overridden by fossil fuel industry alignment, identity-based opposition to climate action, and resistance to any policy that acknowledges the scientific consensus too explicitly |
| Principle consistency | "Free market" principles would logically support correcting the massive externality of unpriced carbon emissions, yet conservatives oppose carbon pricing; "fiscal responsibility" principles would logically favor avoiding the $150+ billion annual cost of climate disasters, yet conservatives oppose spending on prevention; market principles are applied selectively to defend incumbent industries |
| Goalpost stability | Low; conservative positions have shifted from accepting climate science and supporting cap-and-trade (McCain-Lieberman 2003, Waxman-Markey 2009 with some GOP support) to near-universal denial or dismissal; when market-based solutions are proposed, goalposts shift to cost concerns; when costs are addressed, goalposts shift to competitiveness; when border adjustments are proposed, goalposts shift to sovereignty |
| Zero-sum behavior | Significant; climate policy is treated as a proxy battle against government regulation, the environmental movement, and progressive politics broadly; reforms that align with conservative principles (carbon tax with regulatory rollback, nuclear energy expansion) are resisted when they come from political opponents |

**Justification:** Conservatives face a fundamental contradiction on climate policy. Their stated principles -- market efficiency, fiscal responsibility, national security, technological innovation -- all support some form of climate action. Unpriced carbon emissions are the largest market failure in human history. Climate disasters cost the federal government hundreds of billions in response and rebuilding. The Department of Defense identifies climate change as a threat multiplier. Yet the Republican Party has moved from bipartisan climate engagement in the 2000s to near-uniform opposition. The score of 2 reflects the degree to which conservative positions on climate are driven by fossil fuel industry influence, identity politics, and opposition to the environmental movement rather than consistent application of stated principles. When fossil fuel industry interests are removed (as in support for nuclear energy or clean energy manufacturing in red states), conservative engagement with climate-adjacent policy improves markedly.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Republican senators who supported cap-and-trade in 2008 reversed positions by 2010 under Tea Party pressure, not because the policy changed
- Heritage Foundation and AEI shifted from proposing market-based climate solutions to opposing all climate policy over two decades
- Red state Republican governors accept IRA clean energy investments while opposing the law that created them
- Conservative support for nuclear energy and carbon capture reflects genuine technology preference but also serves to delay renewable deployment
- Koch network spending against climate legislation ($100M+) correlates with Republican position shifts more than any change in scientific evidence

### Liberal Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 4/5 - Mostly Consistent**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Genuine commitment to addressing climate change through government action; consistent advocacy for emissions reductions, clean energy investment, and international engagement; clean energy sector is increasingly a Democratic economic constituency |
| Principle consistency | Social responsibility and environmental protection principles consistently applied; support for both regulation and market mechanisms reflects pragmatic engagement; support for the IRA's incentive-based approach demonstrates willingness to use conservative-friendly tools to achieve liberal goals |
| Goalpost stability | Stable; has consistently supported emissions reduction targets, clean energy standards, and international commitments; specific policy preferences have evolved (from cap-and-trade to clean electricity standard to IRA incentives) but the direction is consistent |
| Zero-sum behavior | Low; willingness to support nuclear energy, carbon capture, and permitting reform demonstrates pragmatic flexibility; IRA negotiations showed capacity for significant compromise while maintaining climate ambition |

**Justification:** Liberals maintain a mostly consistent position on climate policy rooted in acceptance of scientific consensus and commitment to government-led solutions. The IRA represents liberal climate policy at its most effective: substantial investment ($370 billion), market-based mechanisms (tax credits rather than mandates), and political durability (benefits distributed across red and blue states). The slight reduction from a perfect score reflects occasional tension between environmental goals and other priorities (permitting reform vs. environmental review, energy costs vs. clean energy transition speed) and the gap between stated ambition (50-52% reduction by 2030) and political willingness to impose costs on consumers to achieve it.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Democratic support for the IRA represented the largest climate investment in history, achieved through pragmatic compromise
- Biden administration's simultaneous support for climate action and record oil production reflects priority balancing
- Liberal support for permitting reform (despite environmental review concerns) shows pragmatic flexibility
- Democratic state legislators have advanced clean energy standards, EV mandates, and building electrification codes
- Center for American Progress and Brookings have published comprehensive climate policy frameworks

### Progressive Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 5/5 - Highly Consistent**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Climate crisis viewed as inseparable from economic inequality, racial injustice, and corporate power; every position flows from structural analysis of fossil fuel capitalism as both cause of and obstacle to climate action |
| Principle consistency | Systemic change principles uniformly applied: support for aggressive emissions targets flows from scientific consensus; support for public investment flows from rejection of market-only approaches; support for environmental justice flows from structural inequality analysis; opposition to fossil fuel subsidies flows from anti-corporate-power principles |
| Goalpost stability | Highly stable; has consistently demanded emissions reductions aligned with science, fossil fuel phase-out, and environmental justice since before these positions entered mainstream Democratic politics; Green New Deal framework has been consistent since 2019 |
| Zero-sum behavior | None on climate substance; frames climate action as win-win (jobs, health, justice, environment); does treat fossil fuel industry as adversary but this reflects the industry's documented role in obstruction |

**Justification:** Progressives have the most structurally coherent analysis of the climate crisis. Their framework -- that fossil fuel capitalism drives both the emissions causing the crisis and the political obstruction preventing solutions -- is directly supported by the root causes analysis documenting industry lobbying ($125M+ annually), think tank funding for denial, and the revolving door between industry and government. The progressive position that market mechanisms alone are insufficient is supported by three decades of failed voluntary and market-based approaches. The Green New Deal framework connecting climate action to jobs, justice, and public investment addresses multiple political economy barriers simultaneously. Every position follows from core principles, and positions have not shifted under political pressure.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Congressional Progressive Caucus climate platform has consistently demanded science-aligned targets
- Green New Deal resolution (2019) established a durable framework connecting climate, jobs, and justice
- Progressive advocacy for fossil fuel subsidy elimination predates the current crisis by decades
- Sunrise Movement and allied organizations maintain consistent demands regardless of political climate
- Progressive opposition to the IRA was principled (insufficient ambition, fossil fuel concessions) while still supporting final passage

### Libertarian Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 4/5 - Mostly Consistent**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Principled opposition to government intervention is genuine and applied consistently; climate policy opposition flows from anti-regulation and anti-tax principles, not from climate denial per se; many libertarians accept climate science while opposing government solutions |
| Principle consistency | Free market and individual liberty principles consistently applied: oppose carbon taxes as taxes, oppose mandates as coercion, oppose subsidies as market distortion; however, the principle of protecting property rights would logically support addressing pollution that damages others' property, creating internal tension on externalities |
| Goalpost stability | Stable; have consistently argued that markets, innovation, and price signals (when not distorted by subsidies) will drive energy transition more efficiently than government; position has not shifted significantly |
| Zero-sum behavior | Low; opposition to climate policy is principled, not partisan; would oppose subsidies for fossil fuels and renewables equally; apply same anti-regulation framework to all industries |

**Justification:** Libertarians bring a principled and internally consistent framework to climate policy, even when that framework produces conclusions at odds with the scientific consensus on urgency. Their identification of fossil fuel subsidies as market-distorting is analytically correct and shared by progressives (for different reasons). Their argument that regulatory barriers slow clean energy deployment is supported by the permitting reform discussion. The slight reduction from a perfect score reflects the fundamental tension between libertarian property rights principles (which should support correcting externalities that damage others' property) and libertarian anti-government principles (which oppose the policy tools needed to correct externalities). Most libertarian climate analysis resolves this tension by minimizing the externality rather than accepting the need for correction.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Cato Institute publications accept climate science while opposing government solutions
- Reason Foundation advocates for permitting reform and subsidy elimination for all energy sources
- Niskanen Center (libertarian-leaning) has argued for carbon pricing as a market correction, but this is a minority position
- Libertarian Party platform consistently opposes energy mandates, subsidies, and environmental regulation
- Some libertarian economists (e.g., Tyler Cowen, Alex Tabarrok) have argued for Pigouvian carbon taxes as consistent with market principles

#### Alternative Proposals

- Green New Deal: comprehensive public investment in renewable energy, public transit, building retrofits, and ecological restoration, funded by progressive taxation and deficit spending
- Public ownership of energy: nationalize fossil fuel companies, create public renewable energy utilities, community-owned energy cooperatives
- Climate reparations: substantial transfers to developing countries reflecting historical emissions responsibility
- Federal jobs guarantee for clean energy transition: every displaced fossil fuel worker guaranteed equivalent employment
- Ban new fossil fuel infrastructure: no new drilling, pipelines, or extraction permits on public or private land
- Free public transit and high-speed rail: eliminate transportation emissions through public investment rather than private vehicle subsidies
- Ecosocialist industrial policy: democratic planning of energy transition rather than market-driven adjustment

## Compromise Proposals

### Compromise 1: American Energy Security and Resilience Act

**Bridges:** Conservative + Liberal + Progressive + Populist + Centrist + Religious Right + Democratic Socialist

**Core Agreement:** Extreme weather costs the United States $150+ billion annually, and investing in infrastructure resilience, permitting reform for all energy sources, and community disaster preparedness reduces those costs regardless of one's position on emissions reduction. Energy security through domestic production of all types strengthens American independence.

**Policy Description:**

Federal legislation establishes a $10 billion annual American Energy Resilience Fund combining three components. First, a $5 billion Infrastructure Resilience Program upgrades critical infrastructure (grid hardening, flood protection, fire management, water systems) to withstand extreme weather, with grants distributed to states based on disaster risk, population, and infrastructure age. Federally funded infrastructure must meet forward-looking resilience standards. Second, a $3 billion Comprehensive Permitting Reform streamlines approvals for all energy projects -- renewables, transmission, nuclear, natural gas, and other domestic energy -- through statutory timelines (2 years maximum for major projects), coordinated federal review, and a dedicated permitting office. NEPA categorical exclusions are expanded for projects on previously disturbed land. Third, a $2 billion Community Resilience Program funds state and local disaster preparedness, including community resilience plans, emergency management upgrades, natural infrastructure restoration, and voluntary property buyouts in repeatedly flooded areas. Faith-based organizations and community groups are eligible grantees. No emissions reduction mandates or carbon pricing provisions are included. An independent advisory board (including engineers, scientists, emergency managers, and community representatives) guides investment priorities.

| Perspective | How This Addresses Their Concerns |
|-------------|-----------------------------------|
| Conservative | Infrastructure investment without emissions mandates; permitting reform benefits fossil fuel and nuclear; no new regulatory authority; state-administered grants |

| Progressive | Resilience investment protects frontline communities; natural infrastructure included; voluntary buyouts with community control; permitting reform accelerates renewables and transmission |
| Populist | Disaster preparedness protects working communities; permitting reform benefits all energy including fossil fuels; no climate mandates or carbon pricing; frames as national security |

| Religious Right | Community resilience protects congregations and faith communities; faith-based organizations eligible for grants; stewardship framing of infrastructure investment; no ideological requirements |
| Democratic Socialist | Resilience investment protects vulnerable communities; community program enables community-controlled planning; natural infrastructure investment; would prefer larger scale but supports directionally |

**Concessions:**

| Perspective | What They Give Up |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Conservative | Accept $10 billion in new federal spending; accept resilience standards for federal infrastructure |
| Liberal | Accept that legislation includes no emissions reduction mechanisms; accept permitting reform that benefits fossil fuel projects |
| Progressive | Accept that no emissions mandates or fossil fuel restrictions are included; accept permitting reform for fossil fuel projects; accept scale below what is needed |
| Populist | Accept significant federal spending program; accept resilience framing that implicitly acknowledges climate risk |
| Centrist | Accept that carbon pricing and emissions targets are excluded; accept permitting reform without differentiation between clean and fossil energy |
| Religious Right | Accept large government spending program |

- **Durability:** High; resilience investment and permitting reform are the least ideologically contentious climate-adjacent policies; disaster costs provide ongoing justification; benefits are distributed across red and blue states; the absence of emissions mandates or carbon pricing removes the primary triggers for partisan opposition
- **Implementation Path:** Bipartisan sponsorship from senators representing disaster-prone states (coastal, wildfire, flood); builds on IIJA infrastructure framework; permitting reform has active bipartisan negotiation in Congress; frame as disaster cost reduction and energy security, not climate policy

### Compromise 2: Clean Energy Innovation and Nuclear Expansion Act

**Bridges:** Conservative + Liberal + Centrist + Libertarian + Constitutionalist + Populist + Religious Right

**Core Agreement:** Advanced nuclear energy, clean energy innovation, and technology development can reduce emissions while maintaining grid reliability, creating domestic manufacturing jobs, and advancing American technological leadership -- without mandates, carbon pricing, or picking winners among technologies.

**Policy Description:**

Federal legislation establishes a $15 billion Advanced Nuclear Acceleration Program including: NRC licensing reform with statutory 3-year review timelines for advanced reactor designs and standardized design approvals; $5 billion for advanced nuclear demonstration projects (small modular reactors, molten salt, and other advanced designs) at Department of Energy sites and willing host communities; $3 billion for nuclear supply chain development including domestic fuel fabrication; and extension of the IRA production and investment tax credits for nuclear at parity with other clean energy sources. A $5 billion Clean Energy Innovation Fund supports ARPA-E expansion, national laboratory research, and university-industry partnerships in long-duration energy storage, green hydrogen, advanced geothermal, carbon capture, and direct air capture. Technology prizes of $1 billion incentivize breakthroughs in hard-to-decarbonize sectors (aviation fuels, industrial heat, cement). A $2 billion Fossil Fuel Subsidy Reform provision phases out five specific tax expenditures for oil and gas production (percentage depletion, intangible drilling costs, etc.) over 5 years, with savings redirected to the innovation fund and worker transition assistance for affected fossil fuel workers. All provisions are technology-neutral and do not include mandates, standards, or carbon pricing.

### Compromise 3: State Climate Action Framework with Federal Incentives

**Bridges:** Liberal + Progressive + Constitutionalist + Centrist + Democratic Socialist

**Core Agreement:** Climate action is most durable when driven by state innovation within a federal incentive framework. States have constitutional authority over energy, land use, and environmental regulation, and federal incentives can encourage ambitious state action without mandates that face constitutional challenge and political backlash.

**Policy Description:**

Federal legislation establishes a $10 billion annual State Climate Action Incentive Fund. States that adopt binding emissions reduction targets (minimum 50% below 2005 levels by 2035, net-zero by 2050) with implementation plans, environmental justice provisions, and annual reporting receive proportional grants based on population, emissions, and ambition. Grants may fund clean electricity deployment, building efficiency, transportation electrification, adaptation, and just transition programs at state discretion. States that adopt the Model State Climate Action Act framework or equivalent receive the full per-capita allocation. States without binding targets receive 25% of the per-capita allocation for voluntary clean energy and resilience programs. A $3 billion State Clean Electricity Incentive provides additional grants to states with clean electricity standards of 80% or higher by 2030, with technology-neutral definitions including renewables, nuclear, and CCS. Worker transition assistance of $2 billion annually is available to all states for communities experiencing fossil fuel employment decline, regardless of whether the state has adopted climate targets. An Interstate Climate Compact provision authorizes states to form regional emissions trading systems (building on RGGI and WCI precedents) with federal legal safe harbor. All provisions are incentive-based; no federal mandates are imposed. Environmental justice provisions require participating states to demonstrate that at least 40% of climate investment benefits flow to disadvantaged communities.

|-------------|-----------------------------------|
| Liberal | $15 billion annual federal investment incentivizes ambitious state action; environmental justice requirements ensure equity; interstate compacts enable regional coordination; technology-neutral approach includes all clean sources |
| Progressive | Binding state targets with environmental justice provisions create accountability; 40% disadvantaged community requirement; worker transition is unconditional; interstate compacts enable progressive states to lead; scale of investment is meaningful |
| Constitutionalist | State-led framework is constitutionally proper; federal incentives operate through voluntary spending power; no federal mandates; interstate compact authorization uses established constitutional mechanism; states retain full implementation discretion |
| Centrist | Evidence-based incentive design; state flexibility enables innovation; technology-neutral definitions; environmental justice adds equity dimension; cost-sharing between federal and state levels; interstate compacts enable regional markets |
| Democratic Socialist | Investment scale is meaningful; environmental justice provisions ensure equity; worker transition is unconditional; states can pursue public ownership within the framework; interstate compacts enable coordination; would prefer federal mandates but supports state action as complementary |

**Concessions:**

| Perspective | What They Give Up |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Liberal | Accept state-led rather than federal-mandate approach; accept that non-participating states will not be required to act; accept slower nationwide progress |
| Progressive | Accept incentive framework rather than federal mandates; accept technology neutrality including CCS and nuclear; accept that many states will not participate |
| Constitutionalist | Accept $15 billion in federal spending that incentivizes state policy adoption; accept that federal incentives implicitly set national climate standards |
| Centrist | Accept that voluntary framework will leave significant gaps; accept that some funding goes to states without targets; accept that interstate compacts may create trade barriers |
| Democratic Socialist | Accept state-by-state approach rather than national standards; accept technology neutrality; accept that conservative states may not participate; accept incentive rather than mandate framework |

**Viability Assessment:**

- **Coalition Size:** 5/9 perspectives (Conservative, Libertarian, Populist, and Religious Right are non-participants; Conservative and Populist oppose the spending scale and implicit climate targets; Libertarian opposes government spending; Religious Right is ambivalent)
- **High Consistency Ratio:** 5/5 coalition members rated 4+ consistency (Liberal, Progressive, Constitutionalist, Centrist, Democratic Socialist)
- **Durability:** Moderate to high; state-led approach insulates from federal political oscillation; states that adopt targets develop economic constituencies for clean energy; interstate compacts create durable regional frameworks; however, the 5-perspective coalition is narrower than the other compromises, and ambitious state action depends on political dynamics in individual states; the framework is most impactful if adopted by states representing a majority of US emissions
- **Implementation Path:** Federal legislation through bipartisan environmental committee; leverage existing state climate leadership (24+ states with targets); RGGI and WCI as proof of concept for interstate compacts; state-level campaigns to adopt model legislation; environmental justice community support for equity provisions

## Strategic Implications

### Most Viable Coalition

The most effective coalition for climate policy operates on three distinct tracks reflecting different levels of political feasibility. The broadest coalition (7-8 perspectives) can advance adaptation and resilience investment, comprehensive permitting reform, and nuclear energy expansion -- policies that avoid the mandate/regulation/pricing debates that define partisan climate warfare. These "pre-competitive" climate policies create the infrastructure (literally and politically) for more ambitious action. A center-right-to-center-left coalition (Conservative, Liberal, Centrist, Libertarian, Constitutionalist, Populist, Religious Right) can advance a Clean Energy Innovation and Nuclear Expansion Act that reduces emissions through technology development without mandates. A center-left coalition (Liberal, Progressive, Centrist, Constitutionalist, Democratic Socialist) can advance state-level climate frameworks with federal incentives, leveraging the 24+ states that already have climate targets.

### Key Obstacles

- **Identity polarization:** Climate policy is more deeply embedded in partisan identity than almost any other policy domain; addressing stated concerns (cost, reliability, competitiveness) often does not secure conservative or populist support because opposition is identity-based rather than policy-based; the gap between stated and actual motivations is largest here
- **Fossil fuel industry influence:** $125M+ in annual lobbying and $100M+ in campaign contributions create a structural obstacle that cannot be addressed through policy design alone; the industry's strategy of funding doubt, delay, and division has been effective for decades and continues
- **Asymmetric urgency:** The atmosphere does not negotiate; delayed action has compounding costs; this creates a structural disadvantage for compromise-oriented approaches that move slower than the science demands but faster than politics allows
- **Institutional veto points:** The filibuster, major questions doctrine, and federalism create multiple veto points for federal climate legislation; the IRA passed through reconciliation only because it fit within budget rules; comprehensive climate legislation faces structural legislative barriers
- **Cost distribution:** Climate policies impose visible, immediate costs (higher energy prices, job displacement) while producing diffuse, long-term benefits (avoided disasters, health improvement, energy savings); this asymmetry advantages opponents

### Low Consistency Partners

Conservative (2/5) and Populist (2/5) perspectives rate at low consistency on climate policy, the lowest ratings in this analysis. This reflects the degree to which both perspectives' climate positions are driven by fossil fuel industry alignment, cultural identity, and opposition dynamics rather than principled engagement with the policy substance. Conservative engagement is most reliable on nuclear energy expansion, permitting reform, and clean energy innovation -- areas where climate action aligns with existing conservative priorities (energy independence, technology leadership, deregulation). Populist engagement is most accessible when climate-adjacent policies are framed as disaster preparedness, energy security, and domestic manufacturing rather than climate action per se.

The Religious Right (3/5) represents the most potentially movable partner among right-of-center perspectives. The creation care theological framework provides a principled basis for climate engagement that is independent of the secular policy debate. Young evangelicals show significantly greater climate concern than older generations, suggesting a generational shift that may strengthen stewardship-based engagement. However, activating Religious Right climate engagement requires framing through faith values (stewardship, protecting the vulnerable, moral obligation) rather than scientific authority or government mandates.

### Recommended Approach

1. **Lead with resilience and permitting reform:** The American Energy Security and Resilience Act (Compromise 1) commands 7/9 perspective support by focusing on disaster cost reduction and energy security rather than emissions reduction; this builds physical infrastructure and political infrastructure for climate action simultaneously

2. **Advance nuclear and innovation:** The Clean Energy Innovation and Nuclear Expansion Act (Compromise 2) delivers emissions reductions through technology that 7/9 perspectives support; nuclear expansion, NRC reform, and clean energy innovation are the areas of greatest bipartisan potential; fossil fuel subsidy reform is politically difficult but becomes feasible when packaged with popular nuclear and innovation provisions

3. **Pursue ambitious action through states:** The State Climate Action Framework (Compromise 3) leverages the 24+ states that already have climate targets and enables progressive states to lead without requiring federal mandates that face structural opposition; interstate compacts expand the geographic reach of state action

4. **Maintain IRA implementation:** The Inflation Reduction Act's incentive-based approach is producing results in red and blue states; protecting and extending IRA provisions should be a priority regardless of federal legislative dynamics; the economic constituencies created by IRA investments (clean energy manufacturers, EV plants, solar installers in red states) will build durable political support

5. **Separate climate from culture war:** Every effort should be made to frame climate-adjacent policies through energy security, disaster cost reduction, technology leadership, and domestic manufacturing rather than through environmental ideology; "clean energy jobs" resonates more broadly than "fighting climate change"

6. **Build creation care bridge:** Invest in the creation care movement as a pathway for Religious Right engagement; faith-based climate advocacy that frames environmental stewardship through biblical values rather than scientific authority can move a constituency that is otherwise locked into the conservative coalition

7. **Accept asymmetric progress:** The political reality is that comprehensive climate legislation faces structural obstacles that adaptation, permitting reform, nuclear expansion, and state-level action do not; the recommended approach delivers less than the science demands but more than political confrontation has achieved; incremental progress through bipartisan channels, combined with ambitious state-level action, may deliver more cumulative emissions reductions than repeated attempts at comprehensive federal legislation that fail
