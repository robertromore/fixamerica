# Data Brokers: Actions

## Overview

While systemic reform of the data broker industry requires legislative and regulatory action, individuals and communities can take meaningful steps to protect their privacy, raise awareness, and build political pressure for change. This document provides practical actions at every level -- from personal data protection to organized political advocacy.

## Individual Actions

### Immediate Steps (This Week)

1. **Search for yourself on people-search sites**: Check Spokeo.com, BeenVerified.com, WhitePages.com, Intelius.com, and MyLife.com to see what information is publicly available about you
2. **Opt out of major people-search sites**: Use the Privacy Rights Clearinghouse opt-out guide (privacyrights.org/data-brokers) to submit removal requests to the largest brokers
3. **Review app permissions on your phone**: Remove location access, contact access, and other permissions from apps that do not need them
4. **Install a tracker-blocking browser extension**: Use uBlock Origin, Privacy Badger, or similar tools to block advertising trackers
5. **Switch to a privacy-respecting search engine**: DuckDuckGo, Brave Search, or Startpage do not track searches

### Ongoing Personal Protection

| Action | Difficulty | Impact | Cost |
|--------|-----------|--------|------|
| Use a VPN for internet browsing | Easy | Medium | $3-10/month |
| Opt out of data broker sites regularly (they re-list you) | Medium (time-intensive) | Medium | Free (or $5-15/month for opt-out service) |
| Use a data removal service (DeleteMe, Kanary, Privacy Duck) | Easy | Medium-High | $100-200/year |
| Limit social media sharing | Easy | Medium | Free |
| Use separate email addresses for different purposes | Medium | Medium | Free |
| Avoid loyalty programs and store discount cards | Easy | Low-Medium | Loss of discounts |
| Pay with cash for sensitive purchases | Easy | Low | Free |
| Use a burner phone number (Google Voice, MySudo) | Medium | Medium | Free-$5/month |
| Freeze your credit at all three bureaus | Easy | High (prevents credit fraud) | Free |
| Review and restrict privacy settings on all accounts | Medium (time-intensive) | Medium | Free |

## Community Actions

### Local Organizing

- **Host a privacy workshop**: Teach community members how to opt out of data brokers and protect their data; Privacy Rights Clearinghouse provides materials
- **Create a mutual aid opt-out group**: Coordinate with neighbors to help each other through the opt-out process, especially for elderly or less tech-savvy community members
- **Work with domestic violence organizations**: Help local DV shelters understand data broker risks and assist survivors with data removal
- **Partner with immigrant rights organizations**: Educate immigrant communities about how ICE uses data broker data and available protective measures
- **Engage local libraries**: Libraries can host privacy education programs and provide resources for data protection

### Institutional Advocacy

- **Urge your employer to minimize data broker use**: Advocate for ethical data procurement policies in your workplace
- **Push local government to restrict data broker purchases**: Advocate for city/county ordinances requiring warrants for government data broker purchases (model: San Francisco surveillance oversight ordinance)
- **Engage your school district**: Advocate for student data protection policies that restrict sharing with data brokers
- **Work with your church, synagogue, or mosque**: Faith communities can organize around the ethical dimensions of surveillance and data exploitation

## Political Actions

### Contact Your Representatives

**Federal Level**:

- Call your US Senators and Representative to support comprehensive data broker legislation
- Specifically ask them to: (1) support federal data broker registration, (2) support warrant requirements for government data purchases, (3) support a private right of action for data privacy violations, and (4) oppose preemption of stronger state laws

**State Level**:

- If your state does not have a data broker registration law, advocate for one (model: California, Vermont)
- If your state has a comprehensive privacy law, advocate for strengthening data broker provisions
- Support state AG enforcement of existing privacy and consumer protection laws against data brokers

### Template Email to Federal Representatives

> Subject: Support Comprehensive Data Broker Regulation
>
> Dear [Senator/Representative] [Name],
>
> I am writing as your constituent to urge you to support comprehensive federal regulation of the data broker industry.
>
> Data brokers collect and sell detailed personal information on virtually every American, often without our knowledge or consent. This information includes financial data, health inferences, location histories, and political affiliations. Government agencies purchase this data to circumvent Fourth Amendment warrant requirements. The result is a surveillance infrastructure that threatens our constitutional rights and personal autonomy.
>
> I urge you to support legislation that would:
>
> 1. Require all data brokers to register with a federal agency and disclose their data practices
> 2. Require government agencies to obtain warrants before purchasing personal data from brokers
> 3. Grant consumers enforceable rights to access, correct, and delete their data
> 4. Ban the sale of sensitive data categories (health, location, children's data) without explicit consent
> 5. Include a private right of action so consumers can hold violators accountable
> 6. Not preempt stronger state privacy protections
>
> I look forward to hearing your position on this important issue.
>
> Sincerely,
> [Your Name]
> [Your Address]

## Talking Points

### For Conversations with Friends and Family

- **"Did you know companies you've never heard of have detailed profiles on you -- including your health conditions, political views, and daily movements?"** Most Americans (66%) are unaware data brokers exist (Annenberg, 2023).
- **"Government agencies buy our personal data from these companies instead of getting a warrant."** ICE, CBP, IRS, FBI, and other agencies have documented purchases of location data, financial data, and more.
- **"Opting out doesn't really work."** Even if you spend hours opting out, brokers re-list you from new data sources within months.
- **"Other countries already have strong protections."** The EU's GDPR gives people real control over their data. The US can do the same.
- **"This isn't about having something to hide -- it's about having control."** Privacy is a fundamental right, not a privilege for those with nothing to fear.

### For Conversations with Elected Officials

- **Economic argument**: The current system creates billions in costs from fraud, identity theft, and discrimination; regulation creates net economic benefits
- **Constitutional argument**: Government data purchases circumvent the Fourth Amendment; this is a core constitutional issue
- **Bipartisan appeal**: Both liberals (civil liberties) and conservatives (government overreach, personal liberty) have reasons to support reform
- **Public support**: 79% of Americans are concerned about data collection (Pew, 2023); this is not a fringe issue
- **State momentum**: 19+ states have acted; federal government is falling behind

## Common Misconceptions

| Misconception | Reality |
|--------------|---------|
| "I have nothing to hide, so data collection doesn't affect me" | Data broker profiles affect credit decisions, insurance rates, employment, and housing -- even if you are not doing anything wrong. Inaccurate data causes real harm. |
| "Opting out permanently removes my data" | Most brokers re-list consumers from new data sources within months. Opt-out is temporary and must be repeated. |
| "Data brokers only have public records" | Brokers combine public records with purchase data, app data, location data, health inferences, and behavioral data from hundreds of non-public sources. |
| "Privacy regulations would destroy the internet" | The EU's GDPR has been in effect since 2018 and the European internet functions normally. Privacy and innovation coexist. |
| "The government needs this data for security" | The government can obtain data with warrants supported by probable cause. The Fourth Amendment is not optional for convenience. |
| "My data is anonymized so it can't identify me" | Studies consistently show that "anonymized" datasets can be re-identified. Location data for just four data points identifies 95% of individuals (de Montjoye et al., *Scientific Reports*, 2013). |
| "Only tech-savvy people need to worry about this" | Data brokers collect information on virtually every American adult regardless of their technology use. Public records, financial transactions, and loyalty programs all feed broker databases. |
