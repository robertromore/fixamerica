# Data Brokers: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

This document presents draft federal and state legislation to regulate the data broker industry. The legislative approach addresses four core problems: (1) the absence of mandatory registration and transparency requirements for data brokers, (2) the lack of enforceable consumer data rights, (3) the government's ability to purchase personal data without a warrant, and (4) the unrestricted sale of sensitive data categories. The legislation is designed to survive constitutional challenge, including First Amendment scrutiny under *Sorrell v. IMS Health*, 564 U.S. 552 (2011), by targeting conduct (commercial data transactions) rather than speech.

## Federal Legislation

### The Data Broker Accountability and Transparency Act

**Purpose**: Establish a comprehensive federal framework for data broker registration, transparency, consumer rights, and enforcement.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Data Broker Accountability and Transparency
Act".

SEC. 2. DEFINITIONS.

(a) DATA BROKER.--The term "data broker" means a commercial entity that
knowingly collects, aggregates, sells, licenses, or otherwise provides
to third parties the personal data of individuals with whom the entity
does not have a direct consumer relationship, where such collection,
aggregation, sale, licensing, or provision constitutes a significant
part of the entity's business.

(b) PERSONAL DATA.--The term "personal data" means any information that
identifies, relates to, describes, is reasonably capable of being
associated with, or could reasonably be linked, directly or indirectly,
with a particular individual or household.

(c) SENSITIVE DATA.--The term "sensitive data" means personal data that
includes--
    (1) precise geolocation data (within 1,000 feet);
    (2) health data, including data from which health conditions,
        diagnoses, treatments, or disabilities may be inferred;
    (3) biometric data used for identification purposes;
    (4) data revealing sexual orientation or gender identity;
    (5) data pertaining to an individual known to be under the age of 17;
    (6) Social Security numbers, driver's license numbers, or passport
        numbers;
    (7) financial account numbers or access credentials;
    (8) data revealing religious beliefs or affiliation;
    (9) data revealing racial or ethnic origin; and
    (10) immigration status.

(d) DIRECT CONSUMER RELATIONSHIP.--The term "direct consumer
relationship" means a relationship in which the individual knowingly
and intentionally interacts with the entity to obtain a product, service,
or benefit from the entity.

(e) COMMISSION.--The term "Commission" means the Federal Trade Commission.

SEC. 3. DATA BROKER REGISTRATION.

(a) REGISTRATION REQUIRED.--Not later than 180 days after the date of
enactment of this Act, every data broker operating in the United States
or processing the personal data of individuals located in the United
States shall register with the Commission.

(b) REGISTRATION INFORMATION.--Each registration shall include--
    (1) the legal name and all trade names of the data broker;
    (2) the categories of personal data collected, aggregated, or sold;
    (3) the sources from which personal data is obtained;
    (4) the categories of third parties to whom personal data is sold,
        licensed, or provided;
    (5) the purposes for which personal data is collected and sold;
    (6) the data broker's data retention and deletion policies;
    (7) the data broker's data security practices;
    (8) a description of the opt-out mechanism available to consumers;
        and
    (9) the number of individuals whose personal data the broker holds.

(c) ANNUAL RENEWAL.--Registrations shall be renewed annually, and
updated within 30 days of any material change.

(d) PUBLIC REGISTRY.--The Commission shall maintain a publicly
searchable registry of all registered data brokers.

(e) REGISTRATION FEE.--The Commission shall establish a registration
fee schedule scaled to the annual revenue of the data broker, with
proceeds deposited into the Data Privacy Enforcement Fund established
under Section 8.

SEC. 4. CONSUMER DATA RIGHTS.

(a) RIGHT OF ACCESS.--Every individual shall have the right to obtain,
upon request and at no charge, a complete accounting of--
    (1) the personal data held by a data broker about that individual;
    (2) the sources of such data;
    (3) the categories of third parties to whom the data has been sold,
        licensed, or provided; and
    (4) the purposes for which the data has been used or sold.

(b) RIGHT OF CORRECTION.--Every individual shall have the right to
require a data broker to correct inaccurate personal data. Corrections
shall be propagated to all entities to which the data was provided
within the preceding 12 months.

(c) RIGHT OF DELETION.--Every individual shall have the right to require
a data broker to delete all personal data pertaining to that individual.
Deletion shall be completed within 30 days and the data broker shall
not re-acquire the individual's data from the same sources.

(d) RIGHT OF PORTABILITY.--Every individual shall have the right to
receive their personal data in a structured, commonly used, and
machine-readable format.

(e) CENTRALIZED MECHANISM.--The Commission shall establish a centralized
portal through which individuals may exercise the rights under this
section with respect to all registered data brokers through a single
request.

(f) PROHIBITION ON RETALIATION.--A data broker may not retaliate against
an individual for exercising any right under this section, including by
denying services, charging higher prices, or providing diminished quality.

SEC. 5. SENSITIVE DATA PROTECTIONS.

(a) PROHIBITION.--No data broker may collect, sell, license, or otherwise
provide to a third party any sensitive data about an individual without
the affirmative, informed, specific, and freely given consent of that
individual.

(b) CONSENT REQUIREMENTS.--Consent under this section must be--
    (1) obtained through a clear and conspicuous disclosure separate from
        any other agreement or terms of service;
    (2) specific to each category of sensitive data and each category of
        intended use;
    (3) revocable at any time, with revocation taking effect within 30
        days; and
    (4) documented and retained by the data broker for the duration of
        data processing plus 3 years.

(c) PROHIBITION ON SALE OF MINORS' DATA.--No data broker may sell,
license, or otherwise provide to a third party the personal data of any
individual known to be under the age of 17 without the verified
affirmative consent of a parent or legal guardian.

SEC. 6. DATA MINIMIZATION AND PURPOSE LIMITATION.

(a) MINIMIZATION.--A data broker shall not collect or retain personal
data beyond what is reasonably necessary and proportionate to the
purposes for which the data is collected, as disclosed in the broker's
registration under Section 3.

(b) PURPOSE LIMITATION.--A data broker shall not use or sell personal
data for purposes materially different from those disclosed at the time
of collection without obtaining new consent from the individual.

(c) RETENTION LIMITS.--The Commission shall promulgate regulations
establishing maximum retention periods by data category. In no event
shall a data broker retain personal data for longer than 3 years after
the data was last used for a disclosed purpose.

SEC. 7. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) FTC ENFORCEMENT.--A violation of this Act shall be treated as a
violation of a rule defining an unfair or deceptive act or practice
prescribed under section 18(a)(1)(B) of the Federal Trade Commission
Act (15 U.S.C. 57a(a)(1)(B)). The Commission shall have authority to
enforce this Act using all available remedies.

(b) CIVIL PENALTIES.--
    (1) IN GENERAL.--Any data broker that violates this Act shall be
        subject to a civil penalty of not more than $50,000 per
        violation per day.
    (2) AGGRAVATED VIOLATIONS.--For knowing or reckless violations, the
        penalty shall be not more than $100,000 per violation per day.
    (3) SENSITIVE DATA.--For violations involving sensitive data, the
        penalty shall be not less than $10,000 per violation per day.

(c) PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.--
    (1) IN GENERAL.--Any individual whose rights under this Act are
        violated may bring a civil action in any court of competent
        jurisdiction.
    (2) STATUTORY DAMAGES.--A prevailing plaintiff shall be entitled to
        the greater of--
        (A) actual damages; or
        (B) statutory damages of not less than $100 and not more than
            $1,000 per violation.
    (3) ATTORNEY'S FEES.--The court shall award reasonable attorney's
        fees and costs to a prevailing plaintiff.
    (4) CLASS ACTIONS.--Actions may be maintained as class actions under
        Rule 23 of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure.
    (5) SAFE HARBOR.--A data broker that demonstrates good-faith
        compliance with this Act, including maintenance of a reasonable
        compliance program, shall not be liable for statutory damages
        under this subsection for unintentional violations.

(d) STATE ATTORNEY GENERAL ENFORCEMENT.--The attorney general of any
State may bring a civil action on behalf of the residents of that State
to enforce this Act.

SEC. 8. DATA PRIVACY ENFORCEMENT FUND.

There is established in the Treasury a fund to be known as the "Data
Privacy Enforcement Fund," to be funded by registration fees collected
under Section 3 and civil penalties collected under Section 7, and to
be used by the Commission exclusively for enforcement of this Act.

SEC. 9. NO PREEMPTION OF STRONGER STATE PROTECTIONS.

(a) Nothing in this Act shall be construed to preempt, supersede, or
limit any State law that provides greater protection to individuals
than the protections provided by this Act.

(b) This Act establishes a floor, not a ceiling, for data privacy
protections.

SEC. 10. EFFECTIVE DATE.

(a) Sections 1, 2, and 3 shall take effect 180 days after the date of
enactment.
(b) Sections 4, 5, and 6 shall take effect 1 year after the date of
enactment.
(c) Section 7 shall take effect 1 year after the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill creates a comprehensive federal framework addressing registration, consumer rights, sensitive data protections, data minimization, and enforcement. The non-preemption clause (Section 9) allows states to maintain or enact stronger protections. The private right of action (Section 7(c)) provides individual enforcement while the safe harbor protects good-faith compliance.

**Potential Challenges**:

- First Amendment challenge under *Sorrell v. IMS Health*: The bill targets commercial conduct (data transactions) rather than speech, but courts may apply heightened scrutiny
- Industry lobbying for weakening amendments, particularly on preemption and private right of action
- Definitional disputes over "data broker," "direct consumer relationship," and "sensitive data"
- Implementation costs for centralized portal

**Refinements**:

- Include explicit findings linking data broker regulation to legitimate government interests (consumer protection, fraud prevention, national security) to support constitutional defense
- Phase in requirements to allow compliance preparation
- Provide small business exemptions for entities below a revenue or data volume threshold

### The Fourth Amendment Is Not For Sale Act

**Purpose**: Require government agencies to obtain warrants before purchasing personal data from data brokers.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Fourth Amendment Is Not For Sale Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that--
    (1) the Fourth Amendment to the Constitution protects the right of
        the people to be secure against unreasonable searches and
        seizures;
    (2) government agencies increasingly purchase personal data from
        commercial data brokers, including location data, financial
        data, and communications metadata, that would otherwise require
        a warrant or court order to obtain;
    (3) in Carpenter v. United States, 585 U.S. 296 (2018), the Supreme
        Court recognized that commercial data can reveal the "privacies
        of life" protected by the Fourth Amendment;
    (4) the purchase of personal data from commercial sources to
        circumvent constitutional protections is inconsistent with the
        purposes of the Fourth Amendment; and
    (5) warrant requirements do not prevent legitimate law enforcement
        and intelligence activities but ensure judicial oversight.

SEC. 3. WARRANT REQUIREMENT FOR GOVERNMENT DATA PURCHASES.

(a) PROHIBITION.--No element of the Federal Government, and no State or
local government entity receiving Federal funds, may purchase, obtain
through contract, or otherwise acquire from a data broker the personal
data of any individual located in the United States without first
obtaining--
    (1) a warrant issued by a court of competent jurisdiction upon
        probable cause, supported by oath or affirmation, and
        particularly describing the data to be obtained; or
    (2) the voluntary, informed, and documented consent of the individual
        whose data is being acquired.

(b) COVERED DATA.--This section applies to all personal data as defined
in section 2(b) of the Data Broker Accountability and Transparency Act,
including but not limited to--
    (1) geolocation data;
    (2) communications metadata;
    (3) financial transaction records;
    (4) biometric data;
    (5) health-related data;
    (6) internet browsing or search history; and
    (7) data derived from or associated with a specific device.

(c) EXCEPTIONS.--This section does not apply to--
    (1) data that is lawfully made available to the general public by a
        Federal, State, or local government entity (e.g., public court
        records, property records);
    (2) emergency circumstances involving imminent threat to life,
        provided that a warrant is sought within 72 hours; or
    (3) data provided voluntarily and directly to the government entity
        by the individual to whom the data pertains.

(d) SUPPRESSION REMEDY.--Personal data obtained in violation of this
section shall not be admissible in any court proceeding, and no evidence
derived therefrom shall be admissible.

(e) TRANSPARENCY.--Each Federal agency shall publish an annual report
disclosing the number of warrants obtained under this section, the
types of data obtained, the categories of investigations involved,
and the total expenditure on data broker purchases.

SEC. 4. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 90 days after the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill closes the constitutional loophole that allows government agencies to purchase data that would otherwise require a warrant. It applies broadly to all government entities and includes a suppression remedy to deter violations.

**Potential Challenges**:

- Intelligence community opposition: Will argue that warrant requirement impedes national security operations
- Scope disputes: Government will argue some purchased data (e.g., aggregated statistics) should be exempt
- Foreign intelligence carve-out pressure: Expect attempts to exempt Section 702-adjacent activities

**Refinements**:

- Include a classified annex process for FISA Court warrant applications involving national security data
- Ensure emergency exception is narrow and subject to after-the-fact judicial review

## State Model Legislation

### Model State Data Broker Registration and Consumer Rights Act

**Purpose**: Provide a template for states that do not yet have data broker registration requirements.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Data Broker Registration and
Consumer Rights Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.

(a) "Data broker" means a business that knowingly collects and sells,
licenses, or otherwise provides to third parties the personal
information of consumers with whom the business does not have a direct
relationship.

(b) "Personal information" means information that identifies, relates
to, or could reasonably be linked to a particular individual.

(c) "Sensitive personal information" means personal information that
reveals precise geolocation, health conditions, biometric identifiers,
sexual orientation, data pertaining to a minor, Social Security numbers,
or financial account numbers.

SECTION 3. REGISTRATION.

(a) Every data broker doing business in this State or processing the
personal information of residents of this State shall register annually
with the [Secretary of State / Attorney General].

(b) Registration shall include: legal name and trade names; categories
of data collected and sold; data sources; categories of purchasers;
opt-out procedure description; and data security practices.

(c) The registration shall be publicly available on the [Secretary of
State's / Attorney General's] website.

(d) Registration fee: $100 for businesses with annual revenue under
$25 million; $500 for businesses with annual revenue $25 million or
above.

SECTION 4. CONSUMER RIGHTS.

(a) RIGHT OF ACCESS.--A consumer may request and obtain from any
registered data broker a copy of all personal information held about
that consumer.

(b) RIGHT OF DELETION.--A consumer may request deletion of their
personal information. Deletion shall be completed within 45 days.

(c) RIGHT OF OPT-OUT.--A consumer may direct a data broker to cease
selling or sharing their personal information.

(d) CENTRALIZED OPT-OUT.--The [Secretary of State / Attorney General]
shall establish a centralized portal through which consumers may
exercise rights under this section with respect to all registered
data brokers.

SECTION 5. SENSITIVE DATA.

No data broker shall sell, license, or provide to a third party any
sensitive personal information without the affirmative consent of the
consumer.

SECTION 6. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) The Attorney General may bring actions to enforce this Act.

(b) Civil penalties: up to $10,000 per violation per day for failure
to register; up to $25,000 per violation per day for violations
involving sensitive data.

(c) Private right of action: Any consumer may bring a civil action
for violations of this Act, with statutory damages of $100-$750 per
violation.

SECTION 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect [180 days after enactment].
```

**Adaptations**: States with existing comprehensive privacy laws may integrate data broker provisions into those frameworks. States without existing privacy laws may use this as a standalone measure.

## Regulatory Framework

### FTC Commercial Surveillance Rulemaking

**Existing Authority**: Section 5 of the FTC Act (unfair or deceptive practices); Advance Notice of Proposed Rulemaking published August 2022.

**Draft Regulatory Focus Areas**:

1. **Data broker disclosure requirements**: Require detailed public disclosures of data practices under Section 5 unfairness authority
2. **Consent standards**: Define minimum standards for meaningful consent in data broker transactions
3. **Dark patterns prohibition**: Prohibit manipulative design practices that undermine consumer opt-out
4. **Data security requirements**: Establish minimum security standards for data brokers
5. **Algorithmic auditing**: Require brokers using AI inference to audit for discriminatory outcomes

### CFPB Data Broker Rulemaking

**Existing Authority**: Fair Credit Reporting Act (15 U.S.C. ss 1681 et seq.); Dodd-Frank Act authority.

**Proposed Action**: CFPB rulemaking to clarify that data brokers selling consumer profiles used for eligibility decisions (credit, insurance, employment, housing) are "consumer reporting agencies" subject to FCRA obligations, regardless of how they characterize their products.

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

**Fourth Amendment**:

- *Carpenter v. United States* (2018) established that the Fourth Amendment protects digital data that reveals "the privacies of life," even when shared with third parties
- Government data purchases remain constitutionally unsettled; the warrant requirement bill addresses this gap legislatively
- The third-party doctrine (*Miller*, *Smith*) has not been overruled but is under increasing pressure

**First Amendment**:

- *Sorrell v. IMS Health* (2011) applied heightened scrutiny to a Vermont law restricting data sales
- The proposed legislation addresses this by regulating commercial conduct (data transactions) rather than speech content
- Courts have upheld FCRA, HIPAA, and GLBA restrictions on data use under intermediate scrutiny
- The legislation includes substantial government interests (consumer protection, prevention of fraud and discrimination, national security) to satisfy any heightened scrutiny

### Preemption Questions

- The federal legislation explicitly provides that it establishes a "floor, not a ceiling" (Section 9)
- States may enact stronger protections
- This approach mirrors environmental law (Clean Air Act) and consumer protection (state lemon laws)
- Industry will push for ceiling preemption; this must be resisted

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- **FTC enforcement**: Civil penalties up to $50,000-$100,000 per violation per day
- **State AG enforcement**: Parallel enforcement authority
- **Private right of action**: Statutory damages, class actions, attorney's fees
- **Safe harbor**: Good-faith compliance provides protection against statutory damages

### Sunset and Review Provisions

- The Commission shall conduct a comprehensive review of the effectiveness of this Act every 5 years
- Data broker definitions shall be reviewed every 3 years to account for technological change
- Maximum retention periods shall be reviewed every 3 years

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Data Broker Accountability and Transparency Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Definition evasion | Companies restructure to claim "direct consumer relationship" through token interactions | Critical |
| De-identification dodge | Brokers claim data is "de-identified" but it can be readily re-identified | High |
| Offshore processing | Brokers process data outside US jurisdiction to avoid registration | High |
| Consent theater | Complex consent flows that technically satisfy requirements but do not produce informed consent | High |
| Corporate restructuring | Brokers create subsidiaries to avoid revenue thresholds or registration triggers | Medium |
| Data sharing vs. selling | Brokers characterize sales as "sharing" to evade coverage | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Centralized portal technical complexity | May take years to build and may not cover all brokers effectively | Technology development challenges |
| Enforcement resource constraints | FTC historically under-resourced for privacy enforcement | Congressional appropriations politics |
| Inference gap | Sensitive data can be inferred from non-sensitive data, evading sensitive data restrictions | Technical limitations of categorical regulation |
| Re-acquisition problem | Brokers can rebuild deleted profiles from new data sources | Interconnected data ecosystem |
| Small broker compliance burden | Small legitimate businesses may face disproportionate compliance costs | Uniform requirements regardless of scale |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Functional definition test**: Define "data broker" by function (what the entity *does* with data), not by corporate structure or self-description. Include "functional equivalents" language.
2. **Re-identification prohibition**: Prohibit attempts to re-identify de-identified data; create presumption that data is "personal data" unless proven otherwise by industry-standard de-identification methodology.
3. **Extraterritorial application**: Apply to any entity that processes data of US residents, regardless of where processing occurs (GDPR model).
4. **Consent design standards**: FTC to promulgate specific consent design standards, including plain language requirements, maximum consent request frequency, and prohibition on dark patterns.
5. **Tiered compliance**: Create simplified compliance requirements for small businesses (under $5 million revenue, under 50,000 consumer records).
6. **Anti-circumvention clause**: Prohibit any action taken with the purpose of evading the requirements of this Act.

### Fourth Amendment Is Not For Sale Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Intelligence community carve-out | Agencies claim national security exemption not in statute | Critical |
| Contractor intermediaries | Government uses contractors to purchase data, claiming contractor is not a government entity | High |
| "Publicly available" data expansion | Agencies argue that data sold commercially is "publicly available" | High |
| Emergency exception abuse | Broad use of emergency exception without after-the-fact judicial review | Medium |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **No implied exemptions**: Explicitly state that no agency, including intelligence agencies, is exempt absent specific statutory exception.
2. **Contractor coverage**: Define "element of the Federal Government" to include any contractor, grantee, or other entity acting on behalf of or at the direction of a federal agency.
3. **Narrow "publicly available" definition**: Limit to data published by government entities in their official capacity; exclude commercially aggregated data.
4. **Emergency reporting requirement**: Require written certification of emergency circumstances, judicial review within 72 hours, and annual reporting of all emergency exceptions used.

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| Regulatory arbitrage between federal and state systems | Federal floor with state freedom to exceed; interagency coordination |
| Technology outpacing regulation | Dynamic definitions updated by agency rulemaking every 3 years |
| Industry consolidation in response to regulation | Antitrust coordination; monitoring of M&A activity post-enactment |
| Consumer "consent fatigue" from multiple consent requests | Centralized consent management infrastructure; limit consent requests |
| Inequitable enforcement (large brokers have legal resources) | Private right of action equalizes enforcement; class actions |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- 5-year comprehensive review of Act effectiveness
- 3-year review of definitions and thresholds
- Annual agency report on enforcement actions, registration compliance, and consumer rights exercise
- GAO study 3 years post-enactment on economic impact
