# Data Brokers: Overview

## Executive Summary

Data brokers are companies that collect, aggregate, analyze, and sell personal information about individuals, often without the knowledge or meaningful consent of those being profiled. This multi-billion-dollar industry operates in a regulatory vacuum, assembling detailed dossiers on nearly every American that include financial records, health inferences, location histories, purchasing behaviors, political affiliations, and social connections. The result is a shadow surveillance infrastructure that rivals government intelligence capabilities but operates with virtually no oversight, accountability, or transparency.

The data broker industry sits at the intersection of corporate surveillance and government power. Brokers acquire information from hundreds of sources -- public records, loyalty programs, social media, mobile apps, internet browsing, financial transactions, and other data brokers -- then package and resell it to advertisers, employers, landlords, insurers, law enforcement, and intelligence agencies. When government agencies purchase commercial data, they effectively circumvent Fourth Amendment protections that would otherwise require a warrant. This government-commercial data pipeline represents one of the most significant threats to constitutional privacy in the digital age.

Despite broad public opposition to unconsented data collection and several high-profile data breaches involving broker firms, the industry has successfully resisted comprehensive federal regulation. Only a handful of states have enacted data broker registration requirements, and none has imposed the kind of substantive restrictions that would fundamentally alter the business model. The European Union's General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) demonstrates that stronger regulation is feasible, but the United States lacks the political will to overcome industry lobbying and the fragmented regulatory landscape that currently governs personal data.

### Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Data broker** | A company whose primary business involves collecting and selling personal information about individuals with whom it has no direct relationship |
| **Data aggregation** | The process of combining personal information from multiple sources into comprehensive profiles |
| **People-search site** | A consumer-facing data broker that provides public records and personal information lookups |
| **Real-time bidding (RTB)** | Automated advertising auctions that expose personal data to thousands of companies in milliseconds |
| **Data enrichment** | Adding additional data points to an existing consumer profile by cross-referencing multiple databases |
| **Opt-out** | A consumer's request to have their data removed from a broker's database, often temporary and burdensome |
| **Shadow profile** | A data profile compiled about an individual who has not directly provided information to the collecting entity |

## Core Tensions

- **Information economy vs. privacy**: Data fuels digital advertising and personalization, but unchecked collection erodes autonomy
- **Transparency vs. security**: Exposing broker practices informs consumers but may reveal intelligence methods
- **Federal standards vs. state innovation**: National law provides consistency but may preempt stronger state protections
- **Free speech vs. data regulation**: Brokers claim First Amendment protection for information they publish
- **Convenience vs. consent**: Consumers benefit from data-driven services but rarely understand the costs
- **Government need for data vs. constitutional limits**: Agencies seek intelligence but the Constitution constrains warrantless surveillance

## Vision of Success

A reformed data broker landscape would feature:

- **Mandatory registration and transparency**: All data brokers publicly registered with detailed disclosures about data types, sources, and customers
- **Affirmative consent requirements**: Individuals must opt in before their data is collected and sold, not bear the burden of opting out
- **Government purchase restrictions**: Warrant requirements for government acquisition of commercial data that would otherwise require a warrant to obtain directly
- **Individual data rights**: Enforceable rights to access, correct, delete, and port personal data held by brokers
- **Accountability mechanisms**: An empowered federal agency with enforcement authority, meaningful penalties for violations, and a private right of action
- **Data minimization**: Brokers limited to collecting and retaining only data necessary for disclosed purposes
- **Prohibition on sensitive data sales**: Bans on selling health data, precise location data, sexual orientation data, and data on minors without explicit consent
