# Data Brokers: Roadmap

## Implementation Strategy

Reform of the data broker industry requires a phased approach that builds political momentum, establishes institutional infrastructure, and progressively strengthens protections. The strategy prioritizes measures with bipartisan support and low political risk in early phases, building toward more transformative reforms as public support and enforcement capacity grow.

## Phase 1: Foundation (Years 1-2)

### Objective

Establish transparency, close the government data purchase loophole, and protect the most sensitive data categories.

### Action Table

| Action | Lead Actor | Timeline | Dependencies | Priority |
|--------|-----------|----------|--------------|----------|
| Enact Federal Data Broker Registration Act | Congress | Year 1 | Bipartisan sponsorship | Critical |
| Pass Government Data Purchase Warrant Requirement | Congress | Year 1-2 | Bipartisan coalition (Wyden-Paul model) | Critical |
| Enact Sensitive Data Sale Prohibition | Congress | Year 1-2 | Registration act provides foundation | High |
| Expand FTC enforcement budget for data broker actions | Congress (appropriations) | Year 1 | None | High |
| States continue enacting registration and privacy laws | State legislatures | Ongoing | None | High |
| FTC finalizes commercial surveillance rulemaking | FTC | Year 1-2 | Existing rulemaking authority | Medium |
| Launch public education campaign on data broker practices | FTC, NGOs | Year 1 | FTC funding | Medium |
| International coordination on data broker regulation | Commerce Department, State Department | Year 1-2 | None | Medium |

### Success Metrics for Phase 1

| Metric | Baseline (2025) | Target (End of Year 2) | Data Source |
|--------|-----------------|----------------------|-------------|
| Registered data brokers (federal) | 0 (no federal registry) | 2,000+ | Federal registry |
| States with data broker registration laws | 5 | 10+ | NCSL, IAPP |
| Government data purchase warrants issued | 0 (no warrant requirement) | Warrant requirement enacted | Federal courts |
| FTC enforcement actions against brokers (annual) | 3-5 | 10+ | FTC enforcement database |
| Public awareness of data brokers | 34% (Annenberg, 2023) | 50%+ | Polling |

### Key Risks in Phase 1

| Risk | Probability | Impact | Mitigation |
|------|------------|--------|------------|
| Industry lobbying defeats registration bill | Medium | High | Build bipartisan coalition; leverage state momentum |
| Intelligence community blocks warrant requirement | Medium-High | High | Classified briefings for members; bipartisan privacy caucus |
| Federal bill preempts stronger state laws | Medium | High | Federal floor language; oppose preemption provisions |
| FTC rulemaking challenged in court | Medium | Medium | Ensure statutory authority; careful Administrative Procedure Act compliance |

## Phase 2: Comprehensive Framework (Years 2-4)

### Objective

Establish enforceable individual data rights, create dedicated enforcement capacity, and require data minimization.

### Action Table

| Action | Lead Actor | Timeline | Dependencies | Priority |
|--------|-----------|----------|--------------|----------|
| Enact Federal Individual Data Rights Act (access, correction, deletion, portability) | Congress | Year 2-3 | Phase 1 registration provides infrastructure | Critical |
| Establish Federal Data Protection Agency (or enhanced FTC authority) | Congress | Year 2-4 | Political will; appropriations | Critical |
| Implement centralized federal opt-out portal (Delete Act model) | Federal agency | Year 3-4 | Registration act; agency establishment | High |
| Enact data minimization and purpose limitation requirements | Congress | Year 3-4 | Enforcement agency operational | High |
| Begin CFPB rulemaking to bring data brokers under FCRA | CFPB | Year 2-3 | CFPB authority affirmed | Medium |
| Negotiate international data protection agreements | Commerce/State | Year 2-4 | Federal framework established | Medium |
| Fund state AG enforcement capacity | Congress (appropriations) | Year 2 | None | Medium |
| Require data broker annual transparency reports | Federal agency | Year 3 | Agency operational | Medium |

### Success Metrics for Phase 2

| Metric | Baseline (End of Phase 1) | Target (End of Year 4) | Data Source |
|--------|--------------------------|----------------------|-------------|
| Consumer data rights requests (annual) | 0 (no federal right) | 10+ million | Federal portal |
| Deletion requests fulfilled | 0 | 5+ million | Federal portal; broker reports |
| Federal privacy agency staffing | 0 | 500+ FTE | Agency budget |
| Enforcement actions (federal + state, annual) | 10+ | 50+ | Agency reports; state AG offices |
| Average data points per consumer broker profile | 1,500+ (FTC, 2014) | Measurable reduction | Agency audits |
| Data broker industry compliance rate | Unknown | 80%+ registered brokers in compliance | Agency audits |

### Key Risks in Phase 2

| Risk | Probability | Impact | Mitigation |
|------|------------|--------|------------|
| New federal agency captured by industry | Medium | Very High | Strong independence provisions; diverse appointments; congressional oversight |
| Centralized portal technically fails | Low-Medium | High | Phased rollout; learn from California Delete Act implementation |
| Industry challenges data rights law as unconstitutional | Medium | High | Draft to survive First Amendment scrutiny; distinguishing *Sorrell* |
| Political leadership change blocks implementation | Medium | High | Embed reforms in statute, not executive order; bipartisan support |

## Phase 3: Full Implementation (Years 4-6)

### Objective

Implement opt-in consent requirements, establish private right of action, and achieve comprehensive reform comparable to GDPR.

### Action Table

| Action | Lead Actor | Timeline | Dependencies | Priority |
|--------|-----------|----------|--------------|----------|
| Enact opt-in consent requirement for data collection and sale | Congress | Year 4-5 | Enforcement infrastructure operational; public support | Critical |
| Establish private right of action with statutory damages | Congress | Year 4-5 | Federal framework in place; judicial system readiness | Critical |
| Implement automated consent management infrastructure | Federal agency, industry | Year 5-6 | Technical standards developed | High |
| Achieve GDPR adequacy determination for US-EU data flows | Commerce Department, EU Commission | Year 5-6 | Comprehensive US framework | High |
| Mandate algorithmic auditing for broker inference systems | Federal agency | Year 5-6 | Agency rulemaking authority; technical capacity | Medium |
| Review and strengthen enforcement based on first years of operation | Congress, Federal agency | Year 6 | Operational experience | Medium |

### Success Metrics for Phase 3

| Metric | Baseline (End of Phase 2) | Target (End of Year 6) | Data Source |
|--------|--------------------------|----------------------|-------------|
| Consumer consent rate for data broker collection | N/A (no consent requirement) | Opt-in rates by data category tracked | Agency reports |
| Data broker industry size | $200+ billion | Measurable reduction in surveillance-based revenue | Industry reports |
| Private enforcement actions filed (annual) | 0 | Measurable litigation activity | Federal court filings |
| GDPR adequacy status | Not applicable (no EU adequacy) | Adequacy determination achieved | EU Commission |
| Consumer trust in data practices | 21% feel in control (Pew, 2023) | 50%+ feel in control | Polling |
| Data breaches at broker firms (annual) | Multiple major incidents | Measurable reduction | Federal agency reports |

### Key Risks in Phase 3

| Risk | Probability | Impact | Mitigation |
|------|------------|--------|------------|
| Opt-in consent leads to "consent fatigue" (meaningless clicking) | Medium | Medium | Design standards for meaningful consent; limit consent requests |
| Frivolous litigation under private right of action | Low-Medium | Medium | Safe harbor for good-faith compliance; judicial gatekeeping |
| Industry restructures to evade definitions | Medium | Medium | Broad definitions; functional test (not entity-based); agency authority to update |
| International compliance conflicts | Low | Medium | Mutual recognition agreements; harmonization efforts |
