# Data Brokers: History

## Overview

The data broker industry did not emerge overnight. It evolved over more than a century, from early mailing list companies and credit reporting agencies to the global digital surveillance infrastructure that exists today. Each era brought new technologies, new data sources, and new business models -- but the regulatory framework never kept pace. Understanding this history reveals how the industry systematically expanded its reach while eroding the privacy norms that once constrained it.

---

## Era 1: Credit Reporting and Mailing Lists (1841-1969)

### Origins of Commercial Data Collection

The American data broker industry traces its roots to the 19th century credit reporting industry. The Mercantile Agency, founded in 1841 by Lewis Tappan, compiled information about businesses' creditworthiness by hiring correspondents across the country. It became R.G. Dun & Co. and eventually merged with its rival Bradstreet to form Dun & Bradstreet in 1933.

Consumer credit reporting followed a parallel path. Local "retail credit bureaus" emerged in the early 1900s, maintaining files on individual consumers. The Retail Credit Company (founded 1899, later renamed Equifax in 1975) compiled dossiers that included not just financial information but rumors about lifestyle, morals, and political activities.

### The Mailing List Industry

Direct mail marketing created the first mass-market data broker industry:

- **1920s-1930s**: Companies began compiling and selling mailing lists based on magazine subscriptions, catalog purchases, and voter registration records
- **1940s-1950s**: List brokers became a distinct industry, connecting data compilers with direct mailers
- **1960s**: Computer technology enabled the first large-scale consumer databases; Acxiom (then Demographics Inc., founded 1969) began digitizing consumer records

### Key Developments

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 1841 | Mercantile Agency founded | First commercial data collection firm in the US |
| 1899 | Retail Credit Company (Equifax) founded | Origins of consumer credit reporting |
| 1933 | Dun & Bradstreet formed | Consolidation of business credit data |
| 1956 | First commercial computer for data processing (UNIVAC) | Enabled mass data aggregation |
| 1969 | Demographics Inc. (later Acxiom) founded | First dedicated consumer data aggregation company |

## Era 2: Regulatory Response and Database Marketing (1970-1995)

### The Fair Credit Reporting Act

Public concern about the intrusiveness of credit bureau files -- which contained subjective assessments of character, drinking habits, and sexual behavior -- led to the first major federal privacy law:

- **1970**: Congress passed the **Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA)**, giving consumers the right to access and dispute their credit reports and limiting who could access them
- **Limitation**: FCRA applied only to "consumer reporting agencies" producing "consumer reports" used for credit, employment, or insurance decisions. Companies that sold data for marketing purposes fell outside its scope

This distinction -- between regulated "consumer reports" and unregulated "marketing data" -- created the loophole that the modern data broker industry would exploit for the next five decades.

### The Rise of Database Marketing

| Year | Development | Impact |
|------|-------------|--------|
| 1970 | FCRA enacted | Created regulated/unregulated data distinction |
| 1974 | Privacy Act | Limited federal government data collection (but not private sector) |
| 1976 | *United States v. Miller* | Supreme Court held no reasonable expectation of privacy in bank records shared with third parties (third-party doctrine) |
| 1979 | *Smith v. Maryland* | Extended third-party doctrine to phone call metadata |
| 1980s | Personal computer revolution | Enabled smaller companies to build consumer databases |
| 1986 | Electronic Communications Privacy Act | Addressed wiretapping but left data broker activity unregulated |
| 1988 | Video Privacy Protection Act | Narrow response to Robert Bork's video rental records being leaked |
| 1989 | Acxiom InfoBase launched | First comprehensive, commercially available consumer database with 200+ million records |
| 1993 | Metromail sells consumer database (including SSNs) to direct marketers | Early public controversy over data broker practices |

### Failed Regulatory Efforts

Several attempts to regulate data brokers beyond the FCRA framework failed during this period:

- **1974 Privacy Act**: Applied only to federal agencies, not the private sector
- **Privacy Protection Study Commission (1977)**: Recommended extending fair information practices to the private sector; recommendations were not enacted
- **Various congressional hearings (1980s-1990s)**: Raised concerns about database marketing but produced no legislation

## Era 3: The Internet Transforms the Industry (1996-2012)

### From Mailing Lists to Digital Profiles

The internet revolution fundamentally changed the data broker industry by creating vast new data sources and enabling real-time data collection at unprecedented scale.

| Year | Development | Impact |
|------|-------------|--------|
| 1996 | Telecommunications Act | Included limited data provisions but did not address brokers |
| 1998 | COPPA enacted | Protected children's data online, but narrow scope |
| 1999 | Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act | Required financial institutions to disclose data sharing; did not restrict downstream brokers |
| 2000 | DoubleClick cookie tracking controversy | Revealed scale of online behavioral tracking |
| 2001 | USA PATRIOT Act | Expanded government access to commercial data for counterterrorism |
| 2003 | ChoicePoint data breach exposed (163,000 records) | First major data broker breach to gain national attention |
| 2005 | ChoicePoint sold consumer data to identity thieves | FTC fined ChoicePoint $10 million; led to breach notification laws |
| 2007 | iPhone launched | Began era of continuous mobile location data generation |
| 2008 | Facebook opens platform to third-party apps | Created massive new data pipeline exploited by brokers |
| 2010 | Real-time bidding (RTB) advertising ecosystem matures | Billions of ad auctions daily expose personal data to thousands of companies |
| 2012 | FTC report "Protecting Consumer Privacy in an Era of Rapid Change" | Called for data broker legislation; Congress took no action |

### The Post-9/11 Expansion

The September 11 attacks dramatically expanded the intersection of government surveillance and commercial data:

- **Data broker contracts with government surged**: Acxiom, ChoicePoint, LexisNexis, and others won major government contracts to provide consumer data for counterterrorism screening
- **Total Information Awareness (TIA)**: DARPA's 2002 proposal to mine commercial data for terrorist patterns was defunded after public backlash, but similar programs continued under different names
- **Fusion centers**: Federal-state-local intelligence centers established after 9/11 became major consumers of commercial data broker products
- **National security letters**: FBI dramatically expanded use of NSLs to obtain financial records, phone records, and other data without judicial oversight

### The People-Search Explosion

The internet enabled a new category of data broker: the people-search site.

- **Spokeo (2006)**, **BeenVerified (2007)**, **Intelius**, and dozens of others made individual lookup services available to anyone for a few dollars
- These sites compiled public records, social media data, and commercial data into searchable profiles
- Domestic violence advocates warned that people-search sites endangered victims by making it easy for abusers to locate them
- FTC sued Spokeo in 2012 for FCRA violations, but the core people-search business model remained legal

## Era 4: Big Data, Mobile, and the Surveillance Economy (2013-2020)

### The Snowden Revelations and Commercial Data

Edward Snowden's 2013 disclosures about NSA surveillance programs revealed the deep connections between government intelligence and commercial data:

- NSA's PRISM program collected data from major tech companies, not traditional data brokers
- But the revelations highlighted how government agencies also purchased commercial data to supplement their surveillance capabilities
- Public debate focused primarily on government surveillance; the commercial data broker industry largely escaped scrutiny

### Mobile Data Transforms the Industry

| Year | Development | Impact |
|------|-------------|--------|
| 2013 | Snowden revelations | Public focus on government surveillance, less on commercial brokers |
| 2014 | FTC report "Data Brokers: A Call for Transparency and Accountability" | Documented nine major brokers holding 1.4 billion transactions and 700 billion data elements; called for legislation |
| 2015 | Location data brokers emerge as distinct category | Firms like Placed, Factual, SafeGraph begin selling mobile location data |
| 2016 | Cambridge Analytica harvests Facebook data | Revealed how commercial data enables political manipulation |
| 2017 | **Equifax breach exposes 147 million Americans** | Largest data broker breach in history; SSNs, birth dates, addresses compromised |
| 2018 | EU GDPR takes effect | First comprehensive regulation affecting data brokers globally |
| 2018 | Vermont enacts first US data broker registration law | Required data brokers to register with Secretary of State |
| 2018 | California passes CCPA | First comprehensive US state privacy law; required data brokers to register |
| 2019 | New York Times "One Nation, Tracked" investigation | Revealed that location data brokers could track individual movements with alarming precision |

### The Equifax Watershed

The 2017 Equifax breach was a defining moment for the data broker industry:

- **147 million Americans** had Social Security numbers, birth dates, and addresses exposed
- Equifax executives sold stock before disclosing the breach
- The FTC reached a $700 million settlement in 2019 -- the largest data breach settlement in history at the time
- Despite widespread outrage, Congress did not pass comprehensive data broker legislation in response

## Era 5: Government Purchases, AI, and State Action (2021-Present)

### The Government Data Purchase Scandal

Beginning in 2020, a series of investigations revealed the extent to which government agencies were purchasing commercial data to circumvent warrant requirements:

| Year | Revelation | Source |
|------|-----------|--------|
| 2020 | CBP and ICE purchasing cell phone location data from Venntel | Sen. Ron Wyden investigation; *The Wall Street Journal* |
| 2020 | IRS purchasing cell phone location data from Venntel | *The Wall Street Journal* |
| 2021 | DIA acknowledging purchase of commercial location data on Americans | DIA memo obtained by *The New York Times* |
| 2022 | Georgetown Law "American Dragnet" report documents systematic federal data purchases | Comprehensive FOIA-based investigation |
| 2023 | DNI declassified report acknowledging intelligence community purchases of commercial data | ODNI, "Senior Advisory Group Report on Commercially Available Information" |
| 2024 | Executive Order 14117 restricting bulk data transfers to countries of concern | White House executive action |

### State Legislative Wave

| Year | State Action | Significance |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| 2023 | California "Delete Act" (SB 362) | Created centralized opt-out mechanism for all registered data brokers |
| 2023 | Texas data broker registration law | Third state to require broker registration |
| 2024 | Oregon data broker registration law | Fourth state with registration requirement |
| 2024 | New Jersey Data Broker Transparency Act | Fifth state with registration |
| 2024 | FTC begins rulemaking on commercial surveillance | Potential federal rules (ongoing) |
| 2025 | 19+ states with comprehensive privacy laws | Growing patchwork touching broker activities |

### AI and Inference

Artificial intelligence is transforming data brokerage by enabling brokers to infer sensitive information from seemingly innocuous data:

- Health conditions inferred from purchase patterns, app usage, and location data
- Political beliefs predicted from consumer behavior
- Sexual orientation and gender identity inferred from browsing and app data
- Financial vulnerability scores generated from behavioral patterns
- "Lookalike" audiences enabling targeting of people similar to known populations

| Date | Event | Why It Mattered |
|------|-------|-----------------|
| 1970 | FCRA enacted | Created the regulated/unregulated data distinction that data brokers exploit to this day |
| 1976 | *United States v. Miller* | Third-party doctrine eliminated Fourth Amendment protection for data shared with businesses |
| 2001 | USA PATRIOT Act | Government became a major customer of data brokers |
| 2005 | ChoicePoint scandal | First public reckoning with data broker practices |
| 2014 | FTC data broker report | Documented industry scope; Congress failed to act |
| 2017 | Equifax breach | 147 million Americans compromised; still no comprehensive legislation |
| 2018 | GDPR takes effect | Demonstrated that comprehensive data regulation is feasible |
| 2018 | Vermont registration law | First US state to require data broker registration |
| 2020 | Government data purchase revelations | Exposed constitutional end-run via commercial data |
| 2023 | California Delete Act | Most ambitious US attempt to centralize consumer opt-out |

## Lessons from History

1. **Regulation consistently lags technology by decades**: The FCRA responded to 1960s-era credit reporting problems; no comparable law has addressed 21st-century data brokerage
2. **Narrow laws create exploitable gaps**: The FCRA's distinction between "consumer reports" and "marketing data" became the foundation for an unregulated industry
3. **Crises do not guarantee reform**: The Equifax breach, Cambridge Analytica scandal, and government purchase revelations all failed to produce comprehensive federal legislation
4. **Industry self-regulation has never worked**: Every industry pledge to self-regulate data practices has proven inadequate
5. **State action fills federal vacuums**: Vermont, California, and other states have moved because Congress has not
6. **Government demand sustains the industry**: Federal agencies are among the largest and most politically protected customers of data brokers
7. **Third-party doctrine enables commercial surveillance**: The Supreme Court's 1976 Miller decision remains the legal foundation allowing warrantless data collection
8. **International models prove reform is possible**: The EU's GDPR demonstrates that comprehensive data broker regulation is technically and economically feasible
