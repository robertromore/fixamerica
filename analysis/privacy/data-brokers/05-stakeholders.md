# Data Brokers: Stakeholders

## Overview

The data broker ecosystem involves a complex web of stakeholders with competing interests. Those who profit from the status quo wield disproportionate political and economic power, while those who bear the costs -- ordinary consumers, vulnerable populations, and civil liberties -- lack the concentrated organizational structure to effectively counter industry influence. Understanding these dynamics is essential for building reform coalitions.

## Primary Stakeholders

### Those Harmed by the Status Quo

| Stakeholder | How They Are Affected | Scale of Impact | Awareness Level |
|-------------|----------------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **General public (all consumers)** | Data collected and sold without meaningful consent; privacy eroded | 230+ million American adults profiled (Duke University, 2023) | Low -- 34% aware data brokers exist (Annenberg, 2023) |
| **Domestic violence survivors** | People-search sites enable abusers to locate victims | 10+ million DV victims annually (NCADV, 2023) | Moderate among advocates |
| **Immigrant communities** | ICE and CBP purchase commercial data for enforcement | 11+ million undocumented immigrants at risk (Pew, 2023) | Growing awareness |
| **Minority communities** | Data profiles enable discriminatory targeting in housing, lending, employment | Disproportionate impact documented (Brookings, 2023) | Moderate |
| **Financially vulnerable consumers** | Predatory targeting based on financial stress indicators | Millions targeted for payday loans, scams | Low |
| **Job seekers** | Background check errors and data broker inaccuracies lead to denial of employment | 30%+ error rate in background checks (NELP, 2021) | Low-moderate |
| **Renters** | Tenant screening reports from brokers contain errors, enable discrimination | Millions of rental applications affected annually | Moderate |
| **People with mental health conditions** | Health inferences sold by brokers; potential for discrimination | Mental health data readily available (Duke, 2023) | Low |
| **Political dissidents and activists** | Surveillance profiles chill political speech and organizing | Unknown scope | Moderate among activists |
| **Children and minors** | Data collected despite COPPA; used to profile and target | 50+ million minors with some data in broker systems (est.) | Low |

### Those Who Benefit from the Status Quo

| Stakeholder | How They Benefit | Economic Interest |
|-------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| **Data broker companies** | Primary revenue from collecting and selling consumer data | $200+ billion US market (IBISWorld, 2024) |
| **Digital advertising industry** | Broker data enables targeted advertising | $280 billion US ad spending (IAB, 2024) |
| **Tech platforms** | Both data sources and customers of brokers | Multi-trillion-dollar market cap |
| **Government intelligence agencies** | Purchase data to supplement surveillance without warrants | $200+ million in annual contracts (USASpending.gov) |
| **Law enforcement agencies** | Access to commercial data for investigations | Thousands of agencies with broker subscriptions |
| **Insurance companies** | Granular data enables risk pricing | Improved loss ratios at consumer expense |
| **Political campaigns** | Voter microtargeting via enriched voter files | $14.4 billion in 2024 cycle political spending (AdImpact) |
| **Financial institutions** | Fraud detection and risk assessment using broker data | Reduced fraud losses; increased marketing ROI |
| **Employers** | Background screening via data brokers | Reduced perceived hiring risk |
| **Landlords** | Tenant screening via data broker reports | Reduced perceived rental risk |

## Secondary Stakeholders

| Stakeholder | Interest | Position |
|-------------|----------|----------|
| **Privacy researchers and academics** | Study data practices; advocate for reform based on evidence | Generally pro-regulation; provide expert testimony |
| **Cybersecurity professionals** | Data broker databases are high-value targets for hackers | Concerned about breach risk; support data minimization |
| **Journalists** | Use data broker tools for investigations; also subject to surveillance | Mixed -- value access but concerned about press freedom |
| **Small businesses** | Use broker data for marketing but also subject to data collection | Mixed -- need affordable marketing tools but want fair competition |
| **Foreign governments** | Some seek to acquire Americans' data for intelligence | Adversarial interest in blocking reform; allies may support reform |
| **International businesses** | Must navigate different US and EU data rules | Support harmonization; prefer weaker US standards |
| **State attorneys general** | Primary enforcers of state privacy laws | Generally pro-enforcement; but resource-constrained |
| **Trial lawyers** | Potential to litigate under private right of action | Strongly support private right of action provisions |

## Decision Makers

| Decision Maker | Authority | Current Position | Influence Level |
|----------------|-----------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **US Congress** | Can enact comprehensive federal data broker law | Deadlocked over preemption and private right of action | Decisive -- but paralyzed |
| **Federal Trade Commission** | Can pursue unfair/deceptive practices; potential rulemaking authority | Active enforcement; proposed surveillance rulemaking | Significant but limited by statutory authority |
| **State legislatures** | Can enact state registration, privacy, and data broker laws | Increasingly active (19+ states with privacy laws) | Growing -- filling federal vacuum |
| **State attorneys general** | Enforce state consumer protection and privacy laws | Several active (CA, IL, TX, others) | Moderate -- case-by-case |
| **Supreme Court** | Can expand or contract Fourth Amendment protections | *Carpenter* (2018) narrowly expanded; future uncertain | Decisive for constitutional questions |
| **White House / Executive Branch** | Executive orders, agency directives | EO 14117 (2024) on bulk data transfers; limited domestic scope | Moderate -- cannot substitute for legislation |
| **CFPB** | Authority over financial data broker practices under FCRA | Active rulemaking on data broker FCRA coverage | Growing -- could bring brokers into FCRA framework |
| **Intelligence community** | Advises on national security implications of data restrictions | Opposes broad restrictions on data purchases | Significant behind-the-scenes influence |

## Coalition Mapping

### Pro-Reform Coalition

```text
Core Coalition
├── Civil Liberties Groups (ACLU, EFF, EPIC, CDT)
├── Consumer Organizations (Consumer Reports, PIRG)
├── Domestic Violence Advocates (NNEDV)
├── Racial Justice Organizations (Color of Change)
└── Academic Researchers (Georgetown, Duke, Princeton)

Potential Allies
├── State Attorneys General (bipartisan enforcement interest)
├── Libertarian/Conservative Privacy Advocates (opposition to government surveillance)
├── Small Business (unfair competition from data monopolies)
├── Cybersecurity Industry (data minimization reduces breach risk)
└── International Trade Advocates (GDPR-compatible US law enables data flows)

Conditional Allies
├── Trial Lawyers (support contingent on private right of action)
├── Some Tech Companies (Apple positions as privacy-forward)
└── Financial Institutions (support fraud prevention but resist data restrictions)
```

### Anti-Reform Coalition

```text
Core Opposition
├── Data Broker Industry (Acxiom, Experian, LexisNexis, etc.)
├── Digital Advertising Industry (IAB, ANA, Google, Meta)
├── US Chamber of Commerce
└── Industry Trade Associations

Institutional Opposition
├── Intelligence Community (opposes restrictions on data purchases)
├── Law Enforcement Agencies (oppose warrant requirements for purchased data)
└── Some Federal Agencies (resist oversight of data procurement)

Conditional Opposition
├── Some Tech Companies (support weak federal law to preempt strong state laws)
├── Political Campaigns (both parties use broker data for targeting)
└── Financial Services (support some regulation but resist broad restrictions)
```

## Influence Mechanisms

### How Industry Maintains the Status Quo

| Mechanism | Description | Evidence |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| **Lobbying** | Direct advocacy to Congress and agencies | $70+ million annually from top 10 tech companies (OpenSecrets, 2024) |
| **Campaign contributions** | Financial support for sympathetic legislators | $150+ million per election cycle from data-intensive sectors |
| **Revolving door** | Former regulators and Hill staff move to industry | Documented at FTC, Commerce Committee, and elsewhere |
| **Astroturfing** | Industry-funded groups presenting as consumer-friendly | "Consumer" groups funded by industry opposing privacy legislation |
| **Complexity strategy** | Framing regulation as technically complex and economically harmful | Industry white papers emphasizing compliance costs and innovation harm |
| **Preemption leverage** | Playing federal vs. state regulation against each other | Simultaneously opposing federal law and using its absence to argue against state laws |
| **National security appeal** | Invoking security concerns to shield data practices | Intelligence community testimony against data purchase restrictions |
| **First Amendment litigation** | Challenging data regulations as speech restrictions | *Sorrell v. IMS Health* (2011) and subsequent challenges |

### How Advocates Push for Reform

| Mechanism | Description | Effectiveness |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| **Research and reports** | Documenting industry practices and harms | High -- FTC, Georgetown, Duke reports shift debate |
| **FOIA requests** | Uncovering government data purchases | High -- revelations drive congressional inquiries |
| **Litigation** | Challenging broker practices under existing law | Moderate -- limited legal tools available |
| **State advocacy** | Winning state-level reforms | High -- 19+ states have acted |
| **Public education** | Raising consumer awareness of data practices | Growing but still limited reach |
| **Congressional testimony** | Expert testimony in hearings | Moderate -- provides evidence but does not guarantee action |
| **Coalition building** | Uniting diverse stakeholders around reform | Essential but challenging across ideological lines |
| **International pressure** | GDPR creating global norms | Moderate -- US has resisted international convergence |

## Power Dynamics Summary

| Factor | Reform Advocates | Industry / Status Quo |
|--------|------------------|----------------------|
| **Financial resources** | Limited (nonprofit budgets) | Vast ($200B+ industry) |
| **Political access** | Moderate (some congressional allies) | Extensive (revolving door, donations) |
| **Public opinion** | Strong (79% concerned about data collection) | Weak (industry positions unpopular) |
| **Institutional allies** | State AGs, some agencies (CFPB) | Intelligence community, law enforcement |
| **Legal tools** | Limited (no comprehensive federal law) | Extensive (First Amendment, preemption arguments) |
| **Expertise** | Strong (researchers, former regulators) | Strong (industry lawyers, compliance teams) |
| **Urgency** | Diffuse (no single galvanizing crisis) | Low urgency to change (profitable status quo) |
