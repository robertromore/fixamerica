# International Privacy: Opposition

## Overview

Opposition to aligning US privacy law with international standards comes from several powerful constituencies. Some oppose comprehensive privacy legislation entirely, while others oppose specific features that would be necessary for international compatibility. Understanding the arguments, motivations, and strategies of these opponents is critical for developing effective reform strategies.

---

## Major Opposition Groups

### Technology and Advertising Industry

**Who:** Major technology companies (particularly advertising-supported), digital advertising trade associations (IAB, ANA), data broker industry

**Stated Arguments:**

- Comprehensive privacy regulation would stifle innovation and harm American competitiveness
- GDPR-style regulation has reduced innovation and economic growth in Europe
- Privacy regulation disproportionately burdens small businesses
- American consumers benefit from data-driven services and would lose value from strict regulation
- The US notice-and-choice model respects consumer autonomy

**Underlying Motivations:**

- Advertising-supported business models depend on extensive data collection and profiling
- Data broker revenue would decline under consent-based frameworks
- Compliance costs would reduce profit margins
- Strong privacy rights would give consumers tools to limit the data ecosystem
- A private right of action would create litigation risk

**Strategies:**

- Lobby against comprehensive legislation; support narrow, industry-friendly bills
- Promote preemption to eliminate stronger state laws while ensuring federal law is weak
- Fund think tanks producing research framing regulation as economically harmful
- Propose self-regulatory frameworks as alternatives to legislation
- Use technical complexity to slow legislative processes

**Counterarguments:**

- GDPR has not destroyed the European tech economy; European companies comply and compete
- Innovation can occur within privacy constraints; privacy-enhancing technologies are a growing sector
- The US notice-and-choice model has failed -- consumers cannot meaningfully consent to thousands of data collection practices
- Small business exemptions can be designed into comprehensive legislation
- International compliance costs are already high because of fragmented state laws; a single federal standard could reduce costs

### National Security Establishment

**Who:** NSA, CIA, FBI, DOJ National Security Division, DHS, congressional intelligence committees

**Stated Arguments:**

- FISA Section 702 is essential for counterterrorism and counterintelligence
- Reforming surveillance authorities to satisfy European courts would create intelligence gaps
- The "going dark" problem -- encrypted communications and restricted data access would prevent detection of threats
- Privacy reforms could benefit adversaries (China, Russia) by limiting US intelligence capabilities
- National security must take precedence over commercial data flow considerations

**Underlying Motivations:**

- Institutional interest in maintaining broad surveillance authorities
- Resistance to oversight and transparency that reform would require
- Bureaucratic inertia: existing programs are built around current authorities
- Government purchases of commercial data provide a cost-effective complement to traditional intelligence methods

**Strategies:**

- Classify arguments to prevent public debate
- Brief congressional leaders in closed sessions on alleged intelligence value
- Invoke terrorism and national security threats during reform debates
- Use the "going dark" narrative to shift the burden to reform advocates
- Ensure intelligence committee members oppose privacy reforms

**Counterarguments:**

- The President's Review Group on Intelligence (2013) found that Section 702 had not prevented any terrorist attack that could not have been prevented through targeted surveillance
- Proportionality and necessity requirements -- as imposed by EO 14086 -- can be codified without eliminating surveillance capabilities
- Mass surveillance creates false positives and diverts resources from targeted investigation
- The intelligence community's resistance to oversight is itself a reason for stronger legislative controls
- Allied nations' intelligence agencies operate effectively under stronger privacy constraints

### States' Rights Advocates (Anti-Preemption)

**Who:** State attorneys general (particularly California), state legislators, consumer advocacy organizations concerned about preemption

**Stated Arguments:**

- State privacy laws (especially CCPA/CPRA) are stronger than any proposed federal law
- Federal preemption would eliminate hard-won state protections
- States serve as "laboratories of democracy" for privacy innovation
- Federal preemption benefits industry, not consumers

**Underlying Motivations:**

- States with strong laws want to preserve their regulatory advantage
- State AGs want to maintain enforcement authority
- Consumer advocates fear that federal preemption would be a net loss if the federal law is weaker than existing state laws

**Strategies:**

- Oppose any federal privacy bill that includes broad preemption
- Advocate for federal "floor" (minimum standards) rather than "ceiling" (maximum standards)
- Coordinate opposition among states with strong privacy laws

**Counterarguments:**

- A strong federal law with limited preemption could provide both a national baseline and international compatibility
- The current state patchwork creates compliance burdens without ensuring comprehensive protection
- Federal legislation could preserve state enforcement authority while setting uniform standards
- International adequacy requires a federal framework; state laws alone cannot achieve adequacy

### Free Speech Absolutists

**Who:** First Amendment scholars, civil liberties organizations (selectively), media organizations, political data firms

**Stated Arguments:**

- Data regulation constitutes restriction of speech protected by the First Amendment
- *Sorrell v. IMS Health* (2011) confirms that data restrictions are subject to heightened scrutiny
- Privacy regulation could be used to suppress journalism and political speech
- Government should not determine what information can be shared between willing parties

**Underlying Motivations:**

- Genuine concern about government regulation of information flows
- Some organizations use First Amendment arguments instrumentally to protect data-dependent business models
- Media organizations fear that privacy regulation could create new legal tools against journalism

**Strategies:**

- Challenge privacy laws on First Amendment grounds
- Frame data collection as information gathering protected by press freedoms
- Invoke the specter of censorship to create unlikely alliances
- Litigate to create favorable precedents

**Counterarguments:**

- Most democracies with strong data protection laws also have robust free speech protections -- these rights are not incompatible
- The First Amendment does not protect commercial conduct (like data collection and sale) to the same degree as political speech
- Privacy regulation protects autonomy, which is itself a precondition for free expression
- Narrowly tailored privacy laws can include carve-outs for journalism and political speech

## Opposition Effectiveness

### Track Record

| Effort | Opposition Strategy | Outcome |
|--------|-------------------|---------|
| ADPPA (2022) | Preemption debate; private right of action opposition | Bill died in committee despite bipartisan markup support |
| Consumer Online Privacy Rights Act (COPRA) | Industry opposition to FTC rulemaking authority | Never advanced to floor vote |
| FISA 702 reform proposals | National security establishment opposition | 702 reauthorized in 2024 with expanded (not reduced) scope |
| State privacy law challenges | Industry lobbying against strong state bills | Some states weakened provisions; others enacted strong laws anyway |
| EU adequacy advocacy | Industry supported DPF as alternative to domestic reform | DPF adopted, reducing pressure for comprehensive legislation |

### Assessment

Opposition to comprehensive privacy reform remains highly effective because:

1. **Multiple veto points**: Each opposition group can block reform at different stages
2. **Resource asymmetry**: Industry lobbying resources vastly exceed those of consumer advocates
3. **Issue complexity**: Technical complexity makes public mobilization difficult
4. **Competing priorities**: Privacy competes with healthcare, economy, and other issues for legislative attention
5. **Divided reformers**: Preemption and private right of action disagreements split the pro-reform coalition

## Overcoming Opposition

### Strategies That Have Shown Promise

| Strategy | Target Opposition | Evidence |
|----------|------------------|----------|
| Framing privacy as competitiveness issue | Industry | Companies spending heavily on GDPR compliance increasingly support a single US standard |
| Emphasizing national security benefits of privacy | Intelligence community | Strong privacy protections reduce foreign intelligence collection against US persons |
| Federal floor with state enforcement | States' rights advocates | Preserves state authority while creating national baseline |
| Sector-specific carve-outs | Various | Journalism exceptions, small business thresholds reduce opposition |
| International pressure (Schrems litigation) | All | EU enforcement creates economic urgency that domestic politics alone has not |
| Bipartisan privacy champions | Political | Senators and Representatives from both parties have introduced privacy bills |

### What Would Change the Dynamic

- **Major data breach or scandal**: Past events (Equifax, Cambridge Analytica) created momentum but not enough; a breach with direct, visible consumer harm could be different
- **CJEU invalidation of DPF**: A *Schrems III* ruling would create immediate economic pressure
- **Competitive disadvantage evidence**: Clear evidence that US companies are losing business due to lack of federal privacy law
- **Public mobilization**: Privacy becoming a voting issue (currently low-salience)
- **Executive leadership**: A president making privacy legislation a top priority
