# International Privacy: Current State

## Global Privacy Law Landscape

### Overview of Global Frameworks

The global movement toward comprehensive data protection legislation has accelerated dramatically since the GDPR's adoption. As of early 2025, more than 160 countries have enacted data protection laws, and the trend continues to expand.

| Region | Countries with Comprehensive Laws | Key Frameworks | Trend |
|--------|----------------------------------|----------------|-------|
| Europe | 30+ (EU/EEA + UK) | GDPR, UK GDPR/DPA 2018 | Mature; enforcement intensifying |
| Asia-Pacific | 25+ | China PIPL, Japan APPI, South Korea PIPA, India DPDPA, Australia Privacy Act | Rapid adoption; China and India most significant recent additions |
| Latin America | 15+ | Brazil LGPD, Argentina PDPA, Colombia Law 1581, Chile (reform pending) | Growing enforcement capacity |
| Africa | 35+ | South Africa POPIA, Kenya DPA, Nigeria NDPR, AU Convention on Cyber Security | Adoption outpacing enforcement capacity |
| Middle East | 10+ | UAE Federal Decree-Law No. 45, Saudi PDPL, Bahrain PDPL | Emerging regulatory infrastructure |
| North America | US (sectoral only), Canada (PIPEDA + provincial), Mexico (LFPDPPP) | US is the notable outlier | US uniquely lacks comprehensive law among developed democracies |

Source: UNCTAD Data Protection Legislation Worldwide (2024); DLA Piper Global Data Protection Laws of the World (2025).

### Major International Privacy Frameworks

#### European Union: General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR)

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Effective date** | May 25, 2018 |
| **Scope** | All organizations processing personal data of EU residents, regardless of location |
| **Legal basis for processing** | Consent, contract, legal obligation, vital interests, public interest, legitimate interests |
| **Individual rights** | Access, rectification, erasure ("right to be forgotten"), data portability, restriction of processing, objection |
| **Enforcement** | National Data Protection Authorities (DPAs) with fines up to 20 million euros or 4% of global annual revenue |
| **Cross-border transfers** | Adequacy decisions, SCCs, BCRs, or derogations |
| **Total enforcement fines** | Over 4.5 billion euros cumulative (January 2025) |
| **Largest single fine** | 1.2 billion euros against Meta (2023) for transatlantic data transfers |

Source: European Commission GDPR resources; GDPR Enforcement Tracker (January 2025).

#### China: Personal Information Protection Law (PIPL)

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Effective date** | November 1, 2021 |
| **Scope** | Applies to processing of Chinese residents' personal information, including by foreign entities |
| **Key provisions** | Consent requirements, data minimization, cross-border transfer restrictions, data localization for critical information infrastructure operators |
| **Enforcement** | Cyberspace Administration of China (CAC); fines up to 50 million yuan or 5% of annual revenue |
| **Data localization** | Critical information infrastructure operators must store personal data within China; security assessments required for cross-border transfers |
| **Significance for US** | Creates significant compliance obligations for US companies operating in China; data localization requirements conflict with free-flow principles |

Source: Cyberspace Administration of China; Covington & Burling, "China's PIPL: A Practical Guide" (2023).

#### India: Digital Personal Data Protection Act (DPDPA)

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Enacted** | August 11, 2023 (rules pending as of 2025) |
| **Scope** | Processing of digital personal data in India or relating to offering goods/services to persons in India |
| **Key provisions** | Consent-based processing, data fiduciary obligations, cross-border transfer restrictions (blacklist model), children's data protections |
| **Enforcement** | Data Protection Board of India (being established); penalties up to 250 crore rupees (~$30 million) |
| **Cross-border transfers** | Permitted except to countries on government blacklist (not yet published) |
| **Significance for US** | India's 1.4 billion population and major IT outsourcing industry make this law critical for US businesses |

Source: Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology, Government of India (2023).

#### Brazil: Lei Geral de Protecao de Dados (LGPD)

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Effective date** | September 18, 2020 (enforcement from August 2021) |
| **Scope** | Processing of personal data of individuals in Brazil, regardless of processor location |
| **Key provisions** | GDPR-inspired; ten legal bases for processing; data subject rights; DPO requirement |
| **Enforcement** | National Data Protection Authority (ANPD); fines up to 2% of revenue in Brazil, capped at 50 million reais per infraction |
| **Significance for US** | Brazil is the largest economy in Latin America; LGPD alignment with GDPR principles reinforces global convergence |

Source: ANPD (Brazilian National Data Protection Authority) (2024).

### The US as Global Outlier

| Dimension | United States | EU (GDPR) | Other Democracies |
|-----------|--------------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Comprehensive federal law** | No | Yes | Yes (Canada, Japan, Australia, South Korea, etc.) |
| **Privacy as a right** | Not constitutionally enumerated for data privacy | Fundamental right (EU Charter, Art. 8) | Constitutional right in many nations |
| **Regulatory model** | Sectoral (HIPAA, FCRA, COPPA, GLBA) | Omnibus/comprehensive | Generally comprehensive |
| **Enforcement body** | FTC (limited authority), state AGs | National DPAs | Dedicated privacy commissioners/authorities |
| **Consent model** | Generally opt-out (notice and choice) | Opt-in for most processing | Generally opt-in or mixed |
| **Private right of action** | Limited (state laws, FCRA) | Yes (Art. 82) | Varies; many include it |
| **Cross-border transfers** | No federal restrictions on outbound transfers | Restricted; requires adequacy or safeguards | Generally restricted |

## Transatlantic Data Transfers

### History of EU-US Frameworks

The transatlantic data transfer relationship has been marked by repeated legal failures:

| Framework | Dates | Status | Why It Failed |
|-----------|-------|--------|---------------|
| **Safe Harbor** | 2000-2015 | Invalidated | CJEU ruled in *Schrems I* (2015) that US surveillance programs (particularly NSA Section 702) provided inadequate protection for EU data |
| **Privacy Shield** | 2016-2020 | Invalidated | CJEU ruled in *Schrems II* (2020) that US law still failed to provide adequate protection; FISA 702 and EO 12333 cited as primary concerns |
| **EU-US Data Privacy Framework** | 2023-present | Active but legally challenged | Based on Executive Order 14086 (October 2022) establishing new safeguards and a Data Protection Review Court; NOYB and others have signaled challenges |

Source: Court of Justice of the European Union, *Schrems I* (C-362/14, 2015) and *Schrems II* (C-311/18, 2020); European Commission adequacy decision for EU-US DPF (July 2023).

### EU-US Data Privacy Framework (DPF)

| Component | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Legal basis** | Executive Order 14086 (October 7, 2022); European Commission adequacy decision (July 10, 2023) |
| **Certified companies** | 2,800+ (as of December 2024) |
| **Key safeguards** | Proportionality and necessity requirements for US signals intelligence; redress mechanism via Data Protection Review Court |
| **Data Protection Review Court** | Independent body to review complaints from EU individuals about US intelligence access; binding decisions |
| **Vulnerabilities** | Based on executive order, not statute -- can be revoked by future president; untested redress mechanism; CJEU challenge expected |
| **Max Schrems/NOYB position** | Has indicated plans to challenge; argues EO 14086 is insufficient and that FISA 702 fundamentally conflicts with EU law |

Source: US Department of Commerce, Data Privacy Framework Program (2024); European Commission, "Questions and Answers: EU-US Data Privacy Framework" (2023).

### Current Legal Risks

| Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation |
|------|-----------|--------|------------|
| CJEU invalidation of DPF (*Schrems III*) | Moderate-High (expected challenge by 2025-2026) | Very High -- would disrupt data flows for 2,800+ companies | Enact comprehensive US federal privacy law; reform FISA 702 |
| New US administration revokes EO 14086 | Low-Moderate | Very High -- would immediately undermine DPF | Congressional codification of EO 14086 safeguards |
| FISA 702 reauthorization without privacy reforms | Already occurred (April 2024 reauthorization with expanded scope) | High -- strengthens Schrems III challenge arguments | Future legislative reform |
| UK data bridge instability | Low-Moderate | Moderate -- affects US-UK data transfers under UK extension of DPF | Maintain alignment with UK DPA requirements |

## Cross-Border Data Flow Mechanisms

### Transfer Mechanisms Currently in Use

| Mechanism | Description | Usage | Limitations |
|-----------|-------------|-------|-------------|
| **EU-US Data Privacy Framework** | Self-certification by US companies | 2,800+ companies | Vulnerable to legal challenge; executive order basis |
| **Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs)** | Pre-approved contractual terms adopted between data exporter and importer | Most widely used mechanism globally | *Schrems II* requires transfer impact assessments; supplementary measures may be needed |
| **Binding Corporate Rules (BCRs)** | Corporate-wide data protection policies approved by EU DPAs | ~170 companies with approved BCRs | Expensive and time-consuming to obtain (18-24 months); only for intra-group transfers |
| **Derogations (Art. 49 GDPR)** | Consent, contract necessity, public interest, legal claims, vital interests | Limited; intended for occasional transfers only | Not viable for systematic, large-scale transfers |
| **Codes of Conduct / Certifications** | Industry-specific approved frameworks | Emerging; few adopted to date | Limited adoption so far |

Source: European Data Protection Board (EDPB), "Recommendations on Supplementary Measures" (2021); IAPP, "Data Transfer Mechanisms Survey" (2024).

### Transfer Impact Assessments

Following *Schrems II*, organizations using SCCs must conduct transfer impact assessments evaluating:

- The legal framework of the recipient country (including surveillance laws)
- The specific circumstances of the transfer
- Whether supplementary measures can ensure adequate protection

For US transfers, assessments must address FISA Section 702, Executive Order 12333, and other surveillance authorities. The EDPB's guidance makes clear that technical measures (such as encryption where the importer cannot access keys) may be necessary when legal protections are insufficient.

## Data Localization Trends

### Countries with Significant Data Localization Requirements

| Country | Requirement | Scope | Impact on US Companies |
|---------|------------|-------|----------------------|
| **China** | Critical information infrastructure operators must store personal data locally; security assessment for cross-border transfers | Broad (expanding) | Major -- affects all US companies operating in China |
| **Russia** | Personal data of Russian citizens must be stored on servers in Russia | Broad | LinkedIn blocked; many US companies affected |
| **India** | Payment data must be stored locally (RBI mandate); DPDPA allows blacklisting countries for transfers | Financial sector; potentially broader | Significant -- India is major IT outsourcing destination |
| **Indonesia** | Government data must be processed locally | Government sector | Growing concern for cloud providers |
| **Vietnam** | Data localization for certain types of personal data | Expanding | Affects US tech companies with Vietnamese users |
| **Turkey** | Requires explicit consent or adequacy determination for cross-border transfers | Broad | Turkey lacks adequacy agreements with most countries |
| **Nigeria** | Certain data categories must be stored locally | Selective | Growing market affected |

Source: ITIF, "Cross-Border Data Flows: Where Are the Barriers, and What Do They Cost?" (2024); Brookings Institution, "Data Localization" (2023).

### Economic Impact of Data Localization

| Metric | Estimate | Source |
|--------|----------|--------|
| Global cost of data localization | $1.1 trillion in reduced GDP globally | ECIPE, "The Costs of Data Localization" (2023) |
| Increased costs for affected companies | 30-60% increase in data management costs | Leviathan Security Group, "Quantifying the Cost of Forced Localization" (2015, updated estimates) |
| Number of data localization measures enacted globally | 200+ | ITIF Data Localization Tracker (2024) |
| Impact on cloud computing market | Fragmentation of global cloud services | McKinsey, "The Future of Cloud Computing" (2024) |

### International Enforcement Cooperation

| Mechanism | Participants | Purpose | Status |
|-----------|-------------|---------|--------|
| **Global Privacy Assembly (GPA)** | 130+ data protection authorities worldwide | Information sharing, joint statements, enforcement cooperation | Active; US FTC is a member |
| **Global Cross-Border Enforcement Cooperation Arrangement** | 30+ DPAs | Facilitate cross-border enforcement cooperation | Growing participation |
| **APEC Cross-Border Privacy Rules (CBPR)** | US, Japan, South Korea, Canada, others (15 economies) | Certification-based framework for Asia-Pacific data transfers | Active; US participates; rebranded as "Global CBPR Forum" (2022) |
| **OECD Privacy Guidelines** | 38 OECD member countries | Non-binding principles for data protection | Updated 2013; foundational but not enforceable |
| **Council of Europe Convention 108+** | 55 parties (open to non-European states) | Binding treaty on data protection | US has not ratified |

Source: Global Privacy Assembly (2024); APEC CBPR System (2024); Council of Europe (2024).

### US Enforcement Gaps in International Context

| Issue | Description | Consequence |
|-------|-------------|-------------|
| No dedicated privacy regulator | FTC has limited authority; no DPA equivalent | Cannot participate as peer in international enforcement cooperation |
| No comprehensive federal law to enforce | Sectoral laws create gaps | Foreign regulators cannot rely on US providing equivalent protection |
| Limited cross-border enforcement tools | No domestic law requiring cooperation with foreign DPAs | US companies may face enforcement in foreign jurisdictions with limited US government support |
| State-level fragmentation | 19+ state privacy laws with different standards | Foreign companies and regulators struggle to determine applicable US law |

## The Gap

| What Exists | What Is Needed | Current Status |
|-------------|----------------|----------------|
| Sectoral federal privacy laws | Comprehensive federal privacy legislation | No bill has passed; ADPPA came closest in 2022 |
| EU-US DPF based on executive order | Statutory codification of surveillance safeguards | EO 14086 remains uncodified |
| FTC with limited privacy authority | Dedicated federal privacy agency or empowered FTC | No legislation to expand authority |
| State-level patchwork | Federal baseline with state floor (not ceiling) | Preemption remains politically contentious |
| APEC CBPR participation | Full engagement with global privacy standards | US not party to Convention 108+ |
| Post-hoc enforcement | Proactive privacy-by-design requirements | No federal mandate |
