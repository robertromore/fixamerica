# International Privacy: Roadmap

## Overview

Aligning the United States with international privacy standards is a multi-year effort requiring legislative action, institutional reform, diplomatic engagement, and sustained political will. This roadmap sequences reforms to build momentum, create durable frameworks, and achieve the ultimate goal of a stable, internationally recognized US privacy regime.

---

## Phase 1: Foundation Building (Years 1-2)

### Objective

Establish the political groundwork and enact initial reforms that demonstrate commitment to privacy protection while building toward comprehensive legislation.

### Actions

| Action | Timeline | Lead Actor | Dependencies |
|--------|----------|-----------|--------------|
| Codify EO 14086 surveillance safeguards into statute | Year 1, Q1-Q2 | Congress (Senate Intelligence + Judiciary) | Bipartisan sponsors; administration support |
| Enact Fourth Amendment Is Not For Sale Act (warrant for commercial data purchases) | Year 1, Q2-Q3 | Congress (Judiciary Committee) | None -- standalone bill |
| Establish bipartisan privacy legislative commission | Year 1, Q1 | Congress | None |
| Begin FTC rulemaking on commercial surveillance under existing authority | Year 1, Q1 | FTC | FTC commissioner votes |
| Initiate negotiation for Convention 108+ accession | Year 1, Q3 | State Department | Executive decision |
| Fund comprehensive study of state privacy law harmonization | Year 1, Q2 | Congressional Research Service / GAO | Congressional request |

### Milestones

- [ ] Surveillance safeguards codified in statute
- [ ] Warrant requirement for government commercial data purchases enacted
- [ ] Bipartisan privacy commission established with mandate to report in 12 months
- [ ] FTC commercial surveillance rulemaking initiated
- [ ] Convention 108+ accession negotiations begun

### Success Metrics

| Metric | Target | Measurement |
|--------|--------|-------------|
| Surveillance reform legislation | Passed and signed | Congressional record |
| Commercial data purchase restrictions | Enacted | Federal Register |
| Privacy commission established | Operational within 6 months | Commission reports |
| FTC rulemaking | Notice of Proposed Rulemaking published | Federal Register |

## Phase 2: Comprehensive Legislation (Years 2-3)

### Objective

Enact comprehensive federal privacy legislation that establishes a rights-based framework aligned with international standards while respecting American legal traditions.

### Actions

| Action | Timeline | Lead Actor | Dependencies |
|--------|----------|-----------|--------------|
| Privacy commission delivers comprehensive legislative recommendations | Year 2, Q1 | Privacy commission | Phase 1 commission establishment |
| Draft comprehensive federal privacy bill | Year 2, Q1-Q2 | Congress (Commerce + Judiciary) | Commission recommendations |
| Negotiate preemption provisions with states | Year 2, Q2-Q3 | Federal-state working group | California, other states at table |
| Committee markup and floor votes | Year 2, Q3 - Year 3, Q1 | Congress | Sufficient votes |
| Enact comprehensive federal privacy law | Year 3, Q1-Q2 | Congress + President | Passage through both chambers |
| Publish implementing regulations | Year 3, Q2-Q4 | FTC or new privacy agency | Statutory authority from new law |

### Key Legislative Design Decisions

| Decision | Options | Recommended Approach |
|----------|---------|---------------------|
| **Preemption** | Full preemption, floor preemption, no preemption | Floor preemption: federal baseline with states free to exceed |
| **Private right of action** | Full PRA, limited PRA, no PRA | Limited PRA for actual damages + statutory damages for willful violations |
| **Enforcement body** | FTC, new agency, FTC with enhanced authority | FTC with enhanced authority in Phase 2; transition to independent agency in Phase 3 |
| **Consent model** | Opt-in, opt-out, hybrid | Opt-in for sensitive data; legitimate interest basis for non-sensitive data with opt-out |
| **Small business** | Revenue threshold, data volume threshold, no exemption | Tiered obligations based on data processing volume |

### Milestones

- [ ] Comprehensive privacy bill introduced with bipartisan co-sponsors
- [ ] Committee markup completed in both chambers
- [ ] Floor votes in both chambers
- [ ] Law enacted and signed
- [ ] Implementing regulations published

## Phase 3: Institutional Development and International Recognition (Years 3-5)

### Objective

Build the institutional infrastructure for privacy enforcement, achieve EU adequacy, and establish the US as a constructive participant in international privacy governance.

### Actions

| Action | Timeline | Lead Actor | Dependencies |
|--------|----------|-----------|--------------|
| Establish or transform privacy enforcement agency | Year 3, Q3 - Year 4, Q2 | Administration + Congress | Statutory authority from comprehensive law |
| Apply for EU adequacy decision | Year 3, Q4 | Department of Commerce | Comprehensive law + surveillance reform enacted |
| European Commission adequacy assessment process | Year 4, Q1 - Year 5, Q1 | European Commission + EDPB | US application; adequate legal framework in place |
| Complete Convention 108+ accession | Year 4, Q1-Q3 | Senate (ratification) | State Department negotiation (Phase 1) |
| Negotiate bilateral adequacy agreements with key partners (UK, Japan, Canada, etc.) | Year 4-5 | Privacy agency + State Department | Comprehensive law in place |
| Join Global Cross-Border Enforcement Cooperation Arrangement | Year 4 | Privacy agency | Institutional capacity established |

### Milestones

- [ ] Privacy enforcement agency operational
- [ ] EU adequacy application submitted
- [ ] Convention 108+ ratified
- [ ] EU adequacy decision issued
- [ ] Bilateral adequacy agreements with at least 3 major trading partners

---

## Phase 4: Maturation and Leadership (Years 5-10)

### Objective

Establish the US as a global leader in privacy governance, ensure the durability of reforms, and adapt to emerging challenges.

### Actions

| Action | Timeline | Lead Actor | Dependencies |
|--------|----------|-----------|--------------|
| First comprehensive review of federal privacy law (statutory review provision) | Year 5 | Congress + privacy agency | Sunset/review provision in original law |
| Develop US adequacy framework for evaluating other countries | Year 5-6 | Privacy agency | Institutional maturity |
| Lead multilateral privacy standards development | Year 5-7 | State Department + privacy agency | International credibility established |
| Address emerging technology challenges (AI, biometrics, IoT) | Ongoing | Privacy agency + Congress | Technical expertise developed |
| Strengthen enforcement cooperation with international partners | Ongoing | Privacy agency | Bilateral agreements in place |
| Monitor and respond to data localization trends | Ongoing | Commerce Department + privacy agency | Trade negotiation capacity |

### Milestones

- [ ] Statutory review completed; amendments enacted as needed
- [ ] US adequacy framework operational
- [ ] US participating in leadership role in GPA and other international forums
- [ ] Emerging technology privacy rules promulgated
- [ ] Stable, durable transatlantic data transfer framework in place for 3+ years

## Risk Assessment

| Risk | Probability | Impact | Mitigation |
|------|------------|--------|------------|
| Congressional gridlock prevents comprehensive legislation | High | Very High | Build bipartisan coalition; use Schrems III pressure as catalyst |
| New administration revokes EO 14086 before codification | Moderate | Very High | Prioritize codification in Year 1 |
| CJEU invalidates DPF before US reforms enacted | Moderate-High | High | Accelerate Phase 1-2 timeline; use invalidation as legislative catalyst |
| Industry lobbying weakens legislation below adequacy threshold | High | High | Ensure EU Commission input during legislative design |
| National security establishment blocks surveillance reform | High | Very High | Frame surveillance reform as national security benefit (stable intelligence partnerships) |
| Preemption debate splits reform coalition | High | High | Federal floor approach; negotiate with California specifically |
| State opposition to federal framework | Moderate | Moderate | Include states in legislative design; preserve enforcement authority |

## Indicators of Progress

### Leading Indicators

- Number of bipartisan co-sponsors on privacy legislation
- FTC rulemaking advancement
- EU Commission statements on US reform progress
- Industry support for federal legislation (vs. opposition)

### Lagging Indicators

- EU adequacy decision
- Reduction in cross-border compliance costs for US companies
- International enforcement cooperation actions
- Stable transatlantic data flows (measured by DPF certifications and legal challenges)
