# International Privacy: Actions

## Overview

While comprehensive reform requires legislative action, individuals, organizations, and communities can take meaningful steps to advance international privacy alignment, protect their own data in cross-border contexts, and build political momentum for reform.

---

## Individual Actions

### Protect Your Own Data

| Action | Difficulty | Impact | Details |
|--------|-----------|--------|---------|
| **Use privacy-respecting services** | Low | Moderate | Choose services with strong privacy practices; prefer companies that apply GDPR-level protections globally |
| **Exercise data rights under state laws** | Low-Moderate | Low-Moderate | If you live in a state with a privacy law (CA, CO, CT, VA, etc.), submit access and deletion requests |
| **Use a VPN** | Low | Low | Encrypt internet traffic; choose a VPN with strong privacy policies and no-logging commitments |
| **Enable end-to-end encryption** | Low | Moderate | Use Signal, ProtonMail, and other encrypted services for sensitive communications |
| **Review app permissions** | Low | Low-Moderate | Restrict location, camera, microphone, and contact access for apps that do not need them |
| **Opt out of data brokers** | Moderate | Moderate | Submit opt-out requests to major data brokers; use services like DeleteMe or Privacy Duck if available |
| **Use browser privacy tools** | Low | Moderate | Install uBlock Origin, Privacy Badger; use Firefox or Brave; disable third-party cookies |

### Political Engagement

| Action | Difficulty | Impact | Details |
|--------|-----------|--------|---------|
| **Contact your representatives** | Low | Moderate | Call or write your US Senators and Representative about comprehensive privacy legislation |
| **Support privacy-focused candidates** | Low | Moderate | Research candidates' positions on privacy; make privacy a factor in voting decisions |
| **Sign petitions and public comments** | Low | Low-Moderate | Participate in FTC rulemakings; sign petitions supporting privacy legislation |
| **Share your data stories** | Low | Moderate | Personal stories about data misuse or privacy violations make the issue concrete for policymakers |
| **Attend town halls** | Low-Moderate | Moderate | Ask elected officials about their position on comprehensive privacy legislation |
| **Testify at state legislature hearings** | Moderate | High | State-level privacy bills benefit from public testimony |

### Education and Awareness

| Action | Difficulty | Impact | Details |
|--------|-----------|--------|---------|
| **Learn about international privacy standards** | Low | Low | Read about GDPR, PIPL, and other frameworks to understand what comprehensive privacy looks like |
| **Discuss privacy with family and friends** | Low | Low-Moderate | Help others understand data collection practices and available protections |
| **Follow privacy news** | Low | Low | Subscribe to IAPP, EFF, CDT, or other privacy organization newsletters |

## Organizational Actions

### For Businesses

| Action | Difficulty | Impact | Details |
|--------|-----------|--------|---------|
| **Adopt GDPR-level protections globally** | High | Very High | Apply strongest available standard to all users, not just EU residents |
| **Support comprehensive federal privacy legislation** | Moderate | High | Sign industry letters; provide testimony; engage trade associations |
| **Conduct privacy impact assessments** | Moderate | High | Evaluate new products and services for privacy implications before launch |
| **Implement privacy by design** | High | Very High | Build data minimization, purpose limitation, and consent mechanisms into systems |
| **Appoint a Data Protection Officer** | Moderate | High | Even without a US legal requirement, DPOs improve privacy practices |
| **Publish transparency reports** | Moderate | Moderate | Disclose government data requests, data collection practices, and privacy metrics |

### For Advocacy Organizations

| Action | Difficulty | Impact | Details |
|--------|-----------|--------|---------|

| **File regulatory comments** | Moderate | Moderate-High | FTC rulemakings, state legislative processes, and international consultations |
| **Conduct research on privacy harms** | High | High | Document specific harms from inadequate privacy protections to build the case for reform |
| **Engage with international counterparts** | Moderate | Moderate | Coordinate with EU advocacy organizations and DPAs |
| **Support strategic litigation** | High | Very High | Fund and support cases that advance privacy rights |
| **Develop model legislation** | High | High | Create templates for comprehensive privacy legislation at federal and state levels |

### For Academic Institutions

| Action | Difficulty | Impact | Details |
|--------|-----------|--------|---------|
| **Research comparative privacy law** | Moderate | Moderate | Publish analyses comparing US and international frameworks |
| **Train privacy professionals** | Moderate | Moderate-High | Develop curricula for privacy law, policy, and technology |
| **Host cross-border research collaborations** | Moderate | Moderate | Demonstrate that privacy protections and research can coexist |
| **Provide expert testimony** | Low-Moderate | Moderate | Offer academic expertise to congressional committees and state legislatures |

## Priority Actions by Audience

### For the Average Citizen

1. Contact your members of Congress to support comprehensive federal privacy legislation
2. Exercise your data rights under state laws (if applicable)
3. Use privacy-respecting tools (encrypted messaging, browser privacy extensions)
4. Make privacy a factor in your consumer and voting choices

### For Business Leaders

1. Apply GDPR-level protections to all users, not just European customers
2. Publicly support comprehensive federal privacy legislation
3. Implement privacy by design and data minimization in your products
4. Conduct and publish privacy impact assessments

### For Advocates and Organizers

1. Build broad coalitions connecting privacy to civil rights, consumer protection, and democratic governance
2. Document specific harms from privacy failures to make the issue concrete
3. Engage in both federal and state legislative processes
4. Coordinate with international privacy advocacy organizations
