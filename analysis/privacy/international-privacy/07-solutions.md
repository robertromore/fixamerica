# International Privacy: Solutions

## Overview

Aligning the United States with international privacy standards requires action on three fronts: enacting comprehensive federal privacy legislation, reforming surveillance authorities, and engaging constructively in international privacy governance. Each of these is necessary but insufficient alone -- meaningful progress requires coordinated action across all three.

---

## Solution 1: Comprehensive Federal Privacy Legislation

### Description

Enact a federal privacy law that establishes baseline data protection rights comparable to GDPR and other international frameworks. The law should be designed to achieve an EU adequacy decision while respecting American legal traditions.

### Key Components

| Component | Description | International Alignment |
|-----------|-------------|------------------------|
| **Individual rights** | Access, correction, deletion, data portability, right to object | Core GDPR rights (Arts. 15-22) |
| **Legal basis for processing** | Require lawful basis (consent, contract, legitimate interest, legal obligation) beyond notice-and-choice | GDPR Art. 6 model |
| **Purpose limitation** | Data collected for specified purposes; secondary use requires new basis | GDPR Art. 5(1)(b) |
| **Data minimization** | Collect only data necessary for stated purpose | GDPR Art. 5(1)(c) |
| **Transparency** | Clear, accessible privacy notices; algorithmic transparency | GDPR Arts. 13-14 |
| **Accountability** | Data protection officers; privacy impact assessments; records of processing | GDPR Arts. 24-25, 30, 35-36 |
| **Enforcement** | Federal privacy agency or empowered FTC; state AG enforcement; private right of action | DPA model (GDPR Art. 51) |
| **Cross-border transfers** | Mechanism for restricting transfers to countries without adequate protections | GDPR Art. 45-49 model |
| **Breach notification** | Uniform federal standard (72 hours for significant breaches) | GDPR Arts. 33-34 |

### Design Considerations

- **Preemption**: Federal floor with limited preemption; states may exceed federal standards but must not impose conflicting obligations
- **Small business accommodations**: Tiered obligations based on data processing volume and sensitivity, not revenue
- **Sector-specific preservation**: Existing stronger sectoral laws (HIPAA, FCRA, GLBA) preserved where they exceed federal baseline
- **First Amendment compatibility**: Exemptions for journalism, academic research, and political speech narrowly drawn to prevent abuse

### Precedents

- California Consumer Privacy Act / California Privacy Rights Act (CCPA/CPRA)
- Colorado Privacy Act, Virginia CDPA, Connecticut DPA
- ADPPA (2022 -- advanced through committee but not enacted)
- GDPR (international benchmark)

### Estimated Impact

- Enable EU adequacy decision, stabilizing transatlantic data flows
- Reduce compliance costs for companies navigating 19+ state laws
- Provide enforceable privacy rights to all Americans
- Strengthen US credibility in international privacy governance

## Solution 2: Surveillance Reform

### Description

Codify and strengthen the safeguards established by Executive Order 14086, reform FISA Section 702, and create statutory limits on government access to commercial data. These reforms are necessary for any EU-US data transfer framework to survive legal challenge.

### Key Components

| Reform | Current State | Proposed Change |
|--------|--------------|-----------------|
| **Codify EO 14086** | Executive order; can be revoked by future president | Statutory requirement for proportionality and necessity in signals intelligence |
| **FISA 702 reform** | Reauthorized 2024 with expanded scope | Require court approval for queries of US and allied-nation persons' data; limit bulk collection |
| **Data Protection Review Court** | Established by EO 14086; binding decisions on EU complaints | Statutory establishment with independence protections and expanded jurisdiction |
| **Commercial data purchases** | No warrant requirement | Require warrant for government purchase of data that would require a warrant to obtain directly (Fourth Amendment Is Not For Sale Act model) |
| **EO 12333 reform** | Largely unconstrained by statute | Statutory framework for intelligence collection conducted outside FISA |
| **Transparency** | Limited annual reports | Enhanced public reporting on surveillance scope and scale |

### Precedents

- USA FREEDOM Act (2015) -- ended NSA bulk phone records collection
- *Carpenter v. United States* (2018) -- warrant required for cell-site location data
- Executive Order 14086 (2022) -- imposed proportionality requirements
- Fourth Amendment Is Not For Sale Act (introduced but not passed)

### Estimated Impact

- Make DPF sustainable by addressing root cause of *Schrems* litigation
- Protect both American and foreign persons from disproportionate surveillance
- Enable genuine adequacy rather than executive-order-dependent frameworks
- Reduce legal uncertainty for transatlantic commerce

## Solution 3: Federal Privacy Agency

### Description

Establish a dedicated federal data protection authority with the institutional capacity, independence, and mandate to serve as a peer to European DPAs and other international regulators.

### Key Components

| Component | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Independence** | Fixed-term commissioners; protection from removal except for cause; independent budget |
| **Rulemaking authority** | Power to issue binding regulations interpreting privacy legislation |
| **Enforcement** | Authority to investigate, impose fines, and issue binding orders |
| **International cooperation** | Mandate to participate in GPA, CBPR, and bilateral cooperation with foreign DPAs |
| **Adequacy assessments** | Authority to evaluate foreign privacy frameworks for data transfer purposes |
| **Technical expertise** | Capacity to evaluate emerging technologies and data practices |

### Precedents

- Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) -- model for independent agency with enforcement authority
- EU national DPAs -- institutional model for privacy regulators
- FTC (existing, but limited authority) -- could be transformed rather than replaced

### Estimated Impact

- Provide the institutional infrastructure necessary for sustained privacy enforcement
- Enable peer-to-peer cooperation with international regulators
- Build technical expertise for regulating emerging technologies
- Demonstrate to foreign counterparts that the US takes privacy enforcement seriously

## Solution 4: International Privacy Diplomacy

### Description

Shift US engagement in international privacy governance from defensive (protecting the status quo) to constructive (seeking harmonization and mutual recognition).

### Key Components

| Initiative | Description | Benefit |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| **Ratify Convention 108+** | Join the Council of Europe's binding data protection treaty | Demonstrate commitment to international standards; participate in treaty governance |
| **Expand CBPR/Global CBPR Forum** | Strengthen the Asia-Pacific certification framework | Provide alternative transfer mechanism; build multilateral privacy infrastructure |
| **Bilateral adequacy agreements** | Negotiate adequacy recognitions with key trading partners | Reduce compliance burden; build stable transfer frameworks |
| **Digital trade agreements** | Include strong privacy provisions in trade agreements | Prevent data localization while preserving regulatory space for privacy |
| **Multilateral privacy standards** | Support development of ISO/IEC privacy standards and interoperability frameworks | Create technical standards that bridge legal differences |

### Precedents

- EU adequacy decisions (16 countries currently recognized)
- Japan-EU mutual adequacy (2019)
- Global CBPR Forum (2022 -- multilateral privacy certification)
- UK adequacy bridge to EU and US frameworks

---

## Solution 5: Data Localization Response Strategy

### Description

Develop a coherent US strategy for responding to global data localization requirements that balances open data flows with legitimate privacy and sovereignty concerns.

### Key Components

| Action | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Domestic privacy law as countermeasure** | Strong US privacy law reduces other countries' justification for data localization |
| **Mutual recognition agreements** | Negotiate bilateral or multilateral agreements recognizing equivalent protections |
| **Technical standards** | Promote encryption and technical safeguards that protect data regardless of location |
| **Trade negotiations** | Use digital trade agreements to limit unjustified data localization requirements |
| **Distinguish types of localization** | Differentiate between sovereignty-based localization (China, Russia) and privacy-based localization (EU adequacy) |

## Solution Prioritization

| Solution | Impact | Feasibility | Timeframe | Priority |
|----------|--------|-------------|-----------|----------|
| Comprehensive federal privacy law | Very High | Moderate (political obstacles significant) | 2-5 years | Highest |
| Surveillance reform (codify EO 14086) | Very High | Moderate (national security opposition) | 2-4 years | Highest |
| Federal privacy agency | High | Moderate (institutional design challenges) | 3-5 years | High |
| Commercial data purchase restrictions | High | Moderate-High (bipartisan support possible) | 1-3 years | High |
| International privacy diplomacy | Moderate | High (executive authority sufficient for much) | 1-3 years | Moderate |
| Data localization response | Moderate | Moderate (requires domestic reform first) | 3-7 years | Moderate |
