# International Privacy: History

## Overview

The divergence between American and international approaches to data privacy did not emerge suddenly. It developed over decades as different legal traditions, historical experiences, and political cultures produced fundamentally different frameworks for protecting personal information. Europe's experience with totalitarian surveillance during the 20th century catalyzed a rights-based approach to data protection, while the United States prioritized market freedom and limited government intervention. Understanding this history is essential to grasping why the US remains an outlier and what it would take to change course.

---

## Era 1: Foundational Principles (1948-1979)

### Post-War Human Rights and Privacy

The modern international privacy framework has its roots in the post-World War II human rights movement. The horrors of Nazi and Soviet surveillance programs -- which used census data, registration records, and informant networks to identify, track, and persecute targeted populations -- drove European leaders to enshrine privacy as a fundamental right.

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 1948 | Universal Declaration of Human Rights (Art. 12) | Established privacy as a universal human right: "No one shall be subjected to arbitrary interference with his privacy" |
| 1950 | European Convention on Human Rights (Art. 8) | Right to respect for private and family life, home, and correspondence |
| 1970 | Hesse, Germany enacts world's first data protection law | First legislation specifically addressing computerized personal data |
| 1973 | Sweden enacts Data Act (Datalagen) | First national comprehensive data protection law |
| 1974 | US Privacy Act | Applied only to federal agencies, not the private sector |
| 1977 | Germany enacts Federal Data Protection Act (BDSG) | Comprehensive national data protection law |
| 1978 | France enacts Loi Informatique et Libertes | Created CNIL, the first national data protection authority |
| 1979 | OECD begins work on privacy guidelines | Effort to harmonize privacy principles across developed democracies |

### The US Path Diverges

While European countries adopted comprehensive data protection laws in the 1970s, the United States took a fundamentally different path:

- **Privacy Act of 1974**: Applied only to federal government records, not the private sector
- **Fair Credit Reporting Act (1970)**: Narrow sectoral law covering credit reporting
- **No comprehensive private-sector law**: Congress rejected proposals for omnibus data protection legislation
- **Third-party doctrine**: The Supreme Court in *United States v. Miller* (1976) and *Smith v. Maryland* (1979) held that individuals have no reasonable expectation of privacy in information voluntarily shared with third parties -- a doctrine incompatible with European privacy principles

### Why the Paths Diverged

| Factor | United States | Europe |
|--------|--------------|--------|
| **Historical experience** | Freedom from government tyranny as primary concern | Surveillance states used personal data for persecution |
| **Constitutional tradition** | First Amendment protection of information flows; Fourth Amendment focused on government searches | Privacy as affirmative right; data protection as autonomy |
| **Economic philosophy** | Free market; data as commodity | Social market economy; data as extension of personality |
| **Political culture** | Skepticism of government regulation | Acceptance of regulatory frameworks for individual protection |
| **Industry influence** | Strong technology and advertising lobbies from early era | Less dominant data-intensive industry in 1970s-1980s |

## Era 2: OECD Guidelines and European Harmonization (1980-1994)

### The OECD Privacy Guidelines (1980)

The Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development adopted its Guidelines on the Protection of Privacy and Transborder Flows of Personal Data in 1980. These voluntary principles represented the first major international attempt to establish common privacy standards:

**Eight Core Principles:**

1. **Collection limitation**: Data should be collected lawfully, with knowledge or consent
2. **Data quality**: Data should be accurate, complete, and kept up-to-date
3. **Purpose specification**: Purposes for collection should be specified at collection time
4. **Use limitation**: Data should not be used beyond specified purposes without consent or legal authority
5. **Security safeguards**: Reasonable security measures required
6. **Openness**: General policy of openness about data practices
7. **Individual participation**: Right to access and challenge personal data
8. **Accountability**: Data controllers accountable for compliance

The US endorsed these principles but treated them as aspirational, not legislative mandates. European countries used them as the basis for binding legislation.

### Council of Europe Convention 108 (1981)

The Council of Europe adopted Convention 108, the first legally binding international instrument on data protection. Key features:

- Applicable to automated and (later) manual processing of personal data
- Required parties to enact domestic data protection legislation
- Established principles similar to the OECD Guidelines but with binding force
- Created a framework for cross-border data flow regulation

The United States has never ratified Convention 108, despite its open-accession structure.

### European National Laws Proliferate

| Year | Country | Law | Key Features |
|------|---------|-----|-------------|
| 1984 | United Kingdom | Data Protection Act | First UK data protection law; established DPA |
| 1988 | Netherlands | Wet Persoonsregistraties | Comprehensive data protection framework |
| 1988 | Ireland | Data Protection Act | Established Data Protection Commissioner |
| 1992 | Spain | LORTAD (later LOPD) | Strong data protection with constitutional basis |

## Era 3: The EU Data Protection Directive and Safe Harbor (1995-2012)

### EU Data Protection Directive (95/46/EC)

The European Union's 1995 Data Protection Directive was a watershed moment in international privacy law:

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Adopted** | October 24, 1995 |
| **Effective** | October 25, 1998 |
| **Scope** | All EU member states required to implement through national law |
| **Key innovation** | Article 25 restricted data transfers to countries without "adequate" data protection |
| **Impact on US** | The adequacy requirement meant EU-US data transfers required a special framework |

### The Safe Harbor Negotiation (1998-2000)

Article 25 of the Directive created an immediate crisis for transatlantic commerce. The European Commission could not declare the US "adequate" because the US lacked a comprehensive privacy law. The solution was the Safe Harbor framework:

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 1998 | EU threatens to block data transfers to US | US Commerce Department begins negotiations |
| 2000 | EU-US Safe Harbor framework adopted | US companies could self-certify adherence to privacy principles |
| 2000 | European Commission issues adequacy decision for Safe Harbor | Enabled data transfers to certified US companies |

**Safe Harbor Principles:**

- Notice, choice, onward transfer, security, data integrity, access, enforcement
- Self-certification administered by US Department of Commerce
- FTC enforcement for deceptive practices by certified companies

**Weaknesses:**

- Self-certification with minimal oversight
- FTC enforcement was reactive, not proactive
- No independent oversight of US surveillance programs
- No redress mechanism for EU citizens regarding US government access

### Post-9/11 Expansion of US Surveillance

The September 11, 2001 attacks dramatically expanded US surveillance capabilities, creating new tensions with European privacy frameworks:

| Year | Development | Impact on International Privacy |
|------|-------------|-------------------------------|
| 2001 | USA PATRIOT Act | Expanded government access to commercial and financial data |
| 2002 | FISA Amendments (Section 215) | Allowed bulk collection of business records |
| 2005 | NSA warrantless surveillance revealed by *New York Times* | First public indication of mass surveillance programs |
| 2007 | Protect America Act | Legalized warrantless surveillance of foreign communications transiting US |
| 2008 | FISA Amendments Act (Section 702) | Codified targeting of non-US persons outside US; incidental collection of US and EU persons' data |

### APEC Privacy Framework (2004)

The Asia-Pacific Economic Cooperation forum adopted its Privacy Framework in 2004, offering a more business-friendly alternative to the European model:

- Based on OECD principles but with emphasis on avoiding barriers to information flows
- Led to the Cross-Border Privacy Rules (CBPR) system
- The US actively participated, seeing APEC as a counter-model to the EU approach
- Critics argued CBPR was weaker than GDPR and lacked meaningful enforcement

## Era 4: Snowden, Schrems, and the GDPR (2013-2020)

### The Snowden Revelations (2013)

Edward Snowden's disclosures about NSA surveillance programs transformed the international privacy landscape:

| Program | Description | Impact on International Privacy |
|---------|-------------|-------------------------------|
| PRISM | Collection of communications content from major tech companies (Google, Apple, Facebook, etc.) | Revealed that EU citizens' data held by US companies was accessible to NSA |
| Upstream | Tapping fiber-optic cables carrying internet traffic | Showed mass surveillance of international communications |
| XKeyscore | NSA tool for searching collected data | Demonstrated scope of searchable data on foreign nationals |
| MUSCULAR | Joint NSA-GCHQ program tapping internal links between Google/Yahoo data centers | Showed surveillance beyond FISA framework |

### *Schrems I* and the Fall of Safe Harbor (2015)

Austrian privacy activist Max Schrems challenged Facebook's transfer of his data to the US under Safe Harbor. The Court of Justice of the European Union ruled:

- The European Commission's Safe Harbor adequacy decision was **invalid**
- US surveillance programs (particularly Section 702) did not provide adequate protection for EU data
- Mass surveillance without judicial review was incompatible with EU fundamental rights
- National DPAs must be able to examine whether transfers to a third country comply with the Directive

Impact: 4,400+ companies relying on Safe Harbor had to find alternative transfer mechanisms immediately.

Source: CJEU, *Schrems v. Data Protection Commissioner*, C-362/14 (October 6, 2015).

### Privacy Shield (2016-2020)

The US and EU negotiated a replacement framework:

| Feature | Safe Harbor | Privacy Shield |
|---------|------------|---------------|
| Principles | 7 self-certification principles | Enhanced principles with stronger enforcement |
| US government commitments | None specific | Letters from ODNI, DOJ committing to limits on surveillance |
| Ombudsperson | None | State Department Ombudsperson for EU complaints about surveillance |
| Annual review | None | Annual joint review by EU and US |
| Certification | Commerce Department | Commerce Department with enhanced oversight |

**Criticism:** Privacy advocates argued the Ombudsperson mechanism was inadequate (lacked independence, binding authority, or ability to confirm/deny surveillance), and that FISA Section 702 still allowed mass processing of EU citizens' data.

### The GDPR Replaces the Directive (2018)

The General Data Protection Regulation, adopted in 2016 and effective May 25, 2018, replaced the 1995 Directive with directly applicable regulation:

| Innovation | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Direct applicability** | No need for national implementing legislation (though member states have some discretion) |
| **Extraterritorial scope** | Applies to any entity processing EU residents' data |
| **Massive fines** | Up to 4% of global annual revenue |
| **Data Protection Officers** | Required for large-scale processors |
| **Data breach notification** | 72-hour notification requirement |
| **Privacy by design** | Required at system design stage |
| **Consent standards** | Freely given, specific, informed, and unambiguous |

### *Schrems II* and the Fall of Privacy Shield (2020)

Max Schrems again challenged Facebook's data transfers, this time under Privacy Shield. The CJEU ruled on July 16, 2020:

- Privacy Shield was **invalid** because US surveillance law (FISA 702 and EO 12333) did not meet EU standards for proportionality, necessity, and judicial oversight
- Standard Contractual Clauses remained valid in principle but required case-by-case assessment of the recipient country's legal framework
- Organizations must conduct Transfer Impact Assessments and implement supplementary measures if necessary

Impact: Threw transatlantic data flows into legal uncertainty affecting thousands of companies and billions of dollars in digital commerce.

Source: CJEU, *Data Protection Commissioner v. Facebook Ireland and Maximillian Schrems*, C-311/18 (July 16, 2020).

## Era 5: Data Privacy Framework and Global Convergence (2021-Present)

### Executive Order 14086 and the New Framework

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 2021 | EU-US negotiations on new framework begin | Biden administration engages in intensive diplomacy |
| 2022 | Executive Order 14086 issued (October 7) | Imposed proportionality and necessity requirements on US signals intelligence; created Data Protection Review Court |
| 2023 | European Commission adopts adequacy decision for EU-US DPF (July 10) | Third attempt at transatlantic data transfer framework |
| 2023 | NOYB announces intention to challenge DPF | Expected *Schrems III* case |
| 2024 | FISA Section 702 reauthorized with expanded scope | New provision allowing compulsion of additional service providers may strengthen Schrems III arguments |

## Key Turning Points

| Date | Event | Why It Mattered |
|------|-------|-----------------|
| 1948 | Universal Declaration of Human Rights, Art. 12 | Established privacy as international human right |
| 1980 | OECD Privacy Guidelines | Created common principles that diverged in implementation |
| 1995 | EU Data Protection Directive | Created adequacy requirement that exposed US privacy gaps |
| 2000 | Safe Harbor adopted | First attempt to bridge EU-US privacy divide |
| 2001 | USA PATRIOT Act | Expanded US surveillance in ways incompatible with EU norms |
| 2008 | FISA Amendments Act (Section 702) | Created legal framework for mass foreign surveillance |
| 2013 | Snowden revelations | Exposed scale of US surveillance, galvanized EU reform |
| 2015 | *Schrems I* invalidates Safe Harbor | First framework struck down; US surveillance cited |
| 2018 | GDPR takes effect | Global standard-setting with extraterritorial reach |
| 2020 | *Schrems II* invalidates Privacy Shield | Second framework struck down; pattern established |
| 2023 | EU-US Data Privacy Framework adopted | Third attempt, based on executive order |

## Lessons from History

1. **Executive agreements are inherently unstable**: Safe Harbor and Privacy Shield both fell because they relied on US executive branch commitments rather than statutory protections
2. **US surveillance law is the core problem**: Every EU-US framework has failed because of the same fundamental issue -- FISA Section 702 and related authorities
3. **The Brussels Effect is real**: GDPR has become the de facto global standard because US companies adopt it for European operations and sometimes globally
4. **Rights-based frameworks are more durable than market-based ones**: European privacy law has deepened over time, while US sectoral laws have not kept pace
5. **Trade pressure alone does not produce reform**: Despite billions of dollars at stake, the US has not enacted comprehensive privacy legislation
6. **Civil society actors drive change**: Max Schrems, as an individual litigant, has shaped transatlantic privacy policy more than any government actor
7. **Historical memory matters**: European privacy law is rooted in the experience of totalitarian surveillance, giving it a moral weight that market-based arguments struggle to counter
