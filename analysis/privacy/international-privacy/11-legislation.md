# International Privacy: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

The legislative approach to aligning the United States with international privacy standards requires action on two fronts: enacting comprehensive federal data protection legislation and reforming surveillance authorities that undermine cross-border data transfer frameworks. This document presents draft legislation addressing both needs, along with regulatory frameworks and model state provisions.

## Constitutional Amendments

No constitutional amendment is strictly necessary for comprehensive privacy legislation, as Congress has authority under the Commerce Clause to regulate data practices. However, a constitutional data privacy amendment would provide the strongest possible foundation for durable reform and international alignment.

### Digital Privacy Rights Amendment

**Purpose**: Establish data privacy as a constitutional right, providing a foundation comparable to the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights, Articles 7 and 8.

**Draft Text**:

```text
Section 1. The right of the people to privacy in their personal data
and digital communications shall not be violated. No person's personal
data shall be collected, processed, or transferred without lawful
authority and adequate safeguards.

Section 2. Every person shall have the right to access, correct, and
request deletion of personal data held about them, subject to
reasonable limitations established by law.

Section 3. Congress and the several States shall have the power to
enforce this article by appropriate legislation, provided that no
State law shall provide less protection than federal law enacted
under this section.

Section 4. This article shall take effect two years after the date
of ratification.
```

**Explanation**: Section 1 establishes data privacy as a constitutional right applicable to both government and private actors. Section 2 codifies core data subject rights. Section 3 provides concurrent enforcement authority with a federal floor. Section 4 allows time for implementing legislation.

**Potential Challenges**: Constitutional amendments require two-thirds majorities in both chambers and ratification by three-fourths of state legislatures. Industry opposition and First Amendment concerns would be significant obstacles.

**Refinements**: Could be narrowed to apply only to government data collection if private-sector application proves too contentious, though this would not fully address international alignment.

## Federal Legislation

### The American Data Protection Act (ADPA)

**Purpose**: Establish comprehensive federal data protection legislation aligned with international standards, enabling stable cross-border data flows and an EU adequacy decision.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "American Data Protection Act".

SEC. 2. DEFINITIONS.

(a) "Covered entity" means any person or entity that, alone or
jointly with others, determines the purposes and means of the
processing of personal data, and--
    (1) is subject to the Federal Trade Commission Act;
    (2) is a common carrier subject to the Communications Act; or
    (3) is a nonprofit organization.

(b) "Personal data" means any information that identifies, relates
to, describes, is reasonably capable of being associated with, or
could reasonably be linked, directly or indirectly, to an
identified or identifiable individual.

(c) "Sensitive data" means personal data that reveals--
    (1) racial or ethnic origin;
    (2) religious or philosophical beliefs;
    (3) health, mental health, or disability status;
    (4) sexual orientation or gender identity;
    (5) biometric data used for identification;
    (6) genetic data;
    (7) precise geolocation data;
    (8) financial account information;
    (9) Social Security number or government-issued identifier;
    (10) data concerning a known child under age 17; or
    (11) private communications content.

(d) "Processing" means any operation performed on personal data,
including collection, recording, storage, adaptation, retrieval,
consultation, use, disclosure, dissemination, restriction, erasure,
or destruction.

SEC. 3. LAWFUL BASIS FOR PROCESSING.

(a) In General.--A covered entity may process personal data only
when at least one of the following conditions is met:
    (1) The individual has given affirmative express consent;
    (2) Processing is necessary for the performance of a contract;
    (3) Processing is necessary for compliance with a legal
        obligation;
    (4) Processing is necessary to protect the vital interests of
        any individual;
    (5) Processing is necessary for the performance of a task
        carried out in the public interest; or
    (6) Processing is necessary for a legitimate interest pursued
        by the covered entity, provided that such interest is not
        overridden by the interests, rights, and freedoms of the
        individual.

(b) Sensitive Data.--A covered entity may process sensitive data
only with the affirmative express consent of the individual, except
where processing is--
    (1) necessary to protect the vital interests of the individual;
    (2) required by federal or state law; or
    (3) necessary for the establishment, exercise, or defense of
        legal claims.

SEC. 4. INDIVIDUAL RIGHTS.

(a) Right of Access.--An individual shall have the right to obtain
from a covered entity confirmation of whether personal data
concerning them is being processed and, if so, access to such data.

(b) Right of Correction.--An individual shall have the right to
obtain correction of inaccurate personal data.

(c) Right of Deletion.--An individual shall have the right to
obtain deletion of personal data concerning them, subject to
exceptions for legal obligations, contract performance, and
archival purposes in the public interest.

(d) Right to Data Portability.--An individual shall have the right
to receive personal data they have provided to a covered entity in
a structured, commonly used, machine-readable format.

(e) Right to Object.--An individual shall have the right to object
to processing based on legitimate interest or public interest
grounds.

(f) Right Regarding Automated Decision-Making.--An individual
shall have the right not to be subject to a decision based solely
on automated processing that produces significant legal or
similarly significant effects, and shall have the right to obtain
human review of such decisions.

SEC. 5. DATA MINIMIZATION AND PURPOSE LIMITATION.

(a) A covered entity shall collect only personal data that is
adequate, relevant, and limited to what is necessary in relation to
the purposes for which it is processed.

(b) Personal data shall be collected for specified, explicit, and
legitimate purposes and not further processed in a manner
incompatible with those purposes.

SEC. 6. CROSS-BORDER DATA TRANSFERS.

(a) Transfers to Adequate Jurisdictions.--The Commission may
determine that a foreign jurisdiction provides an adequate level of
protection for personal data. Transfers to adequate jurisdictions
shall be permitted without additional safeguards.

(b) Transfers with Appropriate Safeguards.--In the absence of an
adequacy determination, a covered entity may transfer personal data
to a foreign jurisdiction only if appropriate safeguards are in
place, including--
    (1) standard data protection clauses approved by the
        Commission;
    (2) binding corporate rules approved by the Commission; or
    (3) an approved code of conduct or certification mechanism.

(c) Prohibition on Transfers Undermining Protection.--No covered
entity shall transfer personal data to a foreign jurisdiction for
the purpose of circumventing the protections of this Act.

SEC. 7. FEDERAL DATA PROTECTION COMMISSION.

(a) Establishment.--There is established the Federal Data
Protection Commission (the "Commission") as an independent agency.

(b) Composition.--The Commission shall consist of 5 commissioners
appointed by the President, by and with the advice and consent of
the Senate, for terms of 5 years, staggered. No more than 3
commissioners may be members of the same political party.

(c) Independence.--Commissioners may be removed by the President
only for inefficiency, neglect of duty, or malfeasance in office.

(d) Powers.--The Commission shall have authority to--
    (1) promulgate regulations implementing this Act;
    (2) investigate potential violations;
    (3) impose civil penalties up to the greater of $50,000 per
        violation or 4 percent of global annual revenue;
    (4) issue binding orders;
    (5) conduct audits and assessments;
    (6) approve standard data protection clauses, binding
        corporate rules, codes of conduct, and certification
        mechanisms; and
    (7) cooperate with foreign data protection authorities.

SEC. 8. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) Commission Enforcement.--The Commission shall enforce this Act
through investigation, administrative proceedings, and civil
actions in federal court.

(b) State Attorney General Enforcement.--State attorneys general
may bring civil actions on behalf of their state's residents to
enforce this Act.

(c) Private Right of Action.--
    (1) Any individual whose rights under this Act have been
        violated may bring a civil action in federal or state court.
    (2) A prevailing plaintiff may recover actual damages or
        statutory damages of $100 to $1,000 per violation for
        willful or reckless violations, and reasonable attorney's
        fees.
    (3) In actions involving willful violations affecting 100 or
        more individuals, a court may award statutory damages of
        up to $5,000 per individual per violation.

SEC. 9. PREEMPTION.

(a) Floor Preemption.--This Act establishes a floor of protection.
State laws providing greater protection to individuals are not
preempted.

(b) Conflicting State Laws.--State laws that directly conflict with
the requirements of this Act are preempted to the extent of the
conflict.

(c) Preservation of State Enforcement.--Nothing in this Act
shall be construed to limit the authority of state attorneys
general to enforce state consumer protection laws.

SEC. 10. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 24 months after the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**: The ADPA is modeled on GDPR principles adapted to American legal traditions. It establishes a lawful basis requirement for data processing, comprehensive individual rights, a dedicated enforcement agency with meaningful penalties, and mechanisms for cross-border data transfers. The floor-preemption approach preserves state innovation while creating a federal baseline.

**Potential Challenges**: Industry opposition to consent requirements and private right of action; preemption debate with states; First Amendment challenges to data collection restrictions; enforcement agency funding.

**Refinements**: Small business tiering could be added (exempting entities processing fewer than 100,000 records annually from certain requirements); journalism and academic research exemptions should be developed in regulation.

### The Surveillance Safeguards Codification Act

**Purpose**: Codify the surveillance reforms established by Executive Order 14086 and impose additional constraints on intelligence collection to support international data transfer frameworks.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Surveillance Safeguards Codification
Act".

SEC. 2. PROPORTIONALITY AND NECESSITY REQUIREMENTS.

(a) In General.--Signals intelligence collection activities
conducted under the authority of the Foreign Intelligence
Surveillance Act of 1978 or Executive Order 12333 shall be--
    (1) conducted only to the extent necessary to advance a
        validated intelligence priority; and
    (2) proportionate to the validated intelligence priority,
        balancing the intelligence value against the impact on the
        privacy and civil liberties of all persons, regardless of
        nationality.

(b) Bulk Collection Limitation.--Bulk collection of signals
intelligence shall be authorized only when targeted collection
methods cannot reasonably achieve the intelligence objective.

SEC. 3. DATA PROTECTION REVIEW COURT.

(a) Establishment.--There is established the Data Protection Review
Court as an independent body within the executive branch.

(b) Jurisdiction.--The Court shall have jurisdiction to review
complaints from any person, regardless of nationality, who
believes their personal data was collected through US signals
intelligence in violation of this Act.

(c) Independence.--Judges of the Court shall serve fixed terms of
6 years and may be removed only for cause. The Court shall have
access to all relevant classified information.

(d) Binding Decisions.--Decisions of the Court shall be binding on
the intelligence community and shall include appropriate remedies,
including deletion of unlawfully collected data.

SEC. 4. WARRANT REQUIREMENT FOR COMMERCIAL DATA PURCHASES.

(a) In General.--No element of the federal government shall
purchase or otherwise obtain personal data from a data broker or
other commercial source if such data would require a warrant,
court order, or subpoena to obtain directly from the individual
or the individual's electronic communications service provider.

(b) Exceptions.--Subsection (a) shall not apply to--
    (1) data that is freely available to the general public;
    (2) purchases necessary to protect against an imminent threat
        to life; or
    (3) purchases made pursuant to a warrant issued under the
        Federal Rules of Criminal Procedure or FISA.

SEC. 5. TRANSPARENCY.

(a) The Director of National Intelligence shall publish annually
a report detailing, to the extent consistent with national
security--
    (1) the number of persons targeted under FISA Section 702;
    (2) the number of US persons and persons of allied nations
        whose data was incidentally collected;
    (3) the number of queries of collected data using US person
        or allied-nation person identifiers;
    (4) the volume of commercial data purchased by intelligence
        agencies; and
    (5) the number and outcomes of complaints filed with the
        Data Protection Review Court.

SEC. 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect upon enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This Act codifies the key elements of EO 14086 -- proportionality, necessity, and the Data Protection Review Court -- making them statutory rather than executive, and therefore resistant to revocation by a future president. It also addresses the commercial data purchase loophole.

**Potential Challenges**: National security establishment opposition; classification issues limiting public debate; concerns about intelligence gaps.

**Refinements**: Could include a classified annex detailing specific surveillance program constraints; sunset and review provisions could be added.

## State Model Legislation

### Model State Privacy Alignment Act

**Purpose**: Enable states to align their privacy laws with federal legislation and international standards while preserving their authority to provide greater protections.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Privacy Alignment Act".

SECTION 2. ADOPTION OF FEDERAL BASELINE.

(a) The provisions of the American Data Protection Act (or
successor federal privacy legislation) shall serve as the minimum
standard for data protection within this State.

(b) This State retains authority to enact and enforce privacy
protections exceeding the federal baseline.

SECTION 3. STATE DATA PROTECTION AUTHORITY.

(a) The [Attorney General/State Privacy Office] shall serve as the
state data protection authority for purposes of enforcement.

(b) The state data protection authority shall cooperate with the
Federal Data Protection Commission and data protection authorities
of other states and foreign jurisdictions.

SECTION 4. INTERNATIONAL DATA TRANSFER ALIGNMENT.

(a) State agencies transferring personal data to foreign
jurisdictions shall comply with transfer requirements established
by the Federal Data Protection Commission.

(b) State agencies shall not transfer personal data to foreign
jurisdictions that have not received an adequacy determination or
provided appropriate safeguards.
```

**Explanation**: This model act ensures state laws complement rather than conflict with federal legislation while preserving state enforcement authority.

**Adaptations**: States with existing comprehensive privacy laws (California, Colorado, Connecticut, etc.) would need reconciliation provisions.

## Regulatory Framework

### Federal Data Protection Commission Regulations

**Existing Authority**: Statutory authority from the American Data Protection Act (proposed).

**Draft Regulation Areas**:

| Regulation | Purpose | Priority |
|-----------|---------|----------|
| Adequacy assessment criteria | Standards for evaluating foreign privacy frameworks | High |
| Standard data protection clauses | Pre-approved contractual terms for cross-border transfers | High |
| Transfer impact assessment guidance | Requirements for evaluating recipient-country legal frameworks | High |
| Consent standards | Defining "affirmative express consent" and consent mechanisms | High |
| Small business compliance guidance | Tiered obligations for entities with limited data processing | Moderate |
| Data breach notification procedures | Standardized notification timelines and content | Moderate |
| Automated decision-making standards | Requirements for human review and algorithmic transparency | Moderate |

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

- **First Amendment**: Data regulation must survive scrutiny under *Sorrell v. IMS Health*; narrowly tailored provisions addressing commercial data practices (not political speech or journalism) are most defensible
- **Commerce Clause**: Strong basis for federal legislation regulating interstate and international data flows
- **Fourth Amendment**: Warrant requirement for commercial data purchases aligns with *Carpenter* trajectory
- **Non-delegation**: Federal Data Protection Commission must have sufficiently detailed statutory guidance

### Preemption Questions

- **Floor preemption approach**: Federal law sets minimum standards; states may exceed but not contradict
- **California CCPA/CPRA**: Would need reconciliation to determine which provisions are "greater protection"
- **Sectoral law preservation**: HIPAA, FCRA, GLBA, COPPA preserved where they provide stronger protection in their specific domains

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- Federal Data Protection Commission: Investigations, administrative proceedings, civil penalties
- State attorneys general: Independent enforcement authority preserved
- Private right of action: Available for willful violations with statutory damages; actual damages for all violations
- International cooperation: Mutual assistance agreements with foreign DPAs

### Sunset and Review Provisions

- Statutory review every 5 years by Congress with GAO assessment
- Federal Data Protection Commission annual report to Congress
- Adequacy determinations reviewed every 4 years
- Standard data protection clauses updated as needed based on technological and legal developments

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### American Data Protection Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Legitimate interest basis | Broad interpretation of "legitimate interest" could allow extensive processing without consent, similar to industry exploitation under GDPR | High |
| Small business threshold gaming | Companies could structure operations to stay below processing thresholds | Medium |
| Consent fatigue | Frequent consent requests could desensitize consumers and render consent meaningless | Medium |
| Jurisdictional gaps | Entities not subject to FTC Act may fall outside "covered entity" definition | High |
| De-identification loopholes | Companies may claim data is "de-identified" when re-identification is feasible | High |
| Preemption ambiguity | "Greater protection" standard may generate litigation over which state provisions survive | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Enforcement depends on agency funding | Under-resourced Commission would be ineffective | Congressional appropriations process |
| 24-month implementation period | Gap between enactment and protection | Legislative design choice |
| No criminal penalties for executives | Individual accountability limited to civil penalties | Political resistance to criminal liability for data practices |
| Cross-border enforcement challenges | US courts may not enforce foreign DPA decisions | International comity limitations |
| Algorithmic transparency requirements may lag technology | AI systems evolve faster than regulation | Inherent regulatory challenge |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Define "legitimate interest" narrowly through regulation, with enumerated categories and a mandatory balancing test documented for each processing activity
2. Base thresholds on data processing volume across all related entities (anti-evasion provision)
3. Require layered consent notices with standardized privacy labels (similar to nutrition labels)
4. Expand "covered entity" definition to include all entities engaged in commercial data processing
5. Adopt robust de-identification standards requiring expert determination and prohibiting re-identification attempts
6. Include a statutory interpretation provision clarifying that in preemption disputes, the interpretation more protective of individuals controls

### Surveillance Safeguards Codification Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| "Validated intelligence priority" undefined | Executive branch could define priorities broadly to justify extensive collection | High |
| Public data exception for commercial purchases | "Freely available to the general public" could be interpreted to include most commercial data | Critical |
| Classified proceedings limit accountability | Data Protection Review Court proceedings are classified, limiting transparency | High |
| Allied-nation definition | Which nations qualify as "allied" may shift with geopolitical changes | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Intelligence community self-assessment of proportionality | Fox guarding henhouse | Operational necessity and classification |
| Limited scope of transparency report | National security exceptions may render reports uninformative | Tension between transparency and security |
| No retroactive application | Does not address data already collected | Legal constraints on retroactive legislation |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Define "validated intelligence priority" through a statutory list of authorized objectives, with requirements for Director of National Intelligence certification
2. Narrow public data exception to data that is published by the individual or by a government entity in the normal course of business; exclude commercial aggregation
3. Require unclassified summaries of Data Protection Review Court decisions
4. Define "allied nations" by reference to existing treaty obligations and mutual defense agreements, with annual review

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| Industry will litigate aggressively to weaken enforcement | Ensure statute includes clear congressional findings supporting constitutional authority; anticipate First Amendment challenges in legislative design |
| International regulatory fragmentation continues | Use US law as basis for bilateral and multilateral adequacy agreements; participate actively in international standards bodies |
| Technology outpaces regulation | Grant Commission authority to issue guidance and binding rules on emerging technologies; require privacy impact assessments for novel processing activities |
| Political vulnerability to future administrations | Statutory protections rather than executive orders; independent agency with removal-for-cause protections; bipartisan commission structure |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- American Data Protection Act: Comprehensive congressional review at 5-year intervals with mandatory GAO assessment; no automatic sunset (to provide regulatory stability)
- Surveillance Safeguards Codification Act: 7-year reauthorization requirement for intelligence collection authorities, with mandatory privacy impact assessment
- Federal Data Protection Commission: Annual report to Congress; Inspector General with independent reporting authority
- Cross-border transfer adequacy determinations: Reviewed every 4 years or upon material change in foreign legal framework
