# International Privacy: Root Causes

## Overview

The United States' failure to enact comprehensive data privacy legislation -- while more than 160 other countries have done so -- is not accidental. It results from a convergence of structural, political, economic, and cultural factors that have repeatedly blocked reform. Understanding these root causes is essential for developing strategies that can overcome them.

---

## Root Cause 1: Constitutional and Legal Framework Divergence

### The First Amendment as Privacy Obstacle

Unlike most democracies, the United States lacks a constitutional right to data privacy. The First Amendment's protection of free speech has been invoked to resist data regulation:

- **Data as speech**: Industry advocates argue that collecting, processing, and sharing data constitutes protected expression under the First Amendment
- **Sorrell v. IMS Health (2011)**: The Supreme Court struck down a Vermont law restricting the sale of pharmacy prescription data, holding that data restrictions are subject to heightened First Amendment scrutiny
- **Commercial speech doctrine**: Even commercial data practices receive some First Amendment protection, creating legal uncertainty for comprehensive regulation
- **Chilling effect arguments**: Industry claims that data regulation would chill innovation and information flows

### The Third-Party Doctrine

The Supreme Court's third-party doctrine -- holding that individuals lose their reasonable expectation of privacy in information voluntarily shared with third parties -- creates a legal environment fundamentally incompatible with European privacy principles:

- *United States v. Miller* (1976): No privacy interest in bank records
- *Smith v. Maryland* (1979): No privacy interest in phone call metadata
- *Carpenter v. United States* (2018): Narrowly limited the doctrine for cell-site location information but did not overrule it broadly
- The doctrine allows the government to acquire vast amounts of personal data without a warrant, which is precisely the concern that drives European courts to invalidate transatlantic data transfer frameworks

### No Constitutional Data Protection Right

| Jurisdiction | Constitutional Basis for Privacy | Implication |
|-------------|--------------------------------|-------------|
| EU | Charter of Fundamental Rights, Art. 7 (private life) and Art. 8 (data protection) | Privacy is a fundamental right that constrains all actors |
| Germany | Basic Law Art. 1 (dignity) + Art. 2 (personality) = informational self-determination (*Volkszahlungsurteil*, 1983) | Data protection flows from human dignity |
| United States | Fourth Amendment (search and seizure -- government only); no enumerated data privacy right | Privacy protections depend on legislation, not constitutional mandate |

## Root Cause 2: Industry Lobbying and Political Economy

### Technology and Advertising Industry Opposition

The US technology and digital advertising industries are the most powerful opponents of comprehensive privacy legislation. Their economic interests directly conflict with rights-based data protection:

| Industry | Annual US Revenue | Lobbying Spending | Key Trade Groups |
|----------|------------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Digital advertising | $225+ billion (2024) | $100+ million annually (tech sector total) | IAB, ANA, DMA, NetChoice |
| Technology (FAANG+) | $1.5+ trillion combined | Apple, Google, Meta, Amazon among top lobbying spenders | TechNet, CCIA, ITI, BSA |
| Data brokers | $200+ billion | Significant through trade associations | DMA (Data and Marketing Association) |

Source: OpenSecrets.org, lobbying data (2024); IAB, "Internet Advertising Revenue Report" (2024).

### Campaign Finance and Revolving Door

- Technology companies are among the largest campaign contributors to members of Congress on both commerce and judiciary committees
- Former congressional staffers from privacy-relevant committees frequently move to industry lobbying positions
- The FTC Chair position has been filled by individuals with backgrounds in both consumer advocacy and industry -- creating shifting regulatory priorities with each administration
- Industry-funded think tanks (e.g., ITIF, Progressive Policy Institute) produce research framing privacy regulation as economically harmful

### Divide-and-Conquer Strategies

The technology industry has deployed sophisticated strategies to prevent comprehensive legislation:

- **Pitting small business against big tech**: Framing privacy regulation as disproportionately burdening small companies
- **Preemption debates**: Using preemption of state laws as a wedge issue to prevent coalition-building between state-level advocates and federal reformers
- **Private right of action opposition**: Making the private right of action a dealbreaker, knowing consumer advocates insist on it
- **Sector-specific proposals**: Offering narrow, industry-friendly alternatives to comprehensive regulation

## Root Cause 3: National Security Establishment Resistance

### Surveillance as Structural Impediment

US intelligence and law enforcement agencies are a less visible but equally powerful obstacle to comprehensive privacy reform. Their interests conflict with the surveillance reforms that would be necessary for international compatibility:

- **FISA Section 702**: The intelligence community considers Section 702 essential for national security and opposes reforms that would limit collection of foreign persons' data (which incidentally captures American and European data)
- **Executive Order 12333**: Governs intelligence collection conducted outside the US; largely unconstrained by statute and unreviewable by courts
- **Data purchases**: Government agencies purchase commercial data specifically to avoid warrant requirements; comprehensive privacy law could constrain this practice
- **Mutual legal assistance**: MLAT processes are slow (months); agencies prefer direct access to data through surveillance authorities or commercial purchases

### The "Going Dark" Narrative

Intelligence and law enforcement officials consistently argue that privacy reforms would cause them to "go dark" -- losing access to critical intelligence:

- This argument has been deployed against every significant privacy reform proposal since the 1990s
- The FBI and DOJ have used it to oppose encryption standards, warrant requirements for digital data, and FISA reform
- Independent analyses (including by the President's Review Group on Intelligence and Communications Technologies, 2013) have found that claims of going dark are overstated

## Root Cause 4: Political Dysfunction and Fragmentation

### Congressional Gridlock

Several structural features of the US political system prevent privacy legislation:

| Factor | Description | Impact |
|--------|-------------|--------|
| **Committee jurisdiction** | Privacy touches commerce, judiciary, intelligence, and government affairs committees -- no single committee controls the issue | Turf wars prevent unified legislative action |
| **Preemption stalemate** | Industry wants federal law to preempt stronger state laws; consumer advocates oppose preemption | Disagreement on preemption has killed multiple bills |
| **Private right of action** | Consumer advocates insist on private lawsuits; industry and some moderates oppose | Single most contentious provision in every privacy bill |
| **Partisan fragmentation** | Neither party has made privacy a top legislative priority | Privacy consistently loses to other priorities |
| **Filibuster** | 60-vote threshold in Senate | Even bills with majority support may not pass |

### The ADPPA Example

The American Data Privacy and Protection Act (ADPPA) in 2022 illustrates how these dynamics prevent reform:

- Achieved bipartisan committee support -- the closest any comprehensive bill has come
- Died because of disagreements over preemption (California opposed losing its stronger CCPA/CPRA protections) and private right of action scope
- Demonstrated that even when stakeholders come close to agreement, structural obstacles prevent passage

## Root Cause 5: Cultural and Ideological Factors

### American Exceptionalism in Privacy

A distinctive set of cultural beliefs contributes to US privacy exceptionalism:

- **Market fundamentalism**: Deep-seated belief that markets self-correct, and that regulation harms innovation and economic growth
- **Individualism**: Expectation that individuals bear primary responsibility for protecting their own privacy (notice-and-choice paradigm)
- **Government skepticism**: Greater concern about government surveillance than corporate surveillance (the opposite of the European emphasis)
- **Innovation ideology**: Silicon Valley's narrative that privacy regulation would have prevented the creation of major technology platforms
- **Consumerism**: Willingness to trade personal data for free services, reinforcing the data-for-services business model

### Privacy Paradox

Research consistently shows that Americans express strong concern about privacy but take limited protective action:

| Finding | Source |
|---------|--------|
| 79% of Americans find current data practices unacceptable | Pew Research Center (2023) |
| 73% believe they have little to no control over their data | Pew Research Center (2023) |
| Only 5% have opted out of a major data broker | Consumer Reports (2023) |
| Majority continue using services with poor privacy practices | Various academic studies |

This paradox reduces political urgency: politicians perceive that voters care about privacy but will not make it a voting issue.

## Root Cause 6: Regulatory Fragmentation

### The Sectoral Approach and Its Consequences

The US approach of addressing privacy sector-by-sector has created a regulatory landscape that is simultaneously over-regulated in some areas and entirely unregulated in others:

| Sector | Governing Law | What It Covers | What It Misses |
|--------|--------------|----------------|----------------|
| Health | HIPAA | Covered entities (providers, insurers, clearinghouses) | Health apps, fitness trackers, data brokers selling health inferences |
| Financial | GLBA | Financial institutions | Downstream data sharing, fintech, commercial data |
| Children | COPPA | Websites/apps targeting children under 13 | Teens 13-17; social media platforms not "directed at" children |
| Credit | FCRA | Consumer reporting agencies | Marketing data brokers, people-search sites |
| Communications | ECPA/SCA | Government access to electronic communications | Outdated (1986); does not address modern data collection |
| Education | FERPA | Educational institutions receiving federal funds | EdTech companies, commercial testing services |

### No Comprehensive Gap-Filler

Unlike every other developed democracy, the US has no omnibus law covering the gaps between sectoral statutes. The FTC's Section 5 authority over "unfair or deceptive practices" is the closest approximation, but it:

- Requires proving deception or unfairness (high bar)
- Cannot set prospective rules without rulemaking authority
- Is resource-constrained relative to the scope of the digital economy
- Lacks the institutional expertise and independence of dedicated DPAs

## Root Cause 7: International Power Dynamics

### US Technology Dominance

US companies dominate the global digital economy, which creates unique incentives against privacy regulation:

- The top 7 global technology companies by market capitalization are all American (Apple, Microsoft, Alphabet/Google, Amazon, Nvidia, Meta, Tesla as of 2024)
- These companies have extensive data collection operations and business models that depend on lightly regulated data practices
- The US government views the competitive advantage of these companies as a national interest
- Privacy regulation is sometimes framed as European protectionism against American technology companies

### Geopolitical Considerations

- The US has resisted privacy harmonization partly because it would constrain intelligence-sharing within the Five Eyes alliance (US, UK, Canada, Australia, New Zealand)
- Data access is increasingly a dimension of US-China technological competition
- The US views data localization requirements (especially China's and Russia's) as threats to the open internet and US commercial interests
- These geopolitical considerations make comprehensive domestic privacy reform more complex

---

## Root Cause Interaction Map

The root causes reinforce each other in a self-perpetuating cycle:

1. **Constitutional framework** provides no mandate for comprehensive privacy law
2. **Industry lobbying** exploits the absence of constitutional mandate to block legislation
3. **National security establishment** opposes surveillance reforms needed for international compatibility
4. **Political dysfunction** prevents Congress from overcoming industry and intelligence community opposition
5. **Cultural factors** reduce political urgency by making privacy a low-salience voting issue
6. **Regulatory fragmentation** creates gaps that industry benefits from and defends
7. **International power dynamics** give the US incentives to maintain the status quo

Breaking this cycle requires addressing multiple root causes simultaneously -- comprehensive legislation must overcome industry opposition, reform surveillance authorities, navigate political fragmentation, and shift cultural expectations about privacy rights.
