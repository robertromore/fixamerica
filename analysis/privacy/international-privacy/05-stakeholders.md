# International Privacy: Stakeholders

## Overview

The international privacy landscape involves a complex web of stakeholders with competing interests across national boundaries. Technology companies, governments, advocacy organizations, and individual citizens all have stakes in how cross-border data flows are governed. Understanding these stakeholders -- their motivations, power, and relationships -- is essential for crafting viable reform strategies.

---

## Primary Stakeholders

### US Technology Companies

| Stakeholder | Interest | Power Level | Position |
|-------------|----------|-------------|----------|
| **Alphabet/Google** | Advertising revenue depends on data collection; global operations require stable transfer mechanisms | Very High | Supports federal privacy law that preempts state laws and provides compliance certainty; opposes strong private right of action |
| **Meta (Facebook/Instagram)** | Advertising model dependent on data; subject to largest GDPR fines; *Schrems* litigation directly involves Facebook | Very High | Supports DPF; lobbies for federal law to reduce compliance costs; subject of record 1.2 billion euro fine |
| **Apple** | Positions privacy as competitive advantage; less dependent on advertising data | Very High | Supports stronger privacy protections; has implemented App Tracking Transparency |
| **Amazon** | E-commerce, cloud services (AWS), advertising | Very High | Subject of 746 million euro GDPR fine; supports pragmatic compliance framework |
| **Microsoft** | Enterprise cloud services; significant government contracts | Very High | Generally supportive of privacy legislation; early supporter of GDPR-style principles |

### US Government Entities

| Stakeholder | Interest | Power Level | Position |
|-------------|----------|-------------|----------|
| **Intelligence Community (NSA, CIA, DIA)** | Maintaining surveillance capabilities under FISA 702 and EO 12333 | Very High | Opposes reforms that would constrain foreign intelligence collection |
| **Department of Commerce** | Administers DPF; promotes US commercial interests abroad | High | Supports frameworks that enable data flows; negotiated Safe Harbor, Privacy Shield, DPF |
| **Federal Trade Commission** | Consumer protection enforcement; limited privacy authority | High | Favors expanded authority; supports comprehensive federal privacy law |
| **Department of Justice** | Law enforcement data access; defends surveillance authorities in court | High | Opposes warrant requirements for commercial data purchases; defends FISA |
| **State Department** | Diplomatic relationships; human rights advocacy | Moderate | Balances trade, security, and human rights considerations |
| **State Attorneys General** | Enforce state privacy laws; protect state regulatory authority | High | Generally oppose federal preemption of stronger state laws |

### Foreign Regulators and Governments

| Stakeholder | Interest | Power Level | Position |
|-------------|----------|-------------|----------|
| **European Commission** | Protecting EU citizens' data; maintaining adequacy framework | Very High | Adopted DPF adequacy decision; monitors US compliance |
| **EU Data Protection Authorities** | Enforcing GDPR; reviewing cross-border transfers | Very High | Varying views on DPF adequacy; some DPAs more skeptical than Commission |
| **Court of Justice of the EU (CJEU)** | Interpreting EU fundamental rights in data protection context | Very High | Invalidated Safe Harbor and Privacy Shield; may review DPF |
| **UK Information Commissioner's Office** | Post-Brexit UK data protection; UK-US data bridge | High | Maintaining adequacy with both EU and US |
| **China Cyberspace Administration** | Data sovereignty; control of information flows | High | Imposes data localization; restricts cross-border transfers |

### Advocacy Organizations

| Stakeholder | Interest | Power Level | Position |
|-------------|----------|-------------|----------|
| **NOYB (None of Your Business)** | Max Schrems' organization; litigated *Schrems I* and *II*; plans DPF challenge | High | Argues DPF is inadequate; seeks comprehensive US surveillance reform |
| **Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF)** | Digital civil liberties | Moderate | Supports comprehensive privacy legislation with strong individual rights |
| **ACLU** | Civil liberties; surveillance reform | Moderate | Opposes mass surveillance; supports warrant requirements for data |
| **Center for Democracy and Technology (CDT)** | Internet policy; privacy rights | Moderate | Supports comprehensive federal privacy law with private right of action |
| **Electronic Privacy Information Center (EPIC)** | Privacy research and advocacy | Moderate | Supports strongest possible privacy protections |
| **Access Now** | International digital rights | Moderate | Advocates for global privacy standards aligned with human rights law |

### Industry Trade Groups

| Stakeholder | Interest | Power Level | Position |
|-------------|----------|-------------|----------|
| **US Chamber of Commerce** | Business interests broadly | High | Supports federal privacy law with preemption; opposes private right of action |
| **Internet Association (dissolved 2022) / NetChoice** | Internet company interests | Moderate-High | Opposes strong regulation; supports industry self-regulation |
| **BSA / The Software Alliance** | Software industry; enterprise tech | Moderate | Supports interoperable privacy frameworks; CBPR advocate |
| **Information Technology Industry Council (ITI)** | Technology companies broadly | Moderate | Supports federal privacy law; opposes data localization |

## Secondary Stakeholders

### Affected Populations

| Group | Interest | How They Are Affected |
|-------|----------|----------------------|
| **American consumers/citizens** | Privacy protection; control over personal data | Receive weaker protections than Europeans despite data processed by same companies |
| **EU/EEA residents** | Data protection when data transferred to US | Subject of repeated framework failures; uncertain whether current protections are adequate |
| **Employees of multinational companies** | HR data transferred cross-border | Employment data subject to different protections depending on transfer mechanism |
| **Small and medium businesses** | Compliance costs; access to cross-border markets | Disproportionately burdened by complex compliance requirements |
| **Academic researchers** | International data sharing for research | Privacy restrictions can impede cross-border research collaboration |
| **Journalists and dissidents** | Protection from surveillance | US surveillance powers can be directed at foreign journalists and activists |

### Other Institutional Stakeholders

| Stakeholder | Interest | Power Level |
|-------------|----------|-------------|
| **Multinational corporations (non-tech)** | Cross-border data transfers for operations (HR, finance, supply chain) | Moderate |
| **Cloud service providers** | Data hosting across jurisdictions; subject to data localization demands | High |
| **Financial services industry** | Cross-border data flows for transactions, compliance, fraud detection | High |
| **Healthcare and pharmaceutical companies** | International clinical trials, health data sharing | Moderate |
| **Universities and research institutions** | International research collaboration requiring data sharing | Low-Moderate |

## Stakeholder Power Analysis

### Power and Interest Matrix

| | High Interest in Privacy Reform | Low Interest in Privacy Reform |
|---|---|---|
| **High Power** | EU regulators, CJEU, NOYB, State AGs | Intelligence community, DOJ, some tech companies |
| **Low Power** | Consumer advocates, privacy researchers, affected individuals | Small businesses, general public |

### Key Power Dynamics

- **EU-US power asymmetry**: The EU has more leverage because it can restrict data flows to the US; the US cannot similarly restrict flows to the EU (since EU protections exceed US standards)
- **Industry-government alliance**: US technology companies and the intelligence community are often aligned in opposing comprehensive privacy reform, albeit for different reasons
- **State-federal tension**: California and other states with strong privacy laws resist federal preemption, splitting the reform coalition
- **Civil society limitation**: Privacy advocates have moral authority but limited financial resources compared to industry lobbying

## Stakeholder Interaction Dynamics

### Alliance Map

**Pro-Reform Coalition:**
- Consumer advocacy organizations (EFF, ACLU, CDT, EPIC)
- EU regulators (seeking US adequacy)
- Some technology companies (Apple, Microsoft -- selectively)
- State attorneys general (on enforcement authority)
- Academic researchers and privacy professionals

**Anti-Reform Coalition:**
- Data-dependent technology companies (advertising-supported)
- Intelligence community
- Department of Justice
- Industry trade associations (Chamber of Commerce, NetChoice)
- Data broker industry

**Swing Stakeholders:**
- Department of Commerce (supports stability, may support reform for adequacy)
- Federal Trade Commission (wants authority, but position depends on chair)
- Centrist members of Congress (support concept but divided on details)
- Small businesses (want simplicity; could support either preemption or status quo)

### Key Relationships

| Relationship | Dynamic | Impact on Reform |
|-------------|---------|-----------------|
| EU Commission -- US Commerce Dept. | Cooperative but under strain from repeated framework failures | Drives incremental improvements but not structural reform |
| Tech industry -- Congress | Heavy lobbying and campaign contributions | Blocks comprehensive legislation |
| Intelligence community -- Congressional oversight | Limited oversight effectiveness; deference to national security claims | Prevents surveillance reform needed for adequacy |
| NOYB -- CJEU | Strategic litigation to test frameworks | Creates external pressure for US reform |
| California -- Federal government | California defends stronger state protections | Preemption debates stall federal legislation |
