# International Privacy: Overview

## Executive Summary

The United States stands as a global outlier in its approach to data privacy. While more than 160 countries have enacted comprehensive data protection laws, the US relies on a fragmented patchwork of sector-specific federal statutes, state laws, and self-regulatory frameworks that leaves significant gaps in coverage. This discrepancy creates practical, economic, and geopolitical consequences: American companies face compliance burdens navigating dozens of foreign privacy regimes, cross-border data flows are subject to increasing legal uncertainty, and the US government's credibility in advocating for digital rights abroad is undermined by its failure to protect privacy at home.

The European Union's General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), which took effect in 2018, established the global benchmark for data protection law. Its extraterritorial reach -- applying to any organization processing EU residents' data regardless of where the organization is located -- forced American companies to adopt GDPR-compliant practices for European users while maintaining weaker protections for Americans. The invalidation of the EU-US Privacy Shield framework in 2020 (*Schrems II*) and the subsequent negotiation of the EU-US Data Privacy Framework in 2023 highlight the ongoing instability of transatlantic data flows caused by divergent privacy standards.

Beyond Europe, countries across Asia, Latin America, and Africa are adopting data protection laws that reflect GDPR principles. China's Personal Information Protection Law (PIPL), India's Digital Personal Data Protection Act, Brazil's LGPD, and Japan's amended APPI all impose requirements that exceed US federal law. As the global privacy landscape converges around a rights-based model, the American approach of relying on market forces and narrow sectoral laws becomes increasingly untenable for both US businesses and US citizens.

### Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Adequacy decision** | A determination by one jurisdiction that another jurisdiction's data protection laws provide an adequate level of protection, enabling unrestricted data transfers |
| **Data localization** | Laws requiring personal data about a country's residents to be stored and/or processed within that country's borders |
| **Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs)** | Pre-approved contractual terms that organizations can use to ensure adequate data protection when transferring data across borders |
| **Binding Corporate Rules (BCRs)** | Internal data protection policies adopted by multinational companies, approved by regulators, to govern intra-group cross-border data transfers |
| **Mutual Legal Assistance Treaty (MLAT)** | Bilateral agreements between countries for gathering and exchanging information for law enforcement purposes |
| **Extraterritorial application** | A law's reach beyond the jurisdiction's borders to cover conduct affecting its residents |
| **Data sovereignty** | The principle that data is subject to the laws and governance structures of the nation in which it is collected or stored |
| **Brussels Effect** | The phenomenon whereby EU regulations become global standards because multinational companies adopt them universally rather than maintaining separate systems |

## Core Tensions

- **Global data flows vs. national sovereignty**: The internet enables borderless data movement, but nations assert sovereignty over their citizens' data
- **Commercial interests vs. rights-based privacy**: US approaches privacy as a consumer protection issue; most other democracies treat it as a fundamental human right
- **National security vs. privacy rights**: US surveillance authorities (FISA Section 702, Executive Order 12333) create conflicts with foreign privacy regimes
- **Regulatory harmonization vs. regulatory competition**: Some argue uniform global standards reduce compliance costs, while others see divergent regulations as enabling regulatory competition
- **Extraterritoriality vs. comity**: The GDPR and CLOUD Act both assert extraterritorial jurisdiction, creating potential conflicts of law
- **Trade liberalization vs. data protection**: Digital trade agreements may constrain nations' ability to enact strong privacy protections

## Key Questions

1. Why has the United States failed to enact comprehensive federal privacy legislation when over 160 countries have done so?
2. How does the absence of US federal privacy law affect American companies competing internationally?
3. Is the EU-US Data Privacy Framework legally durable, or will it face the same fate as Safe Harbor and Privacy Shield?
4. How do US surveillance authorities (FISA 702, EO 12333) undermine transatlantic data transfer arrangements?
5. What would a US federal privacy law need to include to achieve an EU adequacy decision?
6. How do data localization requirements affect the global internet architecture and US technology companies?
7. Can the US maintain its current approach as the global privacy landscape converges around rights-based models?

## Vision of Success

A reformed US approach to international privacy would feature:

- **Comprehensive federal privacy law**: Legislation establishing baseline data protection rights aligned with global standards, enabling stable international data transfers
- **EU adequacy decision**: US law recognized by the European Commission as providing adequate data protection, eliminating legal uncertainty for transatlantic data flows
- **International harmonization leadership**: The US actively participating in multilateral efforts to harmonize privacy standards
- **Surveillance reform**: Meaningful constraints on US intelligence collection that satisfy foreign courts and regulators
- **Interoperable enforcement**: US participation in cross-border enforcement cooperation with peer regulators
- **Balanced data localization stance**: Clear framework for cross-border data flows that respects sovereignty while preventing unnecessary fragmentation
- **Trade-privacy coherence**: Digital trade agreements that preserve policy space for strong privacy protections
