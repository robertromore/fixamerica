# Encryption and Security: Actions

## Overview

This document outlines concrete steps that individuals, communities, organizations, and institutions can take to defend strong encryption and oppose backdoor mandates. Actions range from personal adoption of encrypted tools to systemic political advocacy.

## Individual Actions

### Use Encryption

| Action | How | Impact |
|--------|-----|--------|
| **Switch to end-to-end encrypted messaging** | Use Signal as your primary messaging app; encourage contacts to switch | Normalizes encryption; increases the user base that backdoor mandates would affect |
| **Enable device encryption** | Ensure full-disk encryption is active on your phone (iOS: default; Android: Settings > Security > Encryption) and computer (FileVault on Mac, BitLocker on Windows) | Protects your data from physical access |
| **Use encrypted email** | Use ProtonMail or Tutanota for sensitive communications, or GPG/PGP for existing email | Protects email content from interception |
| **Enable Advanced Data Protection** | On iPhone: Settings > [your name] > iCloud > Advanced Data Protection | Enables end-to-end encryption for iCloud backups |
| **Use encrypted cloud storage** | Use services with E2EE (Proton Drive, Tresorit) or encrypt files before uploading (Cryptomator, VeraCrypt) | Protects stored data from provider access and breaches |
| **Use a VPN** | Use a reputable, no-log VPN service (Mullvad, ProtonVPN, IVPN) for internet browsing | Encrypts internet traffic; protects from ISP surveillance |
| **Use HTTPS everywhere** | Ensure browser enforces HTTPS; use HTTPS-only mode in Firefox, Chrome, Safari | Encrypts web traffic between you and websites |
| **Use encrypted DNS** | Configure DNS over HTTPS (DoH) or DNS over TLS (DoT) in your browser or operating system | Prevents ISP from seeing DNS queries |

### Protect Your Security

| Action | How | Impact |
|--------|-----|--------|
| **Use strong, unique passwords** | Use a password manager (Bitwarden, 1Password, KeePassXC) with a strong master password | Prevents account compromise that could bypass encryption |
| **Enable two-factor authentication** | Use hardware keys (YubiKey) or authenticator apps (not SMS) for all important accounts | Adds a layer of protection beyond passwords |
| **Keep software updated** | Enable automatic updates for OS, apps, and firmware | Patches vulnerabilities that could undermine encryption |
| **Review app permissions** | Regularly audit which apps have access to your microphone, camera, location, and contacts | Limits data exposure that encryption cannot protect |
| **Be cautious with backups** | Ensure backups are encrypted; unencrypted cloud backups are a common way law enforcement accesses otherwise encrypted data | Closes the "backup loophole" |

### Educate Yourself and Others

| Action | How | Impact |
|--------|-----|--------|
| **Learn how encryption works** | Read EFF's Surveillance Self-Defense guide (ssd.eff.org); watch introductory videos on cryptography | Informed advocacy is more effective |
| **Explain encryption to friends and family** | Frame encryption as protecting bank accounts, medical records, and private conversations -- not just "something for criminals" | Builds public understanding and support |
| **Counter the "nothing to hide" argument** | Explain that encryption protects everyone: financial transactions, medical communications, attorney-client privilege, journalist sources, domestic violence survivors | Shifts the narrative from suspicion to protection |
| **Follow encryption policy news** | Subscribe to EFF's newsletter, follow policy developments at CDT and Access Now | Stay informed about legislative threats |

## Community Actions

### Education and Awareness

| Action | How | Target Audience |
|--------|-----|-----------------|
| **Host CryptoParties** | Organize community events teaching people to use encryption tools (Signal, GPG, password managers) | General public; journalists; activists |
| **Partner with libraries** | Work with public libraries to offer digital security and encryption workshops | Community members; older adults |
| **Create accessible explainers** | Develop materials explaining why encryption matters in everyday terms (banking, healthcare, personal safety) | Non-technical audiences |
| **Organize film screenings** | Show *Citizenfour* or other surveillance documentaries followed by discussion | General public |
| **Support digital security trainers** | Fund and volunteer with organizations that train journalists, activists, and vulnerable populations in encryption use | At-risk communities |

### Local Advocacy

| Action | How | Target |
|--------|-----|--------|
| **Advocate for encrypted government communications** | Push local and state governments to adopt E2EE for sensitive communications | City council, state legislators |
| **Oppose local surveillance expansion** | Monitor and oppose acquisition of surveillance tools that rely on encryption weakening | City council, police oversight boards |
| **Support municipal broadband with encryption** | Advocate for community-owned broadband with built-in encryption protections | Local government |
| **Engage school districts** | Advocate for encrypted communications between students, parents, and schools | School boards |

### Coalition Building

| Action | Partners | Goal |
|--------|----------|------|
| **Join local privacy coalitions** | EFF chapters, ACLU affiliates, civil liberties groups, tech meetups | Unified local voice for encryption protection |
| **Connect across political lines** | Libertarian and progressive groups share encryption concerns; build bipartisan coalitions | Broader political support |
| **Engage business community** | Small businesses depend on encryption for transactions and data protection; engage chambers of commerce | Business advocacy for encryption |
| **Partner with journalism organizations** | Press clubs, journalism schools, reporter groups depend on encryption for source protection | Professional advocacy |

## Organizational Actions

### For Technology Companies

| Action | Benefit | Urgency |
|--------|---------|---------|
| **Deploy end-to-end encryption by default** | User trust; competitive advantage; moral imperative | High |
| **Resist government access demands** | Protect users; maintain encryption integrity; preserve market position | Critical when demands arise |
| **Publish transparency reports** | Document government access requests and company responses | Ongoing |
| **Fund open-source encryption** | Strengthen ecosystem; demonstrate commitment; share costs | Medium |
| **Prepare litigation strategy** | Ready legal challenges to backdoor mandates before they pass | Proactive |
| **Support employees who raise encryption concerns** | Retain talent; foster security culture; prevent internal compliance with harmful mandates | Ongoing |

### For Businesses

| Action | Benefit | Urgency |
|--------|---------|---------|
| **Encrypt sensitive data at rest and in transit** | Regulatory compliance; breach protection; customer trust | High |
| **Adopt E2EE for internal communications** | Protect trade secrets; comply with confidentiality obligations | Medium |
| **Advocate against backdoor mandates** | Protect your business from increased breach risk; demonstrate customer commitment | When legislation threatens |
| **Educate employees on encryption** | Security culture; reduced breach risk; informed advocacy | Ongoing |
| **Include encryption requirements in vendor contracts** | Supply chain security; data protection | Medium |

### For Journalists and Media Organizations

| Action | Benefit | Urgency |
|--------|---------|---------|
| **Use Signal for source communications** | Source protection; legal compliance with shield laws | Critical |
| **Implement SecureDrop** | Anonymous, encrypted submission system for whistleblowers | High for investigative outlets |
| **Train all staff in encryption tools** | Organizational security; consistent source protection | High |
| **Report on encryption policy** | Public education; inform democratic debate | Ongoing |
| **Oppose backdoor mandates publicly** | Protect press freedom; demonstrate encryption's importance for journalism | When legislation threatens |

### For Civil Society and Nonprofits

| Action | Benefit | Urgency |
|--------|---------|---------|
| **Adopt encrypted communications** | Protect clients, beneficiaries, and organizational communications | High -- especially for organizations serving vulnerable populations |
| **Train staff and clients in encryption** | Security for at-risk individuals (domestic violence survivors, immigrants, whistleblowers) | High |
| **Advocate for encryption protection legislation** | Systemic change; protect all users | Ongoing |
| **Document encryption's importance for vulnerable populations** | Counter the "nothing to hide" narrative with real stories | Ongoing |

## Political and Advocacy Actions

### Federal Level

| Action | How | Target |
|--------|-----|--------|
| **Contact your members of Congress** | Call, write, and email opposing EARN IT Act, STOP CSAM Act, and similar anti-encryption bills; support the Secure Data Act and ENCRYPT Act | Senators and representatives |
| **Testify at congressional hearings** | Share technical expertise or personal experience with encryption's importance | Senate and House committees (Judiciary, Commerce, Intelligence) |
| **Submit comments on agency rulemaking** | Comment on any proposed rules that would affect encryption (FCC, DOJ, DHS) | Federal agencies |
| **Support organizations defending encryption** | Donate to and volunteer with EFF, ACLU, CDT, Access Now, Fight for the Future, Signal Foundation | Advocacy organizations |
| **Attend congressional townhalls** | Raise encryption as a constituent concern; ask representatives their position on backdoor mandates | Members of Congress |

### State Level

| Action | How | Target |
|--------|-----|--------|
| **Support state encryption protection bills** | Testify, write letters, organize constituent support for bills prohibiting state-level backdoor mandates | State legislators |
| **Oppose state anti-encryption measures** | Monitor and resist state bills that would undermine encryption or mandate scanning | State legislators |
| **Advocate for state government encryption adoption** | Push for state agencies to adopt strong encryption for citizen data | State CIO, governor's office |
| **Engage state attorneys general** | Urge AGs to oppose federal backdoor mandates and protect state residents' encryption rights | State AG offices |

### Voter Actions

| Action | When | Impact |
|--------|------|--------|
| **Evaluate candidates on encryption positions** | Every election cycle | Electoral pressure for encryption protection |
| **Support candidates who defend encryption** | Primary and general elections | Elect encryption-friendly officials |
| **Make encryption a campaign issue** | During campaigns; at candidate forums | Raises issue salience with candidates and media |
| **Vote in primaries** | Primary elections | Primaries determine party positions on encryption |

## When Encryption Is Threatened

### If Anti-Encryption Legislation Advances

| Step | Action | Resource |
|------|--------|----------|
| 1 | **Monitor bill status** | EFF Action Center (eff.org/action); CDT; Access Now |
| 2 | **Contact your representatives immediately** | Phone calls are most effective; be specific about the bill |
| 3 | **Share with your network** | Amplify alerts from EFF, ACLU, Fight for the Future |
| 4 | **Submit comments during public comment periods** | Personal stories about encryption's importance are powerful |
| 5 | **Attend rallies and events** | Physical presence at legislative buildings demonstrates constituent concern |
| 6 | **Contact media** | Write op-eds, letters to the editor, and social media posts explaining the threat |
| 7 | **Support legal challenges** | Donate to organizations preparing court challenges |

### If a Backdoor Mandate Passes

| Step | Action | Resource |
|------|--------|----------|
| 1 | **Support legal challenges** | EFF, ACLU, and technology companies will file First and Fourth Amendment challenges |
| 2 | **Use open-source, non-US encryption tools** | Signal, Tor, and other open-source tools may be difficult to effectively mandate |
| 3 | **Support companies that resist** | Choose products from companies that refuse to weaken encryption |
| 4 | **Advocate for repeal** | Continue political advocacy for reversal of the mandate |
| 5 | **Document harms** | Track and publicize security breaches resulting from weakened encryption |
