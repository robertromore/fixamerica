# Encryption and Security: Solutions

## Overview

Protecting encryption requires a two-pronged strategy: first, establish legal protections that prevent government-mandated backdoors; second, invest in alternative investigative techniques that allow law enforcement to work effectively in a world with strong encryption. The solutions below address both dimensions while also strengthening the broader cybersecurity ecosystem.

---

## Federal Legislative Protections

### Solution 1: Secure Encryption Act

**Description:** Federal legislation explicitly protecting the right to use and develop strong encryption, prohibiting mandated backdoors.

**Key Provisions:**

- Prohibit any federal, state, or local government from requiring encryption providers to design, build, or deploy any mechanism that weakens encryption
- Affirm the right of individuals and organizations to use encryption of any strength
- Prohibit court orders compelling the creation of security vulnerabilities
- Establish that encryption source code is protected speech under the First Amendment
- Preempt state laws mandating encryption backdoors

**Evidence:**

- The Secure Data Act has been introduced in multiple Congresses with bipartisan support
- Every major cryptographic organization supports protective legislation
- Australia's TOLA Act demonstrates the consequences of failure to protect encryption

**Challenges:**

- Opposition from FBI and DOJ
- CSAM framing creates political obstacles
- National security claims used to justify exceptions

### Solution 2: Vulnerability Disclosure Reform Act

**Description:** Statutory framework for government vulnerability disclosure, replacing the current executive-order-based Vulnerabilities Equities Process.

**Key Provisions:**

- Codify the Vulnerabilities Equities Process in statute
- Establish a presumption in favor of disclosure (vulnerabilities should be reported to vendors unless there is a compelling national security reason to retain)
- Create an independent oversight board with access to classified information
- Require annual public reporting on the number of vulnerabilities discovered, disclosed, retained, and used
- Mandate disclosure within 180 days unless the oversight board approves extension
- Create liability for government failure to disclose vulnerabilities that are subsequently exploited by adversaries

**Evidence:**

- Shadow Brokers leak demonstrated catastrophic consequences of vulnerability stockpiling
- WannaCry and NotPetya attacks used NSA tools, causing billions of dollars in damage
- Current VEP has no statutory basis, independent oversight, or accountability

### Solution 3: Law Enforcement Digital Investigation Investment Act

**Description:** Fund and develop alternative investigative techniques that work alongside encryption.

**Key Provisions:**

- Appropriate $500 million over 5 years for law enforcement digital investigation capabilities
- Fund research into lawful investigative techniques that do not require breaking encryption
- Establish training programs for federal, state, and local law enforcement on digital investigation methods
- Create a Digital Investigation Resource Center providing tools and expertise
- Fund academic research on lawful alternatives to exceptional access
- Support development of metadata analysis, financial tracking, and undercover operation capabilities

**Evidence:**

- Law enforcement investment in alternative techniques is minimal compared to advocacy for backdoors
- European law enforcement agencies have demonstrated effective digital investigation without backdoor access
- Academic research has identified numerous effective alternatives to encryption breaking

## International Engagement

### Solution 4: Global Encryption Leadership

**Description:** US leadership in defending strong encryption internationally, opposing backdoor mandates in allied countries.

**Key Components:**

- Establish US policy position explicitly supporting strong E2EE globally
- Oppose Five Eyes encryption-weakening communiques
- Condition trade agreements and technology partnerships on encryption protection
- Support international standards bodies (IETF, ISO) in maintaining strong encryption standards
- Provide diplomatic support for technology companies resisting foreign backdoor mandates
- Fund international civil society organizations defending encryption

**Evidence:**

- US silence on UK's Online Safety Act and Australia's TOLA Act has enabled backdoor proliferation
- Companies cannot maintain different encryption standards for different countries; mandates are global
- Authoritarian governments cite democratic countries' backdoor mandates to justify their own

### Solution 5: Post-Quantum Encryption Transition

**Description:** Accelerate the transition to quantum-resistant encryption standards to protect against future quantum computing threats.

**Key Components:**

- Fund NIST's post-quantum cryptography standardization (completed initial standards 2024)
- Mandate federal government transition to post-quantum algorithms by 2030
- Provide technical assistance to critical infrastructure for migration
- Fund academic research on quantum-resistant cryptography
- Establish a quantum readiness assessment for federal contractors

## Institutional Reforms

### Solution 6: Separate NSA Offensive and Defensive Missions

**Description:** Resolve the inherent conflict of interest in NSA's dual role by separating signals intelligence from cybersecurity defense.

**Key Components:**

- Elevate CISA to have authority equal to or exceeding NSA on domestic cybersecurity
- Create an independent body to adjudicate conflicts between offensive and defensive interests
- Establish a statutory "defense first" principle prioritizing US cybersecurity over foreign intelligence collection
- Mandate that NIST standards-setting be fully independent of intelligence community influence

**Evidence:**

- NSA's Dual_EC_DRBG backdoor undermined trust in US encryption standards
- CISA has consistently supported strong encryption while NSA has weakened it
- Other countries (Germany) separate offensive and defensive cyber missions

### Solution 7: Transparency and Accountability

**Description:** Require honest public accounting of law enforcement capabilities and encryption's actual operational impact.

**Key Components:**

- Mandate annual reports on encryption's impact on investigations with verifiable data
- Require GAO audits of "going dark" claims
- Create an independent technical advisory panel for Congress on encryption policy
- Fund academic research on the relationship between encryption and crime investigation
- Publish anonymized case data on investigations affected by encryption

---

## Technical Solutions

### Solution 8: Support Open-Source Encryption Development

**Description:** Federal investment in publicly auditable, open-source encryption tools.

**Key Components:**

- Fund Signal Foundation and similar open-source E2EE projects through grants
- Support development of user-friendly encryption tools for non-technical users
- Fund security audits of widely used open-source cryptographic libraries
- Establish a federal open-source encryption laboratory
- Support encryption standards development through open processes

### Solution 9: Encryption Education and Literacy

**Description:** Public education about encryption's importance for everyday security.

**Key Components:**

- Develop public education campaigns explaining encryption's role in protecting financial transactions, healthcare data, and personal communications
- Integrate cybersecurity and encryption literacy into K-12 education
- Provide encryption training for journalists, lawyers, and other professionals
- Create resources for legislators and their staff on encryption technology

## International Models

| Country | Approach | Key Features | Lessons |
|---------|----------|-------------|---------|
| **Germany** | Strong encryption protection | Federal BSI supports strong encryption; Constitutional Court protects "confidentiality of IT systems" | Demonstrates that democratic governance and strong encryption are compatible |
| **Netherlands** | Government position supporting encryption | Official government policy endorses strong encryption without backdoors (2016) | Small country can take principled position |
| **Finland** | No backdoor mandates | Strong cybersecurity culture; encryption protected | Nordic model prioritizes citizen security |
| **Switzerland** | Strong privacy and encryption | ProtonMail headquarters; strong legal protections | Neutral jurisdiction attracts privacy-focused companies |
| **Australia** | Backdoor mandate (TOLA Act) | Negative consequences: companies relocated, talent left, trust eroded | Cautionary tale of what to avoid |
