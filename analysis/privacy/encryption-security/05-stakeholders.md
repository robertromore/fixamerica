# Encryption and Security: Stakeholders

## Overview

The encryption debate involves a wide range of stakeholders with deeply held and often conflicting interests. The technical community is nearly unanimous in opposing backdoors, but law enforcement and intelligence agencies have institutional incentives to seek access. Technology companies are caught between user expectations, government demands, and market competition. Civil society organizations defend encryption as a fundamental right. Understanding these stakeholder dynamics is essential for navigating the policy landscape.

---

## Primary Stakeholders

### The Public

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Population** | 330+ million Americans; 5+ billion global internet users |
| **Interest** | Secure communications, financial transactions, personal data protection |
| **Power** | Diffuse; public opinion generally supports encryption but can be swayed by safety arguments |
| **Impact** | Highest -- everyone who uses digital communications, banking, healthcare, or commerce |

**Key Concerns:**

- Security of financial transactions and personal data
- Privacy of personal communications
- Protection from hacking, identity theft, and fraud
- Limited understanding of how encryption works or why it matters
- Susceptible to "nothing to hide" arguments

### Journalists, Activists, and Vulnerable Populations

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Population** | Tens of thousands of journalists; millions of activists; domestic violence survivors; political dissidents |
| **Interest** | Source protection, personal safety, freedom of expression |
| **Power** | Moderate -- moral authority; organized advocacy; public sympathy |
| **Impact** | Very high -- lives and safety depend on encrypted communications |

**Key Concerns:**

- Source confidentiality for investigative journalism
- Safety of domestic violence survivors communicating with shelters
- Protection of political dissidents in authoritarian-leaning environments
- LGBTQ+ individuals in hostile environments
- Whistleblowers communicating with oversight bodies

## Technical Community

### Cryptographers and Security Researchers

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Population** | Thousands of academic and industry cryptographers globally |
| **Interest** | Mathematical integrity of encryption; security of systems; academic freedom |
| **Power** | High -- technical authority; ability to demonstrate vulnerabilities |
| **Impact** | Critical -- their designs determine the security of global communications |

**Key Figures and Organizations:**

| Entity | Role | Position on Backdoors |
|--------|------|----------------------|
| Bruce Schneier | Cryptographer, author, Harvard Kennedy School fellow | Strongly opposed; "Keys Under Doormats" co-author |
| Whitfield Diffie | Co-inventor of public-key cryptography | Consistently opposed government access mandates |
| Matt Blaze | Cryptographer, Georgetown University | Discovered Clipper Chip flaw; opposed backdoors |
| Moxie Marlinspike | Signal creator | Designed Signal Protocol; opposed all access mandates |
| IACR (International Association for Cryptologic Research) | Academic cryptography society | Institutional opposition to backdoors |
| ACM (Association for Computing Machinery) | Computing professional society | Formally opposes mandated backdoors |

## Industry Stakeholders

### Technology Companies

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Population** | Major platforms, device manufacturers, security companies |
| **Interest** | User trust, market position, international competitiveness, compliance burden |
| **Power** | Very high -- control platform design, lobbying resources, public influence |
| **Impact** | Critical -- their engineering decisions determine encryption availability |

**Company Positions:**

| Company | Encryption Position | Key Actions |
|---------|-------------------|-------------|
| **Apple** | Strong E2EE advocate | Refused FBI backdoor order (2016); launched Advanced Data Protection; withdrew ADP from UK under pressure |
| **Meta (WhatsApp)** | Strong E2EE supporter | WhatsApp E2EE default (2016); Messenger E2EE default (2023); opposes scanning mandates |
| **Google** | Moderate E2EE supporter | RCS E2EE default (2023); supports encryption but cooperates with government on other access |
| **Microsoft** | Cautious | Some E2EE features; more compliance-oriented approach |
| **Signal Foundation** | Strongest E2EE advocate | Would exit any market mandating backdoors; threatened UK exit |
| **Amazon** | Limited public position | AWS encryption strong; Ring camera data provided to law enforcement without warrants (changed policy 2023) |
| **Proton (ProtonMail)** | Strong encryption advocate | Swiss jurisdiction; designed to prevent company access to user data |

### Cybersecurity Industry

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Population** | Thousands of cybersecurity companies; $180+ billion market |
| **Interest** | Strong encryption as business foundation; customer protection |
| **Power** | Moderate -- industry expertise; but split between offensive and defensive segments |
| **Impact** | High -- their products protect businesses and government systems |

## Government Stakeholders

### Law Enforcement

| Entity | Position | Key Arguments |
|--------|----------|---------------|
| **FBI** | Strongly opposes E2EE without government access | "Going dark"; responsible encryption; public safety |
| **DOJ** | Opposes E2EE; seeks legislative solutions | Lawful access mandates; EARN IT Act support |
| **State/local law enforcement** | Generally oppose E2EE | Case-specific access needs; CSAM investigations |
| **IACP (International Association of Chiefs of Police)** | Opposes "warrant-proof" encryption | Coalition advocacy for backdoor mandates |

### Intelligence Community

| Entity | Position | Key Concerns |
|--------|----------|-------------|
| **NSA** | Dual role: offensive (weaken) and defensive (strengthen) | SIGINT collection; counterterrorism; also secures government systems |
| **CIA** | Seeks offensive capabilities | Access to foreign communications; endpoint attacks |
| **ODNI** | Coordinates IC position | Balanced messaging; classified capabilities |
| **DIA** | Military intelligence consumer | Tactical SIGINT |

### Defensive Agencies

| Entity | Position | Key Concerns |
|--------|----------|-------------|
| **CISA** | Supports strong encryption | Critical infrastructure protection; national cybersecurity |
| **NIST** | Supports strong encryption standards | Standards integrity; post-quantum preparedness |
| **FTC** | Supports encryption as security measure | Consumer protection; data security |

## Stakeholder Power Analysis

| Stakeholder | Power Level | Resources | Organization | Influence on Policy |
|-------------|-------------|-----------|--------------|-------------------|
| NSA/Intelligence community | Very high | Classified budgets; executive branch access | Highly organized | Dominant (behind closed doors) |
| FBI/DOJ | Very high | Federal budget; congressional access | Highly organized | Very strong (public advocacy) |
| Technology companies | High | Massive lobbying budgets; public trust | Industry associations | Strong |
| Cryptographers | Moderate | Academic credibility; technical authority | Professional associations | Moderate (limited political access) |
| Privacy advocates | Moderate | Nonprofit budgets; moral authority | Well organized but small | Moderate |
| The public | Low-moderate | Diffuse; expressed through elections | Minimal organization on this issue | Low (outside crisis moments) |
| Journalists | Moderate | Public platform; institutional protection | Press freedom organizations | Moderate |

## Coalition Map

| Coalition | Members | Shared Interest | Strength |
|-----------|---------|-----------------|----------|
| **Pro-encryption** | Cryptographers, tech companies, privacy advocates, journalists, cybersecurity industry | Protect strong encryption; oppose backdoors | Strong; technically authoritative; growing public support |
| **Anti-encryption** | FBI, DOJ, NCMEC, some legislators, Five Eyes intelligence | Government access to encrypted communications | Powerful; institutional resources; emotional framing |
| **Defensive government** | CISA, NIST, some DOD elements | Strong encryption for national defense | Growing influence; cybersecurity awareness |
| **International pressure** | Five Eyes, EU regulators | Coordinated backdoor mandates | Significant; creates global momentum |
