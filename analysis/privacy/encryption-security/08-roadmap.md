# Encryption and Security: Roadmap

## Overview

This roadmap sequences encryption policy reforms across four phases, beginning with defensive legislative action to block backdoor mandates, proceeding to investment in alternative investigative techniques, and building toward a comprehensive encryption protection framework. Each phase builds on the previous one while responding to the ongoing legislative threat environment.

---

## Phase 1: Defense and Education (Years 1-2)

### Objectives

- Block current anti-encryption legislation (EARN IT Act, STOP CSAM Act)
- Build bipartisan coalition for encryption protection
- Educate Congress and the public on encryption's importance
- Fund initial law enforcement alternative investigations

### Key Actions

| Action | Lead Actors | Timeline | Success Metric |
|--------|-------------|----------|----------------|
| Defeat EARN IT Act and STOP CSAM Act | Tech companies, EFF, ACLU, CDT | Ongoing | Bills do not become law |
| Introduce Secure Encryption Act | Bipartisan sponsors (Wyden, Lofgren, Massie) | Year 1, Q1 | Bill introduced with 20+ co-sponsors |
| Launch public education campaign on encryption | Tech companies, civil society | Year 1, Q1-Q4 | 10 million Americans reached |
| Establish Congressional encryption advisory panel | Congressional leadership | Year 1, Q2 | Panel created with cryptographer membership |
| Commission GAO study of "going dark" claims | Congressional committees | Year 1, Q1 | Study commissioned |
| Fund law enforcement digital investigation training | DOJ, DHS | Year 1, Q3 | $100 million appropriated |
| Engage Five Eyes allies on encryption protection | State Department | Year 1 | Diplomatic engagement initiated |

### Milestones

- [ ] EARN IT Act and STOP CSAM Act blocked from floor vote
- [ ] Secure Encryption Act introduced with bipartisan support
- [ ] Public education campaign launched
- [ ] GAO study of "going dark" claims commissioned
- [ ] Congressional advisory panel established
- [ ] Initial law enforcement training funded

## Phase 2: Protective Legislation and Alternatives (Years 2-4)

### Objectives

- Pass the Secure Encryption Act
- Reform the Vulnerabilities Equities Process
- Fund alternative investigative capabilities
- Establish transparency requirements

### Key Actions

| Action | Lead Actors | Timeline | Success Metric |
|--------|-------------|----------|----------------|
| Pass Secure Encryption Act | Congress | Year 2-3 | Signed into law |
| Pass Vulnerability Disclosure Reform Act | Congress | Year 2-3 | Signed into law |
| Fund Law Enforcement Digital Investigation Center | Congress, DOJ | Year 2 | Center established |
| Publish GAO report on "going dark" claims | GAO | Year 2 | Report published with verifiable data |
| Mandate annual transparency reports on encryption's investigative impact | Congress | Year 3 | First report published |
| Begin post-quantum cryptography federal transition | NIST, OMB | Year 2-4 | Transition plan published |
| Negotiate international encryption protection agreement | State Department | Year 2-4 | Framework agreement |

### Milestones

- [ ] Secure Encryption Act signed into law
- [ ] Vulnerabilities Equities Process codified in statute
- [ ] Law Enforcement Digital Investigation Center operational
- [ ] GAO report published
- [ ] Post-quantum transition plan published
- [ ] International engagement produces results

## Phase 3: Institutional Reform and Global Leadership (Years 4-6)

### Objectives

- Reform institutional incentives that drive anti-encryption policy
- Establish US as global leader in encryption protection
- Strengthen cybersecurity infrastructure
- Evaluate and expand alternative investigative methods

### Key Actions

| Action | Lead Actors | Timeline | Success Metric |
|--------|-------------|----------|----------------|
| Elevate CISA authority on domestic cybersecurity | Congress | Year 4-5 | Legislative reform |
| Establish independent VEP oversight board | Congress | Year 4 | Board operational |
| Fund open-source encryption development | NSF, NIST | Year 4-6 | $200M+ in grants |
| Complete initial post-quantum federal migration | NIST, federal agencies | Year 5-6 | Critical systems migrated |
| Support international encryption protection treaty | State Department | Year 4-6 | Treaty framework |
| Evaluate law enforcement alternative methods | GAO, independent researchers | Year 5 | Evaluation published |
| Integrate encryption literacy into federal training | OPM, DOD | Year 4-6 | Training programs updated |

### Milestones

- [ ] CISA authority elevated
- [ ] VEP oversight board operational with independent membership
- [ ] Open-source encryption grants distributed
- [ ] Post-quantum migration underway
- [ ] International encryption protection framework

| Establish permanent encryption research program | NSF | Year 6-10 | Ongoing funding |

### Milestones

- [ ] 5-year review completed

- [ ] International framework strengthened

## Risk Assessment

| Risk | Probability | Impact | Mitigation |
|------|-------------|--------|------------|
| Major terrorist attack attributed to encrypted communications | Medium | Critical | Pre-position evidence on alternative methods; prepare rapid response messaging |
| EARN IT Act or similar bill passes | Medium | Very high | Build opposition coalition; presidential veto; court challenge |
| Five Eyes allies mandate backdoors | High | High | Diplomatic engagement; support companies resisting mandates |
| Quantum computing breaks current encryption faster than expected | Low-medium | Very high | Accelerate post-quantum transition; prioritize critical systems |
| Public opinion shifts against encryption after safety crisis | Medium | High | Sustained public education; prepare counter-narratives |
| Intelligence community undermines standards covertly | Medium | High | Independent oversight; transparency requirements; open-source standards |
| State-level backdoor mandates | Low | Medium | Secure Encryption Act preemption; federal court challenges |

## Dependencies

| Dependency | Impact if Not Met | Alternative Path |
|------------|-------------------|------------------|
| Bipartisan support for Secure Encryption Act | Protective legislation fails | Executive order; judicial protection (First Amendment) |
| FBI/DOJ cooperation on alternatives | Continued anti-encryption advocacy | Congressional mandate; funding conditioned on alternatives |
| International engagement | Allies mandate backdoors unilaterally | Company-level resistance; market pressure |
| NIST independence from NSA | Standards trust eroded | Open international standards; non-US standards bodies |
| Sustained funding for alternatives | Law enforcement claims worsen | Demonstrate return on investment |

## Measuring Success

| Metric | Baseline (2025) | Year 5 Target | Year 10 Target |
|--------|-----------------|---------------|----------------|
| Federal encryption protection law | None | Enacted | Strengthened |
| Anti-encryption bills introduced per Congress | 3-5 | <2 | 0 |
| Vulnerabilities disclosed vs. retained | Opaque | 80%+ disclosed | 90%+ disclosed |
| Law enforcement encryption-alternative funding | ~$50M/year | $200M/year | $300M/year |
| Post-quantum federal migration | Planning | 30% of critical systems | 95% of critical systems |
| Countries with backdoor mandates | 3+ (Australia, UK, India) | No additional | Existing mandates reversed |
| Public understanding of encryption importance | ~30% | 60% | 75% |
