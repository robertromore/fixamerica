# Children's Privacy: Actions

## For Parents

### Learn First

**Understand the Problem**

- [ ] Read [Current State](02-current-state.md) to understand how children's data is collected and monetized
- [ ] Read [Root Causes](04-root-causes.md) to understand why existing laws fail to protect children
- [ ] Review your children's apps and platforms -- read privacy policies (or summaries at Common Sense Media)
- [ ] Check what edtech tools your children's school uses and what data they collect

**Know Your Rights**

- [ ] Under COPPA, you can request deletion of your child's (under 13) data from any platform
- [ ] Under FERPA, you can review your child's educational records and opt out of directory information sharing
- [ ] Check your state's privacy laws for additional rights (California, New York, and other states provide extra protections)

### Take Action at Home

**Audit Your Family's Digital Footprint**

- [ ] Review privacy settings on all devices your children use
- [ ] Check app permissions (location, camera, microphone, contacts)
- [ ] Opt out of targeted advertising where platforms allow
- [ ] Consider using privacy-protective browsers and search engines for children
- [ ] Review and limit connected toy/device data collection settings

**Set Boundaries (Age-Appropriate)**

- [ ] Establish screen time guidelines aligned with American Academy of Pediatrics recommendations
- [ ] Use parental control tools as appropriate for younger children
- [ ] Have age-appropriate conversations about privacy with older children and teens
- [ ] Model good privacy practices yourself, including limiting "sharenting"

**Exercise Your Rights**

- [ ] Submit COPPA data deletion requests to platforms your children have used
- [ ] Opt out of directory information sharing at your children's school
- [ ] Request information about edtech data practices from your school district
- [ ] File complaints with the FTC if platforms violate children's privacy (ftc.gov/complaint)

### Take Action at School

**Engage with Your School District**

- [ ] Ask your school board what edtech tools are used and what privacy assessments have been conducted
- [ ] Request copies of data agreements between the school district and edtech vendors
- [ ] Advocate for school board policies requiring privacy impact assessments before adopting new technology
- [ ] Ask whether the district has a chief privacy officer or designated privacy contact

**Organize with Other Parents**

- [ ] Raise children's digital privacy at PTA/PTO meetings
- [ ] Form a parent working group on student data privacy
- [ ] Invite privacy experts to speak at school events
- [ ] Support school board candidates who prioritize student privacy

## For Educators

### In the Classroom

**Evaluate Edtech Privacy**

- [ ] Before using new tools, check privacy policies and Common Sense Media's privacy evaluations
- [ ] Use only district-approved technology that has undergone privacy review
- [ ] Minimize the personal data you ask students to enter into edtech platforms
- [ ] Be aware of what student data your tools collect -- even "free" tools have costs

**Teach Privacy Literacy**

- [ ] Incorporate age-appropriate lessons on digital privacy into curriculum
- [ ] Teach students how to evaluate app permissions and privacy settings
- [ ] Discuss how advertising and data collection work online
- [ ] Use resources from Common Sense Education's digital citizenship curriculum

### At the District Level

**Advocate for Policy**

- [ ] Support development of district-wide edtech privacy standards
- [ ] Advocate for privacy impact assessments before new technology adoption
- [ ] Push for data deletion policies when students leave the district
- [ ] Support professional development on student data privacy for teachers

## For Citizens (No Children Required)

### Contact Your Representatives

**Federal Action**

- [ ] Call your US Senators to support COPPA 2.0 and KOSA
- [ ] Call your US Representative to demand House floor votes on children's privacy bills
- [ ] Write to the FTC supporting strong COPPA rulemaking updates
- [ ] Submit public comments during FTC rulemaking proceedings (regulations.gov)

**State Action**

- [ ] Contact your state legislators about children's privacy legislation
- [ ] Support state-level age-appropriate design code legislation
- [ ] Oppose state bills that would preempt stronger local protections

**What to Say**

Key talking points:

- "Children deserve stronger privacy protections than adults, not weaker ones"
- "No child's data should be sold to data brokers or used for behavioral advertising"
- "Schools should not be required to hand over student data to edtech companies without privacy safeguards"
- "The Senate passed children's privacy protections with overwhelming bipartisan support -- the House should act"

### Support Organizations

**Join and Donate**

- [ ] Common Sense Media -- commonsensemedia.org
- [ ] Fairplay (Campaign for a Commercial-Free Childhood) -- fairplayforkids.org
- [ ] Electronic Privacy Information Center (EPIC) -- epic.org
- [ ] Center for Digital Democracy -- democraticmedia.org

**Volunteer**

- [ ] Sign up for action alerts from children's privacy organizations
- [ ] Participate in public comment periods for privacy regulations
- [ ] Share information about children's privacy issues on social media
- [ ] Attend town halls and ask representatives about children's digital privacy

### Spread the Word

**Educate Others**

- [ ] Share children's privacy information with parent groups
- [ ] Write letters to the editor about children's privacy legislation
- [ ] Post about children's privacy issues using factual, non-partisan framing
- [ ] Organize community screenings of documentaries about children and technology (e.g., *The Social Dilemma*, *Childhood 2.0*)

## For Technology Professionals

### In Your Workplace

**Advocate for Privacy-Protective Design**

- [ ] If your company's products are used by children, advocate for age-appropriate design principles
- [ ] Push for data minimization -- collect only what is necessary
- [ ] Advocate against dark patterns and manipulative design features
- [ ] Support privacy-by-default settings for all users

**Build Responsibly**

- [ ] Learn the UK Age Appropriate Design Code's 15 standards and apply them
- [ ] Implement privacy impact assessments for products likely accessed by children
- [ ] Design deletion mechanisms that actually work
- [ ] Test products with child safety experts, not just user experience researchers

### In the Broader Community

**Share Your Expertise**

- [ ] Contribute to open-source privacy tools for children and families
- [ ] Speak at school and community events about how technology works
- [ ] Participate in standards development (IEEE, W3C privacy standards)
- [ ] Support journalists investigating children's privacy practices by providing technical expertise

## For State and Local Officials

### Executive Action

- [ ] Direct state AG to investigate children's privacy violations by major platforms
- [ ] Issue executive guidance on edtech procurement privacy standards for state-funded schools
- [ ] Support legislation establishing state-level age-appropriate design requirements
- [ ] Fund student privacy audits in public school districts

### Legislative Action

- [ ] Introduce or support children's privacy legislation modeled on successful state laws
- [ ] Resist industry efforts to weaken children's privacy protections through preemption
- [ ] Appropriate funding for state-level privacy enforcement
- [ ] Require state agencies to assess children's privacy impacts of government technology contracts
