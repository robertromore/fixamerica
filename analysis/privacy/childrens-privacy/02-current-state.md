# Children's Privacy: Current State

## The Problem Today

### A Generation Under Surveillance

American children live in a digital environment designed to extract maximum data and attention from them. From birth announcements on social media to classroom edtech tools to teen social media accounts, children generate vast quantities of personal data that is collected, profiled, and monetized -- often without meaningful consent from parents or understanding from the children themselves.

**Scale of the Problem**

- 95% of US children ages 3-18 have internet access at home (Census Bureau, American Community Survey, 2023)
- Teens spend an average of 8 hours 39 minutes daily on screens outside of schoolwork (Common Sense Media, 2024)
- 95% of teens ages 13-17 use at least one social media platform (Pew Research Center, 2024)
- An estimated 40% of children ages 8-12 use social media platforms despite age restrictions (Common Sense Media, 2024)
- The average school district uses over 1,400 unique edtech products (LearnPlatform/Instructure, 2024)

## The Legal Framework

### Federal Law: COPPA

The Children's Online Privacy Protection Act (15 U.S.C. 6501-6506), enacted in 1998 and most recently updated by FTC rulemaking in 2013, is the primary federal law governing children's online privacy.

**What COPPA Requires**

| Requirement | Detail |
|-------------|--------|
| Age threshold | Applies to children under 13 |
| Verifiable parental consent | Required before collecting personal information |
| Privacy policy | Must post clear, comprehensive privacy policy |
| Data minimization | Cannot condition participation on unnecessary data collection |
| Data security | Must maintain reasonable security for children's data |
| Data deletion | Must delete data at parent's request |
| Third-party disclosure limits | Cannot disclose children's data to third parties without consent |

**What COPPA Does Not Cover**

| Gap | Consequence |
|-----|-------------|
| Teens 13-17 | No special federal privacy protections |
| General audience sites | Only applies to sites "directed to children" or with "actual knowledge" of child users |
| Schools and edtech | FERPA governs, but with weaker protections than COPPA |
| Connected devices | IoT toys and devices often fall outside enforcement focus |
| Foreign-operated platforms | Enforcement difficult for companies outside US jurisdiction |
| Inferred data | COPPA focuses on collected data, not data derived from behavior |

### Federal Law: FERPA

The Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act (20 U.S.C. 1232g) governs student educational records.

**Key Provisions**

- Parents have the right to access and amend student records
- Schools cannot disclose personally identifiable information without consent
- Exception: "School officials" with "legitimate educational interest" (broadly interpreted)
- Exception: "Directory information" (name, address, phone) can be shared unless parent opts out
- No private right of action -- enforcement is solely through federal funding threats

**FERPA's Weaknesses**

- The "school official" exception has been expanded to cover edtech vendors
- No meaningful enforcement mechanism -- the Department of Education has never withdrawn funding
- Does not cover most data generated by edtech platforms
- Predates cloud computing, AI, and modern data analytics

### State Laws

| State | Law | Key Provisions | Effective |
|-------|-----|----------------|-----------|
| California | Age-Appropriate Design Code Act (AB 2273) | Data protection impact assessments for children; default high privacy settings; ban on profiling children by default | 2024 (enjoined, litigation ongoing) |
| California | CCPA/CPRA amendments | Opt-in consent required for selling/sharing data of consumers under 16 | 2023 |
| Utah | Social Media Regulation Act (SB 152) | Parental consent for minors' social media; age verification; curfew features | 2024 (partially enjoined) |
| Texas | Securing Children Online through Parental Empowerment Act (HB 18) | Parental consent for minors on social media; restrictions on targeted advertising to minors | 2024 |
| Arkansas | Social Media Safety Act (SB 396) | Age verification for social media; parental consent for minors | 2023 (enjoined) |
| Maryland | Age-Appropriate Design Code (SB 571) | Modeled on California and UK codes; DPIA requirements | 2024 |
| New York | SAFE for Kids Act (S.7694/A.8148) | Restricts addictive algorithmic feeds for minors | 2024 |
| Louisiana | Act 440 | Age verification for websites hosting material harmful to minors | 2023 |

### International Comparison

| Jurisdiction | Framework | Key Features |
|--------------|-----------|--------------|
| European Union | GDPR Article 8 / Digital Services Act | Parental consent required for processing children's data (age varies 13-16 by member state); platforms must verify age; ban on targeted advertising to minors |
| United Kingdom | Age Appropriate Design Code (2021) | 15 standards for platforms likely accessed by children; privacy by default; no profiling; no nudge techniques; upheld by ICO enforcement |
| Australia | Online Safety Act (2021) / Privacy Act reforms | Online safety expectations; age verification trials; proposed minimum age for social media |
| South Korea | Youth Protection Act amendments | Real-name age verification; gaming curfews (repealed 2021); advertising restrictions |

## Platform Practices

### Social Media

**Meta (Instagram, Facebook)**

- Introduced "teen accounts" with default privacy restrictions in 2024
- Still collects behavioral data from teen users for ad targeting
- Under-13 children routinely create accounts with false ages; detection efforts are limited
- FTC fined Meta $5 billion in 2019 (partly for COPPA violations via Facebook) and proposed new restrictions in 2024 order
- Instagram linked to teen mental health harms per internal research disclosed by whistleblower Frances Haugen (2021)

**TikTok**

- Paid $5.7 million COPPA fine in 2019 (as Musical.ly)
- Created restricted "under 13" experience with limited functionality
- Algorithmic recommendation system raises concerns about content exposure for teens
- FTC and DOJ proposed consent order in 2024 alleging ongoing COPPA violations
- Under investigation for data transfers to China involving children's data

**YouTube/Google**

- FTC fined Google $170 million in 2019 for COPPA violations on YouTube
- Created YouTube Kids as separate platform; separated from main YouTube
- Google Workspace for Education collects student data with limited COPPA/FERPA oversight
- Continued tracking of minors via Android devices documented by researchers

**Snapchat**

- Settled FTC complaint in 2014 over deceptive privacy claims
- "My AI" chatbot available to minors raises data collection concerns
- Snap Map location sharing among minors creates safety risks

### Educational Technology

**Scope of Collection**

- K-12 schools have rapidly adopted edtech, accelerated by COVID-19 pandemic remote learning
- Google Classroom, Canvas, Clever, and similar platforms process data on tens of millions of students
- Student data includes assignments, grades, behavioral assessments, browsing history, and biometric data (some platforms)
- Student Privacy Compass estimates 95% of surveyed teachers use edtech in classrooms daily (2024)

**Key Concerns**

| Concern | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Scope of data | Edtech platforms collect behavioral, academic, and sometimes biometric data |
| Secondary use | Data collected for educational purposes is sometimes used for product development or advertising |
| Vendor contracts | School-vendor agreements often lack meaningful privacy protections |
| Student Awareness | Students are rarely informed about what data is collected or how it is used |
| Long-term retention | Many platforms retain student data indefinitely |
| Security | Multiple data breaches at edtech companies have exposed student records |

**Notable Incidents**

- Illuminate Education breach (2022): Exposed data on 820,000+ current and former New York City students
- Chegg breach (2018-2019): Student data exposed in multiple incidents; FTC enforcement action followed
- PowerSchool breach (2024): Affected students across thousands of districts nationally

### Connected Toys and Devices

**Market Overview**

- Smart toys, children's tablets, GPS watches, and connected baby monitors generate data about children
- Products like Amazon Echo Dot Kids, smart watches, and interactive toys collect voice recordings, location data, and usage patterns
- VTech data breach (2015) exposed personal data on 6.4 million children

**Regulatory Gaps**

- COPPA applies but enforcement is challenging across thousands of products
- FTC has taken limited action (e.g., VTech, Amazon/Ring settlements)
- Many devices manufactured overseas with minimal privacy protections

## Recent Developments

### Federal (2023-2025)

- **COPPA 2.0 (S. 1628)**: Passed Senate Commerce Committee in 2024; would extend protections to teens under 17, ban targeted advertising to minors, create an "Eraser Button" for minors' data. Stalled in House.
- **Kids Online Safety Act (KOSA) (S. 1409)**: Passed Senate 91-3 in July 2024; would require platforms to enable safeguards for minors and conduct audits. Stalled in House. Reintroduced in 119th Congress.
- **FTC COPPA rulemaking (2024)**: Proposed rule to update COPPA regulations, including expanding definition of personal information, strengthening consent requirements, and limiting data retention. Final rule pending.
- **Surgeon General's Advisory (2023)**: US Surgeon General Vivek Murthy issued advisory on social media and youth mental health, calling for legislative action.
- **Surgeon General's call for warning labels (2024)**: Murthy called for tobacco-style warning labels on social media platforms for youth.

### State Level (2023-2025)

- California's Age-Appropriate Design Code Act challenged by NetChoice; preliminary injunction issued by federal court citing First Amendment concerns; appealed to Ninth Circuit
- New York enacted the SAFE for Kids Act and Child Data Protection Act in 2024
- Multiple states introduced or enacted social media age verification requirements, facing ongoing legal challenges
- Texas HB 18 restrictions on minors' social media took effect in 2024

### Industry

- Meta launched "teen accounts" on Instagram with parental supervision features (2024)
- Google expanded parental controls and age-gating across products
- Apple introduced Communication Safety features and expanded Screen Time tools
- Industry trade group NetChoice has challenged multiple state children's privacy laws in court, winning preliminary injunctions in several cases

## The Stakes

**If current trajectory continues:**

- Children's behavioral profiles will follow them for life, influencing employment, insurance, and credit
- Algorithmic manipulation will continue to harm children's mental health and development
- The gap between COPPA's protections and the reality of children's digital lives will widen
- Predators will continue to exploit platforms' inadequate safety measures
- Educational data will be increasingly commercialized without student or parent awareness
- US children will remain less protected than their peers in the EU, UK, and other democracies

**If meaningful reform is enacted:**

- Children's data will be collected only when necessary and with genuine consent
- Platforms will be designed with children's wellbeing as a priority, not an afterthought
- Parents and teens will have real tools to control their digital privacy
- Educational technology will serve students without exploiting their data
- A generation of digital citizens will grow up with a meaningful right to privacy
