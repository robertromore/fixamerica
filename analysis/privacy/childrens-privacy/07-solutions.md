# Children's Privacy: Solutions

## Guiding Principles

### Criteria for Evaluating Reforms

| Criterion | Definition | Why It Matters |
|-----------|------------|----------------|
| **Protects children effectively** | Meaningfully reduces data collection and exploitation | The primary goal of any reform |
| **Covers all minors** | Extends protections to teens, not just children under 13 | The teen privacy gap is the most critical deficiency |
| **Minimizes surveillance** | Does not create new surveillance infrastructure to protect privacy | Age verification can itself threaten privacy |
| **Preserves teen autonomy** | Protects teens from both corporate exploitation and excessive parental control | Older teens need independent access to information and services |
| **Survives legal challenge** | Consistent with First Amendment doctrine | Reforms struck down by courts protect no one |
| **Enforceable** | Clear standards, adequate resources, meaningful penalties | Paper protections without enforcement are meaningless |
| **Technologically adaptable** | Framework can evolve as technology changes | Laws that target specific technologies become obsolete |

### The Central Tension

Protecting children's privacy requires balancing competing values:

- **Protection vs. access**: Restricting data collection is essential, but overly restrictive measures could limit children's access to beneficial technology
- **Safety vs. surveillance**: Age verification protects children but creates identity databases
- **Parental authority vs. teen autonomy**: Parents need tools to protect younger children, but teenagers need privacy -- including sometimes from their parents

The most effective approach focuses on **regulating industry conduct** (what companies can collect and do with children's data) rather than **restricting children's access** (what children can see or do online).

## Policy Proposals

### Tier 1: Update and Expand COPPA

**COPPA 2.0: Extend Age Coverage to 17**

*Core Reform*

- Raise COPPA's effective age threshold from under 13 to under 17
- Create tiered protections: strongest for children under 13, substantial for teens 13-16
- Maintain parental consent for under-13; require teen assent plus parental notification for 13-16
- At age 17, transition to adult privacy protections (with data deletion rights for childhood data)

*Key Provisions*

| Provision | Under 13 | Ages 13-16 |
|-----------|----------|------------|
| Data collection | Verifiable parental consent required | Informed teen assent required; parental notification |
| Behavioral advertising | Prohibited | Prohibited |
| Data sales to third parties | Prohibited | Prohibited without explicit opt-in |
| Data retention | Minimum necessary; deletion upon request | Limited retention; deletion upon request |
| Push notifications | Prohibited without parental consent | Default off; must opt in |
| Algorithmic recommendations | Only age-appropriate, non-manipulative | Chronological default; algorithmic opt-in |

*Advantages*

- Builds on existing legal framework with 25 years of enforcement history
- Addresses the teen privacy gap, the most critical deficiency
- Bipartisan support demonstrated by Senate votes
- Does not require age verification for all users if combined with design-code approach

*Challenges*

- Defining tiered consent mechanisms for different age groups
- Enforcement complexity increases with age extension
- Industry will argue compliance costs are excessive

**Close the "Actual Knowledge" Loophole**

- Replace "actual knowledge" standard with "constructive knowledge" or "should have known" standard
- Platforms that have reason to believe users are minors (based on content, behavioral signals, or other indicators) must comply with children's privacy protections
- FTC has proposed this change in 2024 rulemaking

### Tier 2: Age-Appropriate Design Requirements

**Design Code Modeled on UK ICO Code**

*Core Framework*

Require platforms likely to be accessed by children to adopt age-appropriate design standards as the default, rather than requiring identification of individual children's ages.

*Key Standards*

| Standard | Requirement |
|----------|-------------|
| Best interests of the child | Platform design must prioritize children's wellbeing |
| Data minimization | Collect only data strictly necessary for the service |
| Privacy by default | Highest privacy settings as default for all users likely to be children |
| No profiling by default | Behavioral profiling of children prohibited unless compelling justification |
| No detrimental use | Data cannot be used in ways detrimental to children's wellbeing |
| No nudge techniques | Dark patterns prohibited in children's contexts |
| Connected toys and devices | Physical products must meet same standards |
| Transparency | Privacy information provided in language children can understand |
| Data protection impact assessments | Required before launching products likely accessed by children |

*Implementation Approach*

- FTC develops detailed guidance through rulemaking
- Platforms conduct data protection impact assessments and publish summaries
- Compliance demonstrated through design documentation, not individual age verification
- Enforcement through FTC, state AGs, and private right of action

*Advantages*

- Avoids the need for individual age verification by setting high defaults for all users
- Proven framework: UK code has been effective since 2021
- Addresses platform design, not just data collection
- Adaptable to new technologies

*Challenges*

- NetChoice litigation strategy has targeted similar state laws
- First Amendment challenges to design mandates (though these are weaker than challenges to content restrictions)
- Defining "likely to be accessed by children" requires judgment

### Tier 3: Ban Behavioral Advertising to Children

**Prohibition on Surveillance-Based Advertising to Minors**

*Core Requirement*

- Prohibit targeted advertising based on behavioral profiling for all users under 17
- Allow contextual advertising (ads based on the content being viewed, not the user's profile)
- Prohibit dark patterns designed to extract data or purchases from minors
- Require clear labeling of all advertising displayed to minors

*Economic Impact*

| Factor | Assessment |
|--------|------------|
| Revenue impact on platforms | Significant but manageable -- contextual advertising generates revenue without profiling |
| Impact on advertisers | Reduced targeting precision; shift to contextual and brand advertising |
| Impact on children | Reduced manipulation; less data collection; maintained access to free services |
| International precedent | EU Digital Services Act prohibits targeted advertising to minors (effective 2024) |

*Advantages*

- Directly addresses the economic incentive to collect children's data
- Does not restrict children's access to content or services
- Contextual advertising preserves ad-supported business model
- Strong precedent in EU regulation

### Tier 4: Educational Technology Safeguards

**Student Data Protection Act**

*Core Provisions*

- Prohibit edtech companies from using student data for non-educational purposes
- Require school districts to conduct privacy assessments before adopting edtech tools
- Mandate data deletion when student leaves school or district
- Require edtech companies to meet minimum data security standards
- Create transparency requirements: parents must be informed of all edtech data collection
- Establish private right of action for student data breaches

*FERPA Modernization*

- Add data security requirements
- Create meaningful enforcement mechanism beyond funding withdrawal
- Narrow the "school official" exception to require specific contractual protections
- Add private right of action for FERPA violations
- Require annual data inventories by school districts

### Tier 5: Children's Data Deletion and Portability Rights

**The "Eraser Button"**

*Core Provisions*

- Right for minors (and young adults for data collected as minors) to request deletion of all data
- Platforms must respond within 30 days
- Right applies to primary platform and any third parties that received the data
- No fees or excessive verification burdens for deletion requests
- Young adults (18-21) can request deletion of data collected during childhood

*Implementation*

- FTC establishes standardized deletion request mechanisms
- Platforms must maintain records of third-party data transfers to enable comprehensive deletion
- Annual compliance reporting to FTC

### Tier 6: Enforcement Infrastructure

**Dedicated Enforcement**

*Options*

| Option | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|--------|-----------|---------------|
| FTC Bureau of Children's Digital Privacy | Leverages existing expertise; can act quickly | FTC resource constraints; competing priorities |
| Standalone Children's Digital Privacy Agency | Dedicated focus; independent authority | Requires new legislation and appropriation |
| Enhanced state AG authority | State-level enforcement closer to affected communities | Inconsistency; resource variation among states |

*Recommended Approach*

- Create a new FTC Division of Children's Digital Privacy with dedicated staff and budget
- Authorize state AGs to enforce federal children's privacy law
- Establish private right of action with statutory damages
- Increase civil penalties: minimum $50,000 per violation with no aggregate cap
- Require platforms with over 1 million minor users to fund independent auditors

## Comparative Models

### United Kingdom: Age Appropriate Design Code

**Results Since Implementation (2021)**

- Major platforms made global changes to children's products (TikTok, Instagram, YouTube, Snapchat)
- Default privacy settings strengthened for children
- Geolocation, profiling, and nudge techniques restricted
- No evidence of innovation suppression
- Enforcement by Information Commissioner's Office (ICO) has been measured but effective

**Lessons for the US**

- Design-code approach avoids age verification problems
- Principles-based standards are adaptable to new technologies
- Industry compliance was faster than expected once the law was enforceable

### European Union: GDPR + Digital Services Act

**Key Features**

- GDPR Article 8: Parental consent for processing children's data (age varies 13-16 by member state)
- Digital Services Act (2024): Bans targeted advertising to minors; requires platforms to assess systemic risks to minors
- Right to erasure applies to childhood data

### South Korea

- Strict age verification for online services (real-name system)
- Gaming curfew for minors (repealed in 2021 due to effectiveness concerns)
- Lessons: Heavy-handed age verification creates burdens; focus on data practices is more effective
