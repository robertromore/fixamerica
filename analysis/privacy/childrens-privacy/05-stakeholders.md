# Children's Privacy: Stakeholders

## Who Benefits from the Status Quo

### Technology Platforms

**Who They Are**

- Meta (Instagram, Facebook, WhatsApp)
- Alphabet/Google (YouTube, Google Classroom, Android)
- ByteDance (TikTok)
- Snap Inc. (Snapchat)
- Apple (to a lesser extent -- hardware/subscription model)
- Roblox, Epic Games, and other gaming platforms popular with children

**What They Gain**

| Benefit | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| Advertising revenue | Children's attention and data fuel targeted advertising worth billions |
| User base growth | Acquiring young users builds lifetime customer relationships |
| Behavioral data | Children's engagement patterns train recommendation algorithms |
| Network effects | Young users attract peers, expanding platform reach |
| Reduced compliance costs | Avoiding COPPA obligations by claiming general-audience status |

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **Very High**

- Revenue models depend on data collection from all users, including minors
- Compliance with age-appropriate design standards would require significant platform redesign
- Companies have invested heavily in lobbying against children's privacy legislation

### Advertising Industry

**Who They Are**

- Digital advertising networks (Google Ads, Meta Ads, Amazon Advertising)
- Advertising agencies targeting youth demographics
- Brand advertisers seeking to build early brand loyalty
- Ad-tech companies enabling behavioral targeting

**What They Gain**

- Access to highly engaged, impressionable audience
- Behavioral data that enables precise targeting
- Ability to shape brand preferences during formative years
- Revenue from children's influence on household purchasing ($600+ billion annually in estimated US household spending influenced by children, per the American Academy of Pediatrics citing market research, 2023)

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **High**

- Banning behavioral advertising to children directly threatens revenue
- Industry argues targeting is more efficient and benefits consumers

### Educational Technology Companies

**Who They Are**

- Google (Workspace for Education, Chromebooks)
- PowerSchool, Illuminate Education, Clever
- Canvas (Instructure), Schoology, Seesaw
- Khan Academy, IXL Learning, Duolingo
- Assessment platforms: NWEA, Renaissance Learning
- Hundreds of smaller edtech startups

**What They Gain**

| Benefit | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| Market access | Schools provide captive user base of millions of students |
| Data for product development | Student interaction data improves algorithms and products |
| Long-term user acquisition | Students accustomed to platforms become adult customers |
| Low regulatory burden | FERPA is weaker than COPPA; school-as-intermediary reduces direct obligations |

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **Moderate to High**

- Some companies support reasonable regulation to build trust
- Industry resists requirements that would significantly increase compliance costs or limit data use

### Data Brokers

**Who They Are**

- Companies that aggregate and sell consumer data, including children's data
- People-search sites that include minors' information
- Marketing data firms that build family and household profiles

**What They Gain**

- Children's data enriches family profiles used for marketing
- Educational data, gaming data, and app usage data can be cross-referenced
- Lifetime profiles starting from childhood are more valuable

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **High**

- Any restriction on collecting or selling children's data directly reduces inventory and revenue

## Who Is Harmed

### Children Under 13

**How They Are Harmed**

- Personal information collected and monetized without meaningful consent
- Exposure to manipulative advertising and dark patterns
- Behavioral profiles built that follow them into adulthood
- Algorithmic content recommendations that may expose them to harmful material
- Online predators exploit platforms' inadequate safety measures
- Privacy violations can enable bullying, stalking, and identity theft

**Scale of Impact**: Approximately 50 million children under 13 in the US (Census Bureau, 2023)

### Teenagers (13-17)

**How They Are Harmed**

- No special federal privacy protections despite unique vulnerabilities
- Mental health impacts from algorithmic amplification of harmful content (Surgeon General's Advisory, 2023)
- Sextortion and sexual exploitation facilitated by platform data practices
- Academic and career consequences from digital footprint created as minors
- Cyberbullying enabled by platform design choices
- Addictive design features exploit developing brains

**Scale of Impact**: Approximately 21 million teens ages 13-17 in the US (Census Bureau, 2023)

### Parents and Families

**How They Are Harmed**

- Cannot effectively protect children's privacy in the current landscape
- Forced to accept edtech data collection as condition of children's education
- "Verifiable parental consent" mechanisms are often theater -- easy to circumvent
- Bear responsibility without adequate tools or information
- "Sharenting" norms create pressure to share children's information without understanding consequences

### Students

**How They Are Harmed**

- Mandatory edtech use in schools creates involuntary data subjects
- Student data breaches expose academic records, behavioral assessments, and personal information
- Long-term retention of student data creates lifelong records
- Inequitable impact: lower-income schools may adopt less privacy-protective edtech
- Students of color may face disproportionate surveillance through behavioral monitoring tools

## Who Has Power to Change the System

### Federal Government

**Congress**

- Can update COPPA, enact teen privacy protections, and create new enforcement mechanisms
- Bipartisan support exists but legislative action has been blocked by procedural obstacles and industry lobbying
- Key committees: Senate Commerce Committee, House Energy and Commerce Committee

**Federal Trade Commission**

- Primary COPPA enforcer with rulemaking authority
- Can update COPPA Rule to address modern platforms
- Resource constraints limit enforcement scope
- Current FTC has pursued aggressive children's privacy agenda

**Department of Education**

- Administers FERPA
- Could issue guidance strengthening student data protections
- Historically passive on enforcement

### State Governments

**State Legislators**

- Have enacted innovative children's privacy laws (California, New York, Utah, Texas)
- Can serve as laboratories for federal policy
- Face industry legal challenges under First Amendment and dormant Commerce Clause theories

**State Attorneys General**

- Can enforce COPPA and state privacy laws
- California, New York, and other states have been active enforcers
- Multistate AG investigations and actions increasing

### Industry Leaders

**Platform Companies**

- Have the technical capacity to implement age-appropriate design
- Voluntary measures (teen accounts, parental controls) demonstrate what is technically feasible
- Industry cooperation -- or obstruction -- shapes legislative and regulatory outcomes

### Civil Society

**Advocacy Organizations**

| Organization | Role |
|--------------|------|
| Common Sense Media | Children's media advocacy; rates platforms; supports legislation |
| Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF) | Digital rights; scrutinizes both industry and government overreach |
| Center for Digital Democracy | Children's digital marketing and privacy advocacy |
| Campaign for a Commercial-Free Childhood (Fairplay) | Opposes marketing and surveillance targeting children |
| National PTA | Parent advocacy for student privacy |
| Future of Privacy Forum | Industry-aligned but supports student privacy standards |
| Electronic Privacy Information Center (EPIC) | Privacy advocacy; files FTC complaints |

**Researchers and Academics**

- University researchers documenting harms (e.g., Stanford Internet Observatory, Harvard Berkman Klein Center)
- American Psychological Association -- research on children's cognitive development and technology
- Pediatric and mental health professionals documenting clinical impacts

### International Bodies

**Influential Frameworks**

| Body | Influence |
|------|-----------|
| UK Information Commissioner's Office (ICO) | Age Appropriate Design Code serves as model for US proposals |
| European Data Protection Board | GDPR children's provisions influence global standards |
| UNICEF | Policy guidance on children's rights in digital environment |
| OECD | Recommendation on Children in the Digital Environment (2021) |

## Stakeholder Dynamics

### Alignment Matrix

| Stakeholder | Supports Stronger Protections | Opposes Stronger Protections | Key Concern |
|-------------|------------------------------|------------------------------|-------------|
| Parents | Strong | - | Children's safety and wellbeing |
| Children's advocates | Strong | - | Exploitation and manipulation |
| Pediatric/mental health professionals | Strong | - | Developmental harms |
| State AGs | Strong | - | Consumer protection mandate |
| FTC (current) | Strong | - | Enforcement authority and resources |
| Civil liberties groups | Moderate | Moderate | Free expression vs. protection |
| Teachers/schools | Moderate | - | Student privacy vs. edtech utility |
| Edtech companies | Some | Some | Balance compliance costs with market access |
| Tech platforms | - | Strong | Revenue model depends on data collection |
| Advertising industry | - | Strong | Access to youth market |
| Data brokers | - | Strong | Commercial value of children's data |
| Libertarian/free speech advocates | - | Moderate | Government overreach concerns |

### Power Imbalance

The stakeholder landscape is characterized by a significant power imbalance:

- **Those harmed** (children, parents, students) have diffuse interests and limited political organization
- **Those who benefit** (platforms, advertisers, data brokers) have concentrated interests, vast financial resources, and sophisticated lobbying operations
- Industry spent over $70 million on federal lobbying in 2023 across major tech companies (OpenSecrets)
- Children cannot vote or lobby; parents face collective action problems
