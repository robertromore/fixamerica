# Children's Privacy: History

## Origins

### Pre-Internet Children's Privacy

Long before the internet, concerns about protecting children from commercial exploitation shaped American law and culture. The relationship between children, commerce, and privacy has evolved through several distinct phases.

**Early Protections**

- **Child labor laws (early 1900s)**: Recognized children as a vulnerable population requiring special legal protection
- **FTC Act (1914)**: Established federal authority over unfair and deceptive trade practices, including those targeting children
- **Children's television regulation**: FCC regulated advertising during children's programming; Children's Television Act (1990) limited commercial content
- **FERPA (1974)**: Established parental rights over student educational records, the first federal law to address children's information privacy

**Key Principles Established**

- Children cannot meaningfully consent to complex transactions
- Society has a special obligation to protect minors from exploitation
- Parents have rights and responsibilities regarding their children's information
- Commercial interests targeting children require regulatory oversight

### The Early Internet Era (1990s)

**The Wild West of Children's Data**

The commercial internet emerged in the mid-1990s with virtually no privacy regulation. Websites targeting children collected personal information freely.

- Children's websites routinely collected names, addresses, phone numbers, and email addresses
- Chat rooms and message boards exposed children to predators
- No legal framework governed online data collection from children
- FTC investigations revealed widespread data collection from children's websites

**FTC Investigations (1996-1998)**

The FTC conducted a series of investigations that documented the scope of the problem:

- **1996 FTC workshop**: "Consumer Privacy in the Global Information Infrastructure" raised children's privacy as a key concern
- **1998 FTC report to Congress**: Found that 89% of children's websites collected personal information, and only 24% provided any privacy disclosure (FTC, "Privacy Online: A Report to Congress," 1998)
- **Georgetown Internet Privacy Policy Survey (1998)**: Confirmed widespread data collection from children without parental notice or consent

**Industry Self-Regulation Fails**

- Industry groups like the Children's Advertising Review Unit (CARU) promoted voluntary guidelines
- Self-regulation proved inadequate: compliance was low, enforcement nonexistent
- Congress concluded that legislation was necessary

## Key Turning Points

### 1998: COPPA Enacted

**The Children's Online Privacy Protection Act**

- Signed by President Clinton on October 21, 1998
- Effective April 21, 2000
- Sponsored by Senators Bryan (D-NV) and McCain (R-AZ) -- bipartisan from the start

**Core Provisions**

| Provision | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Scope | Websites and online services "directed to children" under 13 or with "actual knowledge" of child users |
| Verifiable parental consent | Required before collecting, using, or disclosing personal information from children |
| Privacy notice | Must post clear privacy policy describing data practices |
| Parental rights | Parents can review, delete, and refuse further collection of their child's data |
| Data minimization | Cannot condition participation on collection of more data than reasonably necessary |
| FTC enforcement | Empowered FTC to enforce through civil penalties |
| Safe harbor | Industry groups can develop self-regulatory programs approved by FTC |

**Design Choices and Compromises**

- Age 13 threshold chosen as developmentally appropriate dividing line, but excluded teens
- "Directed to children" standard created ambiguity for general-audience sites
- "Actual knowledge" standard allowed platforms to avoid responsibility by not verifying ages
- Verifiable parental consent mechanisms were limited by 1990s technology
- No private right of action -- enforcement entirely through FTC

### 2000-2010: Early COPPA Enforcement

**FTC Actions**

| Year | Target | Fine | Issue |
|------|--------|------|-------|
| 2001 | Toysmart.com | Blocked sale of children's data in bankruptcy | Data sale |
| 2003 | Mrs. Fields/Hershey Foods | Civil penalties | Collection without consent |
| 2006 | Xanga.com | $1 million | Knowingly collecting from children under 13 |
| 2008 | Sony BMG | $1 million | Fan websites collecting from children |
| 2011 | Playdom (Disney subsidiary) | $3 million | Largest COPPA fine at time |

**Emerging Challenges**

- Rise of social networking (MySpace, Facebook) -- platforms not "directed to children" but widely used by them
- Mobile apps began collecting data from children
- Virtual worlds and online games (Club Penguin, Neopets, Webkinz) created new collection contexts
- Industry compliance remained inconsistent

### 2013: COPPA Rule Update

**FTC Modernizes COPPA**

The FTC updated the COPPA Rule effective July 1, 2013 -- the most significant revision since the law's enactment.

**Key Changes**

- Expanded definition of "personal information" to include geolocation data, photos, videos, audio recordings, and persistent identifiers used for behavioral advertising
- Expanded definition of "operator" to include third-party plug-ins and ad networks on children's sites
- Strengthened verifiable parental consent mechanisms
- Added requirement to maintain data security
- Retained the 13-and-under threshold despite calls to raise it

**What Was Not Changed**

- Age threshold remained at 13
- "Actual knowledge" standard unchanged -- general-audience platforms still could claim ignorance
- No expansion to cover teens
- No private right of action added

### 2018-2021: The Social Media Reckoning

**Cambridge Analytica and Facebook (2018)**

- The Cambridge Analytica scandal revealed Facebook's lax data practices affected millions, including minors
- FTC investigation led to $5 billion settlement in 2019, which included enhanced COPPA obligations

**YouTube/Google COPPA Settlement (2019)**

- FTC and New York AG found YouTube illegally collected data from children to target ads
- $170 million fine ($136 million federal, $34 million New York)
- YouTube required to obtain verifiable parental consent or treat content as child-directed
- YouTube created YouTube Kids and content labeling system

**TikTok/Musical.ly Settlement (2019)**

- FTC found Musical.ly (now TikTok) collected personal information from children under 13 without parental consent
- $5.7 million fine -- largest COPPA penalty at the time
- Required TikTok to delete children's data and create under-13 experience

**Frances Haugen Whistleblower Disclosures (2021)**

- Former Facebook employee disclosed internal research showing Instagram's harmful effects on teen mental health
- Documents revealed Facebook knew its platform worsened body image issues for teenage girls
- Disclosed that Facebook had shelved plans for "Instagram Kids" after internal concerns
- Catalyzed bipartisan legislative momentum for children's online safety

### 2022-2025: The Legislative Push

**Federal Activity**

- **2022**: Bipartisan KOSA and COPPA 2.0 introduced in Senate
- **2023**: Senate Commerce Committee advanced both bills; Surgeon General issued advisory on social media and youth mental health
- **2024 (July)**: Senate passed KOSA 91-3 and COPPA 2.0 unanimously; House failed to vote before session ended
- **2024**: FTC proposed COPPA rulemaking to expand protections
- **2025**: KOSA and COPPA 2.0 reintroduced in 119th Congress

**State Innovation**

- **California (2022)**: Enacted Age-Appropriate Design Code Act modeled on UK code; immediately challenged in court
- **Utah (2023)**: Enacted Social Media Regulation Act requiring parental consent and age verification
- **Arkansas (2023)**: Enacted Social Media Safety Act; enjoined by federal court
- **Texas (2023)**: Enacted HB 18 restricting minors' social media use
- **New York (2024)**: Enacted SAFE for Kids Act and Child Data Protection Act
- **Multiple states (2024-2025)**: Additional children's privacy and social media bills introduced in over 40 state legislatures

**Legal Challenges**

- NetChoice v. Bonta: Tech industry trade group challenged California Age-Appropriate Design Code; preliminary injunction granted, appealed
- NetChoice v. Paxton: Challenged Texas HB 18; portions upheld, portions enjoined
- NetChoice v. Griffin: Arkansas social media law enjoined as likely unconstitutional
- First Amendment challenges to age verification requirements have produced mixed results across circuits

## Patterns and Lessons

### Recurring Themes

1. **Technology outpaces law**: COPPA was outdated before its first major revision; each update lags behind innovation
2. **Industry resists then adapts**: Companies resist regulation, then implement minimal compliance while finding workarounds
3. **Bipartisan concern, legislative gridlock**: Children's privacy enjoys rare bipartisan support but still stalls in Congress
4. **Self-regulation fails repeatedly**: Voluntary industry measures have never proven sufficient
5. **Enforcement is resource-limited**: The FTC lacks adequate staff and funding for comprehensive COPPA enforcement
6. **States fill federal gaps**: State innovation drives progress but creates inconsistency and legal challenges

### What Has Changed

| Era | Primary Threat | Data Type | Response |
|-----|---------------|-----------|----------|
| 1990s | Websites collecting names and addresses | Contact information | COPPA (1998) |
| 2000s | Social networks and virtual worlds | Profiles, communications | FTC enforcement |
| 2010s | Mobile apps and behavioral tracking | Location, behavior, persistent IDs | COPPA Rule update (2013) |
| 2020s | Algorithmic manipulation and AI | Behavioral profiles, biometric, AI-generated | KOSA, COPPA 2.0, state laws (pending) |

### What Has Not Changed

- The fundamental business model of monetizing children's attention and data
- Industry lobbying against meaningful regulation
- The gap between stated age policies (13+) and actual platform usage by younger children
- Parents' inability to meaningfully oversee their children's digital privacy
- The absence of comprehensive federal teen privacy protections
