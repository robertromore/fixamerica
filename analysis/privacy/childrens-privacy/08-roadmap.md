# Children's Privacy: Roadmap

## Phase 1: Immediate Actions (2025-2026)

### FTC Rulemaking

**Finalize Updated COPPA Rule**

The FTC proposed updates to the COPPA Rule in 2024. Immediate priorities:

- Finalize expanded definition of "personal information" to cover biometric data, inferred data, and persistent identifiers
- Replace "actual knowledge" with "constructive knowledge" standard
- Strengthen verifiable parental consent mechanisms
- Limit data retention to what is strictly necessary for the service
- Require annual data security assessments for COPPA-covered operators

**Timeline**

| Step | Target Date |
|------|-------------|
| Notice of proposed rulemaking | Completed (2024) |
| Public comment period | Completed |
| Final rule publication | 2025 |
| Compliance deadline | 6-12 months after publication |

### Congressional Action

**Pass COPPA 2.0 and KOSA**

Both bills passed the Senate in the 118th Congress and have been reintroduced in the 119th. The immediate legislative strategy:

- Build House coalition for floor vote
- Address specific House objections (First Amendment concerns in KOSA; preemption in COPPA 2.0)
- Coordinate with children's advocacy organizations for grassroots pressure
- Target key House Energy and Commerce Committee members

**Key House Concerns to Address**

| Concern | Proposed Resolution |
|---------|---------------------|
| KOSA "duty of care" vagueness | Provide specific, enumerable harms rather than broad duty |
| First Amendment challenges | Focus on data practices, not content |
| Preemption of state laws | Floor provision: federal law sets minimum; states can exceed |
| Age verification mandates | Adopt design-code approach as alternative |

### State-Level Advocacy

**Support Existing Laws**

- Defend California Age-Appropriate Design Code in Ninth Circuit litigation
- Support enforcement of New York SAFE for Kids Act
- Monitor Texas HB 18 implementation and litigation outcomes

**Expand State Protections**

| Target States | Opportunity |
|---------------|-------------|
| Illinois | Strong existing biometric privacy law (BIPA); legislature receptive to children's privacy |
| Washington | Active privacy legislation; tech industry presence creates political dynamics |
| Colorado | Existing state privacy law could be extended to children |
| Massachusetts | Strong consumer protection tradition; legislative interest |
| Minnesota | Active children's advocacy community |

### Build Public Awareness

**Public Education Campaign**

- Publish accessible guides explaining how children's data is collected and used
- Partner with schools and PTAs to educate parents about edtech privacy
- Amplify Surgeon General's advisory on social media and youth
- Support investigative journalism on children's privacy violations

## Phase 2: Short-Term Goals (2026-2028)

### Federal Legislation

**Enact Comprehensive Children's Privacy Protection**

If COPPA 2.0 passes, build toward more comprehensive reform:

- Ban behavioral advertising to all minors under 17
- Establish age-appropriate design code requirements through FTC rulemaking
- Create Student Data Protection Act with mandatory security standards
- Authorize private right of action for children's privacy violations
- Create FTC Division of Children's Digital Privacy with dedicated funding

**Budget Requirements**

| Component | Estimated Annual Cost | Source |
|-----------|----------------------|--------|
| FTC Children's Privacy Division (50 staff) | $15-20 million | Federal appropriation |
| State AG enforcement grants | $10 million | Federal grants |
| Research and standards development | $5 million | NIST/FTC |
| Public education and outreach | $3 million | FTC/HHS |
| Total | $33-38 million | Federal appropriation |

### Enforcement Ramp-Up

**FTC Enforcement Priorities**

- Target platforms with documented COPPA violations affecting the largest number of children
- Pursue enforcement against edtech companies misusing student data
- Investigate data broker sales of children's data
- Issue guidance on age-appropriate design compliance

**State AG Coordination**

- Establish multistate children's privacy enforcement working group
- Coordinate investigations to maximize impact and minimize duplication
- Share enforcement resources and legal strategies

### Educational Technology Reform

**School District Action**

- Develop model edtech procurement standards requiring privacy protections
- Create state-level edtech privacy review boards
- Require annual data audits of all edtech vendors used by school districts
- Fund edtech privacy compliance training for teachers and administrators

**Federal Guidance**

- Department of Education issues updated FERPA guidance for the edtech era
- Establish minimum data security standards for educational technology
- Create model contract provisions for school-vendor agreements

### International Coordination

- Pursue mutual recognition agreements with UK ICO and EU data protection authorities
- Align US children's privacy standards with international frameworks
- Participate in OECD children's digital environment initiatives

## Phase 3: Medium-Term Goals (2028-2032)

### Comprehensive Framework

**Establish Children's Digital Rights Framework**

- Codify children's right to privacy in digital environments
- Establish right to be free from algorithmic manipulation
- Create right to have childhood data deleted upon reaching adulthood
- Establish platform duty to design age-appropriate experiences

**FERPA Modernization**

- Comprehensive FERPA reauthorization with modern data protection standards
- Private right of action for student data breaches
- Mandatory data security standards for all recipients of student data
- Annual data inventory and transparency requirements

### Technology Standards

**FTC and NIST Develop Technical Standards**

- Privacy-preserving age estimation guidelines
- Data protection impact assessment methodology for children's products
- Secure deletion standards ensuring data is actually removed
- Interoperability standards for parental control tools

### Evaluation and Adjustment

**Measure Effectiveness**

| Metric | Baseline (2025) | Target (2032) |
|--------|-----------------|---------------|
| COPPA enforcement actions per year | ~3-4 | 15-20 |
| Children's data breach incidents | ~400 per year | Reduce by 75% |
| Platforms compliant with design code | 0 (no code exists) | 90% of major platforms |
| States with children's privacy laws | 15+ | 40+ |
| Parents aware of children's data rights | ~30% | 70% |

**Adjust Framework Based on Results**

- Commission independent evaluation of reform effectiveness
- Sunset and review provisions for all new legislation
- Regular FTC reports to Congress on children's privacy landscape
- Ongoing engagement with children's advocacy organizations and researchers

## Contingency Planning

### If Federal Legislation Stalls

- Accelerate state-level advocacy, prioritizing large states (California, New York, Texas, Illinois)
- Support FTC rulemaking to the maximum extent of existing authority
- Pursue litigation strategy through FTC enforcement actions and state AG cases
- Build toward critical mass of state laws that create de facto national standard

### If Courts Strike Down State Laws

- Redesign regulations to focus on data practices (economic regulation) rather than content or access (speech regulation)
- Pursue Congressional action to create clearer statutory basis
- Develop alternative approaches that survive First Amendment scrutiny
- Focus on FTC unfair practices authority, which has strong constitutional foundation

### If Industry Launches Effective Self-Regulation

- Evaluate honestly -- if voluntary measures achieve measurable results, acknowledge success
- Maintain legislative readiness in case voluntary measures prove insufficient
- Establish independent monitoring to verify industry claims
- History suggests self-regulation alone will not be sufficient, but credit genuine progress
