# Children's Privacy: Root Causes

## Structural Causes

### The Surveillance Advertising Business Model

The most fundamental driver of children's privacy violations is the economic model that dominates the internet. Platforms offer "free" services funded by surveillance-based advertising, which requires collecting as much behavioral data as possible from all users -- including children.

**How the Model Works**

- Platforms provide free access to maximize user base
- User attention and data are the products sold to advertisers
- More data enables more targeted (and more valuable) advertising
- Children are especially valuable because brand loyalty formed young persists for decades
- Every interaction -- click, view, pause, scroll -- generates monetizable data

**Why Children Are Especially Vulnerable**

| Factor | Impact |
|--------|--------|
| Developing cognitive abilities | Children cannot assess privacy risks or understand data consequences |
| High engagement rates | Children interact more frequently and for longer periods, generating more data |
| Lifetime value | Data profiles built in childhood follow individuals into adulthood |
| Susceptibility to manipulation | Children are more vulnerable to dark patterns, persuasive design, and social pressure |
| Trust in technology | Children often trust apps and platforms as they would trust adults |

### The "Actual Knowledge" Loophole

COPPA's most significant structural weakness is the "actual knowledge" standard. Platforms are only subject to COPPA if they are "directed to children" or have "actual knowledge" that a user is under 13.

**How Platforms Exploit This**

- General-audience platforms (Instagram, TikTok, YouTube) set terms of service requiring users to be 13+
- Platforms do not verify age, allowing children to sign up with false birth dates
- By not implementing age verification, platforms maintain plausible deniability
- This creates a perverse incentive: the less platforms know about their users' ages, the less legal obligation they have
- Result: tens of millions of children under 13 use platforms with no COPPA protections

**The Willful Blindness Problem**

| Platform Action | Legal Effect |
|-----------------|--------------|
| Require users to affirm they are 13+ | Shields platform from "actual knowledge" |
| Do not verify ages | Avoids triggering COPPA obligations |
| Ignore obvious indicators (children's content, school email addresses) | Maintains legal defense |
| Offer "kid-friendly" features on general platforms | Blurs the line without accepting COPPA duties |

### The Teen Privacy Gap

COPPA covers children under 13. No federal law provides special privacy protections for teenagers aged 13-17.

**Why This Matters**

- Teenagers are the most active users of social media and digital services
- Teen brain development makes them especially susceptible to manipulative design
- Teens may lack the judgment to weigh long-term privacy consequences
- Data collected during teenage years can affect college admissions, employment, and insurance
- Teens experience the highest rates of cyberbullying, sextortion, and online exploitation

**How We Got Here**

- COPPA's drafters chose 13 as a balance between protection and teen autonomy
- The 1990s internet posed different risks than today's algorithmic platforms
- Privacy advocates have pushed for teen protections since at least 2010
- COPPA 2.0 would extend protections to 17, but has not been enacted

### Inadequate Enforcement Infrastructure

**FTC Resource Constraints**

The FTC is the primary enforcer of COPPA but lacks the resources for comprehensive enforcement.

- The FTC's Bureau of Consumer Protection handles all consumer protection issues, not just children's privacy
- Only a fraction of staff is dedicated to COPPA enforcement
- The FTC has brought approximately 30 COPPA cases in 25 years -- against thousands of non-compliant services
- FTC budget for all operations: approximately $430 million (FY 2024) -- a fraction of the revenue of the companies it regulates

**Structural Limitations**

| Limitation | Impact |
|------------|--------|
| No dedicated children's privacy agency | Enforcement competes with all other FTC priorities |
| No private right of action | Parents cannot sue directly; must rely on FTC |
| Limited state enforcement authority | State AGs can enforce COPPA but most lack specialized staff |
| Penalty caps inadequate | Maximum penalties are trivial relative to company revenues |
| International enforcement gaps | Limited ability to enforce against foreign-operated platforms |

### Weak Educational Privacy Framework

**FERPA's Structural Deficiencies**

FERPA was enacted in 1974, decades before educational technology platforms existed. Its framework is inadequate for the modern edtech environment.

- **No private right of action**: Enforcement is solely through the threat of withdrawing federal education funding -- a penalty never imposed
- **"School official" exception**: Schools can designate edtech vendors as "school officials" with "legitimate educational interest," effectively allowing data sharing without parental consent
- **No data security requirements**: FERPA does not mandate specific security standards for student data
- **Narrow scope**: FERPA covers "educational records" maintained by the school, but much edtech-generated data may fall outside this definition
- **Preemption concerns**: FERPA may preempt stronger state protections in some interpretations

## Political Causes

### Industry Lobbying Power

Technology companies and their trade associations invest heavily in opposing children's privacy legislation.

**Lobbying Expenditures**

- Meta spent $19.7 million on federal lobbying in 2023 (OpenSecrets)
- Alphabet/Google spent $13.4 million in 2023 (OpenSecrets)
- Amazon spent $19.2 million in 2023 (OpenSecrets)
- The Internet Association (before dissolution) and successor groups like NetChoice and the Computer & Communications Industry Association (CCIA) coordinate industry opposition to regulation
- Chamber of Commerce consistently opposes new privacy mandates

**Industry Arguments**

| Argument | Reality |
|----------|---------|
| "Innovation will be stifled" | EU and UK have stronger protections without stifling innovation |
| "Parents should be responsible" | Parents cannot monitor all digital interactions or understand complex data practices |
| "Self-regulation is working" | FTC and independent research consistently show self-regulation is inadequate |
| "Age verification threatens privacy" | Privacy-preserving age estimation technologies exist |
| "First Amendment protects data collection" | Speech protections do not extend to unfair or deceptive data practices targeting children |

### Congressional Dysfunction

Despite bipartisan support, children's privacy legislation has repeatedly stalled in Congress.

**Why Bills Fail**

- House leadership has blocked floor votes on bills that passed the Senate with near-unanimous support
- Industry lobbying targets key committee members
- Broader tech regulation disputes (Section 230 reform, antitrust) create legislative log-jams
- Election cycles and partisan gridlock consume legislative bandwidth
- Some members genuinely concerned about First Amendment implications oppose specific proposals

**The Paradox of Bipartisanship**

- Children's privacy is one of the most bipartisan issues in technology policy
- KOSA passed the Senate 91-3 in 2024
- Yet bipartisan support does not guarantee passage because procedural obstacles, House leadership priorities, and industry lobbying can block action even when majorities support reform

### Federalism Complications

The division of authority between federal and state governments creates both opportunities and obstacles.

**Preemption Debates**

- Industry strongly advocates federal preemption to create a single national standard (and prevent states from enacting stronger laws)
- Privacy advocates generally oppose preemption to preserve state innovation
- COPPA currently does not preempt stronger state laws, but some federal proposals would
- Federal preemption would eliminate California, New York, and other states' pioneering protections

**Constitutional Challenges**

- First Amendment challenges to age verification requirements have succeeded in several courts
- Content-based restrictions for children's safety face strict scrutiny in some circuits
- The Supreme Court's decision in *Brown v. Entertainment Merchants Association* (2011) limited government power to restrict minors' access to content, with implications for privacy regulation

## Cultural Causes

### The "Digital Native" Myth

A widespread cultural assumption holds that children who grow up with technology naturally understand and can manage its risks. This myth undermines support for protective regulation.

**The Reality**

- Children's cognitive and emotional development does not equip them to navigate complex privacy trade-offs
- "Digital literacy" does not equal "privacy awareness"
- Even adults struggle to understand privacy policies and data practices
- The American Psychological Association has documented that children's capacity for risk assessment develops through adolescence (APA, 2023)

### Normalization of Surveillance

Children growing up in a surveillance-rich environment may not recognize the loss of privacy as abnormal.

**Contributing Factors**

- Parents share children's information on social media from birth ("sharenting")
- Schools require technology use, normalizing institutional monitoring
- Peers expect social media participation, creating social pressure
- Surveillance capitalism has become the default business model -- alternatives are scarce

### Parental Capacity Limitations

The current framework places primary responsibility on parents to protect their children's privacy, but parents are structurally unable to fulfill this role effectively.

**Why Parents Cannot Be the Primary Solution**

- Privacy policies are unreadable -- average length exceeds 4,000 words (Carnegie Mellon study, 2019)
- Parents cannot monitor every app, website, and platform their children access
- Schools mandate edtech use, giving parents no meaningful opt-out
- Connected toys and devices collect data passively
- Children routinely circumvent parental controls
- Parents themselves lack privacy literacy
