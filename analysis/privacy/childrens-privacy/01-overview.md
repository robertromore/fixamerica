# Children's Privacy: Overview

## Executive Summary

American children are the most surveilled generation in history. From the moment they go online -- and increasingly from birth -- children generate data trails that are collected, aggregated, profiled, and monetized by technology companies, educational platforms, data brokers, and advertisers. The Children's Online Privacy Protection Act (COPPA) of 1998, the primary federal law protecting children's digital privacy, was designed for an era of desktop computers and has not kept pace with smartphones, social media, connected toys, educational technology, and algorithmic recommendation systems. Meanwhile, teenagers aged 13 and older receive virtually no special privacy protections under federal law, despite being among the most vulnerable populations online.

The consequences are severe. Children's data is used to build behavioral profiles that follow them into adulthood. Predators exploit lax platform safety measures. Algorithmic systems expose minors to harmful content optimized for engagement. Educational technology platforms collect vast amounts of student data with minimal oversight. And an entire ecosystem of advertising technology targets children with manipulative design patterns -- so-called "dark patterns" -- engineered to extract data and attention from developing minds.

Despite broad bipartisan concern about children's online safety and privacy, federal legislation has stalled repeatedly. The Kids Online Safety Act (KOSA) and updates to COPPA have advanced in the Senate but faced House resistance and industry lobbying. States have begun filling the gap, with California's Age-Appropriate Design Code Act, Utah's social media laws, and similar measures, but the result is a patchwork of inconsistent protections that leaves most American children exposed.

### Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **COPPA** | Children's Online Privacy Protection Act (1998); requires verifiable parental consent before collecting data from children under 13 |
| **FERPA** | Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act (1974); governs student educational records |
| **Age-appropriate design** | Product design that considers children's developmental needs and vulnerabilities |
| **Dark patterns** | User interface designs that manipulate users into actions they did not intend, such as sharing data or making purchases |
| **Verifiable parental consent** | COPPA requirement that operators obtain consent from a parent before collecting a child's personal information |
| **Persistent identifier** | A data element that can be used to recognize a user over time or across services (cookies, device IDs, IP addresses) |
| **Directed to children** | FTC standard for determining whether a website or service targets children under 13 |
| **Edtech** | Educational technology platforms and applications used in schools |

## Core Tensions

- **Safety vs. surveillance**: Age verification and content filtering require data collection that can itself threaten privacy
- **Parental rights vs. teen autonomy**: Parents want control; older teens need independence and may require privacy from parents
- **Innovation vs. protection**: Strict regulation may limit beneficial educational and social technology
- **Federal consistency vs. state innovation**: National standards provide uniformity but may preempt stronger state protections
- **Free expression vs. child safety**: Content restrictions raise First Amendment concerns
- **Industry self-regulation vs. government mandates**: Companies resist regulation but voluntary measures have proven inadequate

## Vision of Success

A reformed children's privacy landscape would feature:

- **Updated COPPA protections**: Expanded to cover teens, close loopholes, and address modern platforms
- **Age-appropriate design requirements**: Platforms must design products with children's best interests as the default
- **Strict limits on children's data monetization**: Prohibition on behavioral advertising to minors and sale of children's data
- **Educational technology safeguards**: Clear limits on edtech data collection, use, and retention with mandatory security standards
- **Effective enforcement**: A dedicated FTC division or new agency with adequate resources and authority
- **Algorithmic protections**: Prohibition on addictive design features and harmful content amplification for minors
- **Children's data deletion rights**: Easy, effective mechanisms for parents and young adults to delete childhood data
- **International alignment**: US standards compatible with GDPR-K, UK Age Appropriate Design Code, and other frameworks
