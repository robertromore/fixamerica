# Children's Privacy: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Children's privacy legislation must address the failure of the existing COPPA framework to protect children and teenagers in the modern digital environment. This document provides draft legislation targeting the most critical gaps: extending protections to teenagers, banning behavioral advertising to minors, requiring age-appropriate design, strengthening educational privacy, and establishing meaningful enforcement. Each proposal is designed to survive First Amendment challenge by focusing on data practices rather than content restrictions.

## Constitutional Amendments

No constitutional amendment is required for children's privacy reform. Congress has clear authority under the Commerce Clause to regulate the collection and use of children's personal data in interstate commerce. COPPA has been in effect for over 25 years without constitutional challenge. The proposals below operate within established constitutional boundaries.

## Federal Legislation

### Children and Teens' Online Privacy Protection Act (COPPA 2.0)

**Purpose**: Extend COPPA protections to teenagers, close the "actual knowledge" loophole, ban behavioral advertising to minors, and strengthen enforcement.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Children and Teens' Online Privacy
Protection Act" or "COPPA 2.0".

SEC. 2. DEFINITIONS.

(a) CHILD.--Section 1302(1) of the Children's Online Privacy
Protection Act of 1998 (15 U.S.C. 6501(1)) is amended by striking
"under 13" and inserting "under 17".

(b) TEEN.--Section 1302 is amended by adding the following:
"(11) TEEN.--The term 'teen' means an individual who is at
least 13 years of age and younger than 17 years of age."

(c) CONSTRUCTIVE KNOWLEDGE.--Section 1302 is amended by adding:
"(12) CONSTRUCTIVE KNOWLEDGE.--The term 'constructive
knowledge' means that an operator knows or should reasonably
have known, based on the totality of the circumstances, including
the nature of the service, the composition of its user base, and
the subject matter of the content, that a user is a child."

SEC. 3. KNOWLEDGE STANDARD.

Section 1303 of the Children's Online Privacy Protection Act of
1998 (15 U.S.C. 6502) is amended--
(a) by striking "actual knowledge" each place it appears and
inserting "actual or constructive knowledge"; and
(b) by adding at the end the following:
"(g) CONSTRUCTIVE KNOWLEDGE.--An operator shall be deemed to
have constructive knowledge that a user is a child if--
    (1) the operator's service is reasonably likely to be
    accessed by children based on its subject matter, visual
    content, or the composition of its user base;
    (2) the operator has received credible information
    indicating that a user is a child; or
    (3) the operator uses age-gating mechanisms that are
    easily circumvented."

SEC. 4. TIERED CONSENT.

Section 1303 is further amended by adding:
"(h) TIERED CONSENT REQUIREMENTS.--
    (1) CHILDREN UNDER 13.--For children under the age of 13,
    an operator must obtain verifiable parental consent as
    required under this Act before collecting, using, or
    disclosing personal information.
    (2) TEENS 13-16.--For teens ages 13 through 16--
        (A) the operator shall obtain the informed assent of
        the teen before collecting personal information beyond
        what is strictly necessary to provide the requested
        service;
        (B) the operator shall provide notice to the teen's
        parent or guardian of the data practices to which the
        teen has assented; and
        (C) the parent or guardian may override the teen's
        assent and request deletion of data collected."

SEC. 5. PROHIBITION ON BEHAVIORAL ADVERTISING TO MINORS.

(a) IN GENERAL.--It shall be unlawful for any operator to engage
in targeted advertising directed at a user whom the operator knows
or should reasonably know is a child, based on the collection or
processing of personal information, behavioral data, or inferred
characteristics.

(b) CONTEXTUAL ADVERTISING PERMITTED.--Nothing in this section
prohibits advertising that is--
    (1) based solely on the content of the webpage, application
    screen, or media being viewed at the time of the
    advertisement; and
    (2) not based on prior collection of personal information
    or behavioral profiles.

SEC. 6. ERASER BUTTON.

(a) IN GENERAL.--An operator shall provide a clearly accessible
mechanism for--
    (1) a parent to request deletion of all personal information
    collected from or about their child;
    (2) a teen to request deletion of personal information the
    teen has provided; and
    (3) a young adult (age 17 through 20) to request deletion
    of personal information collected during childhood.

(b) COMPLIANCE TIMELINE.--An operator shall comply with a
deletion request under this section within 30 days and shall
direct any third parties to whom the data was disclosed to
delete the data within 60 days.

SEC. 7. DATA MINIMIZATION AND RETENTION.

(a) An operator shall not collect personal information from a
child beyond what is reasonably necessary to provide the
specific service requested by the child or parent.

(b) An operator shall retain personal information collected
from a child only for so long as reasonably necessary to
fulfill the purpose for which it was collected, and in no
event longer than 2 years, unless the parent or teen
(for teens) provides specific consent for longer retention.

SEC. 8. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) FTC AUTHORITY.--A violation of this Act shall be treated
as an unfair or deceptive act or practice under section 18(a)(1)(B)
of the Federal Trade Commission Act (15 U.S.C. 57a(a)(1)(B)).

(b) CIVIL PENALTIES.--
    (1) IN GENERAL.--Any operator that violates this Act shall
    be subject to a civil penalty of not less than $50,000 and
    not more than $250,000 per violation.
    (2) KNOWING VIOLATIONS.--Any operator that knowingly
    violates this Act shall be subject to a civil penalty of
    not less than $250,000 and not more than $1,000,000 per
    violation.

(c) PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.--Any individual whose personal
information is collected, used, or disclosed in violation of
this Act may bring a civil action and recover--
    (1) statutory damages of not less than $1,000 and not more
    than $5,000 per violation; or
    (2) actual damages, if greater.

(d) STATE ENFORCEMENT.--The attorney general of any State may
bring a civil action on behalf of the residents of that State
to enforce this Act.

SEC. 9. NO PREEMPTION.

Nothing in this Act preempts, limits, or otherwise affects any
State law that provides greater protection to children's privacy
than the protections provided under this Act.

SEC. 10. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 180 days after the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 2**: Extends COPPA's scope from under-13 to under-17 and introduces "constructive knowledge" to close the willful blindness loophole
- **Section 3**: Replaces the exploitable "actual knowledge" standard with "actual or constructive knowledge"
- **Section 4**: Creates tiered consent -- full parental consent for under-13, teen assent plus parental notification for 13-16
- **Section 5**: Bans behavioral advertising to minors while preserving contextual advertising
- **Section 6**: Creates the "eraser button" for children, teens, and young adults deleting childhood data
- **Section 7**: Imposes data minimization and maximum 2-year retention limits
- **Section 8**: Strengthens enforcement with higher penalties, private right of action, and state AG authority
- **Section 9**: Preserves stronger state protections

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Industry compliance costs | Phased 180-day implementation; contextual advertising preserves revenue |
| First Amendment challenge to ad ban | Regulates commercial data practices, not speech; COPPA precedent |
| Age determination without verification | Constructive knowledge + design code approach reduces need for individual verification |
| Parental consent burden for teens | Tiered system balances protection with teen autonomy |

**Refinements**:

- **Stronger version**: Lower teen assent age to 15; mandatory opt-in for all data collection
- **Fallback version**: Focus only on the advertising ban and eraser button without age extension
- **Clarification**: Define "behavioral data" and "inferred characteristics" with specificity to reduce litigation risk

### Student Data Protection Act

**Purpose**: Establish federal standards for educational technology data privacy, modernizing FERPA protections for the digital age.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Student Data Protection Act".

SEC. 2. DEFINITIONS.

(a) STUDENT DATA.--The term "student data" means any
personally identifiable information or behavioral data
collected by an educational technology operator in connection
with a student's use of an educational technology service,
including but not limited to academic records, behavioral
assessments, biometric data, browsing history, communications,
and metadata.

(b) EDUCATIONAL TECHNOLOGY OPERATOR.--The term "educational
technology operator" means any entity that operates a website,
online service, or application that is used primarily for
educational purposes in a K-12 school and that collects, maintains,
or uses student data.

SEC. 3. RESTRICTIONS ON USE OF STUDENT DATA.

(a) PROHIBITED USES.--An educational technology operator shall
not use student data for--
    (1) targeted or behavioral advertising;
    (2) creating profiles for non-educational commercial
    purposes;
    (3) sale or disclosure to data brokers or other third
    parties not essential to the educational service; or
    (4) any purpose unrelated to the educational function
    for which the data was collected.

(b) PERMITTED USES.--Student data may be used for--
    (1) providing, improving, or maintaining the educational
    service for which the data was collected;
    (2) adaptive learning and personalization within the
    educational context;
    (3) de-identified research with institutional review board
    approval; and
    (4) complying with legal requirements.

SEC. 4. DATA SECURITY.

(a) Each educational technology operator shall implement and
maintain reasonable security practices appropriate to the nature
and scope of student data collected.

(b) In the event of a data breach affecting student data, the
operator shall notify the affected school district and parents
within 72 hours.

SEC. 5. DATA DELETION.

(a) An educational technology operator shall delete all student
data within 60 days after--
    (1) the end of the school year in which the data was
    collected, unless the school district requests retention
    for a specific educational purpose; or
    (2) a parent or eligible student requests deletion.

(b) Data retained at a school district's request shall be
deleted within 60 days after the student leaves the district.

SEC. 6. TRANSPARENCY.

(a) School districts shall maintain a public inventory of all
educational technology services used and the categories of
student data collected by each.

(b) Educational technology operators shall publish clear, concise
privacy policies in language accessible to parents and students.

SEC. 7. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) The FTC shall enforce this Act.

(b) A private right of action is established for violations
resulting in unauthorized disclosure or misuse of student data,
with statutory damages of $1,000 to $10,000 per student affected.

(c) State attorneys general may bring enforcement actions under
this Act.

SEC. 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect 1 year after enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- Fills FERPA's gaps by regulating edtech operators directly, not just schools
- Prohibits commercial exploitation of student data while preserving educational uses
- Creates enforceable data security and breach notification standards
- Provides meaningful remedies through private right of action

**Potential Challenges**:

| Challenge | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Compliance burden on edtech companies | 1-year implementation period; applies only to K-12 context |
| Defining "educational purpose" | Section 3(b) provides specific permitted uses |
| Interaction with FERPA | Supplements FERPA without preempting; applies to operators, not schools |

## State Model Legislation

### Model Age-Appropriate Design Code Act

**Purpose**: Require platforms likely accessed by children to adopt privacy-protective design standards.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Age-Appropriate Design
Code Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.

(a) "Covered platform" means any online service, website, or
application that is likely to be accessed by children, as
determined by the nature of the service, its content, its
advertising, and the composition of its user base.

(b) "Child" means any individual under the age of 18.

(c) "Data protection impact assessment" means a documented
assessment of how a covered platform's data practices affect
children's privacy.

SECTION 3. REQUIREMENTS.

(a) PRIVACY BY DEFAULT.--A covered platform shall configure
all privacy settings to the highest level of protection by
default for all users, with the option for adult users to
reduce protections.

(b) DATA MINIMIZATION.--A covered platform shall collect only
personal information that is strictly necessary to provide the
service requested by the user.

(c) NO PROFILING.--A covered platform shall not profile a child
user unless there is a compelling reason related to the
provision of the service.

(d) NO DETRIMENTAL USE.--A covered platform shall not use
children's personal information in ways that are detrimental
to the child's physical or mental wellbeing.

(e) NO NUDGE TECHNIQUES.--A covered platform shall not use
design features that encourage children to provide personal
information or weaken their privacy protections.

(f) DATA PROTECTION IMPACT ASSESSMENTS.--Before offering or
significantly modifying a service likely to be accessed by
children, a covered platform shall complete and publish a
summary of a data protection impact assessment.

SECTION 4. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) The [State Attorney General] shall enforce this Act.
(b) Civil penalties: up to $7,500 per affected child per
violation.
(c) Private right of action: individuals may bring suit for
injunctive relief.

SECTION 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect [1 year after enactment].
```

**Adaptations**: States may adjust the definition of "covered platform," the penalty structure, and whether to include a full private right of action based on their legal traditions and legislative preferences.

## Regulatory Framework

### FTC COPPA Rule Update

**Existing Authority**: 15 U.S.C. 6502(b) directs the FTC to promulgate regulations implementing COPPA.

**Priority Regulatory Changes**:

| Change | Mechanism |
|--------|-----------|
| Expand "personal information" definition | Include biometric data, inferred data, health data |
| Constructive knowledge standard | Adopt through rulemaking (within existing statutory authority, per FTC) |
| Strengthen consent mechanisms | Require methods that verify parental identity, not just check a box |
| Limit data retention | Maximum retention periods for children's data |
| Push notification restrictions | Prohibit push notifications to children under 13 without parental consent |
| Third-party data sharing limits | Require separate consent for sharing data beyond the primary service |

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

**First Amendment**

- Data collection regulations are commercial regulations, not speech restrictions
- COPPA has been in force 25+ years without First Amendment challenge
- *Brown v. Entertainment Merchants Ass'n* (2011) addressed content access, not data practices
- Age-appropriate design codes face First Amendment challenges (see *NetChoice v. Bonta*) but can be restructured to focus on data practices
- Behavioral advertising bans regulate commercial conduct, not expression

**Commerce Clause**

- Congress has clear authority under the Commerce Clause to regulate children's data in interstate commerce
- COPPA establishes precedent for federal regulation in this area

### Preemption Questions

- COPPA currently does not preempt stronger state laws
- Proposed legislation should explicitly preserve state authority to exceed federal minimums
- Industry will lobby aggressively for preemption; resistance to preemption is critical to preserving state innovation

### Enforcement Mechanisms

| Mechanism | Authority | Effectiveness |
|-----------|-----------|---------------|
| FTC enforcement | Civil penalties, injunctions | Effective but resource-constrained |
| State AG enforcement | COPPA and state law authority | Extends enforcement reach |
| Private right of action | Statutory and actual damages | Essential for comprehensive enforcement |
| COPPA safe harbors | Industry self-regulatory programs | Historically weak; should be strengthened or eliminated |

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### COPPA 2.0

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Age determination evasion | Platforms may argue they cannot determine user age without invasive verification | High |
| "Contextual advertising" expansion | Industry may classify behavioral targeting as "contextual" | Medium |
| Third-party data laundering | Data collected from children may be transferred to entities not covered by the Act | High |
| Cross-border enforcement | Foreign-operated platforms may be difficult to reach | Medium |
| Consent fatigue | Tiered consent may create compliance theater rather than meaningful protection | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Does not address algorithmic harm | Harmful recommendations continue if not based on personal data | Focus on data practices, not platform design |
| Enforcement resource gap | FTC still under-resourced even with new authority | Requires separate appropriation |
| 2-year retention limit may be too long | Significant data accumulates over 2 years | Political compromise |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Define "contextual advertising" narrowly in FTC rulemaking to prevent abuse
2. Require platforms to trace data transfers and impose contractual obligations on third-party recipients
3. Pair COPPA 2.0 with age-appropriate design code to address algorithmic harms
4. Appropriate dedicated funding for FTC children's privacy enforcement
5. Establish FTC-OECD cooperation framework for cross-border enforcement

### Student Data Protection Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| "Educational purpose" interpretation | Operators may broadly define product improvement as educational | Medium |
| De-identified data exception | Re-identification techniques may circumvent de-identification provisions | High |
| Platform boundary | Students using general-purpose platforms for school may fall outside coverage | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Applies only to K-12 | College students unprotected | Scope limitation |
| Does not address school procurement practices | Schools may continue adopting privacy-invasive tools | Regulates operators, not schools |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. Define "de-identified" using NIST standards with expert determination method
2. Require school districts to conduct privacy assessments as condition of federal education funding
3. Extend protections to higher education in future legislation

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| Technology evolves faster than law | Principles-based standards rather than technology-specific rules |
| International enforcement gaps | Mutual recognition agreements and OECD cooperation |
| Adequacy of penalties | Index penalties to company revenue, not fixed amounts |
| Intersection with adult privacy law | Align children's protections with comprehensive federal privacy law when enacted |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- Require FTC to report to Congress every 3 years on effectiveness of children's privacy protections
- GAO review of enforcement adequacy after 5 years
- Automatic sunset of safe harbor provisions after 7 years unless renewed based on demonstrated effectiveness
- Require FTC to update regulatory definitions every 5 years to reflect technological change
