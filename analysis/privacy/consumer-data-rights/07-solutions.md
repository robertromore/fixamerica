# Consumer Data Rights: Solutions

## Overview

Effective consumer data rights reform requires both structural changes to the data economy and individual rights that consumers can exercise. The solutions below are sequenced from immediately achievable state-level and executive actions to comprehensive federal legislation and institutional reform. International models, particularly the EU's GDPR, provide evidence for what works.

## Solution 1: Comprehensive Federal Privacy Law

### Description

Enact a comprehensive federal consumer data privacy law establishing enforceable rights, data minimization requirements, and meaningful enforcement mechanisms.

### Key Provisions

- **Consumer rights**: Access, deletion, correction, portability, opt-out of sale and targeted advertising
- **Data minimization**: Companies may collect only data reasonably necessary for the purpose for which it is collected
- **Purpose limitation**: Data may not be used for purposes materially different from those disclosed at collection
- **Consent requirements**: Affirmative opt-in consent for sensitive data (health, financial, biometric, geolocation, children's data)
- **Dark pattern prohibition**: Manipulation of consumer choice through deceptive design is unlawful
- **Enforcement**: FTC authority with civil penalty power; concurrent state AG enforcement; private right of action with reasonable safeguards
- **No ceiling preemption**: Federal law establishes a floor; states may provide stronger protections

### Evidence Base

- GDPR implementation in 31 EEA countries since 2018 demonstrates feasibility of comprehensive approach
- California's CCPA/CPRA experience shows that U.S. companies can comply with rights-based framework
- 19 U.S. states have enacted comprehensive laws, confirming state-level feasibility
- Cisco's 2024 Data Privacy Benchmark Study found that 95% of organizations see privacy investment as a net positive business benefit

### Costs

| Cost Category | Estimated Range | Bearer |
|---------------|----------------|--------|
| Initial compliance (large companies) | $1-5 million per company | Industry |
| Ongoing compliance | $500K-2 million/year | Industry |
| Small business compliance | $5,000-50,000 | Industry (with scaling) |
| FTC enforcement capacity expansion | $100-200 million/year | Federal budget |
| State AG enforcement | $5-20 million/year per state | State budgets |

### Timeline

- Drafting and introduction: 6 months
- Committee process: 6-12 months
- Floor consideration and conference: 6 months
- Implementation: 18-24 months after enactment

### International Models

- **EU GDPR**: Comprehensive rights, data minimization, purpose limitation, DPA enforcement, fines up to 4% global revenue
- **Brazil LGPD**: GDPR-influenced; dedicated authority (ANPD); fines up to 2% of Brazilian revenue
- **Canada PIPEDA (proposed C-27 update)**: Meaningful consent, data mobility, administrative monetary penalties, private right of action

## Solution 2: Dedicated Federal Data Protection Agency

### Description

Establish an independent federal agency dedicated to data privacy and protection, analogous to EU data protection authorities.

### Structure

- Independent commission (5 members, bipartisan, staggered 7-year terms)
- Dedicated budget ($200+ million/year)
- Rulemaking authority for privacy and data protection
- Civil penalty authority for first violations
- Technical staff with expertise in data science, AI, and security
- Authority to conduct investigations, audits, and assessments

### Evidence Base

- EU data protection authorities enforce GDPR more effectively than the FTC enforces U.S. privacy
- The CPPA (California) demonstrates that a dedicated state-level agency can be effective
- The FTC's lack of dedicated privacy authority, rulemaking power, and first-offense penalties undermines enforcement
- Countries with dedicated authorities (UK ICO, France CNIL, Germany BfDI) produce more consistent enforcement

### Costs

| Cost Category | Estimated | Source/Basis |
|---------------|-----------|-------------|
| Annual budget | $200-300 million | Based on UK ICO and scaled for U.S. economy |
| Staff | 500-1,000 | Based on EU DPA staffing ratios |
| Startup costs | $50-100 million | Office establishment, technology, hiring |

### Timeline

- Legislation: 12-18 months
- Agency establishment: 12-18 months after enactment
- Full operational capacity: 3-4 years

### International Models

- **UK Information Commissioner's Office (ICO)**: Budget of GBP 75 million (2024); 900 staff
- **France CNIL**: Budget of EUR 24 million (2024); 280 staff
- **California Privacy Protection Agency**: Budget $24.4 million; ~75 staff (first U.S. dedicated privacy agency)

## Solution 3: Data Broker Regulation

### Description

Establish comprehensive federal regulation of data brokers, including registration, transparency, consent, and deletion requirements.

### Key Provisions

- **Registration**: All data brokers must register with the FTC or new data protection agency
- **Transparency**: Brokers must disclose what categories of data they collect, sources, and categories of buyers
- **Consumer access**: Individuals may access the data a broker holds about them
- **Deletion rights**: Individuals may demand deletion of their data
- **Consent for sensitive data**: Opt-in consent required for sale of health, financial, location, and biometric data
- **Government access restrictions**: Brokers may not sell data to government agencies absent a warrant or valid legal process
- **Annual reporting**: Brokers must report data practices, breach history, and government data requests

### Evidence Base

- Vermont's data broker registration law (2018) identified 375+ brokers; California's identified 534
- Senate Commerce Committee (2014) documented that brokers held 1,500+ data points per consumer
- FTC enforcement actions against X-Mode Social and InMarket (2024) targeted data broker practices
- Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) proposed data broker rule (2024) treating certain brokers as consumer reporting agencies under FCRA

### Costs

| Cost Category | Estimated | Bearer |
|---------------|-----------|--------|
| Registration system | $10-20 million | Federal budget |
| Broker compliance | $100K-1 million per broker | Industry |
| Enforcement | $20-50 million/year | Federal budget |

### Timeline

- Achievable as standalone legislation within 12 months
- CFPB rulemaking under existing FCRA authority possible within 6 months

## Solution 4: Strengthened FTC Enforcement

### Description

Expand FTC authority and resources for privacy enforcement as an immediate measure while working toward a dedicated agency.

### Key Provisions

- **Privacy rulemaking authority**: Explicit authority to issue privacy rules under APA
- **First-offense civil penalties**: Up to $50,000 per violation (currently available only after prior order)
- **Increased budget**: Dedicated privacy enforcement budget of $100+ million
- **Technical staff**: Hire data scientists, engineers, and AI specialists
- **Study authority**: Authority to conduct market studies and compel data from companies
- **Interagency coordination**: Formal coordination with state AGs and international authorities

### Evidence Base

- FTC currently brings only 20-30 privacy cases per year; insufficient for economy of scale
- FTC budget ($430 million total, FY 2025) is a fraction of what EU DPAs spend collectively
- FTC's Section 5 authority requires showing "unfair or deceptive" practice, limiting scope
- FTC lacks first-offense civil penalty authority, reducing deterrence

### Costs

| Cost Category | Estimated | Source/Basis |
|---------------|-----------|-------------|
| Budget increase | $100-150 million | Based on enforcement needs analysis |
| Additional staff | 200-400 | Privacy, technical, legal |

### Timeline

- Achievable through appropriations and FTC Act amendments within 6-12 months
- Rulemaking authority could be delegated more quickly than creating new agency

## Solution 5: Universal Opt-Out Mechanism

### Description

Require all companies to honor a universal opt-out signal (such as Global Privacy Control) that allows consumers to opt out of data sale and targeted advertising with a single browser or device setting.

### Key Provisions

- **Mandatory recognition**: All companies covered by privacy laws must honor GPC or equivalent universal signals
- **Technical standard**: FTC or new agency establishes technical standard for opt-out signals
- **Default settings**: Browsers and operating systems must offer opt-out signal as a prominently available setting
- **No degradation of service**: Companies may not degrade service for users who opt out
- **No dark patterns**: Companies may not use manipulative design to override or discourage opt-out signals

### Evidence Base

- California CPRA requires companies to honor GPC signals (effective 2023)
- Colorado Privacy Act requires universal opt-out mechanism
- Global Privacy Control (GPC) supported by major browsers (Firefox, Brave) and extensions (DuckDuckGo Privacy Essentials)
- California AG enforcement action against Sephora (2022) for failing to honor GPC signals; $1.2 million penalty

### Costs

| Cost Category | Estimated | Bearer |
|---------------|-----------|--------|
| Company technical implementation | $50K-500K per company | Industry |
| Standard development | $2-5 million | Federal budget |
| Enforcement | Part of general privacy enforcement | Federal/state budget |

### Timeline

- Technical standard: 6 months
- Implementation period: 12 months
- Achievable at state level immediately; federal level within 12-18 months

## Solution 6: Data Minimization and Purpose Limitation Rules

### Description

Shift from notice-and-consent to structural limits on data collection and use. Companies may collect only data necessary for the service requested and may not repurpose it without separate consent.

### Key Provisions

- **Collection limitation**: Data collection limited to what is reasonably necessary to provide the requested service
- **Purpose limitation**: Data may not be used for purposes materially different from those for which it was collected
- **Retention limits**: Data must be deleted when no longer necessary for the purpose of collection
- **Prohibition on pay-for-privacy**: Companies may not charge more for privacy-respecting service
- **Sensitive data restrictions**: Heightened restrictions on health, financial, biometric, location, and children's data

### Evidence Base

- GDPR's data minimization principle (Article 5(1)(c)) has been enforced across EU since 2018
- Maryland Online Data Privacy Act (2024) includes strongest U.S. data minimization requirements
- Research shows that notice-and-consent frameworks place impossible burdens on consumers (McDonald & Cranor, 2008; Solove, 2013)
- FTC has advocated for data minimization since 2012 privacy report

### Costs

| Cost Category | Estimated | Bearer |
|---------------|-----------|--------|
| Business model adjustment | Significant for surveillance advertisers | Industry |
| Compliance systems | $500K-5 million per large company | Industry |
| Consumer benefit | Reduced data breach exposure | Consumers (benefit) |

### Timeline

- Included in comprehensive federal legislation (Solution 1)
- State-level adoption possible within 12 months
- Industry implementation: 18-24 months

## Solution 7: Private Right of Action with Safeguards

### Description

Establish a private right of action allowing consumers to sue for privacy violations, with reasonable safeguards to prevent abusive litigation.

### Key Provisions

- **Standing**: Any consumer whose data rights are violated may bring civil action
- **Statutory damages**: $100-750 per consumer per incident (modeled on CCPA data breach provision)
- **Actual damages**: Available where proven
- **Notice and cure**: 30-day cure period for businesses acting in good faith
- **Attorney's fees**: Available to prevailing plaintiffs
- **Small business safe harbor**: Businesses below revenue/data thresholds have cure-first safe harbor
- **Class action availability**: Class actions permitted for systematic violations
- **AG certification option**: State AG may certify class actions for large-scale violations

### Evidence Base

- CCPA's private right of action for data breaches has produced accountability without excessive litigation
- GDPR's Article 82 private right of action has not overwhelmed European courts
- Environmental law's citizen suit provisions demonstrate workability of private enforcement
- FTC enforcement alone cannot scale to millions of data-collecting companies

### Costs

| Cost Category | Estimated | Bearer |
|---------------|-----------|--------|
| Litigation defense (large companies) | $1-10 million/year | Industry |
| Consumer recoveries | Variable | Industry to consumers |
| Court system costs | Moderate | Judicial budget |

### Timeline

- Included in comprehensive federal legislation
- Some states could enact independently within 12 months

## Solution 8: Algorithmic Accountability and Automated Decision-Making Rights

### Description

Establish rights related to automated decision-making, including the right to know when algorithms make significant decisions about individuals and the right to human review.

### Key Provisions

- **Transparency**: Companies must disclose when automated systems make significant decisions affecting consumers
- **Explanation**: Consumers have right to meaningful explanation of automated decisions
- **Human review**: Right to request human review of automated decisions affecting employment, credit, housing, insurance, or education
- **Impact assessments**: Companies using high-risk automated systems must conduct and publish algorithmic impact assessments
- **Non-discrimination**: Automated systems must be tested for discriminatory outcomes
- **Opt-out**: Right to opt out of fully automated decision-making for significant decisions

### Evidence Base

- GDPR Article 22 provides right not to be subject to automated individual decision-making
- Colorado Privacy Act requires profiling opt-out
- EU AI Act (2024) establishes risk-based framework for algorithmic accountability
- White House Blueprint for an AI Bill of Rights (2022) identified algorithmic discrimination as key concern
- EEOC has investigated AI hiring tools for disparate impact

### Costs

| Cost Category | Estimated | Bearer |
|---------------|-----------|--------|
| Impact assessments | $100K-1 million per system | Industry |
| Human review systems | $500K-5 million per company | Industry |
| Enforcement | Part of general privacy enforcement | Federal/state budget |

### Timeline

- Included in comprehensive federal legislation
- Standalone algorithmic accountability legislation possible within 12-18 months

## Dependencies

- Solutions 1, 5, 6, 7, and 8 can be combined in comprehensive legislation
- Solution 2 (dedicated agency) ideally accompanies or follows Solution 1
- Solution 4 (strengthened FTC) is an interim measure pending Solution 2
- Solution 3 (data broker regulation) can proceed independently as standalone legislation
- Solution 5 (universal opt-out) can be implemented at state level immediately
