# Consumer Data Rights: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Consumer data rights legislation in the United States has been pursued primarily at the state level since 2018, with multiple failed federal attempts. This document provides draft federal legislation, state model legislation, and regulatory frameworks to establish comprehensive consumer data rights. The legislative approach prioritizes data minimization over notice-and-consent, establishes enforceable individual rights, creates robust enforcement mechanisms, and maintains a federal floor that does not preempt stronger state protections.

## Federal Legislation

### American Consumer Data Rights Act

**Purpose**: Establish comprehensive federal consumer data rights, including access, deletion, correction, portability, and opt-out rights; require data minimization and purpose limitation; prohibit dark patterns; create enforcement through FTC authority, state AG concurrent enforcement, and private right of action.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "American Consumer Data Rights Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSE.

(a) FINDINGS.—Congress finds that:
(1) The United States is the only major democracy without a
comprehensive federal data privacy law.
(2) Americans generate vast quantities of personal data that
is collected, used, and sold with minimal federal regulation.
(3) Nineteen states have enacted comprehensive privacy laws,
creating inconsistent protections across the country.
(4) Data brokers compile detailed profiles on nearly every
American adult without meaningful consent.
(5) Data breaches exposed 353 million records in 2023, imposing
costs on consumers while companies face limited accountability.
(6) Notice-and-consent frameworks have failed to protect
consumers due to unreadable privacy policies, dark patterns,
and coerced consent.
(7) Self-regulation by industry has consistently failed to
protect consumer privacy.

(b) PURPOSE.—The purpose of this Act is to—
(1) establish enforceable consumer data rights;
(2) require data minimization and purpose limitation;
(3) create robust enforcement mechanisms;
(4) regulate data brokers; and
(5) establish a federal floor of protection without preempting
stronger State laws.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:
(1) COVERED ENTITY.—The term "covered entity" means any
person or entity that, alone or jointly with others, determines
the purposes and means of collecting, processing, or
transferring covered data, and—
    (A) is subject to the Federal Trade Commission Act;
    (B) is a common carrier subject to the Communications
    Act of 1934; or
    (C) is a nonprofit organization.
(2) COVERED DATA.—The term "covered data" means information
that identifies or is linked or reasonably linkable to an
individual or a device that identifies or is linked or
reasonably linkable to an individual, including derived
data and unique identifiers.
(3) SENSITIVE COVERED DATA.—The term "sensitive covered
data" includes—
    (A) government-issued identifiers (Social Security
    numbers, passport numbers, driver's license numbers);
    (B) financial account information;
    (C) biometric information;
    (D) precise geolocation information;
    (E) health information;
    (F) information revealing race, ethnicity, national
    origin, religion, sexual orientation, or gender identity;
    (G) private communications content;
    (H) login credentials;
    (I) information of individuals under 17; and
    (J) any information collected through a device in or
    near a private residence.
(4) DATA BROKER.—The term "data broker" means a covered
entity whose primary revenue is derived from collecting,
processing, or transferring covered data of individuals
with whom the entity does not have a direct relationship.
(5) AFFIRMATIVE EXPRESS CONSENT.—The term "affirmative
express consent" means an affirmative act by an individual
that clearly communicates the individual's authorization,
obtained after the individual is presented with a clear and
conspicuous description of the data practice, and is not
obtained through a dark pattern.
(6) DARK PATTERN.—The term "dark pattern" means a user
interface designed or manipulated with the substantial effect
of subverting or impairing user autonomy, decision-making,
or choice.

SEC. 4. CONSUMER DATA RIGHTS.

(a) RIGHT OF ACCESS.—A covered entity shall, upon verified
request by an individual, provide the individual with—
(1) the categories and specific pieces of covered data
collected about the individual;
(2) the purposes for which the data is used;
(3) the categories of third parties with whom data has
been shared; and
(4) the sources from which the data was obtained.

(b) RIGHT OF DELETION.—A covered entity shall, upon verified
request by an individual, delete all covered data pertaining to
the individual, and shall direct any service provider or third
party to whom the data was transferred to delete such data,
unless retention is required by law.

(c) RIGHT OF CORRECTION.—A covered entity shall, upon verified
request by an individual, correct inaccurate covered data
pertaining to the individual.

(d) RIGHT OF PORTABILITY.—A covered entity shall, upon verified
request by an individual, provide the individual's covered data
in a structured, commonly used, machine-readable format that
allows the individual to transfer the data to another entity.

(e) RIGHT TO OPT OUT.—An individual shall have the right to
opt out of—
(1) the transfer of covered data to third parties;
(2) targeted advertising based on covered data; and
(3) profiling in furtherance of decisions that produce
legal or similarly significant effects.

(f) UNIVERSAL OPT-OUT.—Covered entities shall honor universal
opt-out signals, including the Global Privacy Control
specification or any successor standard designated by the
Commission.

(g) RESPONSE REQUIREMENTS.—Covered entities shall respond to
verified requests under this section within 45 days and shall
not charge a fee for the first two requests per individual
per year.

SEC. 5. DATA MINIMIZATION AND PURPOSE LIMITATION.

(a) COLLECTION LIMITATION.—A covered entity shall not collect,
process, or transfer covered data beyond what is reasonably
necessary and proportionate to provide or maintain a specific
product or service requested by the individual.

(b) PURPOSE LIMITATION.—Covered data shall not be processed
for a purpose materially different from the purpose for which
it was collected without obtaining affirmative express consent.

(c) RETENTION LIMITATION.—Covered data shall not be retained
longer than reasonably necessary to fulfill the purpose for
which it was collected. Covered entities shall establish and
disclose data retention schedules.

(d) SENSITIVE DATA.—Sensitive covered data shall not be
collected or processed without affirmative express consent
obtained prior to collection.

SEC. 6. TRANSPARENCY AND NOTICE.

(a) PRIVACY POLICY REQUIREMENTS.—Covered entities shall
maintain a clear, conspicuous, and accessible privacy policy
that includes—
(1) categories of covered data collected;
(2) purposes for processing;
(3) categories of third parties receiving data;
(4) data retention periods;
(5) instructions for exercising consumer rights; and
(6) contact information for privacy inquiries.

(b) PLAIN LANGUAGE.—Privacy policies shall be written in
clear, plain language at or below a tenth-grade reading level.

(c) SHORT-FORM NOTICE.—Covered entities shall provide a
standardized, machine-readable short-form notice summarizing
key data practices, in a format prescribed by the Commission.

SEC. 7. DARK PATTERNS PROHIBITION.

(a) PROHIBITION.—A covered entity shall not use a dark
pattern to obtain consent, prevent an individual from
exercising rights under this Act, or manipulate an
individual into providing more covered data than necessary.

(b) CONSENT OBTAINED THROUGH DARK PATTERNS.—Any consent
obtained through a dark pattern shall not constitute
affirmative express consent.

(c) DESIGN REQUIREMENTS.—Interfaces for exercising rights
under this Act shall be—
(1) at least as prominent and accessible as interfaces
for providing data;
(2) available without requiring creation of an account;
and
(3) designed to facilitate, not impede, the exercise
of rights.

SEC. 8. DATA BROKER REQUIREMENTS.

(a) REGISTRATION.—Every data broker shall register annually
with the Commission, providing—
(1) the categories of data collected;
(2) the number of individuals whose data is held;
(3) the categories of data purchasers;
(4) opt-out mechanisms available; and
(5) data breach history for the preceding three years.

(b) CONSUMER RIGHTS.—Data brokers shall—
(1) enable individuals to access all data held about them;
(2) enable individuals to delete all data held about them;
(3) enable individuals to opt out of all data collection
and transfer; and
(4) provide a single, centralized mechanism for exercising
these rights.

(c) CENTRALIZED REGISTRY.—The Commission shall maintain a
public registry of all registered data brokers with links to
their opt-out mechanisms.

(d) PROHIBITION ON SENSITIVE DATA SALES.—Data brokers shall
not sell or transfer sensitive covered data without affirmative
express consent from the individual to whom it pertains.

SEC. 9. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) FTC ENFORCEMENT.—
(1) A violation of this Act shall be treated as a violation
of a rule defining an unfair or deceptive act or practice
under Section 18(a)(1)(B) of the Federal Trade Commission
Act (15 U.S.C. 57a(a)(1)(B)).
(2) The Commission may impose civil penalties of not more
than $50,000 per violation per day.
(3) The Commission shall have authority to issue rules
implementing this Act under the Administrative Procedure Act.

(b) STATE ATTORNEY GENERAL ENFORCEMENT.—
(1) The attorney general of any State may bring a civil
action in Federal or State court to enforce this Act.
(2) The attorney general shall notify the Commission prior
to filing suit and the Commission may intervene.
(3) State attorneys general may obtain injunctive relief,
damages, civil penalties, and attorney's fees.

(c) PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.—
(1) Any individual whose rights under this Act have been
violated may bring a civil action in Federal or State court.
(2) STATUTORY DAMAGES.—An individual may recover the greater
of actual damages or statutory damages of $100 to $750 per
individual per incident.
(3) NOTICE AND CURE.—Prior to filing suit, an individual
shall provide 30 days' written notice to the covered entity,
describing the alleged violation. If the covered entity cures
the violation within 30 days and provides written confirmation,
no action may be brought for that violation. The cure provision
shall not apply to repeated or willful violations.
(4) ATTORNEY'S FEES.—A court shall award reasonable
attorney's fees and costs to a prevailing plaintiff.
(5) CLASS ACTIONS.—Class actions may be maintained for
systematic violations affecting 100 or more individuals.

SEC. 10. SMALL BUSINESS PROVISIONS.

(a) EXEMPTION THRESHOLD.—This Act shall not apply to a
covered entity that—
(1) has annual gross revenue of less than $25 million;
(2) collects, processes, or transfers covered data of
fewer than 50,000 individuals annually; and
(3) does not derive more than 50 percent of revenue from
transferring covered data.

(b) SMALL ENTITY COMPLIANCE GUIDE.—The Commission, in
consultation with the Small Business Administration, shall
publish a compliance guide for small entities.

SEC. 11. PREEMPTION.

(a) FEDERAL FLOOR.—This Act establishes minimum protections
and shall not be construed to preempt, limit, or otherwise
affect any State law that provides greater protection to
individuals with respect to covered data.

(b) PRESERVATION OF STATE LAWS.—Nothing in this Act shall
be construed to preempt the California Consumer Privacy Act,
the California Privacy Rights Act, or any State law providing
protections equal to or greater than those provided by this Act.

SEC. 12. EFFECTIVE DATE.

(a) This Act shall take effect 18 months after the date of
enactment.
(b) The Commission shall promulgate implementing regulations
within 12 months of enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- Section 4 establishes comprehensive consumer rights modeled on GDPR and strongest state laws
- Section 5 shifts from notice-and-consent to data minimization as structural protection
- Section 7 prohibits dark patterns, addressing primary mechanism by which consent is manufactured
- Section 8 creates first federal data broker regulation
- Section 9 provides three-tier enforcement: FTC, state AGs, and private right of action with reasonable safeguards
- Section 10 exempts genuine small businesses while preventing data-intensive companies from using thresholds to avoid coverage
- Section 11 establishes federal floor without preempting stronger state laws

**Potential Challenges**:

- Industry lobbying against private right of action and data minimization requirements
- Preemption dispute with states and industry
- First Amendment challenges to data minimization and transfer restrictions
- Scope of "reasonably necessary" in data minimization standard

**Refinements**:

- Phased implementation: large companies (18 months), medium (24 months), small (36 months)
- Safe harbor for companies that adopt approved codes of conduct
- FTC guidance on data minimization standards by industry sector
- Annual reporting requirements on enforcement activity

## State Model Legislation

### Model State Consumer Data Privacy Act

**Purpose**: Provide comprehensive consumer data privacy protections at the state level, including private right of action and data minimization.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Consumer Data Privacy Act".

SECTION 2. CONSUMER RIGHTS.

(a) Every consumer shall have the right to:
(1) Know what personal data a business collects about them;
(2) Access their personal data in a portable format;
(3) Correct inaccurate personal data;
(4) Delete their personal data;
(5) Opt out of the sale or sharing of their personal data;
(6) Opt out of targeted advertising; and
(7) Opt out of profiling with legal or significant effects.

(b) Businesses shall honor universal opt-out signals including
the Global Privacy Control specification.

SECTION 3. DATA MINIMIZATION.

(a) A business shall not collect, process, or share personal
data beyond what is reasonably necessary to provide the
product or service requested by the consumer.

(b) A business shall not retain personal data longer than
reasonably necessary for the purpose of collection.

SECTION 4. SENSITIVE DATA.

(a) A business shall not collect or process sensitive personal
data without the consumer's affirmative express consent.

(b) Sensitive personal data includes biometric data, precise
geolocation, health data, financial data, data revealing
race or ethnicity, sexual orientation, religion, or
immigration status, and data of individuals under 17.

SECTION 5. DARK PATTERNS.

(a) A business shall not use a dark pattern to obtain consent,
impede the exercise of consumer rights, or manipulate a
consumer into providing unnecessary data.

(b) Consent obtained through a dark pattern is void.

SECTION 6. DATA BROKER REGISTRATION.

(a) Every data broker operating in this State shall register
annually with the [Attorney General/Secretary of State].

(b) Registration shall include categories of data collected,
number of individuals profiled, opt-out mechanisms, and
breach history.

SECTION 7. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) The Attorney General may bring actions to enforce this Act
and may seek injunctive relief, civil penalties of up to
$7,500 per intentional violation and $2,500 per unintentional
violation, and attorney's fees.

(b) PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.—Any consumer whose rights under
this Act are violated may bring a civil action for statutory
damages of $100 to $750 per consumer per incident, or actual
damages, whichever is greater.

(c) A 30-day notice and cure period shall apply to
unintentional first-time violations.

SECTION 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect [12 months] after enactment.
```

## Regulatory Framework

### FTC Commercial Surveillance Rulemaking

**Authority**: FTC Act Section 18 (Magnuson-Moss rulemaking)

**Draft Rule Framework**:

```text
COMMERCIAL SURVEILLANCE AND DATA SECURITY RULE

1. DATA MINIMIZATION
(a) Covered entities shall limit data collection to what is
reasonably necessary to provide the product or service
requested by the consumer.
(b) Covered entities shall document the necessity of each
category of data collected.

2. DARK PATTERN PROHIBITION
(a) Covered entities shall not use deceptive design practices
to obtain consent, prevent exercise of consumer rights, or
manipulate consumers.
(b) Consent mechanisms shall be clear, prominent, and use
plain language.

3. AUTOMATED DECISION-MAKING
(a) Covered entities using automated decision-making systems
that produce legal or significant effects shall conduct and
publish impact assessments.
(b) Consumers shall have the right to opt out of automated
decision-making and request human review.

4. DATA SECURITY
(a) Covered entities shall implement reasonable data security
practices proportionate to the volume and sensitivity of
data processed.
(b) The Commission shall publish minimum security standards.

5. ENFORCEMENT
(a) Violations constitute unfair or deceptive acts under
Section 5 of the FTC Act.
(b) Civil penalties apply per Section 5(m)(1)(A).
```

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Authority

- **Commerce Clause**: Federal authority to regulate interstate commercial data practices
- **Spending Clause**: Conditions on federal funding
- **FTC Act Section 5**: Unfair or deceptive practices authority
- **First Amendment**: Data minimization and transfer restrictions face potential commercial speech challenges; *Sorrell v. IMS Health* (2011) applied heightened scrutiny but did not prohibit all data regulation

### Preemption Analysis

- **Federal floor approach**: Preserves stronger state laws; avoids weakening California CPRA
- **Historical precedent**: CAN-SPAM (2003) preempted stronger state email laws, weakening protection; this cautionary example supports non-preemption
- **Industry preference**: Industry strongly prefers ceiling preemption to eliminate California standard

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- **FTC**: Primary federal enforcer; rulemaking and civil penalty authority
- **State AGs**: Concurrent enforcement; critical for scaling
- **Private right of action**: Essential for enforcement at scale; notice-and-cure and damages caps prevent abuse

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Small business exemption gaming | Companies structure to stay below thresholds | High |
| "Reasonably necessary" ambiguity | Data minimization standard subject to broad interpretation | High |
| Consent manufacturing | New dark patterns designed to circumvent prohibition | Moderate |
| De-identification workaround | Companies claim data is de-identified when re-identification is feasible | High |
| Service provider exception | Companies classify data transfers as "service provider" relationships | Moderate |
| Cross-device tracking gaps | Data collected across devices may evade individual opt-out | Moderate |

### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Enforcement resource gap | Federal and state agencies underfunded relative to data economy scale | Budget constraints |
| Technical complexity | Regulators lack technical capacity to audit data practices | Government hiring challenges |
| Jurisdictional gaps | Federal law may not reach foreign data processors | Extraterritorial limits |
| Retroactivity | Existing data hoards not addressed unless deletion requested | Legal constraints |
| Consumer awareness | Rights mean little if consumers do not know about them | Information asymmetry |
| AI training data | Historical data already used to train AI models cannot be "deleted" | Technical reality |

### Rectification Procedures

1. **Anti-circumvention provision**: Prohibit structuring entities to avoid thresholds; apply look-through rules for affiliated entities and common ownership.

2. **Data minimization guidance**: FTC shall issue sector-specific guidance on what constitutes "reasonably necessary" data collection, updated annually.

3. **Technical standards for de-identification**: NIST shall develop standards for de-identification that account for re-identification risk; data that can be re-identified is covered data.

4. **Service provider audit requirements**: Service providers receiving data must be subject to contractual restrictions and periodic audits; transfers to service providers that process data for their own purposes shall be treated as third-party transfers.

5. **Consumer awareness mandate**: Covered entities shall provide annual notice of consumer rights in clear, plain language, separate from privacy policies.

6. **Enforcement funding mechanism**: Establish a privacy enforcement fund capitalized by civil penalties; dedicate fund to FTC and state AG enforcement capacity.

7. **AI training data provisions**: Companies that used personal data to train AI models must disclose this use and provide mechanisms for individuals to request that their data not be used in future training.

### General Implementation Concerns

- FTC rulemaking is slow (Magnuson-Moss process takes 3-5 years); legislation should include direct statutory requirements rather than relying solely on rulemaking
- State AG enforcement depends on political will of individual AGs; some states may not prioritize privacy enforcement
- Private right of action effectiveness depends on court interpretation of standing and damages requirements
- International enforcement cooperation needed for companies operating across borders
