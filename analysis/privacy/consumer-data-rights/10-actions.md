# Consumer Data Rights: Actions

## Overview

While systemic reform requires legislation and institutional change, individuals can take meaningful steps to protect their data, advocate for reform, and support organizations working on consumer data rights. This guide provides concrete actions organized by effort level and impact.

## Individual Data Protection Actions

### Immediate Steps (Low Effort)

- **Install a universal opt-out signal**: Use a browser that supports Global Privacy Control (GPC) -- Firefox, Brave, or DuckDuckGo. Companies in California, Colorado, and Connecticut are legally required to honor GPC signals
- **Use a privacy-focused search engine**: DuckDuckGo, Startpage, or Brave Search do not track search queries
- **Review app permissions**: Remove unnecessary permissions (location, contacts, microphone, camera) from mobile apps
- **Opt out of data broker profiles**: Submit deletion requests to major brokers (see Data Broker Opt-Out List below)
- **Use a password manager**: Generates unique passwords; reduces data exposure from breaches
- **Enable two-factor authentication**: Protects accounts from credential-based attacks

### Moderate Steps (Some Effort)

- **Exercise your state privacy rights**: If you live in a state with a privacy law, submit data access and deletion requests to companies that hold your data
- **Use a VPN for browsing**: Prevents ISP tracking and reduces location data collection
- **Review and reduce account data**: Delete old accounts; minimize data shared with active accounts
- **Opt out of targeted advertising**: Use the Digital Advertising Alliance opt-out (optout.aboutads.info) and NAI opt-out (optout.networkadvertising.org)
- **Use privacy-respecting email**: ProtonMail, Tutanota, or other encrypted email providers
- **Limit social media data sharing**: Review privacy settings; limit profile information; disable data sharing with third-party apps

### Advanced Steps (Higher Effort)

- **Request your data from major companies**: Exercise access rights with Google (takeout.google.com), Facebook (facebook.com/dyi), Amazon, and other major platforms to understand what they hold
- **Use a privacy-focused phone OS**: GrapheneOS or CalyxOS reduce data collection on Android devices
- **Self-host services where possible**: Email, cloud storage, and other services can be self-hosted to reduce third-party data collection
- **Participate in data access research**: Some organizations request volunteer data to study corporate data practices

## Advocacy Actions

### Contact Elected Officials

**Federal**: Urge your U.S. senators and representative to support comprehensive federal privacy legislation with:

- A federal floor (no ceiling preemption of stronger state laws)
- A private right of action for consumers
- Data minimization requirements
- Data broker regulation
- Adequate FTC/agency funding

**State**: If your state does not have a comprehensive privacy law, contact your state legislators to support enactment. If your state has a law, advocate for stronger enforcement funding.

### Template Email to Federal Legislators

> Subject: Support Comprehensive Federal Privacy Legislation
>
> Dear [Senator/Representative] [Last Name],
>
> I am writing as your constituent to urge you to support comprehensive federal privacy legislation that gives Americans meaningful control over their personal data.
>
> The United States is the only major democracy without a comprehensive federal data privacy law. Companies collect vast amounts of personal data with minimal regulation, data brokers compile profiles on nearly every American without consent, and data breaches expose hundreds of millions of records annually. Meanwhile, 19 states have enacted their own privacy laws, creating a patchwork that benefits neither consumers nor businesses.
>
> I urge you to support legislation that includes:
>
> - Enforceable consumer rights to access, delete, correct, and port personal data
> - Data minimization requirements limiting collection to what is necessary
> - A federal floor that does not preempt stronger state protections
> - A private right of action so consumers can enforce their rights
> - Regulation of data brokers including registration and consent requirements
> - Adequate funding for federal enforcement
>
> [Personal story about data privacy concern if applicable]
>
> Thank you for your attention to this important issue.
>
> Sincerely,
> [Name]
> [Address]

### Template Phone Script

> "Hi, my name is [Name] and I am a constituent from [City/Town]. I am calling to urge [Senator/Representative] [Last Name] to support comprehensive federal privacy legislation. Americans deserve the right to control their personal data, and we need a strong federal law that includes data minimization, a private right of action, and does not preempt stronger state laws. Can I count on [Senator/Representative] [Last Name]'s support? Thank you."

## Talking Points

### For Conversations with Friends and Family

- "Did you know that data brokers have profiles with over 1,500 data points on nearly every American adult? You've never consented to this and can't easily stop it."
- "Every major democracy has a comprehensive data privacy law except the United States. We're the outlier."
- "81% of Americans feel they lack control over their personal data. This isn't about having something to hide -- it's about having the right to decide who knows what about you."
- "Data breaches exposed 353 million records in 2023 alone. Companies collect more data than they need and don't protect it adequately."
- "Privacy policies are designed to be unreadable. It would take 76 workdays a year to read every privacy policy you encounter."

### For Conversations with Policymakers

- "19 states have enacted privacy laws because the federal government has not acted. A federal floor would create consistency without eliminating stronger state protections."
- "The FTC has about 100 staff dedicated to privacy for an economy with millions of data-collecting companies. We need a dedicated data protection agency."
- "Industry says self-regulation works, but every self-regulatory initiative in privacy has failed. Do Not Track, P3P, industry codes of conduct -- all failed."
- "A private right of action is essential because government enforcement cannot scale to the data economy. Reasonable safeguards can prevent abusive litigation."
- "Data minimization -- not just notice-and-consent -- is the key structural reform. Companies should collect only what they need."

## Community Actions

- **Organize a privacy workshop**: Host a session at a library, community center, or school on practical data protection steps
- **Support local journalism on privacy**: Share and amplify reporting on data practices by local companies and governments
- **Engage local government**: Advocate for municipal privacy policies, limits on government data purchases, and surveillance technology oversight
- **Join or start a privacy advocacy group**: Connect with national organizations for local chapter support
- **Testify at state legislature hearings**: When privacy bills are considered, provide constituent testimony
