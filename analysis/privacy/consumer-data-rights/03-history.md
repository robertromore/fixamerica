# Consumer Data Rights: History

## Overview

The history of consumer data rights in America is a story of technology consistently outpacing law. Each era of data collection -- from credit reporting to the internet to mobile apps to artificial intelligence -- has generated new privacy harms that existing legal frameworks were not designed to address. The result is a reactive, sector-specific approach that has never caught up to the reality of pervasive data collection.

---

## Era 1: Pre-Digital Data Collection (1960s-1980s)

### Context

Before the internet, consumer data existed primarily in physical files and early computer databases. Credit bureaus, direct mail companies, and government agencies were the main collectors. Public concern about "Big Brother" government databases led to the first wave of privacy legislation.

### Key Developments

- **1966** - Freedom of Information Act establishes government transparency principle
- **1968** - Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA) drafting begins after consumer complaints about inaccurate credit reports
- **1970** - **FCRA enacted** -- first major consumer data law; gives consumers right to access and dispute credit reports (15 U.S.C. SS 1681 et seq.)
- **1973** - HEW Advisory Committee publishes *Records, Computers, and the Rights of Citizens*, establishing Fair Information Practice Principles (FIPPs)
- **1974** - **Privacy Act** -- regulates federal government records; establishes Code of Fair Information Practices for government
- **1977** - Fair Debt Collection Practices Act limits debt collector contact
- **1978** - Right to Financial Privacy Act requires government to obtain subpoena for bank records
- **1980** - OECD publishes Guidelines on the Protection of Privacy, establishing international framework

### Lessons

- Legislation responded to specific harms (credit reporting errors, government databases) rather than establishing general principles
- FIPPs articulated comprehensive privacy principles that the U.S. never enacted into general law
- Other democracies (Sweden 1973, Germany 1977, France 1978) began enacting comprehensive data protection laws while the U.S. chose sector-specific approaches

## Era 2: Early Digital and Internet Commerce (1986-2000)

### Context

The rise of personal computers, electronic communications, and internet commerce created vast new categories of consumer data. The Clinton administration favored industry self-regulation over legislation, a decision with lasting consequences.

### Key Developments

- **1986** - **Electronic Communications Privacy Act (ECPA)** -- extends wiretap protections to electronic communications; already outdated by modern standards
- **1988** - **Video Privacy Protection Act (VPPA)** -- enacted after Robert Bork's video rental records disclosed during Supreme Court confirmation; narrow but strong
- **1991** - **Telephone Consumer Protection Act (TCPA)** -- limits telemarketing calls
- **1993** - First web browser (Mosaic) launched; commercial internet begins
- **1994** - Netscape introduces cookies for web tracking
- **1995** - **EU Data Protection Directive (95/46/EC)** enacted -- comprehensive approach diverges sharply from U.S. sector model
- **1996** - **HIPAA** enacted -- protects health data held by covered entities (insurers, providers), but excludes health apps and most tech companies
- **1997** - FTC reports to Congress on online privacy; recommends industry self-regulation
- **1998** - **COPPA enacted** -- protects children under 13 online; first internet-era privacy law
- **1998** - **EU-U.S. Safe Harbor Framework** -- enables data transfers through self-certification
- **1999** - **Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act (GLBA)** -- requires financial institutions to provide privacy notices; opt-out only
- **2000** - DoubleClick (advertising network) faces scrutiny for merging web browsing data with real names; settles FTC investigation

### Lessons

- Clinton-era preference for self-regulation set lasting precedent against comprehensive legislation
- EU-U.S. divergence on privacy protection began in this era and has deepened since
- Sector-specific statutes (VPPA, COPPA, GLBA) addressed narrow harms while leaving general consumer data unprotected
- Industry successfully framed regulation as a threat to internet growth

## Era 3: Social Media and Mobile Data (2001-2017)

### Context

The explosion of social media, smartphones, and cloud computing created unprecedented data collection at scale. Surveillance advertising became the dominant internet business model. Consumer awareness grew through high-profile breaches and scandals, but legislative action remained absent at the federal level.

### Key Developments

- **2003** - **CAN-SPAM Act** -- weak federal email marketing law that preempts stronger state laws; widely criticized
- **2004** - Facebook launches; begins era of social media data extraction at scale
- **2005** - ChoicePoint data breach exposes 163,000 consumer records; triggers breach notification law movement
- **2005-2010** - **48 states enact data breach notification laws** -- first wave of meaningful state-level consumer data protection
- **2007** - iPhone launch accelerates mobile data collection
- **2009** - FTC issues updated privacy principles; still recommends self-regulation
- **2010** - *Wall Street Journal* "What They Know" series exposes extent of online tracking
- **2012** - White House releases *Consumer Privacy Bill of Rights*; legislation never introduced
- **2012** - FTC issues major privacy report calling for "Do Not Track" mechanism; industry ignores recommendations
- **2013** - **Snowden revelations** -- while focused on government surveillance, increased public awareness of data collection generally
- **2013** - California enacts CalOPPA, requiring websites to disclose if they honor "Do Not Track" signals
- **2014** - Senate Commerce Committee releases *A Review of the Data Broker Industry*, documenting scale of consumer profiling
- **2014** - FTC report *Data Brokers: A Call for Transparency and Accountability* recommends legislation; Congress does not act
- **2015** - **EU-U.S. Safe Harbor invalidated** by Court of Justice of the EU in *Schrems I*; replaced by Privacy Shield
- **2016** - **EU adopts GDPR** (effective 2018); establishes global standard for consumer data rights
- **2017** - Congress repeals FCC broadband privacy rules under Congressional Review Act, preventing ISP regulation

### Lessons

- Despite growing public concern and expert recommendations, federal legislation continued to fail
- Data breach notification laws showed that states could drive privacy reform in the absence of federal action
- The surveillance advertising business model became so dominant that reform threatened the revenue base of the most politically influential companies
- Industry self-regulation consistently failed to protect consumers

## Era 4: State Privacy Law Revolution (2018-Present)

### Context

The Cambridge Analytica scandal (2018), combined with GDPR's global influence, catalyzed state-level action. California's ballot initiative forced the first comprehensive state privacy law, and a wave of state legislation followed. Federal efforts came closer than ever but remained stalled.

### Key Developments

- **2018** - **Cambridge Analytica scandal** -- Facebook data on 87 million users harvested for political targeting; global outrage
- **2018** - **California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA)** passed as legislative compromise to preempt stronger ballot initiative; effective January 2020
- **2018** - **Vermont enacts data broker registration law** -- first state to require broker registration
- **2018** - **GDPR takes effect** in EU; becomes global standard; extraterritorial application affects U.S. companies
- **2019** - FTC settles with Facebook for $5 billion -- largest privacy enforcement action in history, but criticized as insufficient
- **2020** - **California Privacy Rights Act (CPRA)** passed by ballot initiative; strengthens CCPA, creates California Privacy Protection Agency (CPPA); effective January 2023
- **2020** - **Schrems II** decision invalidates EU-U.S. Privacy Shield; deepens transatlantic data transfer crisis
- **2021** - Virginia enacts VCDPA; Colorado enacts CPA; state privacy law movement accelerates
- **2022** - **American Data Privacy and Protection Act (ADPPA)** passes House Energy and Commerce Committee with bipartisan support; fails to reach floor due to preemption dispute with California and private right of action opposition
- **2022-2024** - **16 additional states enact privacy laws** (Connecticut, Utah, Iowa, Indiana, Tennessee, Montana, Texas, Oregon, Delaware, New Hampshire, New Jersey, Nebraska, Kentucky, Maryland, Minnesota, Rhode Island)
- **2023** - FTC proposes Commercial Surveillance Rule (ANPR); industry pushback delays action
- **2023** - EU-U.S. Data Privacy Framework adopted, replacing Privacy Shield
- **2024** - **American Privacy Rights Act (APRA)** passes Senate Commerce Committee; stalls over same disputes as ADPPA
- **2024** - FTC brings first enforcement actions against data brokers (X-Mode Social, InMarket, Kochava)
- **2025** - 19 states have comprehensive privacy laws in effect or signed; no federal law

### Lessons

- State action has become the primary driver of consumer data rights in the absence of federal legislation
- California's ballot initiative process broke the legislative logjam at the state level
- Federal legislation repeatedly fails on two issues: whether federal law preempts stronger state laws, and whether consumers can sue companies directly
- Industry has effectively used these two disputes to block comprehensive federal legislation while accepting weaker state laws

## Turning Points

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 1970 | FCRA enacted | First consumer data rights law; established access and correction rights |
| 1973 | FIPPs articulated | Comprehensive privacy principles that U.S. never fully enacted |
| 1995 | EU Data Protection Directive | Established comprehensive approach; U.S.-EU divergence begins |
| 1997 | FTC endorses self-regulation | Set precedent for hands-off approach that lasted decades |
| 2005 | ChoicePoint breach | Catalyzed state breach notification laws |
| 2013 | Snowden revelations | Raised public awareness of surveillance broadly |
| 2016 | GDPR adopted | Created global standard; pressured U.S. reform |
| 2018 | Cambridge Analytica / CCPA | Broke state-level legislative logjam |
| 2022 | ADPPA passes committee | Closest the U.S. has come to comprehensive federal law |
| 2024 | FTC data broker actions | First federal enforcement targeting data brokers directly |

## Key Lessons from History

1. **Self-regulation has consistently failed** -- every era in which industry was trusted to police itself resulted in expanded data collection and consumer harm
2. **Sector-specific legislation creates gaps** -- each new technology generates data uses that existing laws do not cover
3. **States lead when the federal government will not** -- from breach notification to comprehensive privacy, states have driven reform
4. **Industry uses procedural disputes to block legislation** -- preemption and private right of action have become reliable mechanisms for blocking federal laws that industry actually opposes on substance
5. **International pressure matters** -- EU standards (Data Protection Directive, GDPR) have influenced U.S. company practices even without U.S. law
6. **Crises catalyze action** -- ChoicePoint, Snowden, Cambridge Analytica each produced legislative responses, but those responses have been incomplete
