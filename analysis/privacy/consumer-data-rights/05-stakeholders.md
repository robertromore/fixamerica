# Consumer Data Rights: Stakeholders

## Overview

Consumer data rights involve a complex ecosystem of stakeholders with vastly unequal power. Technology companies, data brokers, and advertising networks control the data economy and dominate the political process. Consumers, who generate the data, have the least power. Regulatory agencies, advocacy organizations, and state legislatures occupy intermediate positions with varying degrees of influence.

## Primary Stakeholders

### Consumers

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Population affected | ~330 million Americans |
| Awareness of data practices | 81% feel they lack control (Pew, 2023) |
| Ability to protect themselves | Very limited; opt-out systems are burdensome |
| Political power | Diffuse; difficult to organize around privacy |
| Most impacted groups | Low-income, elderly, minority communities; targets of predatory data practices |

**Consumer Concerns**:

- Data collected without meaningful consent or knowledge
- Unable to find out what data companies hold
- Opt-out processes deliberately complex
- Data breaches expose sensitive information
- Targeted advertising feels invasive
- No meaningful recourse when data is misused

## Decision Makers

### Federal

| Actor | Role | Current Position |
|-------|------|-----------------|
| FTC Chair | Leads privacy enforcement | Favors stronger enforcement; limited by authority |
| Senate Commerce Committee | Primary jurisdiction over privacy legislation | Bipartisan interest; blocked by preemption/PRA disputes |
| House Energy & Commerce Committee | Primary House jurisdiction | Passed ADPPA from committee (2022); APRA (2024) |
| White House | Sets executive branch privacy priorities | Issued AI Executive Order (2023); supports federal privacy law in principle |

### State

| Actor | Role | Current Position |
|-------|------|-----------------|
| California Privacy Protection Agency | Enforces CPRA; first dedicated state privacy agency | Developing regulations; building enforcement capacity |
| California AG | CCPA/CPRA enforcement authority | Active enforcement; opposes federal preemption |
| State AGs (multistate) | Enforce state privacy and consumer protection laws | Coalition actions against tech companies; 33+ AGs joined Google privacy action (2023) |
| State legislatures | Enacting comprehensive privacy laws | 19 states enacted; 10+ considering in 2025 sessions |

### International

| Actor | Role | Influence on U.S. |
|-------|------|------------------|
| European Commission | GDPR enforcement; adequacy decisions | EU-U.S. Data Privacy Framework requires U.S. reforms |
| European Data Protection Board | Coordinates EU DPA enforcement | Sets standards that affect U.S. companies globally |
| UK Information Commissioner's Office | UK GDPR enforcement | Post-Brexit adequacy decisions affect U.S. data flows |

## Coalition Dynamics

### Pro-Regulation Coalition

| Coalition | Members | Strengths | Weaknesses |
|-----------|---------|-----------|------------|
| Privacy advocacy alliance | EFF, EPIC, CDT, Consumer Reports, ACLU | Technical expertise, public trust, litigation capacity | Limited lobbying budget compared to industry |
| State AG coalition | 30+ state AGs | Enforcement authority, bipartisan | Inconsistent; some AGs are industry-friendly |
| California privacy bloc | CA legislature, CPPA, CA AG | Strongest existing law; large market leverage | Opposes federal preemption, blocking federal legislation |
| Academic researchers | Privacy scholars, computer scientists | Evidence base, credibility | Limited political influence |

### Anti-Regulation Coalition

| Coalition | Members | Strengths | Weaknesses |
|-----------|---------|-----------|------------|
| Big Tech alliance | Google, Meta, Amazon, industry groups | Massive lobbying budget, political influence | Public trust declining; internal disagreements |
| Data broker industry | Acxiom, Experian, LexisNexis | Low public visibility; operates quietly | Increasing scrutiny from FTC and media |
| Small business allies | Chamber of Commerce, small business groups | Sympathetic political narrative | Most small businesses not data-intensive |
| Advertising industry | IAB, ANA, 4A's | Economic impact argument | Public increasingly hostile to targeted ads |

### Swing Actors

| Actor | Position | What Could Shift Them |
|-------|----------|----------------------|
| Apple | Markets privacy; lobbies selectively | App Store privacy rules benefit Apple competitively |
| Republican privacy sponsors | Support federal law with preemption | Strong state laws creating compliance pressure |
| Small tech companies | Compliance costs concern them | Scaled thresholds; simplified compliance |
| Labor unions | Generally pro-consumer; workplace monitoring concerns | Explicit worker privacy provisions |
| Civil rights organizations | Support privacy; concerned about discriminatory data practices | Anti-discrimination data provisions |
