# Consumer Data Rights: Overview

## Executive Summary

Americans generate vast quantities of personal data every day -- through online purchases, social media interactions, app usage, connected devices, and financial transactions -- yet they have remarkably few rights over how that data is collected, used, shared, and sold. Unlike residents of the European Union, who benefit from the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), American consumers lack a comprehensive federal privacy law. Instead, a fragmented patchwork of sector-specific federal statutes (HIPAA for health, COPPA for children, GLBA for finance) and a growing but inconsistent set of state laws (beginning with the California Consumer Privacy Act in 2018) leaves most consumer data essentially unregulated at the federal level.

The consequences are significant. Data brokers compile detailed profiles on nearly every American adult without their knowledge or consent. Companies collect far more data than necessary, retain it indefinitely, and share it with undisclosed third parties. Consumers face an asymmetry of information and power: privacy policies are unreadable, consent is manufactured through dark patterns, and opting out is deliberately burdensome. When data breaches occur -- affecting hundreds of millions of records annually -- individuals bear the costs while companies face limited accountability.

Consumer data rights reform seeks to shift the balance of power by establishing enforceable rights to access, correct, delete, and port personal data; requiring meaningful consent and data minimization; creating accountability through private rights of action; and establishing a federal baseline that does not preempt stronger state protections.

## Core Tensions

- **Innovation vs. protection** - Industry argues regulation will stifle innovation; advocates counter that trust enables growth
- **Federal uniformity vs. state experimentation** - Federal preemption would create consistency but could weaken states like California that lead on privacy
- **Consent vs. data minimization** - Notice-and-consent frameworks put burden on consumers; data minimization limits collection regardless of consent
- **Enforcement mechanisms** - Private right of action empowers consumers but industry argues it invites abusive litigation; FTC-only enforcement is resource-constrained
- **Free services vs. privacy** - Surveillance advertising subsidizes free services; privacy regulation may alter business models
- **Individual rights vs. systemic reform** - Individual opt-out rights are insufficient when default is mass collection; structural rules needed

## Key Questions

1. Why does the United States lack a comprehensive federal privacy law when most peer democracies have one?
2. How effective are state privacy laws in practice, and do they protect consumers or primarily protect companies?
3. What consumer data rights are most impactful -- access, deletion, opt-out, or data minimization?
4. Can notice-and-consent frameworks ever work, or must regulation move toward structural limits on data use?
5. How should enforcement be structured -- FTC authority, state AGs, private right of action, or a dedicated data protection agency?
6. What role do data brokers play, and how should they be regulated?
7. How should the U.S. balance the economic value of data-driven services with privacy protection?

## Vision of Success

A successful consumer data rights framework would:

- Establish clear, enforceable federal rights to access, correct, delete, and port personal data
- Require affirmative, specific consent for data collection beyond what is necessary for the requested service
- Impose data minimization and purpose limitation requirements on all commercial entities
- Regulate data brokers through registration, transparency, and consent requirements
- Provide robust enforcement through FTC authority, state AG concurrent enforcement, and a private right of action for statutory violations
- Prohibit dark patterns and deceptive design practices that undermine consumer choice
- Create a federal floor that does not preempt stronger state protections
- Establish interoperability with international frameworks such as the GDPR to facilitate cross-border data flows
- Shift the burden of privacy protection from individuals to the organizations that profit from data collection
