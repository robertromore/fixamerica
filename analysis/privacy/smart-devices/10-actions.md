# Smart Devices and IoT: Actions

## Overview

While systemic reform requires legislative and regulatory action, individuals and communities can take meaningful steps to protect their privacy from smart device surveillance today. This document outlines practical actions organized by effort level, along with collective action strategies for driving broader change.

## Individual Actions

### Immediate (Low Effort)

| Action | How | Impact |
|--------|-----|--------|
| **Audit your connected devices** | List every device in your home that connects to the internet; include often-forgotten devices like smart TVs, thermostats, and appliances | Understand your personal surveillance exposure |
| **Review and adjust privacy settings** | Go through each device's privacy settings; disable data sharing, telemetry, and advertising features where available | Reduce unnecessary data collection |
| **Mute smart speaker microphones when not in use** | Use the physical mute button on smart speakers | Prevent false activations and ambient recording |
| **Disable Automated Content Recognition on smart TVs** | Navigate to TV privacy settings (often under "Viewing Data" or "Smart Features") and disable ACR | Stop TV from tracking what you watch |
| **Delete voice assistant history** | Clear stored voice recordings in Amazon Alexa, Google Assistant, or Siri settings; set auto-delete | Remove stored audio recordings from company servers |
| **Opt out of vehicle data sharing** | Contact dealership or manufacturer; review connected vehicle privacy settings | Reduce driving data flowing to insurers and brokers |
| **Use strong, unique passwords for all IoT devices** | Change default passwords; use a password manager | Prevent unauthorized access to your devices |

### Short-Term (Moderate Effort)

| Action | How | Impact |
|--------|-----|--------|
| **Enable automatic updates on all devices** | Ensure firmware and software updates install automatically | Patch security vulnerabilities promptly |
| **Segment your home network** | Create a separate Wi-Fi network for IoT devices using your router's guest network feature | Prevent IoT devices from accessing personal computers and data |
| **Install a network monitoring tool** | Set up Pi-hole, Firewalla, or similar tool to monitor outgoing IoT traffic | See what data your devices are sending and to whom |
| **Read Mozilla's *Privacy Not Included* reviews before purchasing** | Check foundation.mozilla.org before buying connected devices | Make informed purchasing decisions |
| **Unpair your phone from rental or borrowed vehicles** | Delete your phone from a vehicle's Bluetooth and infotainment system after use | Prevent personal data from remaining on shared vehicles |
| **Review and revoke unnecessary third-party integrations** | Check what apps and services have access to your device data | Reduce third-party data exposure |
| **Disable voice purchasing** | Turn off the ability to make purchases through voice assistants | Prevent accidental purchases and reduce commercial profiling |

### Long-Term (Higher Effort)

| Action | How | Impact |
|--------|-----|--------|
| **Replace cloud-dependent devices with local alternatives** | Switch to Home Assistant, local NVR security cameras, or devices with local processing | Eliminate cloud data transmission |
| **Use privacy-focused alternatives** | Choose devices from manufacturers that prioritize privacy (compare using privacy reviews) | Support privacy-respecting market alternatives |
| **Set up a home firewall with IoT rules** | Install pfSense, OPNsense, or similar with rules blocking unnecessary IoT connections | Block undisclosed data transmissions |
| **Avoid smart devices in sensitive areas** | Do not place always-on microphones or cameras in bedrooms, bathrooms, or children's rooms | Protect intimate spaces from surveillance |
| **Choose vehicles based on privacy practices** | Review Mozilla Foundation car privacy reviews before purchasing | Send market signal that privacy matters to buyers |
| **Conduct periodic IoT privacy audits** | Quarterly review of all connected devices, their settings, and data practices | Maintain ongoing awareness and control |

## Household and Family Actions

| Action | Who | How |
|--------|-----|-----|
| **Discuss smart device privacy with household members** | All adults in household | Share what devices collect and agree on household privacy standards |
| **Establish rules for children's devices** | Parents and guardians | Limit smart toy data collection; avoid always-on devices in children's rooms |
| **Inform guests about recording devices** | Device owners | Disclose smart speakers, cameras, and doorbells to visitors; offer mute options |
| **Create a household device inventory** | All members | Maintain a shared list of connected devices and their privacy settings |
| **Review and set up parental controls** | Parents and guardians | Use device-level controls to limit children's data exposure |

## Community Actions

### Local Level

| Action | How | Audience |
|--------|-----|---------|
| **Organize smart device privacy workshops** | Partner with libraries, community centers, or local tech groups to host educational events | Community members, especially elderly and low-income |
| **Advocate for transparency in landlord-installed devices** | Push for local ordinances requiring landlord disclosure and tenant consent for smart devices | Renters, local council |
| **Request police transparency on surveillance partnerships** | File public records requests about local police Ring partnerships and IoT data acquisition | Local government, police department |
| **Support right-to-repair legislation at state level** | Contact state legislators; testify at hearings | State legislature |
| **Establish neighborhood privacy norms** | Discuss doorbell camera and outdoor IoT device placement and data sharing with neighbors | Neighborhood associations |

### State Level

| Action | How | Audience |
|--------|-----|---------|
| **Support state IoT privacy legislation** | Contact state legislators; testify at committee hearings; submit written comments | State legislature |
| **File complaints with state attorney general** | Report deceptive IoT data practices to state AG consumer protection division | State AG office |
| **Participate in state privacy law rulemaking** | Submit comments during public comment periods for privacy regulations | State privacy agencies |
| **Support state right-to-repair laws** | Advocate for legislation enabling consumers to repair and modify their IoT devices | State legislature |

### Federal Level

| Action | How | Audience |
|--------|-----|---------|
| **Contact Congress about IoT privacy legislation** | Call, email, or write to US senators and representatives; reference specific bills | Federal legislators |
| **Submit comments on FTC IoT rulemaking** | Participate in public comment periods for FTC privacy and security rules | FTC |
| **Support the Fourth Amendment Is Not For Sale Act** | Contact senators and representatives; express support for warrant requirements | Congress |
| **Demand connected vehicle privacy legislation** | Reference GM/LexisNexis data sharing revelations; demand vehicle data protections | Senate Commerce Committee |
| **Support FTC funding for IoT enforcement** | Contact appropriations committee members | Congress |

## Professional and Industry Actions

| Action | Who | How |
|--------|-----|-----|
| **Advocate for privacy-by-design in product development** | IoT engineers and designers | Push for data minimization and local processing in product requirements |
| **Conduct and publish IoT privacy research** | Academics and researchers | Test devices, document data flows, publish findings |
| **Develop and contribute to open-source IoT alternatives** | Software developers | Build privacy-respecting alternatives to commercial platforms |
| **Include IoT privacy in tech ethics curricula** | Educators | Teach IoT privacy issues in computer science and engineering programs |
| **Refuse to build surveillance features** | Engineers and developers | Raise ethical concerns about unnecessary data collection in product design |

## Collective Action Opportunities

| Opportunity | Target | Strategy |
|------------|--------|----------|
| **Consumer boycott of worst offenders** | Manufacturers with egregious practices | Coordinate purchasing decisions based on privacy reviews |
| **Shareholder advocacy** | Publicly traded IoT companies | Submit shareholder resolutions demanding privacy improvements |
| **Class action litigation** | Manufacturers violating privacy laws | Support organizations bringing privacy class actions |
| **Media amplification** | General public | Share IoT privacy stories; document and report egregious practices |
| **Market demand signaling** | Privacy-focused manufacturers | Visibly support and purchase from privacy-respecting companies |

## Action Priority Matrix

| Priority | Actions | Rationale |
|----------|---------|-----------|
| **Critical** | Audit devices, adjust settings, mute unused microphones, disable ACR | Immediate risk reduction with minimal effort |
| **Important** | Segment network, review before purchasing, update firmware | Significant privacy improvement with moderate effort |
| **Valuable** | Contact legislators, support advocacy organizations, community education | Drives systemic change; multiplied impact |
| **Aspirational** | Replace with privacy-focused alternatives, set up home firewall | Maximum privacy but requires technical skill or investment |
