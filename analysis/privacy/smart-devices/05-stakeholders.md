# Smart Devices and IoT: Stakeholders

## Overview

The smart device ecosystem involves a complex web of stakeholders with competing interests. Device manufacturers profit from data collection, consumers receive convenience in exchange for surveillance, advertisers fund the ecosystem, and law enforcement gains access to unprecedented monitoring capabilities. Understanding these relationships is essential for designing reforms that can gain sufficient support to succeed.

## Primary Stakeholders

### Device Manufacturers

| Company/Segment | Interest | Power Level | Position on Privacy Reform |
|-----------------|----------|-------------|---------------------------|
| **Amazon (Alexa, Ring)** | Data collection for advertising and commerce ecosystem | Very High | Opposes comprehensive regulation; supports voluntary standards |
| **Google (Nest, Pixel)** | Data for advertising targeting and AI training | Very High | Supports limited regulation that preserves advertising model |
| **Apple** | Hardware sales; privacy as competitive differentiator | Very High | Supports privacy regulation that disadvantages advertising-funded competitors |
| **Samsung** | Device sales; growing data monetization | High | Mixed; supports international standards alignment |
| **Automakers (GM, Ford, Tesla, Toyota)** | Vehicle sales; emerging data monetization | Very High | Opposes vehicle data regulation; lobbies against right-to-repair |
| **Smart TV manufacturers (Vizio, Roku, LG)** | Hardware sales subsidized by ACR data revenue | High | Opposes restrictions on viewing data collection |
| **Wearable manufacturers (Fitbit/Google, Garmin)** | Device sales; health data ecosystems | Medium-High | Varies; concerned about health data regulation scope |
| **IoT startups** | Innovation and market entry | Low-Medium | Split; some differentiate on privacy, most adopt surveillance model |

### Consumers and Affected Populations

| Group | Specific Concerns | Vulnerability Level |
|-------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **General consumers** | Data collection without understanding; loss of control | Medium |
| **Children** | Smart toys, always-on devices in homes, lack of capacity to consent | Very High |
| **Domestic violence survivors** | Smart devices used for stalking and control by abusers | Very High |
| **Renters** | Landlord-installed smart devices they cannot remove or control | High |
| **Elderly** | Health monitoring devices with unclear data sharing; reduced digital literacy | High |
| **Low-income households** | Subsidized devices with more surveillance; fewer privacy alternatives | High |
| **Immigrants and undocumented persons** | Smart doorbell footage shared with ICE; location tracking from devices | Very High |
| **People of color** | Disproportionate surveillance through police-Ring partnerships; algorithmic bias in IoT systems | High |
| **Workers** | Employer-mandated wearables and monitoring devices | High |
| **Medical patients** | Health data from wearables used for insurance discrimination | High |

### Government and Law Enforcement

| Entity | Interest | Current Role |
|--------|----------|-------------|
| **Federal law enforcement (FBI, DEA)** | Access to IoT data for investigations | Requests smart device data; opposes warrant requirements |
| **Local police departments** | Real-time surveillance via doorbell cameras and other IoT devices | 2,000+ Ring partnerships; expanding smart device data requests |
| **Intelligence agencies (NSA, CIA)** | IoT devices as intelligence sources | Documented exploitation of smart TV vulnerabilities (Vault 7) |
| **FTC** | Consumer protection enforcement | Limited IoT enforcement; resource-constrained |
| **NHTSA** | Vehicle safety regulation | Expanding into connected vehicle data issues |
| **FCC** | Communications regulation | Jurisdiction over some IoT communications |
| **State attorneys general** | State-level consumer protection | Increasingly active in IoT privacy enforcement |

### Industry Intermediaries

| Entity Type | Role | Privacy Impact |
|-------------|------|---------------|
| **Advertising networks** | Purchase and use IoT data for targeting | Drive demand for device data collection |
| **Data brokers** | Aggregate and resell IoT device data | Amplify privacy impact by combining data across sources |
| **Cloud service providers (AWS, Azure, GCP)** | Host IoT data and processing | Control data storage and security infrastructure |
| **Chip manufacturers (Qualcomm, Broadcom)** | Provide IoT processors and connectivity | Hardware design choices affect what data can be collected |
| **Platform intermediaries (IFTTT, SmartThings)** | Connect devices across ecosystems | Create additional data sharing pathways |
| **Insurance companies** | Use IoT data for risk assessment and pricing | Drive demand for vehicle and health wearable data |

## Power Dynamics

### Who Has the Most Power

| Rank | Stakeholder | Source of Power | How Used |
|------|------------|----------------|----------|
| 1 | Amazon, Google, Apple | Market dominance, lobbying resources, platform control | Shape IoT standards; resist regulation; influence legislation |
| 2 | Automakers | Economic weight, employment leverage, lobbying | Block vehicle data legislation; self-regulatory frameworks |
| 3 | Federal law enforcement | Legal authority, national security framing | Oppose warrant requirements for IoT data |
| 4 | Insurance industry | Financial leverage, data demand | Drive IoT data monetization through purchasing |
| 5 | Advertising industry | Revenue dependence of device manufacturers | Sustain surveillance business model |

### Who Has the Least Power

| Rank | Stakeholder | Barrier to Power | Consequence |
|------|------------|-------------------|-------------|
| 1 | Children | No legal capacity; no market power; depend on adult choices | Subject to surveillance they cannot understand, consent to, or avoid |
| 2 | Domestic violence survivors | Safety depends on secrecy; abusers may control devices | Smart devices become tools of abuse and stalking |
| 3 | Renters | Cannot remove landlord-installed devices; lease terms may require acceptance | Surveillance imposed as condition of housing |
| 4 | Low-income consumers | Cannot afford privacy-friendly alternatives; rely on subsidized devices | Pay for cheaper devices with more surveillance |
| 5 | General consumers | Information asymmetry; no meaningful alternatives; collective action problems | Bear costs of surveillance with no effective recourse |

## Stakeholder Positions on Key Issues

| Issue | Manufacturers | Consumers | Law Enforcement | Privacy Advocates |
|-------|--------------|-----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Mandatory privacy labels | Oppose (cost, liability) | Support (if meaningful) | Neutral | Strongly support |
| Opt-in consent for data collection | Oppose (reduces data) | Support (conceptually) | Oppose (reduces access) | Strongly support |
| Data minimization requirements | Oppose (limits business model) | Support | Oppose | Strongly support |
| Warrant requirement for IoT data | Mixed | Support | Strongly oppose | Strongly support |
| Security standards | Mixed (support if voluntary) | Support | Support | Support (with privacy) |
| IoT data breach notification | Accept (manageable) | Support | Support | Support (faster timeline) |
| Private right of action for IoT privacy | Strongly oppose | Support | Neutral | Strongly support |
