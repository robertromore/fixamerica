# Financial Privacy: History

## Origins of Financial Confidentiality

### Banking Secrecy Traditions

Financial confidentiality has deep roots in Anglo-American law. The banker-customer relationship was understood to carry an implied duty of confidentiality, rooted in contract law and professional custom.

**Key principles:**

- Banks owed a duty of confidentiality to depositors under common law
- Financial records were considered the property of the institution, not the customer
- Government access required legal process (subpoena or court order) in most circumstances
- Cash transactions were inherently private -- no third-party records created
- Financial privacy was seen as essential to commercial liberty

### Pre-Regulatory Era

| Period | Characteristic |
|--------|---------------|
| Colonial-1860s | Minimal government interest in private financial transactions; no income tax |
| 1860s-1913 | Civil War income tax (temporary); banking regulation focused on solvency, not surveillance |
| 1913 | Sixteenth Amendment enables permanent federal income tax; IRS begins collecting financial information |
| 1930s | New Deal banking regulation focuses on safety and soundness, not data collection |
| 1940s-1960s | Financial records remain largely private; cash economy prevalent |

## The Surveillance Foundations (1970-1978)

### Bank Secrecy Act (1970)

The Bank Secrecy Act, formally the Currency and Foreign Transactions Reporting Act, was enacted on October 26, 1970. It represented the first major federal intrusion into financial privacy.

**Context:** The BSA was prompted by concerns about organized crime using cash and foreign bank accounts to launder money and evade taxes.

**Key requirements:**

| Requirement | Purpose |
|-------------|---------|
| Currency Transaction Reports (CTRs) | Report cash transactions over $10,000 |
| Records retention | Banks must retain records for 5 years |
| Foreign account reporting | Report foreign bank accounts |
| Compliance programs | Financial institutions must establish AML programs |

**Opposition and legal challenges:**

- California Bankers Association challenged the BSA's constitutionality
- *California Bankers Association v. Shultz*, 416 U.S. 21 (1974): Supreme Court upheld the BSA against Fourth Amendment and self-incrimination challenges, reasoning that the reporting requirements were directed at financial institutions, not individuals

### Fair Credit Reporting Act (1970)

Also enacted in 1970, the FCRA represented Congress's first attempt to regulate the growing consumer credit reporting industry.

**Context:** Credit bureaus had grown from local merchants' associations into national data aggregators with files on millions of Americans, operating with no regulation.

**Key provisions:**

- Established "permissible purposes" for accessing credit reports
- Gave consumers the right to access their own credit files
- Required investigation of disputed information
- Limited the reporting of adverse information to 7 years (10 for bankruptcy)
- Created FTC enforcement authority

### *United States v. Miller* (1976)

The Supreme Court's decision in *United States v. Miller*, 425 U.S. 435 (1976), was a devastating blow to financial privacy.

**Facts:** Federal agents obtained bank records of Mitch Miller through subpoenas to his banks, without notice to Miller, as part of a moonshine investigation.

**Holding:** The Court held that bank customers have no Fourth Amendment interest in financial records held by banks, because by "voluntarily conveying" information to the bank, customers "assumed the risk" that the information would be disclosed to the government.

**Impact:**

- Established the "third-party doctrine" for financial records
- Government could access bank records without a warrant, probable cause, or even notice to the customer
- Effectively eliminated Fourth Amendment protection for financial transactions
- Remains controlling precedent despite widespread criticism

### Right to Financial Privacy Act (1978)

Congress enacted the RFPA in direct response to *Miller*, recognizing that the decision left customers with no protection against government access to bank records.

**Key provisions:**

| Provision | Effect |
|-----------|--------|
| Notice to customer | Government must notify customer when seeking records (with exceptions) |
| Legal process required | Subpoena, summons, search warrant, or formal written request |
| Customer challenge | Customer can seek to quash in court |
| Record keeping | Government must log access to financial records |

**Limitations from the start:**

- Applied only to federal government, not state or local
- Contained broad exceptions for national security, tax investigations, and grand jury subpoenas
- Did not override *Miller*'s Fourth Amendment holding
- Did not cover records held by entities other than traditional financial institutions

## Deregulation and Consolidation (1980s-1999)

### Financial Deregulation

The 1980s and 1990s saw massive deregulation and consolidation of the financial industry:

| Year | Development | Privacy Impact |
|------|-------------|---------------|
| 1980 | Depository Institutions Deregulation and Monetary Control Act | Began dismantling Depression-era banking restrictions |
| 1982 | Garn-St. Germain Depository Institutions Act | Further deregulation of savings institutions |
| 1994 | Riegle-Neal Interstate Banking and Branching Efficiency Act | Enabled nationwide banking, consolidating customer data |
| 1999 | Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act | Repealed Glass-Steagall; enabled financial conglomerates; added privacy provisions |

### Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act (1999)

The GLBA's primary purpose was to enable the creation of financial conglomerates combining banking, securities, and insurance. Privacy provisions were added as a concession to consumer advocates.

**Privacy provisions:**

- Required financial institutions to provide privacy notices
- Gave consumers the right to opt out of some third-party information sharing
- Required "safeguards" for customer information
- Created broad exceptions for affiliate sharing, joint marketing, and service providers

**Why the privacy provisions were weak:**

- Industry lobbied against opt-in requirements; opt-out was a compromise
- Affiliate sharing exception meant conglomerates like Citigroup could share data across all subsidiaries without consumer consent
- Joint marketing exception allowed third-party sharing through partnerships
- No private right of action
- Enforcement divided among multiple federal regulators

### Credit Reporting Expansion

The credit reporting industry expanded dramatically during this period:

- Three major bureaus (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion) consolidated from hundreds of local bureaus
- Credit scoring (FICO, developed 1989) transformed lending and expanded into insurance, employment, and housing
- Automated decision-making reduced human judgment and increased reliance on data
- Prescreened offers (pre-approved credit) generated from credit bureau data without consumer request

## Post-9/11 Financial Surveillance (2001-2010)

### USA PATRIOT Act (2001)

The PATRIOT Act dramatically expanded financial surveillance:

| Provision | Effect |
|-----------|--------|
| Section 314(a) | Enabled FinCEN to share information between law enforcement and financial institutions |
| Section 326 | Customer Identification Program (CIP) -- verify identity of all account holders |
| Section 312 | Enhanced due diligence for correspondent accounts and private banking |
| Section 356 | Required SAR filing by broker-dealers |
| Section 311 | Special measures against foreign money laundering threats |

**Impact on financial privacy:**

- Millions of additional SARs filed annually
- Know Your Customer (KYC) requirements expanded to virtually all financial relationships
- Information sharing between government and private sector institutionalized
- National security letters used to obtain financial records without judicial oversight

### Terrorist Finance Tracking Program (SWIFT)

In 2006, the New York Times revealed that the CIA and Treasury Department had been secretly accessing records from SWIFT (Society for Worldwide Interbank Financial Telecommunication), which processes most international wire transfers.

**Scale:** Access to billions of international financial transaction records

**Legal basis:** Broadly interpreted executive authority and "compulsion" of SWIFT

**Outcome:** Program eventually formalized through agreement with the EU; continues today

### Fair and Accurate Credit Transactions Act (FACTA, 2003)

FACTA amended the FCRA with some consumer-positive provisions:

- Free annual credit reports from each bureau
- Credit card number truncation on receipts
- Fraud alert and identity theft provisions
- Increased preemption of state credit reporting laws (controversial)

## The Digital Finance Revolution (2010-Present)

### Dodd-Frank and the CFPB (2010)

The Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act created the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB), the first federal agency with a primary mandate to protect financial consumers.

**CFPB and financial privacy:**

- Authority to regulate financial data practices under its consumer protection mandate
- Section 1033: Consumer right to access their financial data (rulemaking began 2023, finalized 2024)
- Enforcement actions against credit bureaus and data practices
- Supervision of large banks, credit bureaus, and nonbank financial companies

### Rise of Fintech

| Year | Development | Privacy Significance |
|------|-------------|---------------------|
| 2009 | Bitcoin launched | First decentralized digital currency; pseudonymous transactions |
| 2010 | Square (now Block) launches | Mobile payment processing mainstream |
| 2012 | Venmo gains popularity | Public-by-default transaction feed; social payments |
| 2013 | Plaid founded | Financial data aggregation connecting apps to bank accounts |
| 2014 | Apple Pay launches | Tokenized mobile payments |
| 2017 | Cryptocurrency boom | Expanded awareness of financial privacy issues |
| 2018 | EU PSD2 (Payment Services Directive 2) | European open banking mandate; influenced U.S. thinking |
| 2019 | Libra/Diem (Facebook) proposed | Corporate digital currency raised privacy alarms; ultimately abandoned |
| 2020 | COVID-19 accelerates digital payments | Cash use declined; digital transaction records increased |
| 2021 | IRS 1099-K threshold lowered to $600 | Expanded financial transaction reporting |
| 2022 | Crypto market crash; FTX collapse | Highlighted lack of consumer protections in crypto |
| 2023 | CFPB Section 1033 proposed rule | Open banking framework with privacy provisions |
| 2024 | CFPB Section 1033 final rule | Consumer financial data rights established |
| 2024-2025 | Federal Reserve CBDC research continues | Central bank digital currency privacy debates intensify |

### Corporate Transparency Act (2021)

The Corporate Transparency Act, enacted as part of the National Defense Authorization Act for FY 2021, required companies to report beneficial ownership information to FinCEN.

**Status:** Implementation began 2024; legal challenges ongoing (*National Small Business United v. Yellen*, 2024, found CTA unconstitutional; government appealed; compliance obligations in flux).

**Privacy implications:**

- Creates federal registry of business owners
- Intended to combat money laundering through shell companies
- Raises concerns about government access to business financial information
- Small business owners concerned about exposure of personal information

## Key Lessons from History

1. **Financial surveillance ratchets upward** -- Each crisis (organized crime, terrorism, financial fraud) has added new surveillance requirements without meaningful sunset or review
2. **The third-party doctrine was a critical failure** -- *Miller* (1976) eliminated constitutional protection for financial records, and neither Congress nor the Court has fully corrected this
3. **Opt-out models do not work** -- GLBA's opt-out approach has been functionally ineffective for 25+ years
4. **Technology outpaces regulation** -- Fintech, cryptocurrency, and digital payments have created vast new categories of financial data outside existing frameworks
5. **Anti-money laundering has become mass surveillance** -- BSA/AML reporting generates millions of reports with modest enforcement outcomes, but the infrastructure is permanent
6. **Credit bureaus became too powerful without adequate accountability** -- What began as local merchants' lists became a national scoring system affecting housing, employment, insurance, and financial access
