# Financial Privacy: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

This document presents draft federal and state legislation to reform financial privacy in the United States. The legislative approach addresses six core problems: (1) the absence of Fourth Amendment protection for financial records under the third-party doctrine, (2) the ineffective opt-out model of the Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act, (3) the mass surveillance system created by the Bank Secrecy Act, (4) the lack of meaningful privacy regulation for fintech and digital payments, (5) the use of credit data for purposes far beyond lending, and (6) the unregulated sale of financial data by brokers. The legislation is designed to be enacted in phases, starting with broadly supported warrant requirements and BSA reform, building toward comprehensive GLBA reform and fintech regulation.

## Constitutional Amendments

### Digital Financial Privacy Amendment

**Purpose**: Overrule the third-party doctrine as applied to financial records, establishing constitutional protection for financial data regardless of who holds it.

**Draft Text**:

```text
Section 1. The right of the people to be secure in their financial
records, transactions, and accounts against unreasonable searches
and seizures shall not be violated. No financial record, transaction
record, or account information shall be obtained by any government
entity without a warrant issued upon probable cause, supported by
oath or affirmation, and particularly describing the records to be
obtained, regardless of whether such records are held by a financial
institution, technology company, data broker, or any other third party.

Section 2. The purchase, acquisition through contract, or other
procurement by any government entity of financial records or
financial data from a commercial entity shall constitute a search
within the meaning of this article and the Fourth Amendment.

Section 3. Congress and the several States shall have the power to
enforce this article by appropriate legislation providing greater
protections than those established herein.

Section 4. This article shall take effect two years after the date
of ratification.
```

**Explanation**: Section 1 directly overrules *United States v. Miller* (1976) by extending constitutional protection to financial records regardless of third-party possession. Section 2 closes the government data purchase loophole by defining data purchases as searches. Section 3 establishes this as a floor, allowing stronger legislative protections. Section 4 provides a two-year implementation period.

**Potential Challenges**:

- Constitutional amendment requires two-thirds of both chambers and three-fourths of state legislatures
- Law enforcement and intelligence community will oppose loss of warrantless access
- Financial institutions may oppose if they interpret it as creating new compliance obligations
- Courts must interpret scope -- does it cover aggregate financial statistics or only individual records?

**Refinements**:

- Pursue statutory reform (Financial Data Warrant Act) as immediate action while amendment process proceeds
- Build bipartisan coalition around Fourth Amendment principles
- Narrowly draft to cover individual financial records, not aggregate economic data
- Include emergency exception with after-the-fact judicial review

## Federal Legislation

### The Financial Data Warrant Act

**Purpose**: Require a judicial warrant based on probable cause for all government access to financial records, close the government data purchase loophole, and modernize the Right to Financial Privacy Act for the digital age.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Financial Data Warrant Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that--
    (1) the Supreme Court's decision in United States v. Miller,
        425 U.S. 435 (1976), holding that individuals have no
        reasonable expectation of privacy in financial records
        held by third parties, is inconsistent with the privacy
        expectations of the American people in the digital age;
    (2) in Carpenter v. United States, 585 U.S. 296 (2018), the
        Supreme Court recognized that digital data held by third
        parties can reveal "the privacies of life" protected by
        the Fourth Amendment;
    (3) government agencies increasingly purchase financial data
        from commercial sources to circumvent even the limited
        protections of the Right to Financial Privacy Act;
    (4) the Right to Financial Privacy Act covers only traditional
        financial institutions and does not extend to fintech
        companies, payment apps, cryptocurrency exchanges, or
        financial data brokers;
    (5) every digital financial transaction creates records held
        by multiple third parties, making warrantless government
        access to financial records far more invasive than in 1976;
        and
    (6) warrant requirements do not impede legitimate law
        enforcement but ensure judicial oversight consistent with
        the Fourth Amendment.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

(a) FINANCIAL RECORD.--The term "financial record" means any record
held by a covered entity that pertains to, is derived from, or
reveals information about--
    (1) a deposit, withdrawal, transfer, or payment involving the
        funds or assets of an individual;
    (2) the balance, transaction history, or account information
        of an individual's financial account;
    (3) an individual's creditworthiness, credit history, or credit
        score;
    (4) an individual's use of a payment instrument, payment
        application, or financial technology service; or
    (5) financial behavior, spending patterns, or financial health
        indicators inferred from data described in paragraphs (1)
        through (4).

(b) COVERED ENTITY.--The term "covered entity" means--
    (1) any financial institution as defined in the Right to
        Financial Privacy Act (12 U.S.C. 3401(1));
    (2) any entity that is a financial institution as defined in
        the Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act (15 U.S.C. 6809(3));
    (3) any money services business registered with FinCEN;
    (4) any payment application, digital wallet, or peer-to-peer
        payment service;
    (5) any entity that collects, processes, or stores financial
        records as a substantial part of its business, including
        financial data brokers, data aggregators, and personal
        finance applications;
    (6) any cryptocurrency exchange, custodial wallet service, or
        virtual asset service provider; and
    (7) any consumer reporting agency as defined in the Fair Credit
        Reporting Act (15 U.S.C. 1681a(f)).

(c) GOVERNMENT ENTITY.--The term "government entity" means--
    (1) any department, agency, bureau, or office of the Federal
        Government;
    (2) any State or local government entity;
    (3) any contractor, grantee, or other entity acting on behalf
        of or at the direction of a government entity described in
        paragraph (1) or (2); and
    (4) any foreign government entity requesting financial records
        of a person located in the United States.

SEC. 4. WARRANT REQUIREMENT FOR FINANCIAL RECORDS.

(a) WARRANT REQUIRED.--No government entity may require or compel a
covered entity to disclose financial records of any individual
without first obtaining a warrant issued by a court of competent
jurisdiction upon a finding of probable cause, supported by oath or
affirmation, and particularly describing the financial records to be
obtained and the individual whose records are sought.

(b) PROHIBITION ON DATA PURCHASES.--No government entity may
purchase, obtain through contract, or otherwise acquire from any
commercial entity the financial records or financial data of any
individual without first obtaining a warrant as described in
subsection (a).

(c) PROHIBITION ON INDIRECT ACQUISITION.--No government entity may
request, encourage, or facilitate a third party to obtain financial
records that the government entity could not obtain directly under
this section.

(d) NOTICE TO INDIVIDUAL.--
    (1) IN GENERAL.--A government entity that obtains financial
        records under this section shall notify the individual
        whose records were obtained within 30 days.
    (2) DELAYED NOTICE.--A court may authorize delayed notice for
        up to 90 days upon a showing that notice would--
        (A) endanger the life or physical safety of any person;
        (B) result in flight from prosecution;
        (C) result in destruction of evidence; or
        (D) seriously jeopardize an ongoing investigation.
    (3) EXTENSIONS.--Delayed notice may be extended in 90-day
        increments upon renewed judicial finding.

(e) EXCEPTIONS.--
    (1) EMERGENCY.--A government entity may obtain financial records
        without a warrant if there is an imminent threat to the life
        or physical safety of a person, provided that--
        (A) a warrant application is submitted within 72 hours;
        (B) if the warrant is denied, the records obtained shall be
            destroyed and may not be used in any proceeding; and
        (C) the government entity files a report with the court
            documenting the emergency.
    (2) VOLUNTARY CONSENT.--This section does not apply to financial
        records voluntarily provided to a government entity directly
        by the individual to whom the records pertain.
    (3) PUBLICLY AVAILABLE RECORDS.--This section does not apply to
        records lawfully published by a Federal, State, or local
        government entity in the course of its official duties,
        such as court records and property records. Commercially
        aggregated data shall not be considered publicly available.

SEC. 5. SUPPRESSION REMEDY.

(a) Financial records obtained in violation of this Act, and any
evidence derived therefrom, shall not be admissible in any court
proceeding, administrative proceeding, or other governmental action.

(b) An individual whose financial records were obtained in violation
of this Act may bring a civil action against the government entity
for actual damages, statutory damages of $1,000 per violation, and
reasonable attorney's fees.

SEC. 6. TRANSPARENCY.

Each Federal agency shall publish an annual report disclosing--
    (1) the number of warrants obtained for financial records;
    (2) the types of financial records obtained;
    (3) the categories of investigations involved;
    (4) the number of emergency exceptions invoked;
    (5) the total expenditure on financial record acquisition; and
    (6) the number of individuals whose financial records were
        obtained.

SEC. 7. SUPERSESSION.

This Act supersedes and replaces the Right to Financial Privacy Act
(12 U.S.C. 3401-3422) to the extent of any conflict.

SEC. 8. NO PREEMPTION OF STRONGER STATE PROTECTIONS.

Nothing in this Act shall be construed to preempt any State law that
provides greater privacy protection to individuals than the
protections provided by this Act.

SEC. 9. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 180 days after the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill modernizes and replaces the Right to Financial Privacy Act (1978) with a comprehensive warrant requirement that covers all entities holding financial data, not just traditional banks. The broad definition of "covered entity" (Section 3(b)) closes the fintech and data broker gaps. The prohibition on data purchases (Section 4(b)) eliminates the constitutional workaround. The suppression remedy (Section 5) creates meaningful deterrence. The transparency provision (Section 6) enables public oversight.

**Potential Challenges**:

- Intelligence community will seek carve-outs for national security investigations
- Law enforcement will argue warrant requirements slow financial crime investigations
- Definitional disputes over "financial record" and "covered entity"
- Emergency exception scope may be contested

**Refinements**:

- Include classified annex process for FISA Court warrant applications for foreign intelligence
- Provide expedited warrant process (24-hour judicial availability) for urgent investigations
- Phase in requirements over 12 months for entities not currently subject to RFPA

### The Financial Privacy Modernization Act

**Purpose**: Reform the Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act from opt-out to opt-in consent, extend privacy protections to fintech companies, and establish a private right of action for financial privacy violations.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Financial Privacy Modernization Act".

SEC. 2. DEFINITIONS.

(a) FINANCIAL INSTITUTION.--The term "financial institution" has the
meaning given in section 509(3)(A) of the Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act
(15 U.S.C. 6809(3)(A)), and shall also include--
    (1) any payment application, digital wallet, or peer-to-peer
        payment service;
    (2) any buy-now-pay-later service;
    (3) any personal finance application or service that accesses,
        collects, or stores consumer financial data;
    (4) any financial data aggregator;
    (5) any neobank or digital banking platform; and
    (6) any entity that is functionally equivalent to a financial
        institution in its collection and use of consumer financial
        data, as determined by the Commission.

(b) CONSUMER FINANCIAL DATA.--The term "consumer financial data"
means any information that--
    (1) is obtained from a consumer in connection with providing
        a financial product or service;
    (2) pertains to transactions, account balances, payment
        history, or financial behavior of a consumer;
    (3) is derived from information described in paragraphs (1)
        and (2), including financial scores, inferences, and
        predictions; or
    (4) could be used to determine a consumer's financial status,
        creditworthiness, or financial behavior.

(c) COMMISSION.--The term "Commission" means the Federal Trade
Commission, acting in consultation with the Consumer Financial
Protection Bureau.

SEC. 3. OPT-IN CONSENT FOR FINANCIAL DATA SHARING.

(a) IN GENERAL.--No financial institution may disclose consumer
financial data to any nonaffiliated third party unless the consumer
has provided affirmative, informed, and specific consent.

(b) CONSENT REQUIREMENTS.--Consent under this section must--
    (1) be obtained through a clear and conspicuous disclosure,
        presented separately from any other agreement or terms of
        service;
    (2) identify the specific categories of data to be shared;
    (3) identify the specific third parties or categories of third
        parties with whom data will be shared;
    (4) describe the purpose for which the data will be used;
    (5) be documented and retained by the financial institution;
    (6) not be made a condition of receiving the financial product
        or service, except where the data sharing is essential to
        providing the product or service; and
    (7) be revocable at any time, with revocation effective within
        15 days.

(c) AFFILIATE SHARING.--
    (1) Sharing of consumer financial data among affiliates shall
        be permitted for the sole purpose of servicing the
        consumer's existing account or relationship.
    (2) Use of shared affiliate data for marketing, cross-selling,
        or any purpose other than servicing the existing account
        requires separate affirmative consent.

(d) JOINT MARKETING EXCEPTION ELIMINATED.--The joint marketing
exception under section 502(b)(2) of the Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act
(15 U.S.C. 6802(b)(2)) is repealed. Joint marketing arrangements
are subject to the consent requirements of this section.

(e) SERVICE PROVIDER SHARING.--
    (1) Financial institutions may share consumer financial data
        with service providers necessary to perform essential
        functions, subject to contractual restrictions on secondary
        use.
    (2) Service providers may not use consumer financial data for
        any purpose other than the service for which the data was
        shared.

SEC. 4. SIMPLIFIED PRIVACY NOTICES.

(a) STANDARDIZED FORMAT.--The Commission, in consultation with the
Bureau, shall prescribe a standardized, one-page privacy notice
that--
    (1) uses plain language at an eighth-grade reading level;
    (2) clearly discloses data sharing practices;
    (3) describes consumer rights under this Act;
    (4) provides instructions for exercising consent and
        revocation rights; and
    (5) is provided at the time of account opening and annually
        thereafter.

(b) ELECTRONIC DELIVERY.--Notices may be provided electronically
with consumer consent.

SEC. 5. CONSUMER DATA RIGHTS.

(a) RIGHT OF ACCESS.--A consumer may request and obtain from any
financial institution a complete accounting of all consumer
financial data held, the sources of such data, the third parties to
whom it has been disclosed, and the purposes for which it was used.

(b) RIGHT OF CORRECTION.--A consumer may require a financial
institution to correct inaccurate consumer financial data within 30
days.

(c) RIGHT OF DELETION.--A consumer may require a financial
institution to delete consumer financial data that is no longer
necessary for the purpose for which it was collected, subject to
regulatory record-keeping requirements.

(d) DATA MINIMIZATION.--Financial institutions shall not collect or
retain consumer financial data beyond what is reasonably necessary
and proportionate to the financial product or service being provided.

SEC. 6. FINTECH PRIVACY REQUIREMENTS.

(a) DEFAULT TO PRIVATE.--Any financial institution that provides a
payment application, digital wallet, or peer-to-peer payment service
shall set transaction visibility to private by default. No
transaction information shall be publicly visible unless the consumer
affirmatively elects public visibility for each transaction.

(b) OPEN BANKING SAFEGUARDS.--Authorized third-party access to
consumer financial data under Section 1033 of the Dodd-Frank Act
shall be subject to--
    (1) the consent requirements of Section 3 of this Act;
    (2) data minimization: third parties may access only data
        necessary for the authorized purpose;
    (3) purpose limitation: third parties may use data only for
        the purpose authorized by the consumer;
    (4) deletion upon revocation: third parties shall delete all
        consumer financial data within 30 days of consent
        revocation; and
    (5) liability: third parties are liable for misuse of consumer
        financial data.

SEC. 7. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) FTC ENFORCEMENT.--A violation of this Act shall be treated as a
violation of a rule defining an unfair or deceptive act or practice
prescribed under section 18(a)(1)(B) of the Federal Trade Commission
Act (15 U.S.C. 57a(a)(1)(B)).

(b) CIVIL PENALTIES.--
    (1) Any financial institution that violates this Act shall be
        subject to a civil penalty of not more than $50,000 per
        violation per day.
    (2) For knowing or reckless violations, the penalty shall be
        not more than $100,000 per violation per day.

(c) PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.--
    (1) Any consumer whose rights under this Act are violated may
        bring a civil action in any court of competent jurisdiction.
    (2) A prevailing plaintiff shall be entitled to the greater
        of--
        (A) actual damages; or
        (B) statutory damages of not less than $500 and not more
            than $5,000 per violation.
    (3) The court shall award reasonable attorney's fees and costs
        to a prevailing plaintiff.
    (4) Actions may be maintained as class actions under Rule 23
        of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure.
    (5) SAFE HARBOR.--A financial institution that demonstrates
        good-faith compliance with this Act, including maintenance
        of a reasonable compliance program, shall not be liable for
        statutory damages for unintentional violations, provided
        the institution cures the violation within 30 days of
        notice.

(d) STATE ATTORNEY GENERAL ENFORCEMENT.--The attorney general of
any State may bring a civil action on behalf of the residents of
that State to enforce this Act.

(e) CFPB ENFORCEMENT.--The Bureau of Consumer Financial Protection
may enforce this Act with respect to entities under its supervisory
and enforcement jurisdiction.

SEC. 8. NO PREEMPTION OF STRONGER STATE PROTECTIONS.

(a) This Act establishes a floor, not a ceiling, for financial
privacy protections.

(b) Nothing in this Act shall be construed to preempt any State law
that provides greater privacy protection to consumers than the
protections provided by this Act.

SEC. 9. EFFECTIVE DATE.

(a) Sections 1, 2, 3, and 4 shall take effect 1 year after the date
of enactment.
(b) Sections 5 and 6 shall take effect 18 months after the date of
enactment.
(c) Section 7 shall take effect 1 year after the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill fundamentally reforms the GLBA by replacing opt-out with opt-in consent (Section 3), eliminating the joint marketing exception, restricting affiliate sharing to account servicing, and extending coverage to fintech companies through an expanded definition of "financial institution" (Section 2(a)). The private right of action (Section 7(c)) ensures consumers can enforce their rights. The non-preemption clause (Section 8) preserves stronger state laws.

**Potential Challenges**:

- Financial industry will argue opt-in disrupts existing business relationships
- Fintech industry will argue expanded coverage stifles innovation
- Definition of "financial institution" may face legal challenges
- Implementation timeline may be contested as too aggressive or too slow

**Refinements**:

- Grandfather existing information-sharing arrangements for 2 years with transition to opt-in
- Provide simplified compliance pathway for small financial institutions (under $1 billion assets)
- Include CFPB rulemaking authority to update "financial institution" definition as industry evolves

### The BSA Modernization Act

**Purpose**: Reform the Bank Secrecy Act from mass surveillance to risk-based, targeted reporting, reduce compliance burden on financial institutions, and improve the effectiveness of financial intelligence.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "BSA Modernization Act".

SEC. 2. CTR THRESHOLD ADJUSTMENT.

(a) Section 5313 of title 31, United States Code, is amended by
striking "$10,000" each place it appears and inserting "$30,000".

(b) INFLATION ADJUSTMENT.--The Secretary of the Treasury shall
adjust the threshold in subsection (a) every 5 years to reflect
changes in the Consumer Price Index.

SEC. 3. SAR REFORM.

(a) RISK-BASED FILING.--The Secretary of the Treasury shall
promulgate regulations within 1 year to implement a risk-based
approach to Suspicious Activity Report filing that--
    (1) establishes clear, specific risk indicators that trigger
        SAR filing obligations;
    (2) reduces defensive filing by providing safe harbor for
        financial institutions that maintain reasonable risk-based
        compliance programs;
    (3) prioritizes quality of reporting over quantity;
    (4) provides clear guidance on when a SAR is not required; and
    (5) distinguishes between high-risk and low-risk transactions
        and institutions.

(b) EFFECTIVENESS REPORTING.--FinCEN shall publish an annual report
to Congress disclosing--
    (1) the total number of SARs filed;
    (2) the number of SARs that led to an investigation;
    (3) the number of SARs that led to a prosecution;
    (4) the number of SARs that led to a conviction;
    (5) the total amount of illicit funds identified through SAR
        analysis; and
    (6) an effectiveness assessment comparing the cost of SAR
        compliance to the enforcement outcomes.

(c) DUE PROCESS FOR ACCOUNT CLOSURES.--
    (1) No financial institution shall close, restrict, or deny an
        account based solely on the filing of a SAR without
        providing the account holder with notice and an opportunity
        to respond.
    (2) Notice shall include the general basis for the action
        (without disclosing the SAR itself) and a procedure for
        the account holder to provide additional information.

SEC. 4. COMMUNITY INSTITUTION RELIEF.

(a) SIMPLIFIED COMPLIANCE.--Financial institutions with total assets
under $1 billion shall be eligible for simplified BSA compliance
requirements, including--
    (1) streamlined examination procedures;
    (2) reduced reporting for low-risk transaction categories; and
    (3) access to FinCEN compliance assistance without penalty.

(b) COMPLIANCE COST STUDY.--Within 1 year, the Government
Accountability Office shall conduct a study of BSA compliance costs
for community banks and credit unions and recommend cost-reduction
measures.

SEC. 5. PRIVACY IMPACT ASSESSMENTS.

Before expanding any reporting requirement under the Bank Secrecy Act,
FinCEN shall conduct and publish a privacy impact assessment that
evaluates--
    (1) the expected effectiveness of the new requirement in
        detecting or deterring financial crime;
    (2) the privacy impact on individuals whose records will be
        reported;
    (3) the cost of compliance for covered institutions;
    (4) less intrusive alternative approaches; and
    (5) the disproportionate impact, if any, on low-income
        communities, immigrants, and minority populations.

SEC. 6. FINCEN DATABASE ACCESS CONTROLS.

(a) The FinCEN database of BSA filings shall be accessible only to
authorized personnel conducting specific, documented investigations.

(b) All database queries shall be logged, including the identity of
the querying individual, the agency, the investigation identifier,
and the records accessed.

(c) FinCEN shall conduct annual audits of database access and report
findings to the Inspector General of the Treasury.

SEC. 7. SUNSET AND REVIEW.

(a) The reporting requirements of the Bank Secrecy Act, as amended
by this Act, shall sunset 10 years after the date of enactment of
this Act unless reauthorized by Congress following a comprehensive
review.

(b) The Government Accountability Office shall conduct a
comprehensive review of BSA effectiveness 5 years after enactment.

SEC. 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.

(a) Section 2 shall take effect 180 days after the date of enactment.
(b) Sections 3 through 6 shall take effect 1 year after the date of
enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill reforms the BSA from mass reporting to targeted, risk-based surveillance. Raising the CTR threshold (Section 2) from $10,000 to $30,000 accounts for 55 years of inflation since 1970. SAR reform (Section 3) focuses on quality over quantity while providing safe harbor for institutions that adopt risk-based approaches. Community institution relief (Section 4) reduces disproportionate burden on small banks. Privacy impact assessments (Section 5) ensure future expansions are justified. The sunset provision (Section 7) forces periodic reassessment of effectiveness.

**Potential Challenges**:

- FinCEN and DOJ will oppose reducing reporting volume
- National security establishment will argue any reduction aids money laundering
- Financial institutions may resist safe harbor provisions that shift liability
- Sunset provision will face opposition from permanent bureaucracy

**Refinements**:

- Phase threshold increase: $10,000 to $20,000 in Year 1, then to $30,000 in Year 3
- Include FinCEN modernization funding to improve analytics capabilities
- Provide explicit liability protection for financial institutions following FinCEN risk-based guidance

### The Credit Reporting Privacy Act

**Purpose**: Reform the Fair Credit Reporting Act to restrict non-lending uses of credit data, strengthen dispute resolution, and establish consumer data rights over credit information.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Credit Reporting Privacy Act".

SEC. 2. RESTRICTIONS ON NON-LENDING USES.

(a) EMPLOYMENT.--Section 604 of the Fair Credit Reporting Act
(15 U.S.C. 1681b) is amended to prohibit the use of consumer
reports for employment purposes, except--
    (1) for positions requiring a government security clearance;
    (2) for positions with fiduciary responsibility at financial
        institutions regulated by federal banking agencies; and
    (3) where specifically required by federal or state law.

(b) INSURANCE.--The use of credit-based insurance scores for
underwriting or rating automobile or homeowner's insurance is
prohibited.

(c) TENANT SCREENING.--Consumer reports used for tenant screening
shall not include--
    (1) medical debt;
    (2) student loan information; or
    (3) adverse information more than 3 years old.

SEC. 3. DISPUTE RESOLUTION REFORM.

(a) INDEPENDENT INVESTIGATION.--Upon receipt of a consumer dispute,
the consumer reporting agency shall conduct an independent
investigation, which may not consist solely of forwarding the
dispute to the furnisher for verification.

(b) BURDEN OF PROOF.--If a consumer disputes the accuracy of
information and the furnisher cannot provide documentary evidence
supporting the accuracy within 30 days, the disputed information
shall be deleted.

(c) TIMELINE.--All disputes shall be resolved within 30 days. The
consumer reporting agency shall provide a written explanation of
the investigation and outcome.

(d) FREE UPDATED REPORT.--Following any dispute resolution, the
consumer shall receive a free updated credit report.

SEC. 4. CONSUMER DATA RIGHTS.

(a) FREE CREDIT MONITORING.--Each consumer reporting agency shall
provide free, continuous credit monitoring to all consumers,
including real-time alerts for new inquiries, new accounts, and
significant changes.

(b) DATA DELETION.--Consumers may request deletion of adverse
information that--
    (1) has been disputed and not verified within 30 days;
    (2) resulted from identity theft or fraud;
    (3) relates to medical debt that has been paid or settled; or
    (4) relates to medical debt under $500.

(c) SCORE TRANSPARENCY.--Consumer reporting agencies shall disclose
to consumers, at no charge--
    (1) their credit score;
    (2) the specific factors affecting the score, ranked by
        impact;
    (3) actions the consumer can take to improve the score; and
    (4) the scoring model used.

SEC. 5. DATA BREACH LIABILITY.

(a) STRICT LIABILITY.--Consumer reporting agencies shall be strictly
liable for data breaches affecting consumer information.

(b) DAMAGES.--Consumers affected by a breach shall be entitled to--
    (1) actual damages;
    (2) statutory damages of $100 to $1,000 per consumer; and
    (3) 10 years of free credit monitoring and identity theft
        protection.

(c) CLASS ACTIONS.--Actions may be maintained as class actions.

SEC. 6. PUBLIC CREDIT BUREAU STUDY.

The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau shall, within 2 years of
enactment, complete a study on the feasibility, costs, and benefits
of establishing a public credit bureau that would--
    (1) provide an alternative to the private credit bureau
        oligopoly;
    (2) incorporate privacy-by-design principles;
    (3) give consumers meaningful control over their credit data;
        and
    (4) reduce the discriminatory impact of current credit scoring
        models.

SEC. 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 1 year after the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**: This bill addresses the most harmful aspects of credit reporting: the use of credit data for purposes beyond lending (Section 2), the dysfunctional dispute resolution process (Section 3), the lack of consumer data rights (Section 4), and the absence of meaningful breach liability (Section 5). The public credit bureau study (Section 6) explores structural reform.

**Potential Challenges**:

- Credit bureau lobbying will be intense (combined revenue $16+ billion)
- Employers and insurers who use credit data will resist restrictions
- Insurance industry will argue credit-based scoring is actuarially sound
- Strict liability for breaches will face strong opposition

**Refinements**:

- Phase in employment restrictions over 2 years to allow alternative screening development
- Include actuarial study on insurance impact to build evidence base
- Provide reduced penalties for breaches with documented advanced security practices

## State Model Legislation

### Model State Financial Privacy Enhancement Act

**Purpose**: Provide a template for states to strengthen financial privacy protections beyond the federal GLBA framework.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Financial Privacy Enhancement
Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.

(a) "Financial institution" means any entity engaged in the business
of providing financial products or services to consumers, including
banks, credit unions, insurance companies, securities firms, payment
applications, personal finance applications, buy-now-pay-later
services, data aggregators, and any other entity that collects
consumer financial data.

(b) "Consumer financial data" means any information pertaining to a
consumer's financial transactions, accounts, balances, credit
history, financial behavior, or financial health, including data
derived from or inferred from such information.

SECTION 3. OPT-IN CONSENT.

(a) No financial institution doing business in this State may
disclose consumer financial data to any nonaffiliated third party
without the affirmative, informed consent of the consumer.

(b) Consent must be specific as to data categories, third parties,
and purposes.

(c) Consent is revocable at any time, effective within 15 days.

SECTION 4. EMPLOYER CREDIT CHECK RESTRICTIONS.

(a) No employer may obtain or use a consumer credit report for
employment purposes unless the position involves--
    (1) a security clearance required by law;
    (2) fiduciary responsibility at a financial institution; or
    (3) access to financial assets exceeding $10,000.

(b) An employer who violates this section is liable to the applicant
or employee for actual damages and statutory damages of $1,000 per
violation.

SECTION 5. CREDIT-BASED INSURANCE SCORING.

The use of credit history or credit scores as a factor in
underwriting, rating, or pricing automobile or homeowner's insurance
is prohibited.

SECTION 6. FINANCIAL DATA BROKER REGISTRATION.

(a) Any entity that collects and sells consumer financial data or
financial inferences to third parties shall register annually with
the [Attorney General / Secretary of State].

(b) Registration shall include data categories, sources, purchasers,
opt-out procedures, and data security practices.

(c) Registration fee: $200 for entities with annual revenue under
$25 million; $1,000 for entities with annual revenue $25 million
or above.

SECTION 7. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) The Attorney General may bring actions to enforce this Act with
civil penalties up to $25,000 per violation per day.

(b) Any consumer may bring a civil action for violations with
statutory damages of $500 to $5,000 per violation, plus attorney's
fees.

SECTION 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect [180 days after enactment].
```

**Adaptations**: States with existing comprehensive privacy laws (California, Colorado, Connecticut, Virginia) may integrate these provisions into existing frameworks. States without comprehensive privacy laws may enact this as a standalone financial privacy measure. States may choose to enact specific sections (e.g., employer credit check restrictions only) based on political feasibility.

## Regulatory Framework

### CFPB Open Banking Privacy Safeguards

**Existing Authority**: Dodd-Frank Act Section 1033; CFPB supervisory and rulemaking authority.

**Draft Regulatory Focus Areas**:

1. **Consent standards for open banking**: Define minimum standards for consumer consent to third-party financial data access, including specific data categories, purpose limitation, and revocation procedures
2. **Data minimization for authorized third parties**: Third parties may access only the data necessary for the service the consumer has authorized
3. **Deletion requirements**: Third parties must delete all consumer financial data within 30 days of consent revocation or cessation of the authorized service
4. **Liability framework**: Third parties are liable for unauthorized use, inadequate security, or secondary sharing of consumer financial data
5. **Screen scraping phase-out**: Require API-based access to replace credential-sharing (screen scraping) with defined transition timeline

### FTC Fintech Privacy Enforcement

**Existing Authority**: FTC Act Section 5 (unfair or deceptive practices); GLBA Safeguards Rule authority over non-bank financial institutions.

**Draft Regulatory Focus Areas**:

1. **Fintech data practices**: Require all fintech companies to disclose data collection, sharing, and monetization practices in plain language
2. **Dark pattern prohibition**: Prohibit manipulative design practices that undermine consumer privacy choices in financial applications
3. **Default privacy settings**: Require privacy-protective default settings for payment apps and financial technology services
4. **Data security standards**: Establish minimum data security standards for fintech companies handling consumer financial data
5. **BNPL regulation**: Ensure buy-now-pay-later services are subject to the same data protection requirements as traditional lending

### FinCEN Risk-Based BSA Implementation

**Existing Authority**: Bank Secrecy Act; FinCEN regulatory authority.

**Draft Regulatory Focus Areas**:

1. **Risk-based SAR guidance**: Publish clear, specific risk indicators that trigger SAR filing obligations; reduce ambiguity that drives defensive filing
2. **Safe harbor for risk-based compliance**: Financial institutions that maintain documented risk-based compliance programs receive safe harbor from enforcement for good-faith compliance decisions
3. **CTR threshold implementation**: Implement inflation-adjusted threshold with simplified reporting for remaining covered transactions
4. **Community institution exemptions**: Streamline BSA examination and reporting for institutions under $1 billion in assets with demonstrated low-risk profiles

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

**Fourth Amendment**:

- *United States v. Miller* (1976) remains controlling precedent eliminating Fourth Amendment protection for financial records held by third parties
- *Carpenter v. United States* (2018) established that some digital data held by third parties warrants Fourth Amendment protection, but the Court explicitly declined to address financial records
- The Financial Data Warrant Act addresses this legislatively; the Constitutional Amendment would address it permanently
- Government data purchases remain constitutionally unsettled; legislative prohibition is the most reliable path

**First Amendment**:

- *Sorrell v. IMS Health* (2011) applied heightened scrutiny to data-use restrictions, but financial privacy legislation regulates commercial conduct (data transactions), not speech
- Courts have consistently upheld GLBA, FCRA, and HIPAA restrictions on data sharing under intermediate scrutiny
- The proposed legislation includes substantial government interests (consumer protection, fraud prevention, financial system integrity) to satisfy heightened scrutiny

### Preemption Questions

- All proposed federal legislation explicitly provides that it establishes a "floor, not a ceiling"
- States may enact and maintain stronger financial privacy protections
- GLBA currently preempts some state financial privacy laws; the Financial Privacy Modernization Act reverses this by eliminating the GLBA preemption provision for state laws that provide greater protection
- Industry will push for ceiling preemption; resisting preemption is essential to preserving state innovation

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- **FTC enforcement**: Civil penalties up to $50,000-$100,000 per violation per day
- **CFPB enforcement**: Supervisory and enforcement authority over covered financial institutions
- **State AG enforcement**: Parallel state enforcement authority
- **Private right of action**: Statutory damages, class actions, attorney's fees for GLBA and FCRA violations
- **Suppression remedy**: Evidence obtained in violation of warrant requirement excluded from proceedings
- **Safe harbor**: Good-faith compliance provides protection against statutory damages for unintentional violations

### Sunset and Review Provisions

- BSA reporting requirements sunset after 10 years unless reauthorized with effectiveness review
- GAO comprehensive review of BSA effectiveness at 5 years
- CFPB review of open banking privacy safeguards at 3 years
- FTC review of fintech privacy enforcement at 3 years
- Commission review of definitions ("financial institution," "consumer financial data") every 3 years to account for technological change
- Annual agency reports on enforcement actions, compliance rates, and consumer rights exercise

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Financial Data Warrant Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Intelligence community implied exemption | Agencies claim national security exemption not explicitly addressed in statute | Critical |
| Contractor intermediaries | Government uses contractors to purchase financial data, claiming contractor is not a "government entity" | High |
| "Publicly available" data expansion | Agencies argue commercially aggregated financial data is "publicly available" under Section 4(e)(3) | High |
| Foreign government requests | Mutual legal assistance treaties used to obtain records through foreign partners without domestic warrant | High |
| Emergency exception abuse | Broad use of emergency exception without meaningful after-the-fact review | Medium |
| Aggregate data exemption | Agencies claim aggregate financial statistics are not "financial records" of individuals | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Does not overturn *Miller* constitutionally | Statutory protection can be repealed by future Congress | Third-party doctrine remains controlling precedent |
| Suppression remedy does not deter non-litigation uses | Government may use improperly obtained data for intelligence purposes that never reach court | Enforcement depends on judicial proceedings |
| Transparency reports may be classified | National security claims may prevent public disclosure of access scope | Intelligence classification authority |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **No implied exemptions**: Explicitly state that no agency, including intelligence agencies, is exempt absent specific statutory exception in this Act
2. **Contractor coverage**: Define "government entity" broadly to include contractors and agents (Section 3(c)(3) addresses this)
3. **Narrow "publicly available" definition**: Exclude all commercially aggregated data from the publicly available exception (Section 4(e)(3) addresses this)
4. **MLAT safeguard**: Require that financial records obtained through foreign government requests comply with the same warrant standards
5. **Emergency reporting**: Require written certification, judicial review within 72 hours, and annual reporting of all emergency exceptions
6. **Inspector General oversight**: Treasury IG audits of warrant compliance and data purchase prohibition

### Financial Privacy Modernization Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Consent theater | Complex consent flows that technically satisfy requirements but do not produce informed consent | Critical |
| Entity restructuring | Fintech companies restructure to fall outside "financial institution" definition | High |
| Service provider data monetization | Service providers use consumer data for secondary purposes despite contractual restrictions | High |
| Affiliate expansion | Financial conglomerates expand affiliate network to maximize exempt sharing | Medium |
| Grandfathered relationships | Existing data-sharing arrangements continue indefinitely during transition | Medium |
| Small institution burden | Opt-in compliance costs disproportionately burden community banks | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Opt-in consent may produce consent fatigue | Consumers click "agree" without reading, recreating opt-out dynamic | Behavioral economics of consent |
| Enforcement resource constraints | FTC and CFPB historically under-resourced | Congressional appropriations politics |
| Financial data inference gap | Non-financial data used to infer financial status, evading coverage | Technology enabling behavioral inference |
| Cross-border data flows | Financial data processed abroad may not be subject to US privacy law | Jurisdictional limits |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Consent design standards**: Commission promulgates specific consent design standards including plain language, maximum request frequency, prohibition on dark patterns, and standardized format
2. **Functional definition test**: Define "financial institution" by function (what entity does with financial data), not corporate structure; include anti-circumvention clause
3. **Service provider auditing**: Require financial institutions to audit service provider data use; liability for service provider misuse
4. **Transition timeline**: Grandfather existing arrangements for no more than 2 years; affirmative consent required for continuation
5. **Tiered compliance**: Simplified compliance for institutions under $1 billion in assets with fewer than 50,000 customer accounts
6. **Inference coverage**: Define "consumer financial data" to include financial inferences from non-financial data (Section 2(b)(3)-(4) addresses this)

### BSA Modernization Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Structuring to avoid new threshold | Criminals structure transactions below $30,000 instead of $10,000 | Medium |
| Risk-based safe harbor gaming | Institutions adopt minimal "risk-based" programs to qualify for safe harbor while reducing compliance | High |
| Effectiveness data manipulation | FinCEN reports SAR outcomes selectively to justify continued mass reporting | Medium |
| Sunset circumvention | Congress routinely reauthorizes expiring programs without meaningful review | Medium |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Structuring remains illegal**: Anti-structuring provisions (31 U.S.C. 5324) continue to apply at the new threshold level
2. **Safe harbor standards**: FinCEN establishes minimum requirements for risk-based compliance programs qualifying for safe harbor; periodic audits
3. **Independent effectiveness review**: GAO, not FinCEN, conducts effectiveness review with public reporting
4. **Sunset enforcement**: Congress must hold hearings and receive GAO report before reauthorization; no automatic extension

### Credit Reporting Privacy Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Alternative data screening | Employers use non-credit data sources to replicate credit screening without triggering restrictions | High |
| Insurance model workaround | Insurers develop alternative scoring models using similar data under different names | High |
| Dispute investigation theater | Agencies conduct investigations that technically satisfy requirements but remain cursory | Medium |
| Breach liability cap | Industry pushes for legislative amendment capping breach damages | Medium |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Alternative data prohibition**: Prohibit employers from using any financial or financial-adjacent data for employment screening outside the enumerated exceptions
2. **Actuarial model oversight**: State insurance regulators review and approve all underwriting factors; prohibit factors correlated with credit data
3. **Investigation standards**: CFPB promulgates minimum investigation standards; consumer reporting agencies must document investigation methodology
4. **No liability cap**: Resist amendments that would cap breach damages; strict liability with no cap is essential deterrence

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| Regulatory arbitrage across federal agencies | Interagency financial privacy coordination committee (CFPB, FTC, OCC, Fed, NCUA) |
| Technology outpacing legislation | Dynamic definitions updated by agency rulemaking every 3 years; functional tests over entity-based tests |
| Industry consolidation in response to regulation | Antitrust coordination; monitoring of M&A activity post-enactment |
| Consumer consent fatigue across multiple financial relationships | Centralized consent management infrastructure; standardized consent format |
| International data flow challenges | Bilateral financial data protection agreements; reciprocity requirements |
| Disparate impact on underserved communities | Equity impact assessments for all new financial privacy requirements; community bank relief |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- BSA reporting requirements sunset after 10 years (Section 7 of BSA Modernization Act)
- GAO comprehensive BSA effectiveness review at 5 years
- 3-year review of all definitions and thresholds by relevant agencies
- Annual agency reports on enforcement actions, compliance rates, and consumer rights exercise
- 5-year comprehensive review of financial privacy framework effectiveness
