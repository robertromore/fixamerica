# Financial Privacy: Overview

## Executive Summary

Financial privacy in America exists in a state of fundamental contradiction. Americans regard their financial affairs as deeply personal, yet virtually every financial transaction they make is recorded, analyzed, shared, and in many cases sold. The federal framework governing financial data -- anchored by the Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act (GLBA) of 1999, the Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA) of 1970, the Bank Secrecy Act (BSA) of 1970, and the Right to Financial Privacy Act (RFPA) of 1978 -- was designed for a world of paper checks, branch banking, and separated financial institutions. It has not been meaningfully updated for an era of real-time digital payments, fintech apps, open banking APIs, cryptocurrency, data brokers selling financial inferences, and government agencies purchasing commercial transaction data.

The result is a landscape in which banks, credit card companies, and payment processors are subject to formal privacy requirements that are largely ineffective in practice -- opt-out rights that fewer than 5% of consumers exercise, privacy notices that virtually no one reads, and information-sharing arrangements that are standard industry practice. Meanwhile, a growing universe of financial technology companies, payment apps (Venmo, Cash App, Zelle), buy-now-pay-later services (Affirm, Klarna), and cryptocurrency platforms operate with minimal or ambiguous privacy regulation. Data brokers assemble detailed financial profiles on nearly every American adult from transaction records, public property records, credit data, and court records, then sell these profiles to marketers, employers, landlords, and insurers.

Government access to financial data is equally problematic. The Bank Secrecy Act requires financial institutions to file Currency Transaction Reports (CTRs) for transactions over $10,000 and Suspicious Activity Reports (SARs) for suspected illegal activity, creating a massive surveillance infrastructure that captures millions of reports annually. The third-party doctrine, established in *United States v. Miller* (1976), means that bank records disclosed to a financial institution lose Fourth Amendment protection. Government agencies increasingly purchase commercial financial data to circumvent even the modest protections of the RFPA.

Financial privacy reform requires modernizing the federal framework to address digital payments and fintech, strengthening consumer rights over financial data, constraining government access through warrant requirements, regulating financial data brokers, and addressing the emerging privacy challenges of cryptocurrency and open banking.

## Scope

### What This Subtopic Covers

- **Banking privacy** -- Account information, transaction records, and bank-government data sharing
- **Credit reporting** -- Credit bureaus, credit scores, and the use of credit data beyond lending
- **Payment data** -- Credit cards, debit cards, digital wallets, and payment app data
- **Fintech and app-based finance** -- Venmo, Cash App, buy-now-pay-later, robo-advisors
- **Government financial surveillance** -- BSA/AML reporting, IRS information reporting, financial intelligence
- **Financial data brokers** -- Companies that aggregate and sell financial data and inferences
- **Open banking** -- Consumer-authorized data sharing and its privacy implications
- **Cryptocurrency privacy** -- Blockchain surveillance, exchange reporting, and CBDC concerns
- **Insurance and employment use** -- Financial data used in non-lending decisions

### Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **GLBA (Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act)** | Federal law requiring financial institutions to explain information-sharing practices and protect sensitive data |
| **FCRA (Fair Credit Reporting Act)** | Federal law regulating consumer reporting agencies and the use of consumer credit information |
| **BSA (Bank Secrecy Act)** | Federal law requiring financial institutions to assist government agencies in detecting and preventing money laundering |
| **SAR (Suspicious Activity Report)** | Report filed by financial institutions when they detect potentially suspicious transactions |
| **CTR (Currency Transaction Report)** | Mandatory report for cash transactions exceeding $10,000 |
| **Third-party doctrine** | Legal principle that information voluntarily disclosed to third parties loses Fourth Amendment protection |
| **Open banking** | Framework allowing consumers to authorize third-party access to their financial data through APIs |
| **FinCEN** | Financial Crimes Enforcement Network, the primary U.S. financial intelligence unit within the Treasury Department |
| **Financial data broker** | Entity that collects, aggregates, and sells financial information or inferences about individuals |

## Core Tensions

- **Security vs. privacy** -- Anti-money laundering and counter-terrorism financing requirements generate mass financial surveillance with limited proven effectiveness
- **Innovation vs. protection** -- Fintech and open banking enable financial inclusion but create new data exposure
- **Transparency vs. confidentiality** -- Beneficial ownership registries and tax reporting serve public purposes but reduce financial privacy
- **Government access vs. constitutional rights** -- The third-party doctrine effectively eliminates Fourth Amendment protection for financial records
- **Consumer convenience vs. data exposure** -- Digital payments and financial apps are convenient but generate detailed transaction histories
- **Credit access vs. credit surveillance** -- Credit reporting enables lending but subjects Americans to pervasive financial scoring with limited control

## Key Questions

1. How has the third-party doctrine undermined constitutional protection for financial records, and can it be reformed?
2. What is the actual effectiveness of BSA/AML mass reporting in preventing financial crime, and do the privacy costs justify the results?
3. How should fintech apps, payment platforms, and buy-now-pay-later services be regulated for privacy?
4. What consumer rights should Americans have over their financial data, including credit reports?
5. How should the United States balance financial transparency (beneficial ownership, tax reporting) with financial privacy?
6. What are the privacy implications of central bank digital currencies (CBDCs) and cryptocurrency regulation?
7. How should financial data brokers be regulated?

## Vision of Success

A reformed financial privacy landscape would feature:

- **Warrant requirements for government access** -- Financial records subject to Fourth Amendment protection regardless of third-party holdings
- **Effective opt-in consent** -- Consumers affirmatively authorize financial data sharing for non-essential purposes
- **Comprehensive fintech coverage** -- All financial technology companies subject to meaningful privacy requirements
- **Credit reporting reform** -- Consumers have real control over their credit data, including meaningful access, correction, and deletion rights
- **Targeted financial surveillance** -- BSA/AML reporting reformed to focus on genuine risk rather than mass collection
- **Financial data broker regulation** -- Registration, transparency, and consent requirements for entities selling financial data
- **Open banking with safeguards** -- Consumer-authorized data sharing with strong privacy protections and liability rules
- **Meaningful enforcement** -- Private right of action, significant penalties, and adequately funded regulators
