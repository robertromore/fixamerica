# Financial Privacy: Solutions

## Overview

Financial privacy reform requires a multi-pronged approach: restoring constitutional protections undermined by the third-party doctrine, modernizing the GLBA from opt-out to opt-in, reforming the BSA/AML system from mass surveillance to targeted enforcement, extending privacy protections to fintech and digital payments, reforming credit reporting, and regulating financial data brokers. These solutions are designed to be mutually reinforcing.

---

## Solution 1: Restore Constitutional Protection for Financial Records

### The Problem

*United States v. Miller* (1976) eliminated Fourth Amendment protection for bank records by holding that customers "assume the risk" that financial institutions will disclose records to the government. The RFPA provides statutory protection but has broad exceptions and does not cover fintech or data brokers.

### The Proposal

Enact federal legislation requiring a judicial warrant based on probable cause for government access to financial records held by any entity.

**Key elements:**

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Warrant requirement | Government must obtain a warrant for financial records, including those held by banks, fintech companies, payment processors, and data brokers |
| Probable cause standard | Warrants require probable cause and specificity about records sought |
| Notice to customer | Customer notified of warrant within 30 days (with limited delay provisions for active investigations) |
| Scope | Covers all entities holding financial records, not just traditional banks |
| Prohibition on data purchases | Government may not purchase commercial financial data that would require a warrant to obtain directly |
| Exclusionary rule | Evidence obtained in violation may not be used in proceedings |

**Model:** Fourth Amendment Is Not For Sale Act (introduced in multiple Congresses) + expansion to all financial records

### Evidence of Effectiveness

- *Carpenter v. United States* (2018) demonstrated that the Court recognizes some third-party data deserves constitutional protection
- States that require warrants for financial records (some state constitutions provide stronger protection) have not experienced law enforcement impairment
- International models (Germany's constitutional protections for financial data) demonstrate feasibility

## Solution 2: GLBA Reform -- Opt-In Consent

### The Problem

GLBA's opt-out model is functionally ineffective: fewer than 5% of consumers exercise opt-out rights. Information-sharing exceptions for affiliates and joint marketing mean most sharing continues regardless.

### The Proposal

Amend GLBA to replace opt-out with opt-in consent:

**Key reforms:**

- **Opt-in for third-party sharing**: Financial institutions must obtain affirmative consent before sharing customer data with non-affiliated third parties
- **Affiliate sharing limits**: Sharing among affiliates permitted only for the specific purpose of servicing the customer's existing account; marketing use requires separate consent
- **Joint marketing reform**: Joint marketing arrangements require opt-in consent
- **Simplified privacy notices**: Standardized, one-page privacy notice with clear description of data sharing practices
- **Revocation right**: Consumers can revoke consent at any time
- **Private right of action**: Consumers may sue for GLBA violations with statutory damages ($500-$5,000 per violation)
- **Preserve federal floor**: Do not preempt stronger state laws

### Evidence of Effectiveness

- EU GDPR's consent-based model has not caused the disruption industry predicted
- Australia's opt-in approach for financial data sharing under Consumer Data Right has been workable
- California CCPA/CPRA opt-out model has higher engagement rates than GLBA, and opt-in would be more effective still

## Solution 3: BSA/AML Reform -- Targeted Over Mass Surveillance

### The Problem

The BSA/AML system generates millions of reports annually with modest enforcement outcomes. Compliance costs the industry $46+ billion per year. The system disproportionately burdens low-income communities and immigrants while intercepting only 1-2% of money laundering.

### The Proposal

Reform BSA reporting from mass collection to risk-based, targeted surveillance:

**Key reforms:**

| Reform | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Raise CTR threshold | Adjust from $10,000 (1970 level) to $30,000, indexed to inflation |
| Risk-based SAR filing | Replace defensive filing culture with genuine risk assessment; reduce volume, increase quality |
| SAR effectiveness review | Require FinCEN to publish annual data on SAR-to-enforcement conversion rates |
| Sunset provision | BSA reporting requirements sunset every 10 years unless reauthorized with effectiveness review |
| Reduce community bank burden | Exempt community banks and credit unions below $1 billion in assets from some reporting requirements |
| Privacy impact assessments | Require FinCEN to conduct privacy impact assessments before expanding reporting |
| Access controls | Limit FinCEN database access to authorized investigations with audit trails |
| Due process for account closures | Prohibit financial institutions from closing accounts based solely on SAR filing without notice and opportunity to respond |

### Evidence of Effectiveness

- UK's risk-based approach has maintained effective AML enforcement with more targeted reporting
- Singapore's model focuses resources on highest-risk transactions
- Academic research (Findley, Nielson, Sharman, *Global Shell Games*, 2014) demonstrates that mass reporting is less effective than targeted enforcement
- FinCEN's own advisory panels have recommended reducing volume in favor of quality

## Solution 4: Fintech Privacy Regulation

### The Problem

Fintech companies collect and share financial data with fewer regulatory constraints than traditional banks. Payment apps, personal finance tools, buy-now-pay-later services, and data aggregators operate in a privacy gap.

### The Proposal

Extend meaningful privacy protections to all entities that process financial data:

**Key elements:**

- **Broad coverage**: All entities that collect, process, or share financial data subject to privacy requirements regardless of charter type
- **Consent for data sharing**: Fintech apps must obtain specific consent before sharing financial data with third parties
- **Data minimization**: Collect only data necessary for the service requested
- **Public transaction feeds**: Prohibit default-public transaction feeds (Venmo-style); default to private
- **Open banking safeguards**: Authorized third-party access must comply with data minimization, purpose limitation, and deletion requirements
- **BNPL data protections**: Buy-now-pay-later data subject to privacy protections equivalent to traditional lending
- **Plaid/aggregator regulation**: Data aggregators subject to consent, data minimization, and purpose limitation requirements

### Evidence of Effectiveness

- EU PSD2 and GDPR combination provides a model for open banking with privacy protections
- Australia's Consumer Data Right includes privacy safeguards for financial data sharing
- FTC enforcement against fintech companies demonstrates that current authority is insufficient and prescriptive rules are needed

## Solution 5: Credit Reporting Reform

### The Problem

Credit bureaus control financial identities of 220+ million Americans with limited accountability. Credit data is used far beyond lending for employment, housing, insurance, and utility decisions. Error rates are high and dispute resolution favors furnishers.

### The Proposal

Comprehensive credit reporting reform:

**Key reforms:**

| Reform | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Restrict non-lending uses | Prohibit use of credit data for employment screening (except financial sector); restrict insurance scoring |
| Meaningful dispute resolution | Require independent investigation with deadlines; shift burden of proof to furnishers |
| Consumer deletion rights | Allow consumers to delete adverse information after proven inaccuracy or after dispute resolution failure |
| Free credit monitoring | Require bureaus to provide free ongoing credit monitoring, not just annual reports |
| Data breach liability | Strict liability for credit bureau data breaches with statutory damages |
| Alternative credit models | Encourage development of privacy-respecting alternative credit assessment methods |
| Public credit bureau option | Study creation of a public credit bureau operated by CFPB or a new agency |

### Evidence of Effectiveness

- 11 states and many cities already restrict employer credit checks without adverse effects
- Colorado and other states restrict credit-based insurance scoring
- CFPB enforcement has demonstrated that bureaus can improve accuracy when required
- Some countries (France) use public credit registries rather than private bureaus

## Solution 6: Financial Data Broker Regulation

### The Problem

Financial data brokers aggregate and sell detailed financial profiles outside the FCRA's permissible purpose framework. This data is used for marketing, insurance pricing, employment screening, and government surveillance.

### The Proposal

- **Registration requirement**: All entities selling financial data or inferences must register with CFPB or FTC
- **Consent requirement**: Financial data may not be sold without affirmative consumer consent
- **Transparency**: Brokers must disclose data sources, categories, and customers
- **Consumer access and deletion**: Individuals may access and delete their financial profiles
- **Government purchase restrictions**: Government may not purchase financial data from brokers without a warrant
- **Prohibition on discriminatory data**: Ban sale of financial data used as proxy for race, religion, or other protected characteristics

## Solution 7: IRS Reporting Reform

### The Problem

The lowering of the Form 1099-K threshold from $20,000/200 transactions to $600 creates mass financial reporting of casual sellers and gig workers.

### The Proposal

- **Raise 1099-K threshold**: Set threshold at $5,000 per year (compromise between $600 and $20,000)
- **Exclude personal transactions**: Explicitly exclude non-business personal payments (splitting dinner, gifts) from reporting
- **Simplify compliance**: IRS provides pre-populated returns using reported information, reducing burden on individuals
- **Privacy safeguard**: IRS may not share 1099-K data with other agencies except for tax enforcement purposes
- **Sunset provision**: Revisit threshold after 5 years with effectiveness review

---

## Solution 8: Cryptocurrency and CBDC Privacy

### The Problem

Cryptocurrency regulation oscillates between requiring full surveillance (exchange KYC) and ignoring privacy concerns (self-custody). CBDC proposals raise fundamental questions about government visibility into every transaction.

### The Proposal

- **Exchange privacy**: Require exchanges to protect customer data with strong privacy standards; limit data sharing to legal process
- **Self-custody protection**: Explicitly protect the right to self-custody of cryptocurrency without registering as a money services business
- **Privacy-preserving CBDC design**: If a CBDC is created, mandate privacy-by-design with technological limits on government transaction visibility
- **Blockchain surveillance limits**: Require warrant for government use of blockchain surveillance tools to identify individuals
- **Mining/staking privacy**: Transaction validation activities should not trigger BSA reporting

|----------|---------------|---------------|----------|
| Warrant requirement for financial records | Government access | Legislation | Medium-term (2-4 years) |
