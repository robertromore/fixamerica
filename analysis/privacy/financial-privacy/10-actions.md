# Financial Privacy: Actions

## Overview

While systemic reform of financial privacy requires legislative and regulatory action, individuals and communities can take meaningful steps to protect their financial data, raise awareness, and build political pressure for change. This document provides practical actions at every level -- from personal financial data protection to organized political advocacy.

## Individual Actions

### Immediate Steps (This Week)

1. **Freeze your credit at all three bureaus**: Contact Equifax (equifax.com/personal/credit-report-services), Experian (experian.com/freeze), and TransUnion (transunion.com/credit-freeze) to place free credit freezes, preventing new accounts from being opened in your name
2. **Opt out of prescreened credit and insurance offers**: Call 1-888-5-OPT-OUT (1-888-567-8688) or visit OptOutPrescreen.com to stop prescreened offers generated from your credit data
3. **Review your free annual credit reports**: Visit AnnualCreditReport.com and review your reports from all three bureaus; dispute any errors
4. **Check your GLBA opt-out options**: Review the privacy notices from your bank, credit card companies, and financial institutions; exercise opt-out rights for third-party information sharing where available
5. **Review payment app privacy settings**: Check settings in Venmo, Cash App, Zelle, PayPal, and other payment apps; set transactions to private (Venmo defaults to public)
6. **Remove unnecessary financial apps**: Delete fintech apps you no longer use; revoke their data access permissions through your bank's connected apps settings

## Community Actions

### Local Organizing

- **Host a financial privacy workshop**: Teach community members how to freeze credit, opt out of data sharing, and protect financial data; National Consumer Law Center and Privacy Rights Clearinghouse provide materials
- **Create a mutual aid credit monitoring group**: Help community members review credit reports, dispute errors, and navigate the opt-out process, especially for elderly or less tech-savvy neighbors
- **Partner with financial literacy organizations**: Connect financial privacy education with existing financial literacy programs at community centers, libraries, and schools
- **Work with immigrant rights organizations**: Educate immigrant communities about how ICE uses financial records and data purchases for enforcement, and available protective measures
- **Engage domestic violence organizations**: Help DV shelters understand how financial records can be used to track survivors; assist with credit freezes and financial data protection
- **Support community banking**: Encourage use of community banks and credit unions that are more likely to prioritize customer privacy over data monetization

### Institutional Advocacy

- **Urge your employer to stop using credit checks in hiring**: Advocate for ending credit checks for non-financial positions; cite studies showing weak correlation between credit scores and job performance
- **Push local government to require warrants for financial data purchases**: Advocate for city/county ordinances requiring warrants for government purchases of financial data from brokers
- **Engage your landlord or property management company**: Ask about tenant screening practices; advocate for using rental payment history rather than credit scores
- **Work with your bank's community advisory board**: Large banks have Community Reinvestment Act advisory boards; raise financial privacy concerns at meetings
- **Advocate at your credit union**: Credit unions are member-owned; attend member meetings and raise privacy policy concerns

## Political Actions

### Contact Your Representatives

**Federal Level**:

- Call your US Senators and Representative to support financial privacy reform
- Specifically ask them to: (1) support warrant requirements for government access to financial records, (2) support BSA/AML reform to reduce mass surveillance, (3) support GLBA reform from opt-out to opt-in, (4) support credit reporting reform, and (5) oppose preemption of stronger state laws

**State Level**:

- If your state does not restrict employer credit checks, advocate for legislation (model: Illinois Employee Credit Privacy Act)
- Support state financial privacy protections that go beyond the federal GLBA framework
- Advocate for state restrictions on credit-based insurance scoring
- Support state AG enforcement of financial privacy and consumer protection laws

### Template Email to Federal Representatives

> Subject: Support Comprehensive Financial Privacy Reform
>
> Dear [Senator/Representative] [Name],
>
> I am writing as your constituent to urge you to support comprehensive reform of America's outdated financial privacy laws.
>
> The federal framework governing financial data -- the Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act, the Bank Secrecy Act, the Fair Credit Reporting Act, and the Right to Financial Privacy Act -- was designed for a world of paper checks and branch banking. Today, every digital transaction I make is recorded, analyzed, and potentially shared or sold. Fewer than 5% of consumers exercise their GLBA opt-out rights because the system is designed to fail. Government agencies purchase our financial data from commercial brokers to avoid getting warrants. Credit bureaus control the financial identities of 220 million Americans with limited accountability.
>
> I urge you to support legislation that would:
>
> 1. Require a judicial warrant for government access to financial records, including prohibiting warrantless purchases of commercial financial data
> 2. Reform the GLBA from opt-out to opt-in consent for financial data sharing
> 3. Raise the BSA Currency Transaction Report threshold from $10,000 (set in 1970) to $30,000, indexed to inflation
> 4. Restrict the use of credit data for non-lending purposes like employment screening and insurance pricing
> 5. Extend meaningful privacy protections to fintech apps, payment platforms, and digital wallets
> 6. Include a private right of action so consumers can enforce their financial privacy rights
> 7. Not preempt stronger state financial privacy protections
>
> Financial privacy should not depend on which company holds your data or which agency wants to see it. I look forward to hearing your position.
>
> Sincerely,
> [Your Name]
> [Your Address]

## Talking Points

### For Conversations with Friends and Family

- **"Did you know that banks can share your financial data with marketing partners without asking you first?"** The GLBA lets banks share customer data through "joint marketing arrangements" and among "affiliates" -- and fewer than 5% of people ever opt out.
- **"The government can see your bank records without a warrant."** The Supreme Court ruled in 1976 that bank records have no Fourth Amendment protection. Government agencies even buy commercial financial data from brokers to avoid the few protections that exist.
- **"Your credit score affects more than just loans."** Employers check credit for hiring. Landlords check credit for housing. Insurance companies use credit-based scores to set rates. And credit reports have errors in 25-30% of files.
- **"Venmo shows your transactions to the public by default."** Researchers have identified people's spending habits, social connections, and even drug purchases through Venmo's public transaction feed.
- **"The Bank Secrecy Act creates a massive financial surveillance system."** Banks file over 25 million reports on Americans every year. The $10,000 cash reporting threshold was set in 1970 and has never been adjusted for inflation.

### For Conversations with Elected Officials

- **Bipartisan appeal**: Conservatives care about government financial surveillance and the Fourth Amendment. Progressives care about corporate data exploitation and credit bureau accountability. Financial privacy reform bridges the divide.
- **Cost-effectiveness argument**: BSA/AML compliance costs the financial industry $46+ billion annually while intercepting only 1-2% of money laundering. Risk-based approaches would save money and be more effective.
- **Consumer demand**: 72% of Americans express concern about financial data privacy (Morning Consult/ABA, 2023). This is a winning issue across party lines.
- **Equity argument**: Credit scoring perpetuates racial wealth gaps. Financial surveillance disproportionately affects low-income communities and immigrants. Reform is a civil rights issue.
- **State momentum**: Multiple states have already acted to restrict employer credit checks, regulate financial data brokers, and strengthen financial data protections. Federal action is overdue.

## Common Misconceptions

| Misconception | Reality |
|--------------|---------|
| "I have nothing to hide in my financial records" | Financial data reveals medical conditions (pharmacy purchases), political views (donations), relationship status, travel patterns, and religious activity. It is among the most sensitive data that exists about you. |
| "My bank protects my financial privacy" | Banks routinely share customer data with affiliates, joint marketing partners, and service providers. The opt-out model ensures most sharing continues because fewer than 5% of customers opt out. |
| "Credit reports are accurate" | FTC research found 25-30% of credit reports contain errors. CFPB continues to receive thousands of complaints annually about inaccurate credit reporting. |
| "Only criminals need to worry about financial surveillance" | The BSA generates over 25 million reports annually on ordinary Americans. Cash-intensive workers, immigrants sending remittances, and small business owners are disproportionately affected regardless of any wrongdoing. |
| "Opt-out protects my privacy if I use it" | Even consumers who opt out are only protected from some third-party sharing. Affiliate sharing, service provider sharing, and joint marketing often continue regardless. And many institutions make opt-out difficult. |
| "Fintech apps are regulated like banks" | Most payment apps, personal finance tools, and buy-now-pay-later services operate with ambiguous or minimal privacy regulation compared to traditional banks. |
| "Cryptocurrency is anonymous" | Most blockchains are pseudonymous, not anonymous. Blockchain surveillance companies can trace most transactions to real identities, especially when exchange KYC data is linked to wallet addresses. |
| "Financial privacy reform would help money launderers" | Targeted, risk-based approaches are more effective than mass surveillance. The current system intercepts only 1-2% of money laundering despite enormous costs. Reform means smarter enforcement, not weaker enforcement. |
