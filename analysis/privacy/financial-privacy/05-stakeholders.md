# Financial Privacy: Stakeholders

## Who Benefits from the Status Quo

### Financial Institutions (Banks, Credit Unions, Insurers)

**Who They Are**

- Major banks: JPMorgan Chase, Bank of America, Wells Fargo, Citigroup
- Regional and community banks
- Credit unions
- Insurance companies
- Securities firms and investment banks

**What They Gain**

| Benefit | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| Data monetization | Sell and share customer data for marketing and analytics revenue |
| Affiliate sharing | Share data freely across conglomerate subsidiaries without customer consent |
| Low compliance burden | Opt-out model requires minimal consumer engagement effort |
| GLBA preemption | Federal framework preempts stronger state laws |
| Customer lock-in | Data advantages make it harder for customers to switch |

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **High**

- Oppose opt-in consent requirements
- Oppose private right of action for privacy violations
- Prefer current fragmented enforcement over strengthened CFPB authority

### Credit Bureaus

**Who They Are**

- Equifax (revenue: $5.6 billion, FY 2023)
- Experian (revenue: $7.1 billion, FY 2023)
- TransUnion (revenue: $3.8 billion, FY 2023)
- Specialty reporting agencies (ChexSystems, LexisNexis, MIB)

**What They Gain**

| Benefit | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| Data monopoly | Control credit profiles of 220+ million Americans |
| Revenue from data sales | Sell credit data, scores, and analytics to lenders, insurers, employers, landlords |
| Limited accountability | FCRA provides framework that also limits alternative approaches |
| Prescreened offers | Generate revenue from prescreened credit and insurance offers |
| Government contracts | Provide data services to government agencies |

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **Very High**

- Oppose restrictions on use of credit data for non-lending purposes
- Oppose stronger dispute resolution requirements
- Resist consumer data deletion rights

### Fintech Companies

**Who They Are**

- Payment apps: PayPal, Venmo, Cash App (Block), Zelle
- Personal finance: Plaid, Mint (Intuit), Rocket Money
- Buy-now-pay-later: Affirm, Klarna, Afterpay
- Neobanks: Chime, Varo, SoFi
- Cryptocurrency exchanges: Coinbase, Kraken

**What They Gain**

- User financial data fuels product development and partnerships
- Lower regulatory burden compared to traditional banks
- Data aggregation creates competitive advantages
- Financial data enables targeted marketing and cross-selling

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **Moderate to High**

- Support open banking (consumer data access benefits them)
- Oppose privacy restrictions that would limit data use
- Prefer industry self-regulation to prescriptive rules

### Financial Data Brokers and Marketing Firms

**Who They Are**

- Acxiom (Interpublic Group)
- Oracle Data Cloud
- Epsilon (Publicis Groupe)
- LexisNexis Risk Solutions (RELX Group)
- Financial marketing data aggregators

**What They Gain**

- Revenue from selling financial profiles and marketing data
- Minimal regulation outside FCRA for non-credit-report data
- Access to transaction data through partnerships with financial institutions

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **High**

- Oppose consent requirements for financial data sales
- Resist registration and transparency requirements

### Government Agencies

**Who They Are**

- FinCEN: Financial intelligence and BSA administration
- IRS: Tax enforcement and information reporting
- DOJ/FBI: Criminal investigations using financial data
- DHS/ICE: Immigration enforcement using financial records
- State law enforcement: Access to financial records through subpoena

**What They Gain**

| Benefit | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| Mass financial intelligence | BSA database of 400+ million records |
| Low-cost surveillance | Third-party doctrine eliminates warrant requirement |
| Commercial data access | Purchase financial data from brokers without legal process |
| Tax enforcement | Extensive information reporting from financial institutions |
| Investigative efficiency | Financial records establish activity patterns |

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **High**

- Oppose warrant requirements for financial records
- Oppose restrictions on commercial data purchases
- Support expanding, not reducing, financial reporting requirements

## Who Is Harmed by the Status Quo

### Consumers Generally

**Scale:** 260+ million adult Americans with financial accounts

**Harms:**

| Harm | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Loss of financial privacy | Every transaction recorded, analyzed, and potentially shared |
| Identity theft | 1.4 million identity theft reports in 2023 (FTC Consumer Sentinel, 2024) |
| Data breaches | 160+ million financial records exposed in 2023 (ITRC, 2024) |
| Credit report errors | 25-30% of reports contain errors (FTC, 2012; CFPB, ongoing) |
| Marketing exploitation | Financial data used for targeted marketing, including predatory lending |
| No meaningful consent | Opt-out model is functionally ineffective |

### Low-Income Communities

**Specific harms:**

- Rely more heavily on cash, which is increasingly surveilled through structuring laws
- BSA reporting disproportionately affects cash-intensive businesses and workers
- Credit scoring penalizes financial instability caused by poverty
- Overdraft fees and financial penalties generate data used against consumers
- "Banking deserts" push people toward less-regulated financial services with fewer privacy protections
- Alternative financial services (payday lenders, check cashers) have minimal privacy standards

### Communities of Color

**Specific harms:**

- Credit scoring reflects and reinforces racial wealth gaps (Brookings Institution, 2023)
- Algorithmic lending discrimination using financial data (CFPB fair lending reports, 2023)
- Higher rates of credit report errors among Black and Latino consumers (NCLC, 2023)
- Financial surveillance disproportionately affects Black and immigrant communities
- Employment credit checks have disparate racial impact (EEOC guidance, 2012)

### Immigrants

**Specific harms:**

- Financial records used by ICE for immigration enforcement
- Remittance transactions trigger BSA reporting
- Lack of credit history creates barriers to financial access
- Government purchase of financial data for immigration surveillance
- Fear of financial surveillance deters formal banking participation

### Small Business Owners

**Specific harms:**

- BSA/AML compliance costs disproportionately burden small financial institutions
- Cash-intensive businesses face heightened scrutiny and reporting
- Corporate Transparency Act reporting requirements add burden (when in effect)
- Financial data exposure through business credit reporting
- IRS 1099-K threshold expansion increases reporting obligations

### Gig Workers and Self-Employed

**Specific harms:**

- 1099-K threshold lowering captures routine transactions
- Payment app data creates detailed record of informal economic activity
- No employer privacy protections for financial data
- Financial data used by platforms for algorithmic management

## Institutional Stakeholders

### Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB)

**Position:** Supportive of stronger financial privacy

- Section 1033 rulemaking establishes consumer financial data rights
- Has brought enforcement actions for unfair data practices
- Advocates for consumer control over financial data
- Limited by statutory authority and political pressures
- Faces ongoing legal challenges to its authority and funding structure (*CFPB v. Community Financial Services Association*, 2024)

### Federal Reserve

**Position:** Cautious on privacy

- Conducts payments research including privacy implications
- CBDC research explicitly considers privacy design
- Generally supports current regulatory framework
- Concerned about financial stability implications of privacy changes

### Federal Trade Commission

**Position:** Increasingly active

- Enforces GLBA Safeguards Rule for non-bank financial institutions
- Has brought actions against fintech companies for deceptive data practices
- Advocates for comprehensive privacy legislation including financial data
- Limited by jurisdiction (cannot regulate banks directly for privacy)

### FinCEN

**Position:** Supports financial surveillance

- Administers BSA/AML reporting system
- Advocates for expanding reporting requirements
- Implemented beneficial ownership registry
- Institutional interest in maintaining and expanding data collection

## Stakeholder Power Map

| Stakeholder | Power Level | Alignment with Reform | Key Leverage |
|-------------|-------------|----------------------|--------------|
| Major banks | Very high | Against | Lobbying, political donations, systemic importance |
| Credit bureaus | High | Strongly against | Data monopoly, industry relationships, lobbying |
| Fintech companies | High | Mixed | Innovation narrative, user base, lobbying |
| Financial data brokers | Moderate | Against | Industry relationships, low public visibility |
| Government agencies (FinCEN, IRS) | High | Against (on surveillance) | Statutory authority, national security framing |
| CFPB | Moderate | Supportive | Rulemaking authority, enforcement |
| FTC | Moderate | Supportive | Enforcement authority, public visibility |
| Privacy advocates | Moderate | Strongly supportive | Public opinion, litigation, media |
| Consumers | Low individually, high collectively | Strongly supportive | Votes, public opinion, consumer behavior |
| Small businesses | Low-moderate | Mixed | Constituency pressure, BSA reform interest |
