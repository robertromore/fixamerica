# Financial Privacy: Roadmap

## Implementation Strategy

Financial privacy reform requires a phased approach that sequences politically achievable measures first, builds enforcement infrastructure, and progressively strengthens protections. The strategy begins with measures that enjoy bipartisan support -- warrant requirements for government access and BSA/AML reform -- before advancing to more contested reforms like GLBA opt-in consent and comprehensive fintech regulation. Each phase creates the political momentum and institutional capacity needed for the next.

## Phase 1: Foundation (Years 1-2)

### Objective

Close the most egregious loopholes in financial privacy: government purchases of financial data without warrants, the outdated BSA/AML mass reporting system, and the fintech regulatory gap. Establish transparency and begin building public awareness.

### Action Table

| Action | Lead Actor | Timeline | Dependencies | Priority |
|--------|-----------|----------|--------------|----------|
| Enact Financial Data Warrant Act (warrant requirement for government access to financial records) | Congress | Year 1 | Bipartisan coalition (Wyden-Paul model) | Critical |
| Prohibit government purchase of commercial financial data without warrant | Congress | Year 1 | Part of warrant act or standalone bill | Critical |
| Raise CTR threshold from $10,000 to $30,000, indexed to inflation | Congress | Year 1 | BSA/AML reform coalition (banks + civil liberties) | High |
| Require FinCEN to publish SAR-to-enforcement conversion data | Congress | Year 1 | BSA/AML reform package | High |
| CFPB implements Section 1033 open banking rule with privacy safeguards | CFPB | Year 1-2 | Final rule issued (2024); compliance phase-in underway | High |
| Enact fintech privacy baseline (consent for data sharing by payment apps) | Congress | Year 1-2 | Bipartisan sponsorship | High |
| States continue adopting financial data protections | State legislatures | Ongoing | None | Medium |
| FTC enforcement actions against fintech data practices | FTC | Year 1-2 | Existing Section 5 authority | Medium |
| Launch public education campaign on financial data rights | CFPB, FTC, NGOs | Year 1 | Agency funding | Medium |

### Success Metrics for Phase 1

| Metric | Baseline (2025) | Target (End of Year 2) | Data Source |
|--------|-----------------|----------------------|-------------|
| Warrant required for government access to financial records | No (third-party doctrine) | Yes (legislation enacted) | Federal statute |
| CTR reporting threshold | $10,000 (1970 level) | $30,000, indexed | BSA amendment |
| SAR-to-enforcement conversion rate published | Not published | Publicly available | FinCEN annual report |
| Government financial data purchases with warrants | 0 (no requirement) | Warrant requirement enacted | Federal courts |
| Fintech apps subject to privacy baseline | Ambiguous | Coverage enacted | Federal statute |
| States with financial data protections beyond GLBA | 6-8 | 12+ | NCSL, IAPP |
| Public awareness of financial data rights | Low (fewer than 5% exercise GLBA opt-out) | Measurable increase | Polling, CFPB reports |

### Key Risks in Phase 1

| Risk | Probability | Impact | Mitigation |
|------|------------|--------|------------|
| Law enforcement and intelligence community block warrant requirement | Medium-High | High | Bipartisan privacy caucus; classified briefings; public disclosure of government data purchases |
| Financial industry lobbying defeats BSA reform | Medium | High | Community bank alliance; cost-savings framing; bipartisan support |
| CFPB Section 1033 rule challenged or weakened | Medium | Medium | Defend rulemaking authority; build industry compliance coalition |
| Federal bill preempts stronger state laws | Medium | High | Explicit federal floor language; no preemption provisions |

## Phase 2: Comprehensive Framework (Years 2-4)

### Objective

Reform GLBA from opt-out to opt-in, enact comprehensive credit reporting reform, regulate financial data brokers, and establish dedicated enforcement capacity for financial privacy.

### Action Table

| Action | Lead Actor | Timeline | Dependencies | Priority |
|--------|-----------|----------|--------------|----------|
| Amend GLBA to replace opt-out with opt-in consent | Congress | Year 2-3 | Phase 1 creates political momentum; industry adjusts to fintech baseline | Critical |
| Enact comprehensive credit reporting reform (restrict non-lending uses, improve dispute resolution) | Congress | Year 2-3 | CFPB enforcement actions build case | Critical |
| Require financial data broker registration with CFPB or FTC | Congress | Year 2-3 | Data broker registration model from broader privacy reform | High |
| Establish private right of action for GLBA violations | Congress | Year 2-3 | Part of GLBA reform package | High |
| Reform IRS 1099-K threshold (set at $5,000; exclude personal transactions) | Congress | Year 2 | Tax legislation vehicle | High |
| Enhance CFPB financial privacy enforcement capacity | Congress (appropriations) | Year 2 | CFPB authority confirmed | Medium |
| CFPB rulemaking on financial data broker coverage under FCRA | CFPB | Year 2-3 | CFPB authority; Phase 1 framework | Medium |
| Fund state AG financial privacy enforcement | Congress (appropriations) | Year 2 | None | Medium |
| International coordination on financial data protection standards | Treasury, Commerce | Year 2-4 | Federal framework established | Medium |

### Success Metrics for Phase 2

| Metric | Baseline (End of Phase 1) | Target (End of Year 4) | Data Source |
|--------|--------------------------|----------------------|-------------|
| GLBA consent model | Opt-out (fewer than 5% exercise) | Opt-in enacted | Federal statute |
| Credit data use for employment screening | Broadly permitted | Restricted (financial sector exception only) | FCRA amendment |
| Financial data brokers registered | 0 (no federal registry) | 500+ | Federal registry |
| Private right of action for GLBA | None | Available with statutory damages | Federal statute |
| CFPB enforcement actions on financial privacy (annual) | 5-10 | 20+ | CFPB enforcement database |
| Credit report error complaint resolution | Favors furnishers | Independent investigation with burden shift | CFPB reports |
| 1099-K reporting threshold | $600 (enacted; delayed) | $5,000 with personal transaction exclusion | Tax code |

### Key Risks in Phase 2

| Risk | Probability | Impact | Mitigation |
|------|------------|--------|------------|
| Financial industry defeats opt-in reform | Medium-High | Very High | EU GDPR precedent; consumer support data; community bank support |
| Credit bureau lobbying blocks FCRA reform | High | High | Public pressure from Equifax breach legacy; CFPB enforcement actions; state momentum |
| Private right of action stripped in negotiations | Medium | High | Make non-negotiable for consumer coalition support; safe harbor as compromise |
| CFPB authority challenged or defunded | Medium | High | Statutory authority embedded in legislation; bipartisan support for enforcement |
| Industry restructures to evade financial data broker definition | Medium | Medium | Functional definition test; anti-circumvention clause |

## Phase 3: Full Implementation (Years 4-6)

### Objective

Complete the financial privacy reform framework: comprehensive fintech regulation, cryptocurrency and CBDC privacy protections, credit reporting structural reform, and achievement of international financial data protection standards.

### Action Table

| Action | Lead Actor | Timeline | Dependencies | Priority |
|--------|-----------|----------|--------------|----------|
| Enact comprehensive fintech privacy regulation (all financial data entities covered) | Congress | Year 4-5 | Phase 2 framework; fintech industry maturation | Critical |
| Establish cryptocurrency privacy protections (exchange standards, self-custody rights, blockchain surveillance warrant requirement) | Congress | Year 4-5 | Crypto regulatory framework development | Critical |
| Mandate privacy-by-design for any CBDC or government digital payment system | Congress, Federal Reserve | Year 4-6 | Federal Reserve CBDC decision | High |
| Study and pilot public credit bureau option | CFPB | Year 4-6 | Credit reporting reform enacted; CFPB capacity | High |
| Implement automated financial data consent management infrastructure | Federal agency, industry | Year 5-6 | Technical standards developed; open banking operational | High |
| Achieve international financial data protection adequacy agreements | Treasury, Commerce | Year 5-6 | Comprehensive domestic framework in place | Medium |
| Review and strengthen BSA/AML reforms based on effectiveness data | Congress, FinCEN | Year 5-6 | Phase 1 reforms operational; data available | Medium |
| Comprehensive review of financial privacy framework effectiveness | Congress, GAO | Year 6 | All phases implemented | Medium |

### Success Metrics for Phase 3

| Metric | Baseline (End of Phase 2) | Target (End of Year 6) | Data Source |
|--------|--------------------------|----------------------|-------------|
| Fintech entities subject to comprehensive privacy requirements | Partial (payment apps only) | All financial data entities covered | Federal statute |
| Cryptocurrency privacy protections | Exchange-level KYC only | Self-custody protected; warrant for blockchain surveillance | Federal statute |
| CBDC privacy design | Under study | Privacy-by-design mandate enacted (if CBDC proceeds) | Federal statute |
| Public credit bureau option | Not studied | Pilot operational or study completed | CFPB reports |
| International financial data adequacy | No agreements | At least 2 major adequacy agreements | Treaty/agreement records |
| BSA/AML effectiveness | 1-2% money laundering intercepted | Measurable improvement with targeted approach | FinCEN, UNODC data |
| Consumer financial privacy satisfaction | 28% feel in control (Pew, 2023) | 50%+ feel in control | Polling |

### Key Risks in Phase 3

| Risk | Probability | Impact | Mitigation |
|------|------------|--------|------------|
| Cryptocurrency industry lobby blocks privacy protections | Medium | Medium | Frame as consumer protection; bipartisan support for self-custody |
| CBDC privacy compromise results in government surveillance tool | Medium-High | Very High | Statutory privacy mandate; technical privacy-by-design requirements; prohibition on transaction-level government access without warrant |
| Public credit bureau opposed by entrenched industry | High | Medium | Start with study and pilot; demonstrate value before full implementation |
| Political leadership change reverses reforms | Medium | High | Embed in statute; bipartisan institutional support; private right of action creates enforcement independent of political will |
| Consent fatigue undermines opt-in model | Medium | Medium | Design standards for meaningful consent; limit consent requests; centralized consent management |
