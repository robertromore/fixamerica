# Educational Privacy: Actions

## Overview

Protecting educational privacy requires action from parents, students, educators, school administrators, advocates, and policymakers. This document provides concrete steps that individuals and communities can take at every level.

---

## For Parents

### Immediate Actions

1. **Learn what data is collected**: Request a copy of your child's education records under FERPA (submit written request to school principal or district records officer)
2. **Review EdTech terms of service**: Ask the school for a list of all technology tools used by your child and review their privacy policies
3. **Opt out of directory information sharing**: Submit a written opt-out to your school district to prevent sharing of your child's name, address, and phone number
4. **Ask about surveillance**: Request information about what monitoring software is installed on school-issued devices
5. **Check for data broker listings**: Search for your child's information on people-search sites and request removal

### Ongoing Engagement

6. **Attend school board meetings**: Raise privacy concerns during public comment periods when new technology is proposed
7. **Join or start a parent privacy group**: Connect with Parent Coalition for Student Privacy or form a local chapter
8. **Review annual FERPA notifications**: Schools must send annual notification of rights; read these and ask questions
9. **Monitor your child's digital experience**: Talk to your children about what technology they use at school and how it makes them feel
10. **Support privacy-protective alternatives**: Advocate for open-source and privacy-protective EdTech tools

### Templates and Tools

- **FERPA records request letter**: Available at studentprivacy.ed.gov
- **Opt-out form for directory information**: Request from your school's front office
- **EdTech privacy evaluation checklist**: Available from Common Sense Media

## For Students

### Understanding Your Rights

1. **Know your FERPA rights**: At age 18 (or when enrolled in post-secondary education), FERPA rights transfer from parents to students
2. **Read school technology policies**: Understand what is monitored on school devices
3. **Ask about monitoring**: Ask teachers and administrators what surveillance software is running and what data is collected
4. **Understand your digital footprint**: Learn what information schools and EdTech companies collect about you

### Taking Action

5. **Advocate in student government**: Propose student privacy policies and resolutions through student council
6. **Organize with peers**: Create student privacy advocacy groups; connect with organizations like the Student Press Law Center
7. **Use privacy-protective practices**: When possible, use separate personal devices for personal communications; use privacy-respecting browsers
8. **Document concerns**: Keep records of privacy violations or excessive surveillance
9. **Speak at school board meetings**: Students have a powerful voice; use it to advocate for privacy protections
10. **Support legislative reform**: Contact state and federal legislators about student privacy; student voices carry weight

---

## For Educators

### Classroom Practices

1. **Minimize data collection**: Collect only the student data you need for instruction; delete what you no longer need
2. **Evaluate EdTech before adopting**: Assess privacy practices before using new tools; use Common Sense Media privacy evaluations
3. **Be transparent with students**: Tell students what technology monitors them and what data is collected
4. **Report concerns**: Raise privacy concerns with administrators and through professional organizations
5. **Seek training**: Request privacy training from your district; pursue professional development on student data protection

### Professional Advocacy

6. **Union engagement**: Advocate for student and teacher privacy provisions in collective bargaining agreements
7. **Professional organization involvement**: Participate in NEA, AFT, or subject-area organizations' privacy committees
8. **Curriculum integration**: Teach students about digital privacy and data literacy
9. **Model privacy practices**: Demonstrate respectful data practices in your own classroom
10. **Mentor new teachers**: Share privacy knowledge with incoming educators

---

## For School Administrators

### Governance

1. **Appoint a privacy officer**: Designate a staff member responsible for student data privacy
2. **Create an EdTech review process**: Establish procedures for evaluating new technology before deployment, including privacy assessment
3. **Develop data governance policies**: Adopt comprehensive data governance that covers collection, use, sharing, retention, and deletion
4. **Require vendor agreements**: Use standardized data protection agreements for all EdTech vendors (Student Data Privacy Consortium provides templates)
5. **Conduct regular audits**: Review what data is collected, where it flows, and whether it is still needed

### Implementation

6. **Train all staff**: Provide annual privacy training for teachers, administrators, and support staff
7. **Notify parents meaningfully**: Go beyond the minimum FERPA notification; explain what technology is used and why
8. **Evaluate surveillance effectiveness**: Require evidence that monitoring systems produce educational benefits before renewing contracts
9. **Establish breach response plans**: Prepare for data breaches with notification procedures and remediation steps
10. **Engage the community**: Create parent and student advisory committees on technology and privacy

---

## For Advocates and Organizations

### Research and Documentation

1. **Document surveillance practices**: FOIA requests for surveillance contracts, data sharing agreements, and vendor relationships
2. **Survey students and parents**: Collect data on privacy awareness, concerns, and experiences
3. **Analyze disparate impacts**: Study how surveillance technologies affect different student populations
4. **Evaluate EdTech data practices**: Test and report on actual data collection by popular EdTech tools
5. **Track state legislation**: Monitor and publish analyses of state student privacy bills

### Campaigns

6. **Build parent coalitions**: Organize parents around specific privacy issues (device monitoring, facial recognition, data breaches)
7. **Support litigation**: Fund or participate in legal challenges to privacy violations
8. **Engage media**: Provide research and case studies to journalists covering educational privacy
9. **File regulatory complaints**: Submit FTC complaints when EdTech companies violate COPPA or engage in unfair practices
10. **Lobby for legislation**: Support FERPA modernization and state student privacy bills

---

## For Policymakers

### Legislative Actions

1. **Introduce FERPA modernization**: Sponsor legislation updating FERPA for the digital age with enforceable rights
2. **Fund student privacy protection**: Appropriate funds for Department of Education privacy enforcement and school privacy infrastructure
3. **Require surveillance evaluation**: Mandate independent evaluation of school surveillance technology before federal funding
4. **Strengthen COPPA for education**: Close the school consent loophole that allows EdTech companies to bypass parental consent
5. **Oppose harmful preemption**: Ensure federal legislation does not preempt stronger state protections

### Oversight

6. **Hold hearings**: Conduct oversight hearings on EdTech data practices and school surveillance
7. **Request GAO studies**: Commission Government Accountability Office reviews of student data protection
8. **Review federal funding**: Ensure federal education technology grants include privacy requirements
9. **Engage with regulators**: Support FTC and Department of Education enforcement capacity
10. **Listen to affected communities**: Hold town halls and listening sessions with students, parents, and educators

| Issue | Contact | How |
|-------|---------|-----|
| FERPA violation | Department of Education SPPO | studentprivacy.ed.gov/file-a-complaint |
| COPPA violation by EdTech company | Federal Trade Commission | ftc.gov/complaint |
| State student privacy law violation | State Attorney General | State AG website |
| School surveillance concerns | School board | Public comment at board meetings |
| Federal legislation | US Senator/Representative | congress.gov; phone calls and letters |
| State legislation | State legislator | State legislative website |
