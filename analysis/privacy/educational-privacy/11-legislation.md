# Educational Privacy: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Educational privacy requires legislative action at multiple levels: modernizing FERPA to address digital data and provide enforceable rights, enacting federal legislation specifically targeting EdTech data practices and school surveillance, and providing model state legislation for states to strengthen protections. This document provides draft text for priority reforms.

## Constitutional Considerations

Educational privacy reform does not require a constitutional amendment but intersects with several constitutional provisions:

- **Fourth Amendment**: School searches subject to reduced standard (*New Jersey v. T.L.O.*, 1985); digital surveillance extends this framework
- **First Amendment**: Student speech rights may be chilled by surveillance; monitoring systems must not target protected expression
- **Fourteenth Amendment**: Equal protection concerns when surveillance disproportionately affects students of color or students with disabilities
- **Proposed Digital Privacy Amendment**: See [Privacy: Legislation](../11-legislation.md) for the proposed amendment that would also protect students

## Federal Legislation

### FERPA Modernization Act

**Purpose**: Comprehensively update the Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act for the digital age, establishing enforceable privacy rights for students and accountability for data holders.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "FERPA Modernization Act of [Year]".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that—
(1) The Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act was enacted
    in 1974 before the internet, cloud computing, and artificial
    intelligence existed;
(2) Students today generate vast quantities of digital data
    through school-mandated technology that was not contemplated
    by the original law;
(3) FERPA's sole enforcement mechanism—termination of federal
    funding—has never been used in 50 years, creating an
    enforcement vacuum;
(4) The Supreme Court in Gonzaga University v. Doe (2002) held
    that FERPA creates no private right of action, leaving
    students and parents without legal remedy;
(5) Education technology companies collect, retain, and share
    student data with minimal oversight under FERPA's "school
    official" exception;
(6) School surveillance technologies—including facial
    recognition, social media monitoring, and device
    tracking—create pervasive monitoring environments that
    disproportionately burden marginalized students.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:

(1) EDUCATION RECORDS.—The term "education records" means any
    information directly related to a student that is—
    (A) maintained by an educational agency or institution; or
    (B) collected, generated, or processed by a third-party
        service provider on behalf of an educational agency or
        institution, including—
        (i) data generated by student use of educational
            technology, including metadata, usage logs, and
            behavioral data;
        (ii) communications sent or received through
            school-provided platforms;
        (iii) biometric data collected in educational settings;
        (iv) data derived from school surveillance systems;
        (v) data inferred or predicted from student information
            through algorithmic processing.

(2) COVERED OPERATOR.—The term "covered operator" means any
    person or entity that—
    (A) operates a website, online service, or mobile
        application; and
    (B) collects, maintains, or uses education records or
        student-generated data pursuant to a contract or
        agreement with an educational agency or institution.

(3) STUDENT SURVEILLANCE SYSTEM.—The term "student surveillance
    system" means any technology used by or on behalf of an
    educational agency to monitor, track, or analyze student
    activity, including—
    (A) device monitoring software;
    (B) social media monitoring;
    (C) facial recognition technology;
    (D) location tracking;
    (E) predictive threat assessment systems;
    (F) content scanning and flagging systems.

(4) SENSITIVE STUDENT DATA.—The term "sensitive student data"
    means—
    (A) health and mental health information;
    (B) disability status and accommodation records;
    (C) disciplinary records;
    (D) biometric information;
    (E) information revealing race, ethnicity, religion,
        sexual orientation, or gender identity;
    (F) immigration status;
    (G) free or reduced lunch eligibility;
    (H) juvenile justice records.

SEC. 4. STUDENT AND PARENT RIGHTS.

(a) RIGHT OF ACCESS.—A parent or eligible student has the right
    to obtain from an educational agency or covered operator—
    (1) confirmation of what education records are maintained;
    (2) a copy of such records in a portable digital format;
    (3) a list of all covered operators and third parties that
        have received such records;
    (4) the purposes for which records were shared.

(b) RIGHT OF CORRECTION.—A parent or eligible student has the
    right to require correction of inaccurate education records
    within 30 days of a verified request.

(c) RIGHT OF DELETION.—A parent or eligible student has the
    right to require deletion of education records that are no
    longer necessary for the educational purpose for which they
    were collected.

(d) RIGHT TO TRANSPARENCY.—An educational agency shall publish
    annually—
    (1) a list of all covered operators with which it contracts;
    (2) the categories of education records shared with each;
    (3) the purposes of each data sharing arrangement;
    (4) a description of all student surveillance systems in
        use, including their capabilities and the data they
        collect.

(e) RIGHT TO LIMIT SURVEILLANCE.—A parent or eligible student
    may opt out of non-mandatory surveillance activities,
    including social media monitoring and predictive threat
    assessment, without academic penalty.

(f) GRADUATED STUDENT AUTONOMY.—
    (1) Students aged 13 and older shall have the right to
        exercise rights under this section independently with
        respect to non-academic records.
    (2) Students aged 16 and older shall have the right to
        exercise all rights under this section independently.

SEC. 5. COVERED OPERATOR OBLIGATIONS.

(a) DATA MINIMIZATION.—A covered operator shall—
    (1) collect only education records necessary to provide the
        contracted educational service;
    (2) retain education records only as long as necessary; and
    (3) delete education records within 60 days of contract
        termination or upon request.

(b) PURPOSE LIMITATION.—A covered operator shall not use
    education records for—
    (1) targeted advertising to students or parents;
    (2) creating marketing profiles of students;
    (3) sale or commercial transfer to third parties;
    (4) training artificial intelligence models without explicit
        authorization; or
    (5) any purpose unrelated to the contracted educational
        service.

(c) SECURITY REQUIREMENTS.—A covered operator shall implement
    reasonable security measures appropriate to the sensitivity
    of the education records maintained, including—
    (1) encryption of data in transit and at rest;
    (2) access controls limiting employee access;
    (3) vulnerability assessments at least annually;
    (4) incident response procedures.

(d) BREACH NOTIFICATION.—A covered operator that experiences a
    breach affecting education records shall—
    (1) notify the educational agency within 72 hours;
    (2) notify affected students and parents within 72 hours;
    (3) notify the Secretary of Education within 72 hours.

(e) TRANSPARENCY.—A covered operator shall publish a clear,
    student-specific privacy policy describing—
    (1) what data is collected and how;
    (2) how data is used;
    (3) with whom data is shared;
    (4) how long data is retained;
    (5) how students and parents can exercise their rights.

SEC. 6. SCHOOL SURVEILLANCE ACCOUNTABILITY.

(a) IMPACT ASSESSMENT.—Before deploying a new student
    surveillance system, an educational agency shall—
    (1) conduct a privacy impact assessment;
    (2) evaluate potential disparate impacts on students based
        on race, disability, gender identity, or other
        protected characteristics;
    (3) solicit public comment for at least 30 days;
    (4) obtain approval from the governing body (school board).

(b) FACIAL RECOGNITION PROHIBITION.—No educational agency
    serving students in kindergarten through grade 12 shall use
    facial recognition technology for student identification or
    monitoring purposes.

(c) ANNUAL REPORTING.—An educational agency using student
    surveillance systems shall report annually to the Secretary
    of Education—
    (1) the systems in use and their capabilities;
    (2) the number of students monitored;
    (3) the number of alerts generated and their disposition;
    (4) any disparate impact data;
    (5) the cost of the systems.

(d) BIAS AUDITS.—Student surveillance systems using algorithmic
    or AI-based analysis shall undergo independent bias audits
    at least every two years.

SEC. 7. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) OFFICE OF STUDENT PRIVACY.—The Secretary of Education shall
    establish an Office of Student Privacy with authority to—
    (1) investigate complaints;
    (2) conduct compliance audits;
    (3) issue binding orders;
    (4) impose civil penalties.

(b) CIVIL PENALTIES.—
    (1) An educational agency that violates this Act is subject
        to a civil penalty of up to $100,000 per violation.
    (2) A covered operator that violates this Act is subject to
        a civil penalty of up to $50,000 per violation, or 4%
        of annual revenue for large operators.

(c) PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.—Any parent or eligible student
    aggrieved by a violation of this Act may bring a civil
    action in Federal or State court for—
    (1) actual damages;
    (2) statutory damages of $1,000 to $5,000 per violation;
    (3) reasonable attorney's fees and costs;
    (4) injunctive relief.

(d) STATE ATTORNEY GENERAL ENFORCEMENT.—A State attorney
    general may bring a civil action for violations affecting
    State residents.

SEC. 8. PREEMPTION.

(a) FEDERAL FLOOR.—This Act establishes minimum standards and
    shall not preempt any State law that provides greater
    protection to students.

(b) PRESERVATION OF EXISTING LAW.—This Act does not limit
    rights under COPPA, IDEA, or State privacy laws.

SEC. 9. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect 18 months after enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- Modernizes the definition of "education records" to include all digital data
- Creates enforceable individual rights (access, correction, deletion, transparency)
- Imposes data minimization and purpose limitation on EdTech vendors
- Prohibits facial recognition in K-12 schools
- Requires impact assessments and community approval for surveillance technology
- Establishes a private right of action with statutory damages
- Creates a dedicated enforcement office
- Preserves stronger state protections

**Potential Challenges**:

- EdTech industry lobbying against private right of action and data minimization
- Preemption debate with states having stronger laws
- School district concerns about compliance costs
- First Amendment challenges to surveillance restrictions

**Refinements**:

- Consider tiered compliance requirements for small school districts
- May need transition provisions for existing EdTech contracts
- Coordination with COPPA reform needed

### Student Data Protection Act

**Purpose**: Targeted federal law addressing EdTech company data practices specifically.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Student Data Protection Act
of [Year]".

SEC. 2. PROHIBITED PRACTICES.

(a) A covered operator shall not—
    (1) use student data for targeted advertising;
    (2) sell student data to any third party;
    (3) use student data to create marketing profiles;
    (4) disclose student data except as necessary to provide
        the educational service;
    (5) retain student data beyond the period necessary for
        the educational purpose;
    (6) use student data for training artificial intelligence
        models without explicit, informed consent of the
        parent or eligible student.

(b) AFFIRMATIVE CONSENT FOR SENSITIVE DATA.—A covered operator
    shall not collect sensitive student data without the
    affirmative express consent of the parent or eligible
    student.

SEC. 3. TRANSPARENCY.

(a) A covered operator shall maintain a public, student-
    specific privacy policy written in plain language.

(b) A covered operator shall annually report to the FTC—
    (1) the number of students whose data is maintained;
    (2) the categories of data collected;
    (3) any data breaches or security incidents;
    (4) third-party data sharing.

SEC. 4. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) FTC ENFORCEMENT.—The Federal Trade Commission shall
    enforce this Act under the Federal Trade Commission Act.

(b) PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.—Parents and eligible students may
    bring civil actions with statutory damages of $500 to
    $2,500 per violation.

(c) PENALTIES.—Civil penalties of up to $50,000 per violation;
    knowing violations subject to treble damages.

SEC. 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect 12 months after enactment.
```

## State Model Legislation

### Model Student Privacy Protection Act

**Purpose**: Comprehensive state-level student data privacy protection.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Student Privacy
Protection Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.

(1) "Covered operator" means any person that operates a
    website, online service, or application that is used
    primarily for educational purposes and collects student
    data.

(2) "Student data" means any information directly related to
    a student that is collected, maintained, or generated by
    a covered operator, including personally identifiable
    information, metadata, and usage data.

(3) "Educational purpose" means a purpose that is directed by
    or customarily requested by the educational institution
    or that aids in the administration of school activities.

SECTION 3. PROHIBITED PRACTICES.

A covered operator shall not—
(1) use student data for targeted advertising;
(2) sell student data;
(3) use student data to create a profile for non-educational
    commercial purposes;
(4) disclose student data except as authorized by this Act;
(5) retain student data beyond the period necessary for the
    educational purpose for which it was collected.

SECTION 4. REQUIRED PRACTICES.

(a) A covered operator shall—
    (1) implement reasonable security procedures;
    (2) delete student data within 60 days of request by the
        school, parent, or eligible student;
    (3) provide notice of what data is collected and how it
        is used;
    (4) allow parents and eligible students to review data.

(b) An educational institution shall—
    (1) enter into written data governance agreements with
        all covered operators before sharing student data;
    (2) publish a list of all covered operators and the data
        shared with each;
    (3) provide annual notice to parents of data practices;
    (4) designate a data privacy officer or coordinator.

SECTION 5. DATA BREACH NOTIFICATION.

A covered operator that experiences a breach of security
affecting student data shall notify—
(1) the educational institution within 72 hours;
(2) affected parents and eligible students within 72 hours;
(3) the [State Attorney General] within 72 hours.

SECTION 6. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) The [Attorney General] may bring a civil action for
    violations.
(b) A parent or eligible student aggrieved by a violation
    may bring a civil action for actual damages, statutory
    damages of $1,000 per violation, and attorney's fees.
(c) Civil penalties of up to $25,000 per violation.

SECTION 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect January 1 following enactment.
```

### Model School Surveillance Transparency Act

**Purpose**: State-level requirements for transparency and accountability in school surveillance.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] School Surveillance
Transparency Act".

SECTION 2. REQUIREMENTS.

(a) Before deploying any new surveillance technology, a public
    school or school district shall—
    (1) conduct a privacy impact assessment;
    (2) hold a public hearing with at least 30 days notice;
    (3) obtain approval from the school board by majority vote.

(b) A public school or school district using surveillance
    technology shall report annually to the [State Education
    Agency] on—
    (1) the technologies in use;
    (2) the costs incurred;
    (3) the number of alerts or referrals generated;
    (4) the outcomes of those alerts;
    (5) any disparate impact data disaggregated by race,
        gender, disability status, and other demographics.

SECTION 3. FACIAL RECOGNITION PROHIBITION.

No public school or school district shall use facial
recognition technology for student identification,
monitoring, or disciplinary purposes.

SECTION 4. STUDENT PROTECTIONS.

(a) No student shall face academic penalty for a parent's
    decision to opt out of non-mandatory surveillance.

(b) Data generated by surveillance systems shall not be
    shared with law enforcement absent a warrant or imminent
    threat to life.

SECTION 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect July 1 following enactment.
```

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

| Issue | Analysis |
|-------|----------|
| Fourth Amendment | Reduced privacy expectations in schools (*T.L.O.*) but digital surveillance may exceed |
| First Amendment | Surveillance of student speech raises free expression concerns |
| Equal Protection | Disparate impact of surveillance on minority students |
| Due Process | Students have liberty interests in freedom from pervasive monitoring |

### Preemption

| Scenario | Recommendation |

| Federal floor | Preferred -- states may exceed federal minimum |
| COPPA interaction | Coordinate with COPPA reform to close school consent loophole |

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| "Educational purpose" interpretation | Broad reading could permit extensive data collection under guise of educational purpose | High |
| De-identification circumvention | Small student populations (rural schools, specialized programs) may be re-identifiable | Medium |
| Parent consent manipulation | Schools may pressure parents into consenting through opt-out penalty concerns | Medium |
| Vendor subcontractor chains | Primary vendors may share data with subcontractors not covered by agreements | High |
| Research exception exploitation | "Research" label could be used to justify commercial data use | Medium |
| Device monitoring scope | Monitoring software on school devices used at home captures personal activity | High |
| AI inference workaround | Companies may claim inferred data is not "collected" student data | Medium |

### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Enforcement resources | Small districts cannot implement without federal support | Funding constraints |
| International vendors | Companies outside US jurisdiction may evade requirements | Cross-border enforcement |
| Legacy systems | Existing data holdings not addressed by new rules | Transition design gap |
| Rapid technology change | Law may not anticipate future EdTech capabilities | Technology outpacing regulation |
| Parental digital literacy | Many parents cannot effectively exercise rights | Information asymmetry |

### Rectification Procedures

1. **Define "educational purpose" narrowly**: Regulations should enumerate permissible educational purposes and exclude all others
2. **Strengthen de-identification standards**: Require k-anonymity or differential privacy for small populations
3. **Prohibit opt-out penalties**: Explicitly protect students whose parents decline surveillance
4. **Require subcontractor flow-down**: Data protection obligations must apply to all subcontractors
5. **Limit research exception**: Require IRB-equivalent review and genuine public interest for research use
6. **Restrict off-campus monitoring**: Limit device monitoring to school hours and school-related activity
7. **Cover inferred data**: Explicitly define "education records" to include data inferred through algorithmic processing
8. **Fund compliance**: Provide federal grants for small-district privacy infrastructure
9. **Technology-neutral drafting**: Frame requirements around data practices, not specific technologies
10. **Parent education mandate**: Require schools to provide privacy training and support to parents

### General Implementation Concerns

- EdTech industry will seek to weaken enforcement provisions
- School districts may resist unfunded mandates
- Balance needed between surveillance accountability and legitimate safety measures
- Transition period required for existing contracts and data practices
- Ongoing technology-specific guidance will be needed as EdTech evolves
