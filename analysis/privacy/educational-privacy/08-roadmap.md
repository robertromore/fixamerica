# Educational Privacy: Roadmap

## Overview

This roadmap sequences educational privacy reforms across four phases, beginning with achievable state-level and institutional actions, building toward comprehensive federal legislation, and culminating in sustained protection and enforcement. Each phase builds on the previous one, creating momentum and demonstrating feasibility.

---

## Phase 1: Foundation Building (Years 1-2)

### Objectives

- Build public awareness and political support
- Pass model state legislation in 5-10 additional states
- Establish district-level privacy governance in major districts
- Document harms and build evidence base

### Key Actions

| Action | Lead Actors | Timeline | Success Metric |
|--------|-------------|----------|----------------|
| Launch national parent education campaign | Privacy advocacy orgs, PTA | Months 1-6 | 1 million parents reached |
| Draft model state student privacy legislation | National Conference of State Legislatures, advocates | Months 1-6 | Model bill completed |
| Pass model state laws in 5-10 states | State legislators, parent coalitions | Months 6-18 | 5+ states enacted |
| Publish district privacy governance toolkit | CoSN, Student Privacy Compass | Months 3-9 | Toolkit distributed to 5,000+ districts |
| Commission independent review of school surveillance effectiveness | RAND, universities | Months 1-12 | Peer-reviewed study published |
| File FTC complaints against EdTech violations | EFF, CDT, parent organizations | Months 1-24 | 5+ complaints filed |
| Create EdTech privacy rating system | Consumer Reports, parent organizations | Months 6-18 | Rating system operational |

### Milestones

- [ ] Model state legislation drafted and published
- [ ] 5 additional states pass comprehensive student privacy laws
- [ ] 50 major school districts adopt privacy governance frameworks
- [ ] Independent study on school surveillance effectiveness published
- [ ] National parent awareness campaign launched

## Phase 2: Federal Action and Industry Reform (Years 2-4)

### Objectives

- Pass federal FERPA modernization legislation
- Establish EdTech vendor accountability framework
- Begin restricting school surveillance technology
- Create enforcement infrastructure

### Key Actions

| Action | Lead Actors | Timeline | Success Metric |
|--------|-------------|----------|----------------|
| Introduce FERPA Modernization Act in Congress | Bipartisan sponsors | Year 2, Q1 | Bill introduced with 20+ co-sponsors |
| Build bipartisan coalition (children's privacy framing) | Advocates, bipartisan legislators | Year 2, Q1-Q4 | Support from both parties |
| Introduce Student Data Protection Act | Congressional sponsors | Year 2, Q2 | Bill introduced |
| FTC rulemaking on EdTech data practices | FTC commissioners | Year 2-3 | Proposed rule published |
| Establish federal student privacy enforcement office | Department of Education | Year 3 | Office operational |
| Pass federal FERPA modernization | Congress | Year 3-4 | Bill signed into law |
| Create EdTech vendor certification program | FTC / private sector | Year 3-4 | Certification operational |

### Milestones

- [ ] FERPA Modernization Act introduced with bipartisan support
- [ ] FTC proposes EdTech-specific rules
- [ ] Federal enforcement office established
- [ ] FERPA modernization signed into law
- [ ] EdTech vendor certification program launches

## Phase 3: Surveillance Reform and Enforcement (Years 4-6)

### Objectives

- Implement and enforce new federal law
- Restrict harmful school surveillance practices
- Build privacy-protective EdTech alternatives
- Establish ongoing monitoring and evaluation

### Key Actions

| Action | Lead Actors | Timeline | Success Metric |
|--------|-------------|----------|----------------|
| Implement FERPA modernization regulations | Department of Education | Year 4-5 | Regulations finalized |
| Introduce School Surveillance Accountability Act | Congress | Year 4 | Bill introduced |
| Fund privacy-protective EdTech development | NSF, Department of Education | Year 4-6 | $100M+ in grants |
| Conduct first enforcement actions under new law | Department of Education, FTC | Year 5 | 10+ enforcement actions |
| Restrict facial recognition in K-12 schools | Congress or states | Year 4-6 | Federal or 25+ state restrictions |
| Require bias audits for school monitoring systems | FTC, states | Year 5-6 | Audit requirements in effect |

### Milestones

- [ ] New federal regulations effective
- [ ] First enforcement actions under modernized FERPA
- [ ] Facial recognition restricted in majority of states
- [ ] Privacy-protective EdTech alternatives available
- [ ] Bias audit requirements established

---

## Phase 4: Sustained Protection (Years 6-10)

### Objectives

- Evaluate and strengthen enacted reforms
- Ensure ongoing enforcement and adaptation
- Develop long-term privacy-protective educational culture
- Address emerging technologies (AI, XR)

### Key Actions

| Action | Lead Actors | Timeline | Success Metric |
|--------|-------------|----------|----------------|
| Conduct 5-year review of FERPA modernization | GAO, Department of Education | Year 6 | Review report published |
| Update regulations for AI in education | Department of Education | Year 6-8 | AI-specific regulations |
| Evaluate surveillance reform effectiveness | Independent researchers | Year 7-8 | Evaluation published |
| Strengthen penalties based on enforcement experience | Congress | Year 7-8 | Amendments enacted |
| Integrate privacy education into teacher preparation | Education schools, states | Year 6-10 | Privacy in accreditation standards |
| Establish student privacy ombudsman | Department of Education | Year 8 | Office operational |

### Milestones

- [ ] 5-year review completed with recommendations
- [ ] AI-specific educational privacy regulations issued
- [ ] Privacy education integrated into teacher preparation
- [ ] Student privacy ombudsman operational

## Risk Assessment

| Risk | Probability | Impact | Mitigation |
|------|-------------|--------|------------|
| Industry lobbying defeats federal legislation | High | Critical | Build parent coalitions; pursue state laws as alternative |
| Federal preemption of stronger state laws | Medium | High | Insist on federal floor, not ceiling |
| School safety crisis shifts narrative | Medium | High | Pre-position evidence on surveillance ineffectiveness |
| EdTech companies relocate to avoid regulation | Low | Medium | Federal law applies regardless of company location |
| AI advances outpace regulations | High | Medium | Build technology-neutral principles into law |
| Enforcement agency lacks resources | Medium | High | Dedicated funding through vendor fees |
| Judicial challenges (First Amendment) | Medium | Medium | Strong congressional findings; narrow tailoring |

## Dependencies

| Dependency | Impact if Not Met | Alternative Path |
|------------|-------------------|------------------|
| Bipartisan support for FERPA reform | Federal legislation fails | State-level action continues |
| FTC enforcement authority | No EdTech-specific rules | Congressional grant of authority |
| Education funding for privacy | Districts cannot implement | Federal grants; vendor fee funding |
| Parent mobilization | Insufficient political pressure | Leverage data breaches for attention |
| Evidence base on surveillance | Safety narrative dominates | Commission independent research |

## Measuring Success

| Metric | Baseline (2025) | Year 5 Target | Year 10 Target |
|--------|-----------------|---------------|----------------|
| States with comprehensive student privacy laws | 12 strong laws | 30 states | 50 states (or federal law) |
| FERPA enforcement actions | 0 (ever) | 10 per year | 25+ per year |
| Districts with dedicated privacy staff | <25% | 50% | 75% |
| EdTech apps disclosing data practices | ~30% | 75% | 95% |
| Student awareness of data collection | 18% | 50% | 75% |
| Schools deploying facial recognition | 10-15% | <5% | <1% |
| Student data breaches (annual) | 300+ | <100 | <50 |
