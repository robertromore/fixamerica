# Educational Privacy: Opposition

## Overview

Opposition to stronger educational privacy protections comes from a coalition of EdTech companies, school safety advocates, government data collection proponents, and ideological opponents of regulation. Each group deploys distinct arguments, but they share a common interest in maintaining the status quo of minimal oversight and maximum data collection. Understanding these opposition arguments and their weaknesses is essential for effective reform advocacy.

---

## Opposition Groups

### EdTech Industry

**Who:** Google, Microsoft, Apple, Instructure, PowerSchool, Clever, and thousands of smaller EdTech companies; represented by trade associations including the Software and Information Industry Association (SIIA), Future of Privacy Forum (FPF), and EdTech trade groups.

**Arguments:**

| Argument | Claim | Counter-Evidence |
|----------|-------|------------------|
| Innovation will suffer | Regulation will stifle educational technology innovation | GDPR has not destroyed European EdTech; SOPIPA in California has not driven companies from the market |
| Self-regulation works | Student Privacy Pledge and voluntary standards are sufficient | FTC found multiple Pledge signatories violating their own commitments |
| Data improves learning | Student data enables personalized learning and better outcomes | Weak evidence that data-intensive tools produce better learning outcomes than simpler alternatives |
| Compliance costs | Small companies cannot afford privacy compliance | Tiered requirements based on company size can address this |
| Parents already have rights | FERPA provides adequate protections | FERPA has never been enforced; no private right of action |

**Strategies:**

- Fund self-regulatory initiatives (Student Privacy Pledge) to preempt legislation
- Lobby for federal preemption of stronger state laws
- Offer "free" products to budget-constrained schools, creating dependency
- Frame privacy regulation as anti-technology and anti-education
- Participate in advisory bodies to shape policy from the inside

### School Safety and Surveillance Advocates

**Who:** School safety organizations, security consultants, surveillance technology vendors (Gaggle, GoGuardian, Securly, Bark), and some parent groups focused on school violence.

**Arguments:**

| Argument | Claim | Counter-Evidence |
|----------|-------|------------------|
| Surveillance saves lives | Monitoring tools prevent school shootings and student suicides | RAND Corporation (2023): no peer-reviewed evidence that surveillance prevents mass violence; suicide prevention evidence is mixed |
| Better safe than sorry | The cost of not monitoring outweighs privacy concerns | Over-monitoring produces false positives (95%+), creates chilling effects, and diverts resources from proven interventions |
| Parents want safety | Parents support surveillance | Surveys show parents support safety but do not understand the scope of surveillance; support drops when specifics are disclosed |
| Students expect monitoring | Digital natives are accustomed to monitoring | CDT (2024): 68% of students self-censor due to monitoring; "accustomed" does not mean consenting |

**Strategies:**

- Leverage school shooting events to push surveillance expansion
- Position surveillance as the only alternative to violence
- Obtain testimonials from parents whose children were identified by monitoring systems
- Partner with law enforcement to present surveillance as crime prevention

### Government Data Collection Proponents

**Who:** Department of Education data offices, state longitudinal data system administrators, education researchers, and accountability advocates.

**Arguments:**

| Argument | Claim | Counter-Evidence |
|----------|-------|------------------|
| Accountability requires data | Educational quality cannot be measured without extensive data collection | Data collection for accountability purposes can be designed with privacy protections; currently no meaningful limits exist |
| Research depends on access | Education research requires student data | Proper de-identification and data governance can enable research without compromising individual privacy |
| Equity requires data | Disaggregated data reveals disparities | True, but data collection must be proportionate and purpose-limited; current practices far exceed equity analysis needs |

### Anti-Regulation Ideological Opposition

**Who:** Free-market think tanks (Heritage Foundation, Cato Institute), some congressional Republicans, and industry-aligned commentators.

**Arguments:**

| Argument | Claim | Counter-Evidence |
|----------|-------|------------------|
| Market solutions preferred | Competition will drive privacy-protective products | Market has failed to produce privacy-protective EdTech because schools, not students, are the customers |
| Government overreach | Privacy regulation expands government power | Regulation of private data collection is a different category from government surveillance expansion |
| Parental responsibility | Parents should manage their children's data exposure | Parents cannot opt out without disadvantaging their children; many lack technical knowledge |
| First Amendment | Data collection is protected speech | Commercial data regulation is permissible under existing First Amendment doctrine |

### Revolving Door

- Former Department of Education officials regularly move to EdTech companies and trade associations
- Industry representatives serve on Department of Education advisory committees
- Lobbyists draft model legislation that weakens privacy protections

### Campaign Contributions

- EdTech PACs and executives contribute to members of education and technology committees
- Contributions target key state legislators considering student privacy bills
- Industry-funded research is used to oppose legislation

## Counterarguments

### Against "Innovation Will Suffer"

- California's SOPIPA (2014) imposed strict requirements on EdTech companies; California remains the global hub of EdTech innovation
- The EU's GDPR has not destroyed European education technology; companies adapted
- Privacy-by-design can be integrated into product development without reducing functionality
- Student privacy features can be a competitive differentiator, not a burden

### Against "Surveillance Saves Lives"

- No peer-reviewed evidence supports the claim that school surveillance prevents mass violence (RAND 2023)
- Countries with strong student privacy protections (Nordic countries, Germany) have far lower rates of school violence, suggesting root-cause interventions are more effective
- False positive rates of 95%+ from monitoring systems waste resources and harm students
- Evidence-based alternatives (mental health services, threat assessment teams, restorative justice) are more effective and less invasive

### Against "Self-Regulation Works"

- The Student Privacy Pledge is voluntary, non-binding, and unenforceable
- FTC actions against Chegg and Edmodo (both Pledge signatories) demonstrate the failure of self-regulation
- No industry self-regulatory program has ever provided adequate consumer protection in any sector
- Self-regulation serves as a delay tactic to prevent legislation

### Against "FERPA Is Sufficient"

- FERPA has never been enforced through its sole mechanism (funding termination)
- The Supreme Court has ruled that FERPA creates no private right of action (*Gonzaga University v. Doe*, 2002)
- FERPA was written before the internet, cloud computing, or AI existed
- The "school official" exception has been expanded to cover virtually any vendor

## Opposition Effectiveness

| Strategy | Effectiveness | Vulnerability |
|----------|---------------|---------------|
| Lobbying against legislation | High at federal level; declining at state level | State-by-state progress continues |
| Self-regulation initiatives | Moderate -- provides political cover | Repeated violations undermine credibility |
| Safety narrative | High -- emotionally powerful | Lack of evidence creates counter-opportunity |
| Free product dependency | Very high -- creates institutional lock-in | Growing awareness among school leaders |
| Federal preemption advocacy | Moderate -- attractive to some legislators | Privacy advocates and states resist |
| Research funding/influence | Moderate -- shapes evidence base | Independent research contradicts |

## Strategies for Overcoming Opposition

1. **Build parent-led coalitions**: Parent opposition defeated inBloom and has driven state legislation; parents remain the most powerful constituency
2. **Demand evidence for surveillance**: Require rigorous, independent evaluation before deploying new surveillance technology
3. **Highlight disparate impacts**: Document how surveillance disproportionately harms students of color and LGBTQ+ students
4. **State-level momentum**: Continue passing state laws to create a de facto national standard
5. **Leverage data breaches**: Each major breach (PowerSchool 2024) creates a window for reform
6. **Reframe the debate**: Privacy protection is not anti-technology; it is pro-student
7. **Support alternatives**: Fund and promote privacy-protective EdTech alternatives
8. **Federal legislation**: Build bipartisan coalition around FERPA modernization
