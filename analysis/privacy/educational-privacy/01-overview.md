# Educational Privacy: Overview

## Executive Summary

Educational privacy in America is under siege from a convergence of outdated federal law, aggressive EdTech commercialization, and expanding school surveillance systems. The Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act (FERPA), enacted in 1974, remains the primary federal law governing student data -- yet it was written before the internet, cloud computing, and algorithmic profiling existed. Today, K-12 schools deploy an average of 1,400 distinct EdTech applications per district, each potentially collecting granular data on student behavior, academic performance, social interactions, and emotional states. Higher education institutions share student data through complex vendor ecosystems with minimal transparency. Meanwhile, school surveillance technologies -- facial recognition, social media monitoring, predictive threat assessment, and constant device tracking -- create a pervasive monitoring environment that normalizes surveillance from childhood.

The consequences are profound. Student data flows freely to third parties, including advertisers, data brokers, and law enforcement, often without meaningful consent or even awareness from students and parents. EdTech companies build detailed longitudinal profiles of children that may follow them into adulthood, influencing college admissions, employment, and insurance decisions. School surveillance systems disproportionately burden students of color, students with disabilities, and LGBTQ+ students, reinforcing rather than reducing inequities. The post-COVID expansion of remote learning vastly accelerated data collection, with proctoring software, learning management systems, and communication platforms harvesting unprecedented volumes of student information.

Despite growing awareness and several state-level reforms, the United States lacks a coherent framework for protecting educational privacy in the digital age. FERPA's enforcement mechanism -- the threat of losing federal funding -- has never been invoked. The Department of Education's Privacy Technical Assistance Center (PTAC) issues guidance but lacks regulatory teeth. The result is a system where student privacy depends largely on the goodwill of technology vendors and the technical sophistication of individual school administrators.

### Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **FERPA** | Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act (20 U.S.C. 1232g); the primary federal law governing student educational records |
| **Education records** | Records directly related to a student maintained by an educational agency or institution, as defined by FERPA |
| **Directory information** | Student information that would not generally be considered harmful if disclosed (name, address, dates of attendance), which schools may release without consent |
| **School official exception** | FERPA provision allowing disclosure of education records to school officials with "legitimate educational interests," including third-party contractors |
| **EdTech** | Education technology; software, platforms, and digital tools used in educational settings |
| **Learning analytics** | Collection and analysis of data about learners and their contexts to understand and optimize learning and educational environments |
| **Proctoring software** | Applications that monitor students during online examinations, often using webcams, screen recording, and behavioral analysis |
| **Student Information System (SIS)** | Database used by schools to manage student enrollment, attendance, grades, and demographic information |
| **Threat assessment** | Systems that monitor student communications and online behavior for indicators of potential violence or self-harm |

## Core Tensions

- **Safety vs. privacy**: Schools use surveillance to prevent violence, but monitoring creates chilling effects and disproportionate harm
- **Innovation vs. protection**: EdTech improves learning outcomes, but data collection enables exploitation
- **Parental rights vs. student autonomy**: Parents have FERPA rights, but older students need independent privacy protections
- **Federal consistency vs. state innovation**: National standards provide uniformity, but states are driving stronger protections
- **Free services vs. data costs**: Schools receive "free" EdTech tools by paying with student data
- **Transparency vs. security**: Disclosing surveillance capabilities may reduce effectiveness but enables accountability

## Key Questions

1. Why has FERPA never been meaningfully enforced, and what would effective enforcement look like?
2. How do EdTech companies monetize student data, and what are the long-term consequences for students?
3. Do school surveillance systems actually improve safety, or do they primarily normalize monitoring and disproportionately harm marginalized students?
4. How should educational privacy protections evolve to address AI-powered learning analytics and predictive systems?
5. What consent models are appropriate when students are required to use school-selected technology?
6. How can schools balance legitimate safety concerns with student privacy and civil liberties?
7. What role should students themselves play in decisions about their educational data?

## Vision of Success

A reformed educational privacy landscape would feature:

- **Modernized FERPA**: Updated law that addresses digital data, EdTech vendors, and surveillance technology with meaningful enforcement
- **Data minimization in schools**: EdTech tools limited to collecting only data necessary for educational purposes
- **Transparency requirements**: Schools and vendors required to disclose what data is collected, how it is used, and who receives it
- **Student and parent data rights**: Enforceable rights to access, correct, delete, and port educational data
- **Surveillance accountability**: School surveillance technologies subject to independent evaluation, bias auditing, and community approval
- **Vendor accountability**: EdTech companies legally prohibited from using student data for non-educational purposes, advertising, or third-party sales
- **Equitable implementation**: Privacy protections that account for and mitigate disparate impacts on marginalized students
- **Age-appropriate autonomy**: Graduated privacy rights that give older students increasing control over their own educational data
