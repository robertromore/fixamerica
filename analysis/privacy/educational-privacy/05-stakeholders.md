# Educational Privacy: Stakeholders

## Overview

Educational privacy involves a complex ecosystem of stakeholders with competing interests. Students and parents bear the consequences of data collection but have the least power. Schools and districts occupy a middle position, constrained by budgets, mandates, and vendor pressure. EdTech companies and surveillance vendors profit from data extraction. Policymakers, advocates, and researchers shape the debate but struggle to overcome industry influence and political inertia.

---

## Primary Stakeholders

### Students (K-12 and Higher Education)

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Population** | 50+ million K-12 students; 20+ million higher education students |
| **Interest** | Privacy, autonomy, equitable treatment, educational quality |
| **Power** | Very low -- minors cannot assert legal rights; limited organized advocacy |
| **Impact** | Highest -- data collection follows them for decades |

**Key Concerns:**

- Data collected without knowledge or meaningful consent
- Surveillance creates chilling effects on expression and exploration
- Disproportionate monitoring of students of color, LGBTQ+ students, and students with disabilities
- Digital records may affect future college admissions, employment, and insurance
- Proctoring software invades home privacy and disadvantages disabled students

### Parents and Families

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Population** | 35+ million households with K-12 students |
| **Interest** | Child safety, child privacy, control over family data, educational quality |
| **Power** | Moderate -- demonstrated political effectiveness (inBloom); but diffuse |
| **Impact** | High -- family data exposed through student data collection |

**Key Concerns:**

- Lack of transparency about what data is collected about their children
- Opt-out processes are burdensome and may disadvantage their children educationally
- Home environments captured by proctoring software and device cameras
- Family income and status exposed through free/reduced lunch data
- No effective legal remedy when data is misused

### Educators and School Staff

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Population** | 3.7+ million public school teachers; 1+ million support staff |
| **Interest** | Effective teaching tools, manageable workload, student welfare |
| **Power** | Low-moderate -- teachers' unions have some policy influence |
| **Impact** | Medium -- their own data is also collected; they implement surveillance |

**Key Concerns:**

- Pressure to adopt EdTech tools without privacy training
- Monitoring tools used to surveil teachers as well as students
- Ethical discomfort with role as surveillance operators
- Learning analytics used to evaluate teacher performance
- Liability concerns when data breaches occur

## Institutional Stakeholders

### School Districts and Boards

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Population** | ~13,000 public school districts |
| **Interest** | Legal compliance, cost reduction, safety, educational outcomes |
| **Power** | Moderate -- control procurement decisions, local policy |
| **Impact** | Medium -- make decisions that determine student data exposure |

**Key Concerns:**

- Budget constraints drive adoption of "free" EdTech tools
- Cybersecurity threats (ransomware, data breaches)
- Liability for data incidents
- Complying with inconsistent state and federal requirements
- Community pressure on school safety

### Higher Education Institutions

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Population** | ~4,000 degree-granting institutions |
| **Interest** | Enrollment management, retention, research, competitive positioning |
| **Power** | Moderate-high -- significant lobbying presence; regulatory influence |
| **Impact** | Medium -- student data practices in admissions, analytics, and proctoring |

**Key Concerns:**

- Balancing learning analytics with student privacy
- Remote proctoring backlash from students
- Data security across complex IT ecosystems
- FERPA compliance in research contexts
- International student data obligations

### State Education Agencies

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Population** | 50 state agencies plus territories |
| **Interest** | Educational standards, accountability, data systems, compliance |
| **Power** | High -- set state policies, manage longitudinal data systems |
| **Impact** | High -- state data systems aggregate student data across districts |

**Key Concerns:**

- Managing state longitudinal data systems securely
- Balancing data-driven accountability with privacy
- Implementing state student privacy laws
- Vendor management at state level

## Industry Stakeholders

### EdTech Companies

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Population** | 10,000+ EdTech companies operating in US schools |
| **Interest** | Market growth, data accumulation, user engagement, profitability |
| **Power** | High -- control platform design, data practices; significant lobbying |
| **Impact** | High -- determine what data is collected and how it is used |

**Major Players:**

| Company | Role | Lobbying/Advocacy |
|---------|------|-------------------|
| Google (Alphabet) | Dominant K-12 platform provider | Major federal and state lobbying presence |
| Microsoft | Enterprise education platform | Education policy lobbying |
| Apple | Device manufacturer, classroom tools | Education advocacy, privacy differentiation |
| Instructure (Thoma Bravo) | LMS provider (Canvas) | Private equity-driven growth |
| PowerSchool | Student information systems | Industry association leadership |
| Clever | Single sign-on, data integration | Student Privacy Pledge signatory |

### Surveillance Technology Vendors

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Population** | 50+ companies focused on school surveillance |
| **Interest** | Market expansion, government contracts, school safety positioning |
| **Power** | Moderate-high -- leverage safety narrative; government funding support |
| **Impact** | High -- deploy surveillance infrastructure affecting millions of students |

**Major Players:**

| Company | Technology | Market Position |
|---------|-----------|-----------------|
| Gaggle | Content monitoring | 1,500+ districts |
| GoGuardian | Device monitoring, web filtering | 27M+ students |
| Securly | Web filtering, monitoring, AI wellness | 20,000+ schools |
| Bark | Social media and device monitoring | 6,000+ schools |
| Verkada | Video surveillance, facial recognition | Growing K-12 presence |
| Evolv Technology | Weapons detection screening | Expanding into schools |

### Data Brokers and Third Parties

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Population** | Hundreds of companies receiving student data |
| **Interest** | Aggregating and monetizing student data |
| **Power** | Low visibility but high impact -- operate in the background |
| **Impact** | High -- student data enters broader commercial data ecosystem |

**Data Flow:**

- EdTech analytics SDKs share data with advertising networks
- College Board and ACT sell student test registration data
- Student data aggregated into marketing profiles
- Background check companies incorporate educational records

## Advocacy and Oversight Stakeholders

### Privacy Advocacy Organizations

| Organization | Focus | Approach |
|-------------|-------|----------|
| Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF) | Digital rights, EdTech surveillance | Litigation, public education, research |
| Center for Democracy and Technology (CDT) | Student privacy policy | Research, policy analysis, advocacy |
| Parent Coalition for Student Privacy | FERPA reform, EdTech accountability | Parent organizing, legislative advocacy |
| Campaign for a Commercial-Free Childhood (Fairplay) | Children's digital rights | Campaigns, FTC complaints |
| Student Privacy Compass (FPII) | Student privacy resources | Research, state law tracking |
| ACLU | Civil liberties, school surveillance | Litigation, policy advocacy |
| National Disability Rights Network | Disability and surveillance intersection | Advocacy, legal assistance |

### Government Oversight

| Entity | Role | Effectiveness |
|--------|------|---------------|
| Department of Education (PTAC) | FERPA guidance, technical assistance | Guidance only; no enforcement power |
| Federal Trade Commission | COPPA enforcement; unfair practices | Limited education-specific jurisdiction |
| State Attorneys General | State law enforcement | Uneven; some active (CA, IL, NY) |
| Congressional committees | Legislative oversight | Hearings but no legislation passed |
| Government Accountability Office | Audits and reports | Informational; no enforcement |

## Stakeholder Power Analysis

| Stakeholder | Power Level | Resources | Organization | Influence on Policy |
|-------------|-------------|-----------|--------------|-------------------|
| EdTech companies | High | Large lobbying budgets; trade associations | Highly organized | Strong |
| Students | Very Low | No independent resources | Minimal formal organization | Negligible |
| Parents | Moderate | Volunteer-driven; diffuse | Episodic; effective when mobilized | Moderate when organized |
| Teachers/unions | Moderate | Union dues; collective bargaining | Well organized | Moderate |
| School districts | Moderate | Institutional resources | National associations (AASA, NSBA) | Moderate |
| Privacy advocates | Low-Moderate | Nonprofit budgets | Well organized but small | Moderate |
| Surveillance vendors | Moderate-High | Revenue-driven; safety narrative | Industry coalitions | Strong |
| Federal regulators | Low | Under-resourced; no clear authority | Institutional | Limited |

## Coalition Map

| Coalition | Members | Shared Interest | Strength |
|-----------|---------|-----------------|----------|
| **Privacy protection** | Parents, advocates, student groups, some educators | Stronger privacy laws, EdTech accountability | Growing; demonstrated effectiveness (inBloom) |
| **Industry self-regulation** | EdTech companies, trade associations | Voluntary pledges over legislation | Well-funded; credibility declining |
| **Safety-first** | Surveillance vendors, some administrators, security-focused policymakers | Surveillance expansion justified by safety | Strong federal funding support |
| **Evidence-based reform** | Researchers, some advocates, progressive educators | Data-driven policy, rigorous evaluation | Small but influential |
