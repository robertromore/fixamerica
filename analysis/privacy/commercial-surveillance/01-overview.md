# Commercial Surveillance: Overview

## Executive Summary

Commercial surveillance -- the systematic tracking, profiling, and monetization of human behavior by corporations -- has become the dominant business model of the internet. Companies including Google, Meta, Amazon, and thousands of lesser-known data intermediaries collect detailed information about billions of people's online and offline activities, constructing behavioral profiles of extraordinary granularity. This data is used to target advertising, manipulate consumer behavior, price discriminate, make consequential decisions about employment, housing, insurance, and credit, and increasingly to train artificial intelligence systems. The result is a surveillance infrastructure operated by private corporations that exceeds the capabilities of any government intelligence agency in history.

The United States is the only major democracy without a comprehensive federal privacy law governing commercial data practices. Instead, a patchwork of sector-specific federal laws (HIPAA for health data, GLBA for financial data, COPPA for children) and a growing number of state laws (led by California's CCPA/CPRA) provide inconsistent, incomplete protection. The Federal Trade Commission, the primary federal enforcer, relies on its general authority over "unfair or deceptive practices" -- a framework designed for fraudulent advertising, not systematic surveillance. Meanwhile, the surveillance advertising industry generates hundreds of billions of dollars annually, funding intense lobbying against meaningful regulation.

The consequences extend far beyond individual privacy. Commercial surveillance enables discrimination, undermines consumer autonomy, concentrates economic power, degrades democratic discourse through algorithmic manipulation, and creates data troves that governments can access through purchase, subpoena, or partnership. Addressing commercial surveillance requires understanding it not as a series of individual privacy violations but as a systemic economic and political structure that demands comprehensive reform.

### Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Surveillance capitalism** | Economic system in which human behavioral data is extracted, processed, and traded as a commodity for prediction and modification of behavior (term coined by Shoshana Zuboff) |
| **Behavioral advertising** | Advertising targeted to individuals based on observation and profiling of their behavior over time |
| **Real-time bidding (RTB)** | Automated auction system in which personal data is broadcast to hundreds of companies in milliseconds to determine which ad to display |
| **Dark patterns** | User interface designs that manipulate users into unintended actions, such as sharing more data or making purchases |
| **Cross-device tracking** | Techniques for linking user activity across multiple devices (phone, computer, tablet, TV) |
| **Fingerprinting** | Identifying users by collecting device and browser characteristics rather than traditional cookies |
| **Data minimization** | Principle that organizations should collect only data necessary for a specific stated purpose |
| **Purpose limitation** | Principle that data collected for one purpose should not be used for unrelated purposes without consent |
| **Privacy by design** | Approach requiring privacy protections to be built into systems from the outset rather than added after the fact |

## Core Tensions

- **Free services vs. privacy**: Surveillance-funded "free" services provide value but extract privacy as payment
- **Innovation vs. regulation**: Industry argues regulation stifles innovation; advocates argue innovation thrives under privacy constraints (EU evidence)
- **Individual consent vs. structural reform**: Consent-based frameworks place burden on individuals; structural approaches constrain corporate conduct
- **Federal uniformity vs. state innovation**: Industry wants single (weak) federal standard; advocates want states free to innovate
- **National security vs. commercial privacy**: Government agencies purchase commercial surveillance data to circumvent warrant requirements
- **Competition vs. privacy**: Large companies can absorb compliance costs; privacy regulation may entrench market leaders
- **Free speech vs. data regulation**: Industry claims data collection is protected speech; this argument conflates commercial conduct with expression

## Vision of Success

A reformed commercial surveillance landscape would feature:

- **Comprehensive federal privacy law**: Clear, enforceable rules governing all commercial data collection and use
- **Data minimization as default**: Companies collect only what is necessary for the service requested
- **End of surveillance advertising**: Shift to contextual advertising and subscription models that do not require behavioral profiling
- **Meaningful consent**: Informed, specific, and freely given -- not buried in unreadable privacy policies
- **Individual data rights**: Access, correction, deletion, portability, and the right to object to automated decisions
- **Strong enforcement**: Adequately resourced federal and state enforcement with meaningful penalties and private right of action
- **Algorithmic accountability**: Transparency and oversight of automated systems that make consequential decisions
- **Corporate accountability**: Data protection officers, mandatory audits, and executive liability for violations
