# Commercial Surveillance: History

## Origins

### The Pre-Internet Era

Commercial surveillance did not begin with the internet. Corporations have long sought to understand and influence consumer behavior, but the methods were limited by technology and logistics.

**Early Commercial Data Collection**

- **Mail-order catalogs (1800s)**: Companies like Sears and Montgomery Ward maintained customer purchase records
- **Credit bureaus (1899)**: The Retail Credit Company (later Equifax) began compiling consumer financial records
- **Market research (1920s-1950s)**: Surveys, focus groups, and Nielsen ratings tracked consumer behavior through sampling
- **Loyalty programs (1980s)**: Frequent flyer programs and store loyalty cards systematized purchase tracking
- **Direct marketing databases (1970s-1990s)**: Mailing list companies compiled demographic and consumer data for targeted advertising

**Key Difference from Digital Surveillance**

Pre-internet commercial data collection was limited by:

- Manual data entry and paper records
- Sample-based measurement (not universal)
- Physical constraints on observation
- Consumer awareness (people knew they were being surveyed)
- Technological inability to merge data sets at scale

### The Early Internet (1990-2000)

**The Original Internet Business Model Crisis**

The early internet had no established revenue model. Academic and government users expected free access. The first commercial websites experimented with subscription, transaction, and advertising models.

**Key Developments**

| Year | Development | Significance |
|------|-------------|--------------|
| 1993 | First clickable web ad (Global Network Navigator) | Advertising reaches the web |
| 1994 | First banner ad (HotWired/AT&T) | Display advertising model established |
| 1994 | Netscape introduces cookies | Technical foundation for tracking |
| 1995 | DoubleClick founded | First major ad network; cross-site tracking begins |
| 1996 | Yahoo! personalizes content | User profiling for content and advertising |
| 1998 | Google founded | Search advertising model that would dominate the industry |
| 1999 | DoubleClick-Abacus merger proposed | First major privacy controversy over combining online/offline data; FTC investigation; merger abandoned under pressure |
| 2000 | Dot-com crash | Ad-supported model survives; subscription models mostly fail |

**The Fateful Choice**

The dot-com crash of 2000 cemented advertising as the dominant internet business model. Subscription and transaction-based alternatives failed commercially. Companies that survived -- Google, Yahoo, and eventually Facebook -- were those that could offer "free" services funded by advertising. This created the structural incentive to maximize data collection: more data meant more targeted ads, which meant more revenue.

## Key Turning Points

### 2000-2004: The Search Advertising Revolution

**Google AdWords (2000)**

- Google introduced AdWords, enabling advertisers to bid on search keywords
- Initially not behaviorally targeted -- ads matched search queries (contextual)
- Revenue model proved massively profitable: $86 million in 2001, $3.2 billion by 2004

**The Shift to Behavioral Targeting**

- Google began tracking search history to improve ad targeting
- Gmail (2004) scanned email content for ad targeting (discontinued in 2017)
- Google Analytics (2005) gave websites free tracking tools in exchange for data flowing to Google

### 2004-2008: Social Media and the Attention Economy

**Facebook (2004-2008)**

- Facebook launched in 2004; opened to public in 2006
- Introduced News Feed in 2006, enabling algorithmic content curation
- Beacon program (2007) tracked user purchases on third-party sites; withdrawn after privacy backlash
- Facebook Platform (2007) opened user data to third-party developers -- setting up Cambridge Analytica

**The Attention Economy**

- Social platforms discovered that user engagement drives advertising revenue
- Algorithmic feeds replaced chronological display -- optimizing for time spent, not user satisfaction
- Network effects created winner-take-all dynamics, concentrating data in a few platforms

### 2008-2013: Mobile and the Surveillance Explosion

**Smartphones Transform Data Collection**

- iPhone (2007) and Android (2008) put always-on, always-connected sensors in users' pockets
- Location data became available continuously, not just during desktop sessions
- Mobile apps accessed contacts, photos, microphones, cameras, and location with minimal permission controls
- App store ecosystems created new data collection channels

**The Mobile Ad Market**

- Mobile advertising grew from negligible to $82 billion by 2016 (IAB)
- Location-based advertising enabled targeting based on physical behavior
- Cross-device tracking linked mobile, desktop, and tablet activity into unified profiles

**Programmatic Advertising and RTB**

- Real-time bidding (RTB) systems automated ad purchasing, broadcasting user data to hundreds of companies per impression
- The advertising technology ("adtech") ecosystem grew to thousands of intermediary companies
- Personal data became a tradable commodity exchanged billions of times daily

### 2013-2018: Revelations and Reckonings

**Snowden Disclosures (2013)**

- Edward Snowden revealed that NSA programs accessed commercial platform data (PRISM program)
- Demonstrated that commercial surveillance infrastructure could be repurposed for government surveillance
- Public awareness of surveillance increased, but commercial practices continued largely unchanged

**Cambridge Analytica (2016-2018)**

- Facebook's developer platform allowed Cambridge Analytica to harvest data on 87 million users
- Data used for political advertising and voter profiling in 2016 election and Brexit referendum
- Scandal became a global turning point in awareness of commercial surveillance practices
- Led to FTC investigation resulting in $5 billion fine (2019) -- largest privacy penalty in US history

**GDPR Enacted (2016, Effective 2018)**

- European Union adopted the General Data Protection Regulation
- Established comprehensive privacy framework with consent requirements, data rights, and substantial penalties
- Created global ripple effect -- companies adopted some protections worldwide
- Demonstrated that comprehensive privacy regulation was feasible without destroying the digital economy

### 2018-2022: The Reform Era

**California Consumer Privacy Act (2018, Effective 2020)**

- First comprehensive US state privacy law
- Gave consumers rights to know, delete, and opt out of sale of personal data
- Strengthened by California Privacy Rights Act (CPRA) ballot initiative in 2020
- Created the California Privacy Protection Agency -- first dedicated state privacy agency

**Apple's Privacy Turn (2021)**

- Apple introduced App Tracking Transparency (ATT), requiring apps to ask permission before tracking
- Meta estimated ATT cost the company $10 billion in revenue in 2022
- Demonstrated that technical privacy changes could disrupt the surveillance business model
- Industry responded with fingerprinting and other circumvention techniques

**Federal Legislative Attempts**

- American Data Privacy and Protection Act (ADPPA) passed House committee in 2022 with bipartisan support
- Stalled over preemption (California delegation opposed weakening CCPA) and private right of action (industry opposed; advocates insisted)
- Multiple other federal privacy bills introduced without advancing

### 2022-Present: AI, State Laws, and FTC Action

**The AI Data Hunger**

- Large language models and generative AI systems require massive training data sets
- Companies scraped websites, social media, and commercial data for AI training
- Privacy implications of AI training data remain largely unregulated
- FTC warned companies about using unfairly collected data for AI training

**State Law Proliferation**

- Number of states with comprehensive privacy laws grew from 5 (2022) to 19+ (January 2025)
- No two state laws are identical, creating a complex compliance landscape
- State laws generally weaker than GDPR -- most lack private right of action
- Industry increasingly supports federal preemption to replace state laws with weaker federal standard

**FTC Aggressive Enforcement**

- FTC pursued major cases against Meta, Amazon (Alexa, Ring), Epic Games ($520 million), and numerous smaller companies
- FTC proposed commercial surveillance rulemaking (ANPR, 2022)
- FTC Chair Lina Khan pursued structural remedies and algorithmic destruction orders (requiring companies to delete algorithms trained on illegally obtained data)

## Patterns and Lessons

### Recurring Themes

1. **"Free" as the trap**: The ad-supported model created structural incentives for surveillance that no individual company can unilaterally abandon
2. **Scandals drive awareness but not reform**: Cambridge Analytica, Snowden, and data breaches increase public concern without producing comprehensive legislation
3. **Self-regulation is a delay tactic**: Industry promises of self-regulation have consistently failed to produce meaningful privacy protections
4. **Technology evolves faster than law**: Each new technology (cookies, mobile, IoT, AI) creates new surveillance capabilities before regulators respond
5. **Concentrated benefits, diffuse harms**: Surveillance profits are concentrated among a few companies; privacy harms are spread across billions of people
6. **State innovation fills federal vacuum**: When Congress fails to act, states legislate -- creating a patchwork that industry then uses as argument for (weak) federal preemption

### What Has Changed

| Era | Primary Technology | Business Model | Regulatory Response |
|-----|-------------------|----------------|---------------------|
| 1990s | Desktop web, cookies | Banner ads | FTC self-regulation guidance |
| 2000s | Search, social media | Targeted search and social ads | Sector-specific laws (COPPA) |
| 2010s | Mobile, RTB, cross-device | Programmatic surveillance advertising | GDPR (EU); state laws begin (US) |
| 2020s | AI, IoT, connected vehicles | AI-powered surveillance and prediction | State law proliferation; FTC enforcement |

### What Has Not Changed

- No comprehensive federal commercial privacy law
- Surveillance advertising remains the dominant internet business model
- Industry successfully lobbies against meaningful regulation
- Consumer consent remains the primary (and failing) privacy mechanism
- The gap between stated privacy preferences and actual protections continues to widen
