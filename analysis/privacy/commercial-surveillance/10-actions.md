# Commercial Surveillance: Actions

## Overview

This document outlines concrete steps that individuals, communities, organizations, and institutions can take to challenge commercial surveillance and advance meaningful privacy reform. Actions range from personal protective measures to systemic advocacy that addresses the structural roots of surveillance capitalism.

## Individual Actions

### Protect Your Own Privacy

| Action | How | Impact |
|--------|-----|--------|
| **Use privacy-focused browsers and search engines** | Switch to Firefox, Brave, or Tor Browser; use DuckDuckGo or Startpage instead of Google | Reduces behavioral data feeding surveillance profiles |
| **Install tracker blockers** | Use uBlock Origin, Privacy Badger, or Ghostery to block advertising trackers and analytics scripts | Blocks 40-70 trackers per major website from collecting your data |
| **Use a VPN for internet activity** | Subscribe to a reputable, audited VPN provider (e.g., Mullvad, ProtonVPN) | Prevents ISPs and network operators from monitoring browsing activity |
| **Minimize app permissions** | Regularly review and revoke unnecessary app permissions for location, contacts, camera, and microphone | Reduces data leakage from mobile devices |
| **Opt out of data sales and sharing** | Exercise opt-out rights under CCPA, CPRA, and other state privacy laws using each company's privacy settings | Legally requires companies to stop selling or sharing your personal data |
| **Use privacy-respecting alternatives** | Switch to Signal (messaging), ProtonMail (email), Nextcloud (cloud storage), and OpenStreetMap (maps) | Removes data from surveillance company ecosystems |
| **Delete unused accounts** | Close accounts on platforms you no longer use; request data deletion | Reduces the data available for profiling and breach exposure |
| **Disable ad personalization** | Turn off ad personalization in Google, Meta, Amazon, Microsoft, and Apple account settings | Reduces behavioral profiling for ad targeting |
| **Use Global Privacy Control (GPC)** | Enable GPC in your browser or use a browser that supports it (Firefox, Brave) | Automatically signals opt-out of data sale/sharing to every website visited |

### Manage Your Data Footprint

| Action | How | Impact |
|--------|-----|--------|
| **Request your data from major platforms** | Submit data access requests to Google (takeout.google.com), Meta, Amazon, and other platforms | Understand the scope of data collected about you |
| **Remove yourself from data brokers** | Use services like DeleteMe or manually submit removal requests to data brokers (Spokeo, Whitepages, BeenVerified) | Reduces availability of personal information in commercial databases |
| **Read privacy policies critically** | Focus on sections covering data sharing, third-party access, and data retention -- not just the "we care about your privacy" preamble | Informed decisions about which services to use |
| **Audit your connected devices** | Review smart home devices, fitness trackers, connected vehicles, and IoT products for data collection practices | Reduce surveillance from physical environment devices |
| **Use strong, unique passwords with a password manager** | Use Bitwarden, 1Password, or KeePass to generate and store unique passwords for every account | Reduces data breach exposure when one service is compromised |

### When You Experience Privacy Violations

| Step | Action | Resource |
|------|--------|----------|
| 1 | **Document the violation** | Save screenshots, privacy policies, notification emails, and evidence of data misuse |
| 2 | **File a complaint with the FTC** | ftc.gov/complaint -- the FTC tracks complaints and uses them to identify enforcement targets |
| 3 | **File a state AG complaint** | Contact your state attorney general's consumer protection division |
| 4 | **File with the California Privacy Protection Agency** | cppa.ca.gov/complaints -- if California law applies to the company |
| 5 | **Report to privacy organizations** | Contact EFF, EPIC, or CDT with evidence of systemic violations |
| 6 | **Contact journalists** | Investigative reporters at The Markup, ProPublica, or local media cover privacy stories |
| 7 | **Consult an attorney** | Consumer privacy or class action attorney for potential legal action under state laws |

## Community Actions

### Education and Awareness

| Action | How | Target Audience |
|--------|-----|-----------------|
| **Host privacy education workshops** | Partner with libraries, community centers, senior centers, and schools to teach digital privacy basics | General public, especially vulnerable populations |
| **Organize "data detox" events** | Community events where participants learn to audit their digital footprint, adjust settings, and install privacy tools | All internet users |
| **Create multilingual privacy resources** | Develop privacy guides in languages commonly spoken in your community | Non-English-speaking communities |
| **Screen documentaries and host discussions** | Show *The Social Dilemma*, *The Great Hack*, or *Terms and Conditions May Apply* followed by discussion | General public |
| **Support digital literacy in schools** | Advocate for K-12 curriculum that includes understanding of surveillance business models, not just "online safety" | Students, parents, school boards |

### Local Advocacy

| Action | How | Target |
|--------|-----|--------|
| **Advocate for local government privacy policies** | Push city and county governments to adopt privacy-protective data practices and limit commercial surveillance tools | City council, county commissioners |
| **Challenge public-private surveillance partnerships** | Monitor and oppose arrangements where local government shares data with or purchases from commercial surveillance companies (e.g., Ring/police partnerships) | Local government, police oversight boards |
| **Demand transparency in government data purchases** | Require disclosure when local agencies purchase commercial surveillance data (location data, social media monitoring tools) | City council, transparency commissions |
| **Support local businesses that respect privacy** | Patronize and publicize businesses that do not require surveillance-linked loyalty programs or invasive data collection | Local business community |
| **Advocate for municipal broadband** | Community-owned internet service does not rely on surveillance advertising | City council, public utilities |

### Coalition Building

| Action | Partners | Goal |
|--------|----------|------|
| **Join or support national privacy organizations** | EFF, EPIC, CDT, ACLU, Consumer Reports, Accountable Tech | Amplify advocacy and fund litigation |
| **Build local privacy coalitions** | Civil liberties groups, consumer organizations, librarians, faith communities, tech workers | Unified local advocacy for privacy reform |
| **Connect with affected communities** | Immigrant communities targeted by location tracking, domestic violence survivors, LGBTQ+ individuals vulnerable to outing | Center affected voices in advocacy |
| **Engage with tech worker organizations** | Tech Workers Coalition, Coworker.org | Support internal advocacy within surveillance companies |
| **Partner with small business associations** | Local chambers of commerce, small business groups | Build cross-interest support for privacy reform as alternative to surveillance advertising dependency |

## Political and Advocacy Actions

### Federal Level

| Action | How | Target |
|--------|-----|--------|
| **Contact your members of Congress** | Call, write, and email in support of comprehensive federal privacy legislation with private right of action and no preemption of stronger state laws | Senators and representatives, especially on Commerce committees |
| **Submit public comments on FTC rulemaking** | Participate in FTC commercial surveillance rulemaking proceedings; submit personal testimony about how surveillance has affected you | FTC (regulations.gov) |
| **Testify at congressional hearings** | Share personal experiences with commercial surveillance harms | Congressional committees |
| **Support privacy-focused campaign contributions** | Donate to candidates who support strong privacy legislation and oppose industry lobbying | Federal candidates |
| **Track corporate lobbying** | Monitor tech company lobbying spending on OpenSecrets.org; share findings | Public, media |

### State Level

| Action | How | Target |
|--------|-----|--------|
| **Support comprehensive state privacy bills** | Testify at committee hearings, write letters, organize constituent support | State legislators |
| **Advocate for private right of action** | Insist that state privacy bills include the right for individuals to sue for violations, not just AG enforcement | State legislators |
| **Defend existing state privacy laws** | Oppose industry efforts to weaken existing state laws or preempt them with weaker federal legislation | State legislators, state AG |
| **Support state AG enforcement** | Urge your state attorney general to investigate and enforce against commercial surveillance abuses | State AG office |
| **Advocate for state data broker regulation** | Push for state data broker registration requirements and consumer rights | State legislators |

### Electoral Engagement

| Action | When | Impact |
|--------|------|--------|
| **Evaluate candidates on privacy positions** | Every election cycle | Electoral pressure for privacy reform |
| **Ask candidates about privacy at town halls** | Campaign events, town halls, debates | Raises issue salience with elected officials |
| **Support ballot initiatives for privacy rights** | When available (California CPRA was passed by ballot initiative) | Direct democracy on privacy |
| **Vote for privacy-supportive candidates at all levels** | Every election | Build pro-privacy governance |

## Professional Actions

### For Technologists

| Action | How | Impact |
|--------|-----|--------|
| **Advocate for privacy by design** | Push for data minimization, purpose limitation, and privacy-protective defaults in systems you build | Change practices from within |
| **Refuse to build surveillance systems** | Exercise professional ethics when asked to build invasive tracking, dark patterns, or discriminatory targeting | Personal integrity; industry norm-setting |
| **Support and use privacy-enhancing technologies** | Contribute to open-source privacy tools; implement differential privacy, encryption, and anonymization | Build technical alternatives to surveillance |
| **Blow the whistle on harmful practices** | Report illegal or unethical surveillance practices to regulators, journalists, or advocacy organizations | Expose hidden harms; build enforcement cases |
| **Participate in standards bodies** | Contribute to W3C, IETF, and NIST processes affecting privacy standards | Shape technical infrastructure toward privacy |

### For Business Owners

| Action | How | Impact |
|--------|-----|--------|
| **Adopt data minimization** | Collect only the data necessary for the service you provide; delete data when no longer needed | Reduce liability; build customer trust |
| **Use contextual advertising** | Switch from behavioral (surveillance-based) advertising to contextual advertising that targets content, not people | Demonstrate viability of privacy-respecting advertising |
| **Be transparent about data practices** | Provide clear, readable privacy disclosures; avoid dark patterns in consent interfaces | Build customer trust; prepare for regulation |
| **Support privacy legislation** | Join business coalitions supporting strong privacy law; counter industry lobbying narratives | Demonstrate that privacy and business can coexist |

### For Lawyers

| Action | How | Impact |
|--------|-----|--------|
| **Bring privacy enforcement cases** | Represent individuals and classes harmed by commercial surveillance under state privacy laws (CCPA, BIPA, state consumer protection statutes) | Legal precedent; deterrence |
| **Provide pro bono privacy representation** | Represent affected individuals who cannot afford legal counsel | Access to justice |
| **Challenge dark patterns and deceptive practices** | File FTC complaints and state AG referrals documenting manipulative design | Build enforcement record |
| **Draft model legislation** | Develop bill text for state and federal privacy legislation | Legislative infrastructure |

### For Researchers and Academics

| Action | How | Impact |
|--------|-----|--------|
| **Conduct surveillance audits** | Test and document commercial surveillance practices, tracking technologies, and data flows | Evidence base for reform |
| **Publish findings publicly** | Make research accessible to policymakers and the public, not just academic journals | Inform public debate |
| **Engage with policymakers** | Testify before legislatures, advise agencies, participate in standard-setting | Translate research into policy |
| **Study the economics of surveillance alternatives** | Research the viability of contextual advertising, subscription models, and cooperative alternatives | Counter industry claims that surveillance is economically necessary |

### For Journalists

| Action | How | Impact |
|--------|-----|--------|
| **Investigate commercial surveillance practices** | Document specific data collection practices, data flows, and consumer harms | Public awareness and accountability |
| **Cover privacy legislation** | Report on industry lobbying, legislative progress, and the real-world impact of privacy laws | Informed public debate |
| **Explain surveillance technology to the public** | Translate technical surveillance practices into understandable terms | Overcome information asymmetry |
