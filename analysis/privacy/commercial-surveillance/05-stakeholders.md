# Commercial Surveillance: Stakeholders

## Who Benefits from the Status Quo

### Platform Companies

**Who They Are**

- Alphabet/Google (search, advertising, Android, YouTube, cloud)
- Meta (Facebook, Instagram, WhatsApp, advertising)
- Amazon (e-commerce, Alexa, advertising, AWS, Ring)
- Apple (hardware, App Store, growing advertising business)
- Microsoft (LinkedIn, Bing, Windows, cloud)
- TikTok/ByteDance (short-form video, advertising)
- Snap, Pinterest, Twitter/X, Reddit (social platforms)

**What They Gain**

| Benefit | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| Advertising revenue | Surveillance data enables targeted advertising worth $225+ billion annually in the US (IAB, 2024) |
| Market dominance | Data advantages create moats against competitors |
| Product development | User data trains algorithms and improves services |
| Pricing power | Knowledge of user behavior enables personalized pricing |
| Strategic intelligence | User data reveals market trends, competitive dynamics |

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **Very High**

- Revenue model depends on data collection
- Compliance costs for comprehensive regulation would be significant
- Companies have invested billions in surveillance infrastructure
- Existential threat perception: some executives view privacy regulation as threatening core business model

### Advertising Industry

**Who They Are**

- Advertising networks (Google Ads, Meta Ads, Amazon Advertising)
- Advertising technology companies (The Trade Desk, LiveRamp, Criteo)
- Advertising agencies (WPP, Omnicom, Publicis, Interpublic)
- Demand-side platforms, supply-side platforms, data management platforms
- Industry associations (Interactive Advertising Bureau, Association of National Advertisers)

**What They Gain**

- Precise targeting capabilities that justify premium pricing
- Measurability: digital advertising can demonstrate ROI more precisely than traditional media
- Scale: automated systems can manage millions of campaigns simultaneously
- Client demand: advertisers prefer targeted over contextual placement

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **Very High**

- The entire advertising technology ecosystem depends on behavioral data
- Contextual advertising generates lower CPMs (cost per thousand impressions) than behavioral targeting
- Industry has organized through trade associations to oppose regulation

### Data Brokers

**Who They Are**

- 530+ companies registered in California alone (California AG Data Broker Registry, 2025)
- Major players: Acxiom (Interpublic), Experian (marketing services), Oracle Data Cloud (until wind-down), LexisNexis Risk Solutions
- People-search sites: Spokeo, Whitepages, BeenVerified
- Specialized brokers: health data, location data, financial data

**What They Gain**

- Revenue from aggregating and selling consumer data
- Commercial surveillance feeds their data supply
- They are both customers and suppliers within the surveillance ecosystem

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **Very High**

- Business model is entirely dependent on unrestricted data flow

### Businesses Using Surveillance Data

**Who They Are**

- Retailers using data for personalized marketing and pricing
- Insurance companies using data for risk assessment
- Financial institutions using alternative data for lending decisions
- Employers using surveillance data for hiring and monitoring
- Political campaigns using microtargeting for voter persuasion
- Real estate companies using data for tenant screening

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **Moderate**

- Depend on surveillance data but are not the primary collectors
- Some would benefit from clearer rules and reduced compliance uncertainty

## Who Is Harmed

### All Internet Users

**How They Are Harmed**

- Loss of autonomy: behavior observed and modified without awareness
- Manipulation: dark patterns, personalized pricing, engagement optimization
- Security risks: centralized data stores are breach targets
- Discrimination: algorithmic decisions based on surveillance data
- Chilling effects: awareness of surveillance changes behavior

**Scale of Impact**: Virtually all 330+ million Americans with internet access

### Marginalized Communities

**Disproportionate Harms**

| Community | Specific Harm | Evidence |
|-----------|---------------|----------|
| Racial minorities | Housing and employment ad discrimination | DOJ/HUD v. Meta (2019-2022); Lambrecht and Tucker research |
| Low-income individuals | Predatory ad targeting (payday loans, for-profit colleges) | FTC enforcement actions; Consumer Financial Protection Bureau reports |
| LGBTQ+ individuals | Outing through inferred data; targeted harassment | Data broker sale of Grindr location data (documented, 2022) |
| Immigrants | Location data sold to immigration enforcement | Georgetown Law, "American Dragnet" (2022) |
| People with health conditions | Health inference data used for discrimination | Duke University mental health data broker study (2023) |
| Domestic violence survivors | Location tracking and data broker exposure enable stalkers | FTC testimony; National Network to End Domestic Violence |

### Small Businesses

**How They Are Harmed**

- Forced to participate in surveillance advertising ecosystem to reach customers
- Platform dependency: small businesses depend on Google and Meta for advertising
- Data asymmetry: platforms know more about a small business's customers than the business does
- Competitive disadvantage: large companies can afford privacy compliance; small businesses cannot

### Democracy and Public Discourse

**Systemic Harms**

- Algorithmic amplification of outrage and misinformation degrades public discourse
- Microtargeted political advertising enables manipulation without public accountability
- Surveillance infrastructure can be used for political suppression
- Commercial data purchases by government circumvent constitutional protections
- Filter bubbles and echo chambers undermine shared factual understanding

## Who Has Power to Change the System

### Federal Government

**Congress**

- Can enact comprehensive federal privacy legislation
- Key committees: Senate Commerce (Cantwell, D-WA); House Energy and Commerce
- ADPPA precedent demonstrates bipartisan committee support
- Legislative gridlock has prevented floor action

**Federal Trade Commission**

- Primary federal privacy enforcer
- Current FTC has pursued aggressive enforcement and rulemaking
- Commissioner composition determines enforcement posture (subject to administration change)
- Resource limitations constrain effectiveness

**Executive Branch**

- Can issue executive orders on federal data practices
- Biden executive order on AI and data broker restrictions (2023-2024)
- Appoints FTC commissioners and other regulators

### State Governments

**State Legislatures**

- 19+ states have enacted comprehensive privacy laws
- California's CCPA/CPRA is the strongest US privacy law
- States serve as laboratories for federal policy

**State Attorneys General**

- California, Texas, New York, and other AGs have pursued privacy enforcement
- Multistate investigations and actions increasing
- Some AGs have dedicated privacy and technology units

### International Bodies

**European Union**

- GDPR enforcement actions set global precedents
- EU-US Data Privacy Framework governs transatlantic data flows
- Digital Services Act and Digital Markets Act expand regulatory tools
- EU regulatory actions force global changes in corporate behavior

**Other Jurisdictions**

- UK ICO enforcement and Age Appropriate Design Code
- Canadian privacy reform (proposed CPPA)
- Brazil, Japan, South Korea, India regulatory developments

### Civil Society

**Privacy Advocacy Organizations**

| Organization | Focus |
|--------------|-------|
| Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF) | Digital rights, surveillance opposition, litigation |
| Electronic Privacy Information Center (EPIC) | Privacy advocacy, FTC complaints, research |
| Center for Democracy and Technology (CDT) | Technology policy, privacy legislation |
| Public Knowledge | Public interest in technology policy |
| Access Now | International digital rights |
| American Civil Liberties Union (ACLU) | Civil liberties including privacy |
| Consumer Reports | Consumer privacy testing and advocacy |
| Accountable Tech | Platform accountability |

**Researchers and Academics**

- Shoshana Zuboff (Harvard): coined "surveillance capitalism" framework
- Woodrow Hartzog (Boston University): privacy law and design regulation
- Daniel Solove (George Washington): privacy law theory
- Julia Angwin (The Markup): investigative journalism on surveillance and algorithms

### Industry Actors with Potential for Change

**Apple**

- App Tracking Transparency demonstrated that technical privacy changes can disrupt surveillance
- Business model less dependent on advertising than competitors
- Privacy marketing creates competitive pressure on other platforms

**Subscription and Privacy-Focused Companies**

- DuckDuckGo, Proton, Signal: demonstrate viable privacy-respecting business models
- These companies have small market share but growing user bases

## Stakeholder Dynamics

### Power Imbalance

The fundamental dynamic in commercial surveillance is the radical power imbalance between surveillance companies and the people they surveil:

- **Financial resources**: The top 5 tech companies had combined revenue exceeding $1.5 trillion in 2023
- **Lobbying power**: Tech industry spends $70+ million annually on federal lobbying
- **Legal resources**: Armies of lawyers challenge every regulatory action
- **Information asymmetry**: Companies know everything about users; users know almost nothing about company practices
- **Market power**: Duopolies and monopolies in key markets eliminate competitive pressure for privacy
