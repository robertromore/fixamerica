# Commercial Surveillance: Solutions

## Guiding Principles

### Criteria for Evaluating Reforms

| Criterion | Definition | Why It Matters |
|-----------|------------|----------------|
| **Addresses structural incentives** | Changes the business model, not just individual practices | Surface-level fixes leave underlying incentives intact |
| **Shifts burden from individuals to companies** | Companies must justify data collection rather than individuals refusing it | Consent-based frameworks have failed; duty-based frameworks work |
| **Enforceable** | Clear standards with adequate enforcement resources and penalties | Rules without enforcement are suggestions |
| **Technologically adaptable** | Principles-based rather than technology-specific | Technology evolves faster than law |
| **Preserves state innovation** | Does not preempt stronger state protections | States have driven every US privacy advance |
| **Internationally compatible** | Aligned with GDPR and other global frameworks | Cross-border data flows require interoperability |
| **Includes private right of action** | Individuals can enforce their rights in court | Government enforcement alone is insufficient |

### The Central Insight

The failure of US commercial surveillance regulation is not a failure of specific rules but a failure of framework. The consent-based approach -- requiring individuals to agree to privacy policies -- has never worked and cannot work given the information asymmetry, power imbalance, and complexity of modern data practices.

The necessary shift is from **consent-based** to **duty-based** regulation: companies have legal duties regarding data regardless of what users click. This is the approach of the GDPR and the most effective state laws.

## Policy Proposals

### Tier 1: Comprehensive Federal Privacy Law

**The American Privacy Rights Act**

*Core Framework*

A comprehensive federal privacy law that establishes baseline privacy rights for all Americans, applies to all commercial data collection, and provides meaningful enforcement.

*Key Provisions*

| Provision | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Data minimization | Companies may collect only data reasonably necessary for the service requested |
| Purpose limitation | Data collected for one purpose may not be used for unrelated purposes |
| Individual rights | Right to access, correct, delete, port, and object to automated decisions |
| Consent reform | Meaningful, specific, informed consent -- no buried checkboxes |
| Sensitive data protections | Heightened protections for health, financial, biometric, location, and children's data |
| Algorithmic accountability | Right to explanation of automated decisions; impact assessments required |
| Data security | Mandatory security standards with breach notification |
| No preemption | Federal law sets floor; states can exceed |
| Private right of action | Statutory damages; class actions permitted |
| FTC enforcement | Enhanced authority, dedicated privacy division, increased budget |
| Dark patterns prohibition | Ban on manipulative design that undermines privacy choices |
| Data broker regulation | Registration, consent requirements, deletion rights |

*Advantages*

- Establishes clear national baseline
- Shifts burden from consumers to companies
- Aligns with international frameworks
- Creates meaningful accountability
- Preserves state innovation

*Challenges*

- Preemption deadlock has killed prior attempts (ADPPA)
- Industry will lobby aggressively against private right of action
- Definition disputes (what is "necessary"? what is "sensitive"?) require careful drafting
- Enforcement requires significant new federal investment

### Tier 2: Ban Surveillance Advertising

**Ending Behavioral Advertising Act**

*Core Requirement*

Prohibit advertising targeted based on behavioral surveillance -- the collection and use of personal data to profile individuals for advertising purposes. Permit contextual advertising (ads based on the content being viewed) and first-party advertising (ads based on a direct relationship between the advertiser and the consumer).

*Key Distinctions*

| Advertising Type | Permitted? | Why |
|-----------------|-----------|-----|
| Contextual | Yes | Based on content, not user surveillance |
| First-party | Yes | Based on direct customer relationship |
| Behavioral/targeted | No | Requires surveillance infrastructure |
| Retargeting | No | Requires cross-site tracking |
| Lookalike audiences | No | Requires profiling |

*Economic Impact*

- Surveillance-targeted advertising commands 2-4x the price of contextual advertising (various industry estimates)
- Contextual advertising generated the entire internet advertising industry before surveillance targeting became dominant
- Academic research suggests behavioral targeting adds only marginal value for advertisers (Marotta et al., *Journal of Marketing Research*, 2019)
- Publishers receive a small fraction of surveillance advertising revenue -- most goes to intermediaries (ISBA Programmatic Supply Chain Transparency Study, 2020)
- EU ban on targeted advertising to minors (Digital Services Act, 2024) has not collapsed the European ad market

*Advantages*

- Eliminates the economic incentive for mass data collection
- Preserves ad-supported internet through contextual advertising
- Reduces discrimination enabled by behavioral targeting
- Decreases security risk by reducing data accumulation
- Levels playing field for small advertisers who cannot afford behavioral targeting platforms

*Challenges*

- Industry will argue this destroys the "free internet" (contextual advertising can fund it)
- Revenue reduction for platforms during transition
- Defining the boundary between contextual and behavioral targeting requires precision
- Constitutional challenge possible (commercial speech) but likely fails under intermediate scrutiny

### Tier 3: Data Minimization and Purpose Limitation

**If comprehensive law is not immediately achievable, standalone data minimization requirements can significantly reduce commercial surveillance.**

*Core Requirements*

- Companies may collect only personal data reasonably necessary for the specific service or transaction requested by the consumer
- Data collected for one purpose may not be repurposed without specific, informed consent
- Data must be deleted when no longer necessary for the stated purpose
- Default settings must be privacy-protective (no pre-checked consent boxes, no dark patterns)

*Implementation*

- FTC promulgates rules defining "reasonably necessary" by industry sector
- Companies must document data collection justifications (data protection impact assessments)
- Annual audits for companies processing data of over 1 million individuals
- Third-party sharing requires separate, specific consent

### Tier 4: Individual Data Rights

**Consumer Data Rights Act**

*Rights Framework*

| Right | Description | Model |
|-------|-------------|-------|
| Access | Right to obtain copy of all data held about you | GDPR Article 15; CCPA |
| Correction | Right to correct inaccurate data | GDPR Article 16 |
| Deletion | Right to have data deleted | GDPR Article 17; CCPA |
| Portability | Right to receive data in usable format and transfer to another service | GDPR Article 20 |
| Objection | Right to object to data processing, including profiling | GDPR Article 21 |
| Non-discrimination | Companies cannot punish users who exercise privacy rights | CCPA |
| Explanation | Right to meaningful explanation of automated decisions | GDPR Article 22 |

*Implementation*

- Standard, machine-readable request formats (reduce compliance friction)
- 30-day response deadline
- No fees for exercising rights
- Prohibition on retaliatory service degradation
- Authorized agents can exercise rights on behalf of individuals

### Tier 5: Enforcement Infrastructure

**Options for Strengthening Enforcement**

| Option | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|--------|-----------|---------------|
| FTC Privacy Bureau | Leverages existing expertise; immediate implementation | Competes for FTC resources; subject to political composition changes |
| Standalone Federal Privacy Agency | Dedicated focus; independent authority; insulated from industry pressure | Requires new legislation and appropriation; slower to establish |
| Enhanced state AG authority | State-level enforcement; closer to affected communities | Inconsistent enforcement; resource variation |
| Private right of action | Supplements government enforcement; empowers individuals | Industry opposition; litigation costs |

*Recommended Approach: All of the above*

- Create FTC Privacy Bureau with dedicated staff (minimum 200 employees) and budget ($100+ million annually)
- Authorize state AGs to enforce federal privacy law
- Establish private right of action with statutory damages ($100-$1,000 per violation per consumer) and class action availability
- Index penalties to company annual revenue (up to 4% of global annual revenue, modeled on GDPR)
- Require large companies to fund independent privacy auditors

### Tier 6: Algorithmic Accountability

**Algorithmic Accountability Act**

*Core Requirements*

- Impact assessments for automated decision systems that affect housing, employment, credit, insurance, education, or criminal justice
- Transparency: companies must disclose when automated systems make consequential decisions
- Right to human review of automated decisions
- Prohibition on automated decisions based solely on profiling without human oversight
- Annual audits for bias, accuracy, and privacy compliance
- FTC enforcement authority

### Tier 7: Real-Time Bidding Reform

**RTB Transparency and Privacy Act**

*Core Requirements*

- Require explicit, informed consent before user data is included in RTB bid requests
- Limit data elements in bid requests to those strictly necessary
- Require all RTB participants to register with the FTC
- Prohibit retention of bid request data beyond the immediate auction
- Annual audits of RTB data practices
- Enforce data security standards for all participants in the RTB ecosystem

## Comparative Models

### European Union: GDPR

**Key Results (2018-2025)**

- Over 2,000 enforcement actions by data protection authorities
- Over 4 billion euros in cumulative fines
- Companies adopted privacy-protective practices globally to comply
- No evidence of innovation suppression: EU tech sector continues to grow
- Consent fatigue and enforcement inconsistency are documented weaknesses

**Lessons for the US**

- Comprehensive, principles-based regulation works
- Strong enforcement authority is essential
- Private right of action supplements government enforcement
- Data protection authorities need adequate funding
- "Consent fatigue" means consent alone is insufficient -- duty-based obligations are necessary

### California: CCPA/CPRA

**Key Results (2020-2025)**

- First comprehensive US privacy law
- California Privacy Protection Agency established as first state privacy agency
- Initial enforcement actions in 2024
- Opt-out model means most consumers remain unprotected (opt-out rates are low)
- Industry lobbied for and achieved partial private right of action (data breaches only)

**Lessons for the US Federal Law**

- Opt-out model is insufficient -- opt-in or duty-based approach needed
- Dedicated enforcement agency is essential
- Private right of action should be broader than data breaches
- No preemption: federal law should preserve California and other states' ability to exceed federal minimums

### Illinois: BIPA

**Key Results (2008-2025)**

- Most effective US privacy enforcement tool, entirely through private right of action
- $650 million Facebook settlement (2022); $228 million TikTok settlement (2023)
- Per-violation statutory damages create meaningful deterrence
- Companies changed biometric data practices across the US to comply

**Lessons**

- Private right of action with statutory damages produces more enforcement than government agencies alone
- Companies respond to financial liability more than regulatory guidance
- Other states are replicating BIPA model for biometric and broader privacy data
