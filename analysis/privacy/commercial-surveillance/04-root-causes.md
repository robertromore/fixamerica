# Commercial Surveillance: Root Causes

## Structural Causes

### The Surveillance Advertising Business Model

The most fundamental cause of commercial surveillance is the economic model that dominates the internet. Surveillance advertising -- tracking user behavior to target ads -- generates hundreds of billions of dollars annually and funds the "free" services that most Americans use daily.

**Why Surveillance Advertising Dominates**

| Factor | Explanation |
|--------|-------------|
| Network effects | Platforms with the most users attract the most advertisers; winner-take-all dynamics |
| Consumer expectations of "free" | Users accustomed to free services will not pay for alternatives |
| Data advantages | More data enables more precise targeting, which commands higher ad prices |
| Lock-in | Switching costs (social networks, email, documents) keep users on surveilling platforms |
| Advertiser demand | Advertisers prefer measurable, targeted advertising over contextual alternatives |
| Investor expectations | Markets reward growth in users and engagement, which require data collection |

**The Structural Trap**

Even companies and executives who recognize privacy concerns cannot unilaterally abandon surveillance advertising without competitive disadvantage. Google cannot stop collecting search data while competitors do not. Meta cannot stop profiling users while TikTok offers similar targeting to advertisers. The business model is structurally embedded -- changing it requires either regulation that applies to all companies or a fundamental market shift.

### Information Asymmetry

The most striking feature of commercial surveillance is the asymmetry between what companies know about users and what users know about companies.

**What Companies Know About Users**

- Detailed behavioral profiles with hundreds or thousands of data points
- Real-time location, browsing behavior, purchase history, social connections
- Inferred characteristics: health conditions, political views, sexual orientation, financial status
- Predictive models: likelihood to purchase, vote, churn, develop health conditions

**What Users Know About Companies**

- Privacy policies averaging 4,000+ words that few read and fewer understand (McDonald and Cranor, Carnegie Mellon, 2019)
- Vague, misleading language ("we may share your information with partners")
- No meaningful visibility into how data is actually used
- No knowledge of who buys their data or what decisions are made about them

### The Consent Fiction

Modern privacy frameworks rely on "consent" as the primary legal basis for data collection. In practice, consent in the commercial surveillance context is a fiction.

**Why Consent Fails**

| Problem | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Comprehension | Privacy policies are unreadable -- it would take 76 workdays per year to read all policies a typical user encounters (McDonald and Cranor, 2008) |
| Voluntariness | Consent is not freely given when the alternative is losing access to essential services (email, maps, social connection) |
| Granularity | Take-it-or-leave-it consent bundles hundreds of data uses into a single "I agree" |
| Revocability | Even when users withdraw consent, data already collected persists across the ecosystem |
| Dark patterns | Design techniques manipulate users into consenting (e.g., making "accept all" prominent while hiding alternatives) |
| Power imbalance | Individual users negotiate against billion-dollar corporations with teams of lawyers |

**The Regulatory Consequence**

Because consent is the primary legal basis for data collection in the US, and because consent in practice is meaningless, companies can claim legal authority to collect virtually any data from users who click "I agree." This is the central failure of the current framework.

### Winner-Take-All Market Dynamics

Commercial surveillance is characterized by extreme market concentration, which both drives and results from data accumulation.

**Concentration Metrics**

- Google controls ~91% of US search (StatCounter, 2024), ~65% of web browsers (Chrome), ~44% of mobile OS (Android)
- Meta controls the dominant social platform (Facebook) and dominant photo/video social platform (Instagram)
- Amazon controls ~38% of US e-commerce (eMarketer, 2024)
- These companies' dominance is self-reinforcing: more users generate more data, enabling better targeting, attracting more advertisers, funding better services, attracting more users

**Privacy Implications of Concentration**

- Users cannot "vote with their feet" when there are no meaningful alternatives
- Network effects make switching costly (social networks) or impossible (search quality depends on data scale)
- Concentrated platforms become essential infrastructure -- opting out means opting out of modern life
- Market power enables platforms to set privacy terms unilaterally

## Political Causes

### The Federal Legislative Vacuum

The most direct political cause of commercial surveillance is the absence of comprehensive federal privacy legislation. The US is the only major democracy without one.

**Why Congress Has Not Acted**

| Factor | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Industry lobbying | Tech companies and trade associations spend $70+ million annually on federal lobbying (OpenSecrets, 2023) |
| Preemption deadlock | California delegation opposes federal preemption of CCPA; industry demands it as condition of support |
| Private right of action dispute | Privacy advocates insist on private right of action; industry absolutely opposes it |
| Partisan disagreement on scope | Republicans generally prefer weaker federal law; Democrats generally prefer stronger protections |
| Complexity | Privacy legislation involves complex technical and legal issues that many members do not understand |
| Section 230 entanglement | Some members link privacy reform to broader platform regulation debates |
| Revolving door | Former congressional staff and regulators join industry; industry alumni join government |

**The ADPPA Case Study**

The American Data Privacy and Protection Act (2022) came closest to passage of any comprehensive federal privacy bill. It passed the House Energy and Commerce Committee with bipartisan support but died because:

- California's congressional delegation opposed preemption of the CCPA/CPRA
- Industry opposed the private right of action
- Senate Commerce Committee Chair had competing priorities
- House leadership did not prioritize floor time

### FTC Structural Limitations

The FTC is the primary federal privacy enforcer but operates under fundamental structural constraints.

**Limitations**

| Constraint | Impact |
|------------|--------|
| No privacy-specific statute | Relies on general "unfair or deceptive practices" authority (FTC Act Section 5) |
| No rulemaking authority (historically) | Could not promulgate privacy rules until recent assertions of Magnuson-Moss authority |
| Budget constraints | ~$430 million total budget (FY 2024) to regulate multi-trillion-dollar industries |
| Commissioner structure | 5 commissioners; partisan composition can shift enforcement priorities dramatically |
| Case-by-case enforcement | Cannot set comprehensive standards; each case is resource-intensive |
| Limited penalties | Fines are often insignificant relative to company revenues (though improving) |
| No criminal authority | Cannot pursue criminal prosecution for privacy violations |

### Industry Capture of Policy Process

The technology industry has systematically shaped the policy environment to prevent meaningful regulation.

**Mechanisms of Influence**

- **Lobbying**: Direct spending on congressional and state lobbying
- **Revolving door**: Industry hires former regulators; former industry executives serve in government
- **Academic funding**: Industry funds university research programs that produce favorable findings
- **Think tank funding**: Industry-funded think tanks produce policy recommendations opposing regulation
- **Standards capture**: Industry participates in standards bodies, shaping technical standards to preserve surveillance capabilities
- **Astroturf campaigns**: Industry funds seemingly grassroots organizations that oppose privacy regulation
- **Litigation**: Industry trade groups (NetChoice, CCIA) challenge state privacy laws in court

## Cultural Causes

### The "Nothing to Hide" Fallacy

A persistent cultural belief holds that privacy concerns are only relevant to those with something illicit to conceal. This fallacy undermines political support for privacy regulation.

**Why "Nothing to Hide" Is Wrong**

- Privacy is essential for autonomy, self-development, and freedom from manipulation
- Even innocent data can be used for discrimination, manipulation, and surveillance
- The question is not whether individuals have secrets but whether corporations should have unchecked power to observe and manipulate behavior
- "Nothing to hide" assumes benign corporate intent and competent data security -- neither is assured
- As Daniel Solove argues, privacy harms are often structural and social, not individual (Solove, "Nothing to Hide," 2011)

### Normalization of Surveillance

After decades of commercial surveillance, many Americans have internalized tracking as normal and inevitable.

**Contributing Factors**

- "Free" services create the perception that data collection is a fair exchange
- Pervasive tracking has been present since childhood for younger generations
- Privacy fatigue: the feeling that resistance is futile
- Lack of meaningful alternatives normalizes the surveillance model
- Media framing often treats privacy violations as individual failures ("why did you click accept?") rather than systemic problems

### The Convenience Trade-Off

Commercial surveillance is intertwined with genuine convenience. Targeted recommendations, personalized services, and "free" access create real value that makes people reluctant to demand change.

**The False Choice**

The framing of "privacy vs. convenience" is itself an industry construction. The choice is not between surveillance-funded free services and no services at all. Alternative models exist:

- Contextual advertising generates revenue without behavioral surveillance
- Subscription models fund services without data extraction
- Public and cooperative alternatives can provide services outside the profit motive
- The EU operates under GDPR without losing access to digital services

## Technological Causes

### Tracking Technology Evolution

Commercial surveillance is enabled by continuously evolving tracking technologies that outpace regulatory and consumer understanding.

**Technology Progression**

| Era | Technology | What It Tracks | Countermeasure | Industry Response |
|-----|-----------|----------------|----------------|-------------------|
| 1990s | Cookies | Website visits | Cookie blockers | Third-party cookies |
| 2000s | Web beacons/pixels | Cross-site behavior | Ad blockers | Embedded trackers |
| 2010s | Device fingerprinting | Device characteristics | Anti-fingerprinting | Probabilistic matching |
| 2010s | Mobile SDKs | App behavior, location | Permission controls | Background collection |
| 2020s | Server-side tracking | Bypasses browser controls | Limited | Conversions API, first-party data |
| 2020s | AI inference | Behavioral predictions | None effective | Predictive profiling without explicit data |

**The Arms Race**

Each privacy-protective technology is met with a tracking circumvention. When browsers blocked third-party cookies, industry developed fingerprinting. When Apple introduced ATT, industry developed server-side tracking and probabilistic matching. When users adopted VPNs, industry used first-party data and logged-in identification. This arms race will continue indefinitely without structural regulation.

### Data Infrastructure Lock-In

The physical and digital infrastructure of the internet has been built around surveillance.

- **Ad-supported internet architecture**: Most websites depend on advertising revenue, requiring tracking infrastructure
- **Cloud services**: Major cloud providers (AWS, Google Cloud, Azure) are also surveillance companies
- **Mobile OS duopoly**: Android (Google) and iOS (Apple) control the two mobile platforms, setting the terms of tracking
- **Payment infrastructure**: Credit card companies and payment processors share transaction data for targeting
- **Physical infrastructure**: Smart city sensors, connected vehicles, and IoT devices extend surveillance into the physical world
