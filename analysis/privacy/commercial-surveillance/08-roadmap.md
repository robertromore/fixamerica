# Commercial Surveillance: Roadmap

## Phase 1: Immediate Actions (2025-2026)

### FTC Rulemaking

**Advance Commercial Surveillance Rule**

The FTC's Advanced Notice of Proposed Rulemaking on commercial surveillance and data security (2022) laid groundwork for comprehensive rulemaking under existing authority.

**Priority Regulatory Actions**

| Action | Authority | Timeline |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| Data minimization requirements | FTC Act Section 5 (unfair practices) | NPRM 2025; final rule 2026 |
| Dark patterns prohibition | FTC Act Section 5 (deceptive practices) | NPRM 2025; final rule 2026 |
| Data security standards | FTC Act Section 5 + Safeguards Rule authority | 2025-2026 |
| Consent standard strengthening | FTC Act Section 5 | 2025-2026 |
| Data broker registration | Potential new rulemaking | 2026 |

**Enforcement Priorities**

- Pursue major enforcement actions against companies with the most egregious surveillance practices
- Target RTB data practices as unfair under Section 5
- Enforce against dark patterns that manufacture consent
- Issue guidance on AI training data and privacy
- Coordinate with state AGs for maximum impact

### Congressional Action

**Build on ADPPA Precedent**

The ADPPA's bipartisan committee passage in 2022 demonstrated that comprehensive federal privacy legislation can attract bipartisan support. The roadblock was preemption and private right of action.

**Strategy for Resolution**

| Issue | Proposed Resolution |
|-------|---------------------|
| Preemption | Federal law sets floor; explicit preservation of state authority to exceed federal minimum. Alternatively, time-limited preemption (3 years) after which states may exceed federal standards |
| Private right of action | Include with reasonable guardrails: 60-day cure period, statutory damages cap ($500-$1,000/violation), court fee-shifting for frivolous claims |
| Enforcement funding | Dedicated appropriation for FTC privacy division; no reliance on general FTC budget |
| Scope | Cover all commercial data processing with heightened requirements for sensitive data |

### State-Level Advocacy

**Defend Existing Laws**

- Support California CPPA enforcement and defend against industry challenges
- Monitor and support enforcement under new state laws (Texas, Oregon, Delaware, others)
- Defend state laws against NetChoice and industry litigation

**Expand State Protections**

| Target States | Opportunity |
|---------------|-------------|
| New York | Strong consumer protection tradition; active AG; pending comprehensive privacy bill |
| Illinois | BIPA success; legislature receptive to privacy expansion |
| Washington | Tech industry presence; failed in prior sessions; renewed legislative interest |
| Massachusetts | Strong consumer protection; progressive legislature |
| Pennsylvania | Large state; bipartisan privacy interest |

### International Coordination

- Support EU-US Data Privacy Framework and push for strengthening
- Pursue mutual recognition of privacy standards with UK, Canada, Japan
- Participate in OECD and G7 privacy initiatives
- Ensure US privacy reforms are compatible with GDPR to facilitate cross-border data flows

## Phase 2: Short-Term Goals (2026-2028)

### Federal Legislation

**Enact Comprehensive Federal Privacy Law**

If comprehensive law passes, implementation priorities:

- FTC establishes Privacy Bureau with minimum 200 dedicated staff
- FTC promulgates implementing regulations within 18 months
- Companies with over 1 million users must comply within 1 year; smaller companies within 2 years
- State AG enforcement authority activated immediately

**If Comprehensive Law Stalls, Pursue Modular Approach**

| Module | Priority | Coalition |
|--------|----------|-----------|
| Data minimization act | High | Bipartisan -- addresses surveillance without banning advertising |
| Algorithmic accountability act | High | Bipartisan -- addresses AI concerns |
| Dark patterns prohibition | High | Bipartisan -- consumer protection framing |
| Data broker registration act | Moderate | Bipartisan -- transparency is broadly supported |
| Surveillance advertising restrictions | Moderate | More partisan -- stronger industry opposition |

**Budget Requirements**

| Component | Estimated Annual Cost | Source |
|-----------|----------------------|--------|
| FTC Privacy Bureau (200 staff) | $60-80 million | Federal appropriation |
| State AG enforcement grants | $20 million | Federal grants |
| Research and standards (NIST) | $10 million | Federal appropriation |
| Public education | $5 million | FTC |
| Total | $95-115 million | Federal appropriation |

### Enforcement Ramp-Up

**Federal**

- FTC Privacy Bureau becomes operational
- First major enforcement actions under new authority
- Guidance documents for key industries
- Coordination protocol with state AGs

**State**

- Multistate AG privacy enforcement working group
- Coordinated investigations of largest surveillance companies
- Shared resources for technical analysis and litigation

### Industry Transition Support

**For Legitimate Business Interests**

- Develop standards for privacy-preserving advertising measurement
- NIST develops technical standards for data minimization and purpose limitation
- Safe harbor provisions for companies demonstrating good-faith compliance during transition
- Small business compliance assistance programs

### RTB Reform

**Immediate Steps**

- FTC enforcement action against RTB practices as unfair under Section 5
- Require bid request data minimization
- Registration requirement for all RTB participants
- Ban on retention of bid request data beyond immediate auction

## Phase 3: Medium-Term Goals (2028-2032)

### Structural Reforms

**Transition Away from Surveillance Advertising**

If an advertising ban is enacted or privacy regulation sufficiently constrains surveillance:

- Industry transitions to contextual and first-party advertising models
- Privacy-preserving measurement technologies mature
- Subscription and hybrid models become more common
- Public alternatives to commercial platforms emerge for essential services

**Federal Privacy Agency**

- Establish standalone federal privacy agency modeled on CFPB or European data protection authorities
- Independent from FTC -- dedicated mission, dedicated budget, dedicated expertise
- Rulemaking, enforcement, research, and public education functions
- International cooperation agreements with EU DPAs, UK ICO, and others

**Algorithmic Accountability Implementation**

- Mandatory impact assessments for automated decision systems in consequential domains
- Public registry of automated decision systems
- Right to human review implemented across industries
- Regular audits for bias and accuracy

### Evaluation and Adjustment

**Measure Effectiveness**

| Metric | Baseline (2025) | Target (2032) |
|--------|-----------------|---------------|
| Data breaches (records exposed) | 1.8 billion (2023) | Reduce by 75% |
| Consumer awareness of privacy rights | ~30% | 70% |
| Companies with data protection impact assessments | <5% | 90% (for covered entities) |
| FTC privacy enforcement actions per year | ~10-15 | 50+ |
| RTB data elements per bid request | Hundreds | Limited to essentials |
| Behavioral advertising market share | ~70% of digital ads | <20% |

**Adjust Framework**

- Commission independent evaluation of reform effectiveness after 5 years
- Sunset and review provisions for all regulatory frameworks
- Regular Congressional reports on privacy landscape
- Technology-neutral framework updates every 5 years

## Contingency Planning

### If Federal Legislation Stalls (Again)

- Accelerate state-level advocacy, targeting the 20+ states without privacy laws
- Maximize FTC rulemaking under existing authority
- Support state AG enforcement actions
- Build toward critical mass of state laws creating de facto national standard
- Pursue international pressure through EU adequacy determinations

### If FTC Rulemaking Is Blocked

- Challenge through litigation if blockage is procedural
- If new administration reverses FTC direction, focus on state enforcement and legislation
- Congressional oversight hearings to document industry practices
- Support private litigation under state laws with private right of action

### If Courts Strike Down Privacy Regulations

- Redesign regulations to survive constitutional challenge (economic regulation framing)
- Congressional action to create clear statutory authority
- Constitutional amendment as long-term option for establishing affirmative privacy right
- Focus on enforcement approaches (unfair practices) rather than content-based restrictions

### If Industry Successfully Self-Regulates

- Evaluate honestly -- credit genuine progress
- Maintain legislative readiness as backup
- Establish independent monitoring to verify industry claims
- History strongly suggests self-regulation alone will not be sufficient
