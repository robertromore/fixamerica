# Commercial Surveillance: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Addressing commercial surveillance requires legislative action at the federal and state levels to establish comprehensive privacy rights, data minimization duties, surveillance advertising reform, enforcement infrastructure, and individual remedies. The United States remains the only major democracy without a comprehensive commercial privacy law. This document provides draft legislative text for priority reforms, drawing on the analysis in preceding files and comparative models from the GDPR, CCPA/CPRA, and Illinois BIPA.

## Constitutional Amendments

### Right to Privacy Amendment

**Purpose**: Establish an affirmative constitutional right to privacy that applies to both government and commercial actors, providing a durable foundation for privacy legislation that cannot be weakened by statutory rollback or narrow judicial interpretation.

**Draft Text**:

```text
Section 1. The right of the people to privacy in their persons,
papers, data, communications, and effects shall not be violated
by any government entity or private actor operating in commerce.

Section 2. Every person shall have the right to control the
collection, use, retention, and disclosure of their personal
data. No entity shall collect, retain, or use personal data
beyond what is reasonably necessary for a purpose to which the
person has given informed, specific, and voluntary consent.

Section 3. Congress and the several States shall have
concurrent power to enforce this article by appropriate
legislation. State laws providing greater protection to
individual privacy shall not be preempted by federal
legislation enacted under this section.

Section 4. This article shall take effect two years after the
date of ratification.
```

**Explanation**:

- Section 1 establishes privacy as a fundamental right applicable to both government and commercial conduct, closing the gap in existing Fourth Amendment jurisprudence that applies only to government action
- Section 2 creates an affirmative right to data control and establishes data minimization and meaningful consent as constitutional standards
- Section 3 grants concurrent federal and state enforcement power and explicitly prevents federal preemption of stronger state protections, resolving the persistent preemption deadlock
- Section 4 provides a two-year implementation period

**Challenges**:

- Constitutional amendment requires two-thirds of both chambers of Congress and three-fourths of state legislatures -- an extremely high political bar
- Industry will argue that applying privacy rights to private actors is unprecedented (though labor, civil rights, and public accommodation amendments provide precedent)
- "Reasonably necessary" and "informed, specific, and voluntary consent" will require judicial interpretation
- This is a long-term goal; statutory reforms should proceed in parallel

## Federal Legislation

### American Privacy Rights Act

**Purpose**: Establish comprehensive federal privacy law governing all commercial data collection, processing, sharing, and use, with strong individual rights, enforceable duties on companies, meaningful penalties, and preservation of state authority.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "American Privacy Rights Act
of [Year]".

SEC. 2. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:

(1) PERSONAL DATA.—The term "personal data" means any
    information that identifies, relates to, describes, is
    reasonably capable of being associated with, or could
    reasonably be linked, directly or indirectly, with a
    particular individual or household, including but not
    limited to—
    (A) identifiers such as name, postal address, email
        address, IP address, account name, Social Security
        number, driver's license number, or passport number;
    (B) commercial information including records of personal
        property, products or services purchased, obtained,
        or considered, or other purchasing or consuming
        histories or tendencies;
    (C) internet or other electronic network activity
        information including browsing history, search
        history, and information regarding interaction with
        websites, applications, or advertisements;
    (D) geolocation data;
    (E) audio, electronic, visual, thermal, olfactory, or
        similar information;
    (F) professional or employment-related information;
    (G) inferences drawn from any of the above to create a
        profile reflecting preferences, characteristics,
        psychological trends, predispositions, behavior,
        attitudes, intelligence, abilities, or aptitudes.

(2) SENSITIVE DATA.—The term "sensitive data" means—
    (A) data revealing racial or ethnic origin, religious
        beliefs, mental or physical health conditions or
        diagnoses, sexual orientation or sexual behavior,
        gender identity, or citizenship or immigration status;
    (B) genetic data or biometric data used for identification;
    (C) precise geolocation data (within 1,750 feet);
    (D) personal data of a known child under 17;
    (E) private communications content;
    (F) financial account numbers, including bank account,
        debit card, or credit card numbers;
    (G) Social Security numbers, passport numbers, and
        government-issued identification numbers;
    (H) log-in credentials.

(3) COVERED ENTITY.—The term "covered entity" means any
    person or entity that—
    (A) collects, processes, retains, or transfers personal
        data in interstate commerce; and
    (B) is not a small business as defined in subsection (4).

(4) SMALL BUSINESS.—The term "small business" means an entity
    that—
    (A) had average annual gross revenues of not more than
        $25,000,000 during the preceding 3 calendar years;
    (B) collected, processed, or transferred the personal
        data of not more than 50,000 individuals during the
        preceding calendar year; and
    (C) does not derive more than 50 percent of revenue from
        transferring personal data.

(5) DATA BROKER.—The term "data broker" means an entity that
    knowingly collects, maintains, or transfers the personal
    data of individuals with whom the entity does not have a
    direct relationship.

(6) TARGETED ADVERTISING.—The term "targeted advertising"
    means advertising directed to an individual based on
    personal data obtained from the individual's activity
    across distinct websites, applications, or services not
    operated by the advertiser.

SEC. 3. DATA MINIMIZATION AND PURPOSE LIMITATION.

(a) DATA MINIMIZATION.—A covered entity may not collect,
    process, or transfer personal data unless such collection,
    processing, or transfer is—
    (1) limited to what is reasonably necessary and
        proportionate to provide or maintain a specific
        product or service requested by the individual; or
    (2) reasonably necessary to carry out one of the
        permissible purposes enumerated in subsection (c).

(b) PURPOSE LIMITATION.—A covered entity that collects personal
    data for a stated purpose may not use such data for a
    materially different purpose without obtaining the
    individual's affirmative express consent.

(c) PERMISSIBLE PURPOSES.—The permissible purposes are—
    (1) completing a transaction or fulfilling an order
        requested by the individual;
    (2) performing a contract to which the individual is
        a party;
    (3) protecting against security incidents, fraud, or
        illegal activity;
    (4) preventing injury to the individual or others where
        there is a serious and imminent threat;
    (5) complying with a legal obligation;
    (6) conducting internal research to improve services
        requested by the individual, provided the data is
        not transferred to third parties;
    (7) providing a first-party advertising impression
        based on the context of the current interaction.

(d) DATA RETENTION.—A covered entity shall not retain personal
    data longer than reasonably necessary for the permissible
    purpose for which it was collected, and in no event longer
    than 3 years after the individual's last interaction with
    the entity, unless a longer period is required by law.

SEC. 4. INDIVIDUAL RIGHTS.

(a) RIGHT TO ACCESS.—An individual has the right to obtain
    from a covered entity confirmation of whether personal
    data concerning the individual is being processed, and
    access to such data in a portable, machine-readable
    format.

(b) RIGHT TO CORRECTION.—An individual has the right to
    obtain correction of inaccurate personal data.

(c) RIGHT TO DELETION.—An individual has the right to obtain
    deletion of personal data, except where retention is
    required by law or necessary for a permissible purpose
    under Section 3(c)(3)-(5).

(d) RIGHT TO PORTABILITY.—An individual has the right to
    receive personal data in a structured, commonly used,
    and machine-readable format, and to transmit such data
    to another entity.

(e) RIGHT TO OPT OUT OF TARGETED ADVERTISING.—An individual
    has the right to opt out of targeted advertising at any
    time, and such opt-out shall be effectuated within 15
    days.

(f) RIGHT TO OPT OUT OF DATA TRANSFERS.—An individual has
    the right to opt out of transfers of personal data to
    third parties, except transfers necessary for a
    permissible purpose under Section 3(c).

(g) RIGHT TO OPT OUT OF PROFILING.—An individual has the
    right to opt out of profiling in furtherance of decisions
    that produce legal effects or similarly significant
    effects concerning the individual.

(h) UNIVERSAL OPT-OUT MECHANISM.—Covered entities shall
    recognize and comply with universal opt-out signals,
    including Global Privacy Control, browser-based
    preference signals, and any mechanism designated by
    the Commission.

(i) NON-DISCRIMINATION.—A covered entity shall not
    discriminate against an individual for exercising any
    right under this section, including by denying service,
    charging different prices, or providing a different
    level or quality of service.

(j) RESPONSE REQUIREMENTS.—A covered entity shall respond
    to requests under this section within 30 days of receipt,
    free of charge.

SEC. 5. SENSITIVE DATA PROTECTIONS.

(a) OPT-IN CONSENT REQUIRED.—A covered entity may not
    collect, process, or transfer sensitive data without
    first obtaining the individual's affirmative express
    consent through a clear, conspicuous, and specific
    mechanism that—
    (1) identifies the categories of sensitive data to be
        collected;
    (2) describes the purpose for which the data will be used;
    (3) identifies any third parties to which the data may
        be transferred; and
    (4) informs the individual of the right to withdraw
        consent at any time.

(b) CHILDREN'S DATA.—With respect to personal data of a
    known child under 17—
    (1) targeted advertising directed to the child is
        prohibited;
    (2) collection shall be limited to data strictly
        necessary for the service;
    (3) design features that maximize time spent or
        encourage compulsive use by children are prohibited.

SEC. 6. DARK PATTERNS PROHIBITION.

(a) PROHIBITION.—A covered entity shall not use any dark
    pattern to—
    (1) obtain consent or opt-in from an individual;
    (2) discourage an individual from exercising rights
        under this Act;
    (3) manipulate an individual into sharing more data
        than is necessary for the requested service.

(b) DEFINITION.—The term "dark pattern" means a user
    interface designed or manipulated with the substantial
    effect of subverting or impairing user autonomy,
    decision-making, or choice, including but not limited to—
    (1) asymmetric design that makes privacy-protective
        choices more difficult than data-sharing choices;
    (2) nagging or repeated prompts to accept data collection
        after a user has declined;
    (3) hidden or obscured privacy controls;
    (4) forced continuity or default enrollment;
    (5) preselected consent options.

SEC. 7. DATA BROKER REGULATION.

(a) REGISTRATION.—Each data broker shall register with the
    Commission annually and provide—
    (1) a description of the categories of personal data
        collected and maintained;
    (2) the sources of such data;
    (3) the categories of entities to which data is
        transferred;
    (4) the purposes for which data is transferred; and
    (5) the number of individuals whose data is maintained.

(b) INDIVIDUAL RIGHTS.—Each data broker shall—
    (1) provide individuals with the right to access,
        correct, and delete their personal data;
    (2) honor opt-out requests within 15 days; and
    (3) provide a publicly accessible, toll-free mechanism
        for exercising these rights.

(c) DATA BROKER REGISTRY.—The Commission shall maintain a
    publicly accessible registry of all registered data
    brokers.

SEC. 8. REAL-TIME BIDDING REFORM.

(a) CONSENT REQUIREMENT.—A covered entity may not include
    an individual's personal data in a real-time bidding
    bid request without the individual's affirmative
    express consent.

(b) DATA MINIMIZATION.—Bid requests shall not include any
    personal data beyond what is strictly necessary to
    determine the relevance of an advertisement to the
    content being viewed.

(c) PARTICIPANT REGISTRATION.—All entities participating in
    real-time bidding systems shall register with the
    Commission and comply with data security requirements
    promulgated by the Commission.

(d) DATA RETENTION PROHIBITION.—Entities receiving bid
    request data may not retain such data beyond the
    immediate auction transaction, and in no event longer
    than 24 hours.

(e) ANNUAL AUDIT.—Entities operating real-time bidding
    exchanges shall conduct annual third-party audits of
    data practices and submit results to the Commission.

SEC. 9. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) FTC ENFORCEMENT.—
    (1) AUTHORITY.—The Commission shall enforce this Act
        under the Federal Trade Commission Act (15 U.S.C.
        41 et seq.).
    (2) CIVIL PENALTIES.—A violation of this Act shall
        constitute an unfair or deceptive act or practice
        under Section 5 of the FTC Act. Civil penalties
        for violations shall be—
        (A) not more than $50,000 per violation per day for
            covered entities that are not small businesses;
        (B) not more than the greater of $50,000 per
            violation per day or 4 percent of the entity's
            global annual revenue for the preceding fiscal
            year for covered entities with annual revenues
            exceeding $250,000,000.
    (3) ALGORITHMIC DISGORGEMENT.—The Commission may order
        a covered entity to delete any algorithm, model, or
        automated system developed using personal data
        collected in violation of this Act.
    (4) EXECUTIVE LIABILITY.—Officers and directors of a
        covered entity who had knowledge of or were
        responsible for violations of this Act may be held
        individually liable.

(b) PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.—
    (1) RIGHT.—Any individual whose personal data has been
        collected, processed, or transferred in violation
        of this Act may bring a civil action in any court
        of competent jurisdiction.
    (2) DAMAGES.—An individual may recover—
        (A) actual damages;
        (B) statutory damages of not less than $100 and not
            more than $1,000 per violation;
        (C) punitive damages where the violation is willful
            or reckless;
        (D) injunctive and declaratory relief; and
        (E) reasonable attorney's fees and costs.
    (3) CLASS ACTIONS.—An action under this subsection may
        be maintained as a class action pursuant to Rule 23
        of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure.
    (4) CURE PERIOD.—Before filing an action for statutory
        damages, an individual shall provide the covered
        entity with 60 days' written notice specifying the
        alleged violation. If the entity cures the violation
        within 60 days and provides written confirmation,
        no statutory damages may be recovered for that
        violation. This cure period does not apply to
        actual damages, injunctive relief, or violations
        involving sensitive data.

(c) STATE ATTORNEY GENERAL ENFORCEMENT.—
    (1) The attorney general of any State may bring a civil
        action in any court of competent jurisdiction on
        behalf of the residents of such State.
    (2) State attorneys general may seek all remedies
        available under this Act and applicable State law.
    (3) Notice to the Commission is required 30 days before
        commencing an action.

(d) NO PREEMPTION.—
    (1) This Act shall not preempt, limit, or otherwise
        affect any State law that provides greater
        protections to individuals with respect to
        personal data.
    (2) This Act establishes a floor, not a ceiling, for
        privacy protections.

SEC. 10. FTC PRIVACY BUREAU.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—There is established within the Commission
    a Bureau of Privacy Protection.

(b) DUTIES.—The Bureau shall—
    (1) enforce this Act;
    (2) promulgate implementing regulations;
    (3) maintain the data broker registry;
    (4) develop standards for data security, consent
        mechanisms, and dark pattern identification;
    (5) publish annual reports on the state of commercial
        privacy;
    (6) coordinate with State attorneys general, sector-
        specific regulators, and international data
        protection authorities.

(c) STAFFING.—The Bureau shall employ not fewer than 300
    full-time employees within 3 years of enactment.

(d) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.—There are authorized to
    be appropriated $150,000,000 for each of fiscal years
    [Year] through [Year+5] to carry out this section.

SEC. 11. EFFECTIVE DATE.

(a) This Act shall take effect 18 months after the date of
    enactment, except that—
    (1) data broker registration under Section 7 shall take
        effect 12 months after enactment;
    (2) the private right of action under Section 9(b) shall
        take effect 24 months after enactment; and
    (3) real-time bidding reforms under Section 8 shall take
        effect 24 months after enactment.

(b) The Commission shall promulgate implementing regulations
    within 18 months of enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- Section 2 defines personal data and sensitive data broadly to capture the full scope of commercial surveillance, including inferred and derived data
- Section 3 establishes data minimization and purpose limitation as affirmative legal duties, shifting the framework from consent-based to duty-based
- Section 4 creates comprehensive individual rights modeled on the GDPR and CCPA/CPRA, including a universal opt-out mechanism
- Section 5 requires opt-in consent for sensitive data and provides enhanced protections for children
- Section 6 prohibits dark patterns, addressing the manipulative design practices that undermine informed consent
- Section 7 regulates data brokers through registration, transparency, and individual rights
- Section 8 reforms real-time bidding, the mechanism through which billions of data exposures occur daily
- Section 9 provides robust enforcement through FTC authority (with revenue-based penalties), private right of action (with cure period to address frivolous suit concerns), and state AG enforcement, with explicit non-preemption
- Section 10 creates a dedicated FTC Privacy Bureau with mandatory staffing and funding
- Section 11 provides phased implementation to allow industry transition

**Potential Challenges**:

- Industry will vigorously oppose the private right of action, non-preemption, and data minimization requirements
- Preemption dispute: California delegation will insist on non-preemption; industry will demand it as condition for support
- Defining "reasonably necessary" requires guidance and will generate compliance questions
- Small business threshold may need adjustment based on empirical data
- Revenue-based penalties will face constitutional challenges under the Excessive Fines Clause (likely to survive given GDPR precedent and proportionality)
- First Amendment challenges to data collection restrictions (likely to fail under intermediate scrutiny for commercial conduct)

**Refinements**:

- Develop sector-specific guidance for data minimization through FTC rulemaking
- Create safe harbor for companies that achieve independent privacy certification
- Coordinate with HIPAA, GLBA, FCRA, and COPPA to avoid gaps and conflicts
- Consider ratcheting mechanism that increases requirements over time as industry adapts

### Ending Surveillance Advertising Act

**Purpose**: Prohibit behavioral advertising -- advertising targeted based on surveillance of individual behavior across websites, applications, or services -- while preserving contextual and first-party advertising.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Ending Surveillance Advertising
Act of [Year]".

SEC. 2. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:

(1) SURVEILLANCE ADVERTISING.—The term "surveillance
    advertising" means advertising targeted to a specific
    individual or device based on personal data obtained
    from the individual's activity across websites,
    applications, or online services not owned or operated
    by the entity placing the advertisement.

(2) CONTEXTUAL ADVERTISING.—The term "contextual
    advertising" means advertising selected for display
    based on the content of the webpage, application, or
    service currently being viewed by the individual,
    without reference to the individual's personal data
    or prior behavior.

(3) FIRST-PARTY ADVERTISING.—The term "first-party
    advertising" means advertising directed to an individual
    based solely on information that the individual has
    directly and knowingly provided to the entity placing
    the advertisement in the context of a direct
    relationship.

SEC. 3. PROHIBITION.

(a) IN GENERAL.—It shall be unlawful for any person to
    engage in surveillance advertising.

(b) EXCEPTIONS.—This section shall not prohibit—
    (1) contextual advertising;
    (2) first-party advertising based on a direct
        relationship;
    (3) broad geographic targeting at the level of a
        designated market area or larger.

(c) DATA COLLECTION PROHIBITION.—It shall be unlawful for
    any person to collect, process, or transfer personal
    data for the purpose of enabling surveillance
    advertising.

SEC. 4. TRANSITION PERIOD.

(a) The prohibition under Section 3 shall take effect 2
    years after the date of enactment.

(b) During the transition period, the Commission shall—
    (1) promulgate regulations defining the boundaries
        between surveillance, contextual, and first-party
        advertising;
    (2) provide guidance to covered entities on compliance;
    (3) study the economic impact of the transition and
        report to Congress within 18 months.

SEC. 5. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) This Act shall be enforced by the Commission under the
    FTC Act.

(b) Civil penalties of not more than $50,000 per violation
    per day.

(c) State attorneys general may enforce this Act.

(d) Private right of action with statutory damages of
    $500-$5,000 per violation.

SEC. 6. ECONOMIC IMPACT STUDY.

The Commission, in consultation with the Department of
Commerce, shall conduct a study within 18 months of
enactment on—
(a) the economic impact of transitioning from surveillance
    to contextual advertising;
(b) the effect on small publishers and media organizations;
(c) the viability of alternative revenue models; and
(d) recommendations for supporting affected entities during
    the transition.
```

**Explanation**:

- Section 2 draws clear distinctions between surveillance advertising (prohibited), contextual advertising (permitted), and first-party advertising (permitted), preserving the ad-supported internet while eliminating the need for mass behavioral surveillance
- Section 3 bans both surveillance advertising and the data collection that enables it
- Section 4 provides a two-year transition period with FTC guidance and economic impact study
- Section 5 provides enforcement through FTC, state AGs, and private right of action
- Section 6 mandates economic impact assessment to inform transition support

**Potential Challenges**:

- Intense industry opposition: surveillance advertising generates the majority of digital advertising revenue
- Defining the boundary between contextual and behavioral targeting requires precision (e.g., is topic-based targeting based on browsing patterns "surveillance"?)
- First Amendment challenges (likely survivable under intermediate scrutiny as regulation of commercial conduct)
- Economic disruption during transition period, particularly for small publishers dependent on programmatic revenue
- International enforcement challenges for foreign-based advertising platforms

## State Model Legislation

### Model State Commercial Privacy Act

**Purpose**: Provide a template for comprehensive state privacy legislation that exceeds the protections of existing state laws and serves as a model for states without privacy legislation.

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Commercial Privacy
Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.

(a) "Personal data" means information that is linked or
    reasonably linkable to an identified or identifiable
    individual, including inferred and derived data.

(b) "Sensitive data" means personal data revealing racial
    or ethnic origin, religious beliefs, health data,
    biometric data, precise geolocation data, sexual
    orientation, children's data, financial account data,
    or private communications content.

(c) "Controller" means an entity that determines the
    purposes and means of processing personal data.

(d) "Processor" means an entity that processes personal
    data on behalf of a controller.

(e) "Dark pattern" means a user interface designed with the
    substantial effect of subverting or impairing user
    autonomy, decision-making, or choice.

(f) "Targeted advertising" means advertising selected based
    on personal data obtained from cross-site or cross-
    service behavioral tracking.

SECTION 3. DATA MINIMIZATION.

A controller shall collect, process, and retain only personal
data that is reasonably necessary and proportionate to the
purpose for which the data is processed, as disclosed to the
consumer.

SECTION 4. CONSUMER RIGHTS.

(a) Consumers have the right to:
    (1) access personal data held by a controller;
    (2) correct inaccurate personal data;
    (3) delete personal data;
    (4) obtain personal data in a portable format;
    (5) opt out of targeted advertising;
    (6) opt out of the sale or sharing of personal data;
    (7) opt out of profiling for decisions with legal or
        similarly significant effects.

(b) Controllers shall respond to consumer requests within
    30 days, free of charge.

(c) Controllers shall recognize universal opt-out
    mechanisms, including Global Privacy Control.

(d) Controllers shall not discriminate against consumers
    who exercise their rights.

SECTION 5. SENSITIVE DATA.

A controller may not process sensitive data without the
consumer's affirmative, specific, informed consent obtained
through a mechanism free of dark patterns.

SECTION 6. DARK PATTERNS PROHIBITION.

A controller shall not use dark patterns to obtain consent,
discourage the exercise of consumer rights, or manipulate
consumers into sharing unnecessary data. Consent obtained
through dark patterns is void.

SECTION 7. DATA PROTECTION ASSESSMENTS.

(a) A controller shall conduct data protection assessments
    for processing activities involving:
    (1) targeted advertising;
    (2) sale of personal data;
    (3) profiling with legal or significant effects;
    (4) sensitive data processing;
    (5) processing data of children.

(b) Assessments shall be made available to the Attorney
    General upon request.

SECTION 8. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) ATTORNEY GENERAL.—The Attorney General shall have
    exclusive authority to enforce this Act on behalf of
    the State, except as provided in subsection (b).

(b) PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.—
    (1) Any consumer whose personal data has been collected,
        processed, or transferred in violation of this Act
        may bring a civil action.
    (2) Relief includes actual damages, statutory damages
        of $200-$1,000 per violation, injunctive relief,
        and reasonable attorney's fees.
    (3) Class actions are permitted under [State Rules of
        Civil Procedure].
    (4) A 45-day cure period applies to statutory damages
        claims, except for violations involving sensitive
        data or dark patterns.

(c) PENALTIES.—Civil penalties of up to $25,000 per
    violation, or $50,000 per violation involving sensitive
    data or children's data.

SECTION 9. DATA BROKER REGISTRATION.

(a) Data brokers operating in this State shall register
    annually with the Attorney General.

(b) Registration shall include categories of data collected,
    sources, categories of recipients, and consumer rights
    mechanisms.

(c) Failure to register is a violation subject to penalties
    under Section 8.

SECTION 10. NO WAIVER.

Any provision of a contract that waives or limits a
consumer's rights under this Act is void and unenforceable.

SECTION 11. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect on [date 12 months after enactment].
```

## Regulatory Framework

### FTC Commercial Surveillance Rulemaking

| Element | Proposed Rule |
|---------|--------------|
| **Authority** | FTC Act Section 5 (unfair or deceptive practices); Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act rulemaking authority |
| **Scope** | All commercial entities that collect, process, or transfer personal data in interstate commerce |
| **Data minimization** | Unfair practice to collect personal data beyond what is reasonably necessary for the service requested |
| **Dark patterns** | Deceptive practice to use dark patterns to obtain consent or discourage exercise of privacy rights |
| **Consent standards** | Meaningful consent requires clear, conspicuous, specific disclosure; pre-checked boxes and bundled consent prohibited |
| **Data security** | Mandatory reasonable security standards proportionate to data sensitivity and volume |
| **Algorithmic disgorgement** | Authority to require deletion of algorithms trained on unlawfully collected data |
| **Coordination** | Parallel enforcement with state AGs; coordination with CFPB, EEOC, and HHS |

### NIST Privacy Framework Implementation

| Element | Standard |
|---------|----------|
| **Data minimization metrics** | Define "reasonably necessary" by sector, with specific guidance for advertising, financial services, healthcare apps, and IoT devices |
| **Consent interface standards** | Technical standards for consent mechanisms that avoid dark patterns; accessibility requirements |
| **Privacy-preserving advertising** | Standards for contextual advertising that complies with data minimization requirements |
| **Data security baselines** | Minimum security standards by data type and volume, updated annually |
| **Universal opt-out technical specification** | Interoperability requirements for Global Privacy Control and successor mechanisms |

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

| Issue | Analysis |
|-------|----------|
| **First Amendment** | Industry will argue data collection is protected speech; courts have consistently upheld commercial regulations that incidentally affect information (HIPAA, GLBA, FCRA, COPPA); data processing is economic conduct, not expression; intermediate scrutiny applies to commercial speech restrictions and privacy regulation serves substantial government interests |
| **Commerce Clause** | Federal authority to regulate commercial surveillance in interstate commerce is well-established; data flows are inherently interstate |
| **Due Process** | Private right of action must provide adequate notice of prohibited conduct and meaningful damages caps; cure period addresses due process concerns |
| **Excessive Fines** | Revenue-based penalties must be proportionate; GDPR model (4% global revenue) provides international precedent; federal courts have generally upheld substantial privacy penalties |
| **Preemption** | Non-preemption clause preserves state authority; Congress has power to preempt or not preempt at its discretion; non-preemption is a policy choice, not a constitutional requirement |

### Preemption

| Dynamic | Analysis |
|---------|----------|
| **Floor-not-ceiling approach** | Federal law sets minimum standards; states retain authority to provide greater protections; resolves the deadlock that killed ADPPA by protecting California's CCPA/CPRA |
| **State innovation** | States have driven every significant US privacy advance (California CCPA, Illinois BIPA, state data broker laws); preemption would eliminate this laboratory of democracy |
| **Industry compliance** | Companies already comply with multiple state and international frameworks; non-preemption does not create a novel compliance challenge |
| **Enforcement diversity** | Multiple enforcement actors (federal, state, private) provide comprehensive coverage; no single enforcer has sufficient resources alone |

### Enforcement Mechanisms

| Mechanism | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|-----------|-----------|---------------|
| **FTC enforcement** | Expertise; national scope; rulemaking authority | Resource-constrained; subject to political composition changes |
| **Private right of action** | Supplements government enforcement; empowers individuals; creates financial deterrent | Industry opposition; potential for frivolous suits (mitigated by cure period) |
| **State AG enforcement** | Proximity to affected communities; enforcement diversity | Inconsistent across states; resource variation |
| **Revenue-based penalties** | Proportionate deterrence for large companies | Constitutional challenges; complexity |
| **Algorithmic disgorgement** | Eliminates economic benefit of violations | Novel remedy; enforcement complexity |

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| **Small business exemption abuse** | Companies structure operations through subsidiaries or shell entities that individually fall below thresholds while collectively processing massive data volumes | High |
| **"Reasonably necessary" elasticity** | Companies argue that virtually any data collection is "reasonably necessary" for service improvement, security, or fraud prevention | High |
| **First-party data accumulation** | Companies expand their direct data collection (logged-in users, first-party cookies) to replace third-party surveillance while maintaining behavioral profiles | High |
| **Consent theater** | Companies design technically compliant but practically meaningless consent interfaces that satisfy the letter of dark pattern prohibitions while still manipulating users | High |
| **Data laundering through intermediaries** | Companies obtain surveillance data through third-party intermediaries, claiming they did not "collect" it | Medium |
| **Jurisdictional evasion** | Foreign-based companies process data outside US jurisdiction, creating enforcement gaps | Medium |
| **Purpose limitation stretching** | Companies define initial purposes so broadly that virtually any secondary use falls within the stated purpose | Medium |
| **Cure period manipulation** | Companies exploit the 60-day cure period by "curing" individual violations while continuing systemic practices | Medium |
| **Contextual advertising redefinition** | Companies argue that inferences drawn from page content (including content the user consumed previously) constitute "contextual" rather than behavioral targeting | Medium |
| **De-identification ruse** | Companies claim data is "de-identified" or "anonymous" when it remains practically re-identifiable | Low |

### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| **Enforcement resource gap** | Even $150 million annually for FTC Privacy Bureau is small relative to the scope of commercial surveillance across millions of entities | Government enforcement budgets chronically inadequate compared to industry resources |
| **Consumer rights fatigue** | Individual exercise of access, deletion, and opt-out rights is burdensome; most consumers will not exercise rights even when available | Structural mismatch between individual rights framework and systemic problem |
| **International enforcement** | US law cannot directly reach data processing by foreign entities operating outside US jurisdiction | Jurisdictional limitations of domestic law |
| **Technology evolution** | Legislation based on current technologies (cookies, RTB, mobile tracking) may not address future surveillance methods (AI inference, IoT, brain-computer interfaces) | Technology evolves faster than law |
| **Market concentration persistence** | Privacy regulation may entrench dominant platforms that can absorb compliance costs, further concentrating the market | Compliance costs favor large incumbents |
| **Alternative revenue model uncertainty** | The economic viability of a post-surveillance internet remains unproven at scale, creating transition risk for publishers and content creators | Decades of market evolution around surveillance model |

### Rectification Procedures

1. **Anti-fragmentation provision**: Define covered entities based on aggregate data processing across all affiliated entities and subsidiaries; apply thresholds to the corporate group, not individual legal entities; create rebuttable presumption that entities under common control constitute a single covered entity

2. **"Reasonably necessary" guidance with bright-line rules**: FTC promulgates sector-specific guidance defining "reasonably necessary" for each major industry, supplemented by bright-line prohibitions (e.g., collection of location data is never "reasonably necessary" for a news website; collection of contact lists is never "reasonably necessary" for a calculator app)

3. **First-party data limitations**: Apply data minimization requirements equally to first-party data; prohibit combining data from distinct services operated by the same company without specific consent (e.g., Google cannot combine Search, YouTube, Gmail, and Maps data into a single profile without service-specific consent)

4. **Dark pattern certification and testing**: FTC develops and publishes consent interface standards with specific design requirements; mandatory third-party usability testing for consent interfaces used by entities processing data of more than 100,000 individuals; certification process with safe harbor for compliant designs

5. **Data provenance chain**: Require covered entities to maintain and disclose the complete provenance chain for personal data, including all intermediaries through which data was obtained; strict liability for using data obtained through intermediaries in violation of this Act

6. **Effects-based jurisdiction**: Apply law to any entity that collects, processes, or transfers the personal data of individuals located in the United States, regardless of where the entity is incorporated or where processing occurs; coordinate enforcement with international data protection authorities

7. **Mandatory purpose specification**: Require covered entities to specify purposes at a granularity sufficient for a reasonable consumer to understand the specific uses of their data; prohibit purposes stated in terms of general categories (e.g., "improve our services" or "business purposes" are insufficient)

8. **Cure period safeguards**: Limit cure period availability to the first occurrence of a specific type of violation; systemic or repeated violations are not eligible for cure; violations involving sensitive data, children's data, or dark patterns are never eligible for cure; require the entity to demonstrate cure through independent verification

9. **Contextual advertising boundary**: Define contextual advertising strictly as targeting based solely on the content of the specific page being viewed at the time of the ad impression, without reference to any data about the individual user, including past page views; prohibition on combining contextual signals with any user-level data

10. **De-identification standards**: Establish specific technical standards for de-identification that go beyond removal of direct identifiers; require formal risk assessment of re-identification; data that does not meet the standard is treated as personal data regardless of label

### General Implementation Concerns

- **Agency coordination**: Risk of regulatory overlap between FTC Privacy Bureau, CFPB (financial data), HHS (health data), and EEOC (employment data); address through interagency MOUs and clear jurisdictional delineation in implementing regulations
- **Small business support**: Compliance costs may disproportionately burden small businesses; consider FTC-funded compliance assistance programs, template privacy notices, and simplified compliance tools for small entities
- **Transition economics**: Surveillance advertising generates hundreds of billions in annual revenue; transition to contextual advertising will reduce publisher revenue in the short term; economic impact study and transition support (tax credits, grants for alternative revenue model development) are necessary
- **International coordination**: Pursue mutual recognition agreements with EU data protection authorities, UK ICO, and other international counterparts to facilitate cross-border enforcement and reduce compliance burden for companies operating globally
- **Technical standards**: Consent interface standards, de-identification standards, and data minimization metrics require ongoing technical development by NIST and FTC in consultation with civil society and industry
