# Biometric Privacy: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Biometric privacy requires dedicated federal and state legislation that recognizes biometric data as categorically different from other personal information. Because biometric identifiers -- fingerprints, facial geometry, iris patterns, voiceprints, and DNA -- are immutable and permanently compromised once breached, the legislative framework must establish strict consent requirements, use limitations, retention and destruction schedules, accuracy and bias standards, and robust enforcement through a private right of action. The most effective model is Illinois's Biometric Information Privacy Act (BIPA), which has generated over $6 billion in settlements and judgments since 2008, demonstrating both the scale of unauthorized biometric data collection and the power of enforceable individual rights.

## Federal Legislation

### Federal Biometric Information Privacy Act

**Purpose**: Establish comprehensive federal protections for biometric data modeled on Illinois BIPA, with a private right of action, consent requirements, retention limits, and a prohibition on the sale of biometric data, without preempting stronger state laws.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Federal Biometric Information
Privacy Act of [Year]".

SEC. 2. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:

(1) BIOMETRIC IDENTIFIER.--The term "biometric identifier"
    means a retina or iris scan, fingerprint, voiceprint,
    scan of hand or face geometry, or any other biological
    characteristic that can be used to identify a specific
    individual.

(2) BIOMETRIC INFORMATION.--The term "biometric information"
    means any information, regardless of how it is captured,
    converted, stored, or shared, based on an individual's
    biometric identifier used to identify an individual.

(3) PRIVATE ENTITY.--The term "private entity" means any
    individual, partnership, corporation, limited liability
    company, association, or other group, however organized,
    but does not include a State or local government agency
    or any court of Illinois, a clerk of the court, or a
    judge of that court.

(4) WRITTEN RELEASE.--The term "written release" means
    informed written consent or, in the context of employment,
    a release executed by an employee as a condition of
    employment. Such release shall be executed voluntarily
    and shall not constitute consent to purposes beyond those
    specifically described in the release.

(5) COLLECT.--The term "collect" means to gather, capture,
    receive, obtain, or otherwise acquire biometric
    identifiers or biometric information by any means,
    including by actively scanning, requesting, or requiring
    submission, or by passively obtaining without the
    individual's awareness.

SEC. 3. RETENTION; COLLECTION; DISCLOSURE; DESTRUCTION.

(a) RETENTION SCHEDULE.--A private entity in possession of
    biometric identifiers or biometric information shall
    develop a written policy, made available to the public,
    establishing a retention schedule and guidelines for
    permanently destroying biometric identifiers and
    biometric information when--
    (1) the initial purpose for collecting or obtaining
        such identifiers or information has been satisfied;
        or
    (2) within 3 years of the individual's last interaction
        with the private entity, whichever occurs first.

(b) CONSENT REQUIREMENT.--No private entity may collect,
    capture, purchase, receive through trade, or otherwise
    obtain a person's biometric identifier or biometric
    information, unless it first--
    (1) informs the subject in writing that a biometric
        identifier or biometric information is being
        collected or stored;
    (2) informs the subject in writing of the specific
        purpose and length of term for which a biometric
        identifier or biometric information is being
        collected, stored, and used; and
    (3) receives a written release executed by the subject
        of the biometric identifier or biometric information
        or the subject's legally authorized representative.

(c) PROHIBITION ON SALE AND DISCLOSURE.--No private entity
    in possession of a biometric identifier or biometric
    information may--
    (1) sell, lease, trade, or otherwise profit from a
        person's biometric identifier or biometric
        information;
    (2) disclose, redisclose, or otherwise disseminate a
        person's biometric identifier or biometric
        information unless--
        (A) the subject of the biometric identifier or
            biometric information or the subject's legally
            authorized representative consents to the
            disclosure or redisclosure;
        (B) the disclosure completes a financial transaction
            requested or authorized by the subject of the
            biometric identifier or biometric information
            or the subject's legally authorized
            representative;
        (C) the disclosure is required by Federal, State,
            or local law or municipal ordinance; or
        (D) the disclosure is required pursuant to a valid
            warrant or subpoena issued by a court of
            competent jurisdiction.

(d) DATA SECURITY.--A private entity in possession of
    biometric identifiers or biometric information shall--
    (1) store, transmit, and protect from disclosure all
        biometric identifiers and biometric information
        using the reasonable standard of care within the
        private entity's industry; and
    (2) in a manner that is the same as or more protective
        than the manner in which the private entity stores,
        transmits, and protects other confidential and
        sensitive information.

SEC. 4. RIGHT OF ACTION.

(a) RIGHT OF ACTION.--Any person aggrieved by a violation
    of this Act shall have a right of action in a Federal
    or State court of competent jurisdiction and may
    recover for each violation--
    (1) against a private entity that negligently violates
        a provision of this Act, liquidated damages of
        $1,000 or actual damages, whichever is greater;
    (2) against a private entity that intentionally or
        recklessly violates a provision of this Act,
        liquidated damages of $5,000 or actual damages,
        whichever is greater;
    (3) reasonable attorneys' fees and costs, including
        expert witness fees and other litigation expenses;
        and
    (4) other relief, including an injunction, as the
        court may deem appropriate.

(b) PER-VIOLATION LIABILITY.--Each collection, capture,
    purchase, receipt through trade, sale, lease, trade,
    disclosure, redisclosure, or other dissemination of a
    biometric identifier or biometric information in
    violation of this Act constitutes a separate violation.

(c) NO INJURY REQUIREMENT.--A person need not allege or
    prove actual injury or adverse effect beyond the
    violation itself to have standing under this section.

(d) CLASS ACTIONS.--Actions under this section may be
    maintained as class actions under Rule 23 of the
    Federal Rules of Civil Procedure.

SEC. 5. CONSTRUCTION.

(a) NO PREEMPTION.--Nothing in this Act shall be construed
    to preempt, limit, or otherwise affect the rights or
    remedies available under any Federal, State, or local
    law, including the Illinois Biometric Information
    Privacy Act (740 ILCS 14/).

(b) FLOOR, NOT CEILING.--This Act establishes minimum
    protections for biometric identifiers and biometric
    information. States may adopt or maintain protections
    that are more stringent than those provided in this Act.

(c) LIMITATION.--This Act does not apply to--
    (1) biometric identifiers or biometric information
        processed entirely on a device in the possession
        and control of the individual whose data is being
        processed, where such data is not transmitted to
        or accessible by any other device, person, or
        entity; or
    (2) biological samples, genetic information, or DNA
        regulated under the Genetic Information
        Nondiscrimination Act (42 U.S.C. 2000ff et seq.)
        to the extent of such regulation.

SEC. 6. STATUTE OF LIMITATIONS.

An action under this Act must be commenced within 5 years
of the date the alleged violation occurred or was
discovered, whichever is later.

SEC. 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 12 months after the date of
enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- Section 2 defines biometric identifiers and information broadly, consistent with Illinois BIPA but extending to emerging biometric modalities
- Section 3 establishes the core substantive requirements: public retention policy, written consent before collection, prohibition on sale, and data security obligations
- Section 4 provides robust enforcement through a private right of action with $1,000/$5,000 statutory damages, per-violation liability (consistent with *Cothron v. White Castle*), no injury requirement (consistent with *Rosenbach v. Six Flags*), and class action availability
- Section 5 explicitly establishes a floor-not-ceiling approach, preserving Illinois BIPA and other stronger state laws
- Section 6 sets a 5-year statute of limitations, consistent with *Tims v. Black Horse Carriers* (2023)
- The on-device processing exclusion (Section 5(c)(1)) distinguishes between consensual, local biometric authentication (e.g., Apple Face ID processed on-device) and non-consensual, centralized biometric data collection

**Potential Challenges**:

- Strong industry opposition to private right of action and per-violation damages
- Law enforcement agencies will lobby for broad exemptions
- Industry will push for federal preemption of BIPA; the no-preemption provision will face intense lobbying
- Constitutional challenges under the First Amendment (Clearview AI has argued facial recognition is protected speech)
- Business groups will argue that per-violation damages from *Cothron* create excessive liability

**Refinements**:

- Consider a notice-and-cure provision for first-time technical violations (failure to publish retention policy) while maintaining strict liability for substantive violations (collection without consent, sale of data)
- Develop safe harbor for good-faith compliance efforts that does not shield entities from liability for actual unauthorized collection
- Address the specific situation of employer biometric collection where consent may be coerced (see Worker Biometric Protection Act below)

### Government Facial Recognition Moratorium Act

**Purpose**: Impose a moratorium on government use of facial recognition technology in public spaces until mandatory accuracy standards, bias testing, and oversight mechanisms are established.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Government Facial Recognition
Moratorium Act of [Year]".

SEC. 2. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:

(1) FACIAL RECOGNITION TECHNOLOGY.--The term "facial
    recognition technology" means any automated or
    semi-automated process that--
    (A) assists in identifying or verifying an individual
        based on the physical characteristics of the
        individual's face; or
    (B) logs, tracks, or otherwise records the movements
        or activities of an individual based on the
        physical characteristics of the individual's face.

(2) PUBLIC SPACE.--The term "public space" means any area
    accessible to the general public, including streets,
    sidewalks, parks, transit systems, airports, government
    buildings open to the public, and commercial
    establishments.

(3) COVERED AGENCY.--The term "covered agency" means any
    Federal, State, or local government agency or
    instrumentality, including any law enforcement agency.

(4) ONE-TO-MANY IDENTIFICATION.--The term "one-to-many
    identification" means the use of facial recognition
    technology to compare an individual's facial image
    against a database of facial images to identify the
    individual.

(5) ONE-TO-ONE VERIFICATION.--The term "one-to-one
    verification" means the use of facial recognition
    technology to compare an individual's facial image
    against a single known facial image to verify the
    individual's claimed identity.

SEC. 3. MORATORIUM.

(a) PROHIBITION.--No covered agency may acquire or use
    facial recognition technology for one-to-many
    identification in any public space for a period of
    3 years beginning on the date of enactment of this Act.

(b) FEDERAL FUNDING RESTRICTION.--No Federal funds may be
    used to support, maintain, or expand facial recognition
    technology for one-to-many identification in public
    spaces during the moratorium period.

(c) DATA PURCHASE PROHIBITION.--During the moratorium
    period, no covered agency may purchase, license, or
    otherwise obtain facial recognition data or services
    from a commercial entity for use in one-to-many
    identification.

SEC. 4. EXCEPTIONS.

(a) WARRANT-BASED TARGETED SEARCH.--A covered agency may
    use facial recognition technology to conduct a one-to-
    one verification or a targeted one-to-many search
    for a specific individual where--
    (1) a warrant has been issued by a court of competent
        jurisdiction based on probable cause; and
    (2) the warrant specifically authorizes the use of
        facial recognition technology.

(b) IMMEDIATE THREAT.--A covered agency may use facial
    recognition technology without a warrant where--
    (1) there is an imminent threat to life;
    (2) the use is limited to identifying a specific
        individual in connection with that threat; and
    (3) the agency reports the use to a court of competent
        jurisdiction within 48 hours.

(c) VOLUNTARY VERIFICATION.--This section does not apply
    to one-to-one verification systems used voluntarily by
    individuals to access government services or verify
    their own identity.

SEC. 5. ACCURACY AND BIAS STANDARDS.

(a) NIST STANDARDS.--Within 18 months of enactment, the
    National Institute of Standards and Technology shall
    develop mandatory accuracy and bias standards for facial
    recognition technology used by covered agencies,
    including--
    (1) minimum accuracy thresholds for one-to-one
        verification and one-to-many identification;
    (2) maximum permissible false positive and false
        negative rates;
    (3) demographic performance requirements ensuring that
        error rates do not vary by more than a factor of 3
        across racial, ethnic, gender, and age groups; and
    (4) testing methodology under real-world conditions,
        not solely laboratory conditions.

(b) PRE-DEPLOYMENT CERTIFICATION.--After the moratorium
    period, no covered agency may deploy facial recognition
    technology unless--
    (1) the specific system has been tested and certified
        by NIST as meeting the standards developed under
        subsection (a);
    (2) the agency has completed a privacy and civil
        rights impact assessment; and
    (3) the system is subject to annual independent bias
        auditing.

SEC. 6. REPORTING.

(a) ANNUAL REPORT.--Each covered agency that uses or has
    used facial recognition technology shall submit an annual
    report to Congress and make publicly available a report
    including--
    (1) the number of times facial recognition was used;
    (2) the purpose of each use;
    (3) the racial and ethnic demographics of individuals
        subjected to facial recognition, to the extent
        known;
    (4) the number of identifications made and their
        accuracy outcomes;
    (5) the number of misidentifications discovered; and
    (6) any disciplinary or corrective actions taken.

SEC. 7. PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.

(a) Any individual who is subjected to facial recognition
    technology by a covered agency in violation of this Act
    may bring a civil action and recover--
    (1) actual damages;
    (2) liquidated damages of $10,000 per violation;
    (3) reasonable attorneys' fees and costs; and
    (4) injunctive and declaratory relief.

(b) Qualified immunity shall not be a defense to a civil
    action under this section.

SEC. 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect on the date of enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- Section 2 distinguishes between one-to-many identification (searching faces against databases) and one-to-one verification (confirming a claimed identity), applying the moratorium to the higher-risk one-to-many use
- Section 3 imposes a comprehensive moratorium including direct government use, federal funding for state/local use, and data purchases from commercial vendors
- Section 4 provides narrowly defined exceptions for warrant-based searches, imminent threats with judicial reporting, and voluntary verification
- Section 5 requires NIST to develop mandatory accuracy and bias standards during the moratorium period, including the critical requirement that error rates not vary by more than 3x across demographic groups
- Section 6 mandates transparency through annual public reporting on all facial recognition use
- Section 7 provides a private right of action with $10,000 statutory damages and eliminates qualified immunity for government violations, reflecting the heightened civil rights risk

**Potential Challenges**:

- Intense law enforcement opposition; national security agencies will seek broad exemptions
- Political framing as "soft on crime" or "soft on terrorism"
- Defining "public space" to avoid loopholes
- Enforcement of the federal funding restriction on state and local agencies
- Qualified immunity elimination will face strong police union opposition

**Refinements**:

- Consider extending the moratorium period if NIST standards are not ready within 18 months
- Develop explicit requirements for what constitutes a meaningful privacy and civil rights impact assessment
- Address the disposition of existing government facial recognition databases during the moratorium

### Worker Biometric Protection Act

**Purpose**: Establish specific protections for workers subject to employer biometric data collection, addressing the power imbalance that makes consent in employment contexts inherently coercive.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Worker Biometric Protection
Act of [Year]".

SEC. 2. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:

(1) BIOMETRIC DATA.--The term "biometric data" means any
    biometric identifier or biometric information as defined
    in the Federal Biometric Information Privacy Act.

(2) EMPLOYER.--The term "employer" means any person engaged
    in commerce or in the production of goods for commerce
    who employs 1 or more employees.

(3) NON-BIOMETRIC ALTERNATIVE.--The term "non-biometric
    alternative" means a method of timekeeping, access
    control, or identity verification that does not require
    the collection of biometric data, including but not
    limited to key cards, PINs, passwords, or manual sign-in.

SEC. 3. EMPLOYER BIOMETRIC DATA REQUIREMENTS.

(a) ALTERNATIVE REQUIRED.--Any employer that uses biometric
    data for timekeeping, access control, or identity
    verification shall provide a non-biometric alternative
    that is equally accessible and functional.

(b) VOLUNTARY PARTICIPATION.--Employee use of biometric
    systems must be voluntary. Consent to biometric data
    collection is not valid unless a non-biometric alternative
    is simultaneously available.

(c) NO RETALIATION.--An employer may not discharge,
    discipline, or otherwise retaliate against an employee
    for choosing a non-biometric alternative.

(d) DESTRUCTION.--An employer shall permanently destroy all
    biometric data of an employee within 30 days of the
    termination of the employment relationship.

(e) NO THIRD-PARTY SHARING.--An employer may not share
    employee biometric data with any third party, except
    as strictly necessary for the operation of the biometric
    system and only under a contractual obligation of
    confidentiality and data destruction.

(f) ANNUAL NOTICE.--An employer that collects biometric data
    shall provide annual written notice to employees
    describing--
    (1) all biometric data collected;
    (2) the purpose of collection;
    (3) how the data is stored and protected;
    (4) the retention schedule; and
    (5) the employee's right to a non-biometric alternative.

SEC. 4. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.--An employee or former employee
    may bring a civil action for violations of this Act
    and recover--
    (1) liquidated damages of $1,000 per negligent violation
        or $5,000 per intentional violation, or actual
        damages, whichever is greater;
    (2) reasonable attorneys' fees and costs; and
    (3) injunctive relief.

(b) DEPARTMENT OF LABOR ENFORCEMENT.--The Secretary of Labor
    may investigate and bring enforcement actions under this
    Act.

(c) NO WAIVER.--An employee may not waive rights under
    this Act as a condition of employment. Any agreement
    purporting to waive such rights is void.

SEC. 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 6 months after the date of
enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- Section 3 addresses the core problem: employer biometric collection occurs in a context where employees cannot meaningfully refuse, making consent inherently coercive
- The non-biometric alternative requirement ensures that consent is genuine, not compelled
- The anti-retaliation provision prevents employers from penalizing workers who opt out
- The 30-day destruction requirement after termination prevents employers from maintaining biometric data after the employment relationship ends
- Section 4 provides a private right of action and DOL enforcement, with a non-waiver provision preventing employers from requiring biometric consent as a condition of employment

**Potential Challenges**:

- Employer association opposition (Chamber of Commerce, National Association of Manufacturers)
- Cost argument for maintaining dual systems (biometric and non-biometric)
- Definition of "voluntary" in employment context

### Consumer DNA Privacy Act

**Purpose**: Establish dedicated protections for consumer genetic data, addressing the unique risks of DNA information including familial exposure, law enforcement access, and data disposition in company insolvency.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Consumer DNA Privacy Act
of [Year]".

SEC. 2. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:

(1) CONSUMER GENETIC DATA.--The term "consumer genetic data"
    means any data that results from the analysis of a
    biological sample from a consumer and that concerns
    the consumer's genetic characteristics, including--
    (A) raw sequence data;
    (B) genotype data;
    (C) any data derived from the analysis of a biological
        sample that reveals genetic information; and
    (D) self-reported health information provided in
        connection with genetic testing.

(2) DIRECT-TO-CONSUMER GENETIC TESTING COMPANY.--The term
    "direct-to-consumer genetic testing company" means any
    entity that--
    (A) provides genetic testing directly to consumers; or
    (B) collects, stores, or analyzes consumer genetic data.

(3) BIOLOGICAL SAMPLE.--The term "biological sample" means
    any material of human origin, including saliva, blood,
    tissue, or any other substance containing DNA.

SEC. 3. CONSENT AND USE LIMITATIONS.

(a) SEPARATE CONSENT.--A direct-to-consumer genetic testing
    company shall obtain separate, affirmative written
    consent from a consumer for each of the following:
    (1) initial genetic testing;
    (2) storage of the biological sample beyond the period
        necessary for testing;
    (3) use of genetic data for research purposes;
    (4) sharing genetic data with any third party; and
    (5) participation in familial matching services.

(b) PROHIBITION ON SALE.--No direct-to-consumer genetic
    testing company may sell, lease, or trade consumer
    genetic data to any third party, including insurance
    companies, employers, educational institutions, or
    data brokers.

(c) LAW ENFORCEMENT ACCESS.--
    (1) A direct-to-consumer genetic testing company may not
        voluntarily provide consumer genetic data to any law
        enforcement agency.
    (2) Disclosure to law enforcement shall require a warrant
        issued by a court of competent jurisdiction based on
        probable cause, specifying the individual whose data
        is sought.
    (3) A company that receives a warrant under this section
        shall make reasonable efforts to notify the consumer
        whose data is sought within 30 days, unless a court
        orders delayed notification based on specific findings
        that notification would endanger life or impede an
        active investigation.

SEC. 4. FAMILIAL PRIVACY.

(a) OPT-OUT RIGHT.--Consumers shall have the right to opt
    out of familial matching services that could identify
    genetic relatives.

(b) NOTICE TO RELATIVES.--When a consumer's genetic data
    could reveal information about identifiable relatives,
    the company shall provide clear notice of this
    possibility before testing.

SEC. 5. DATA PORTABILITY AND DELETION.

(a) PORTABILITY.--A consumer has the right to receive a
    complete copy of their genetic data in a commonly used,
    machine-readable format.

(b) DELETION.--Upon consumer request, a company shall--
    (1) permanently destroy the biological sample;
    (2) delete all consumer genetic data from its systems
        and from third-party systems to which data was
        shared; and
    (3) confirm destruction and deletion in writing within
        30 days.

SEC. 6. INSOLVENCY PROVISIONS.

(a) PROHIBITION ON TRANSFER.--In the event of a
    direct-to-consumer genetic testing company's bankruptcy,
    acquisition, merger, or dissolution--
    (1) consumer genetic data and biological samples shall
        not be treated as transferable assets;
    (2) all biological samples shall be destroyed; and
    (3) all consumer genetic data shall be deleted,

    unless consumers provide affirmative consent to transfer
    to a specific, identified successor entity.

(b) NOTICE.--Consumers shall be notified of any proposed
    transfer at least 60 days before it occurs, with a clear
    opportunity to opt out and request deletion.

SEC. 7. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.--A consumer may bring a civil
    action for violations and recover--
    (1) liquidated damages of $2,500 per violation or
        actual damages, whichever is greater;
    (2) reasonable attorneys' fees and costs; and
    (3) injunctive relief.

(b) FTC ENFORCEMENT.--The Federal Trade Commission may
    enforce this Act under Section 5 of the FTC Act,
    with civil penalties of up to $50,000 per violation.

(c) STATE AG ENFORCEMENT.--State attorneys general may bring
    civil actions to enforce this Act.

SEC. 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 12 months after the date of
enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- Section 3 requires granular, separate consent for each use of genetic data, preventing blanket consent that obscures downstream uses
- The law enforcement access provision (Section 3(c)) closes the loophole exploited in forensic genealogy cases, requiring a warrant and consumer notification
- Section 6 directly addresses the 23andMe insolvency scenario by prohibiting transfer of genetic data as a corporate asset
- The prohibition on sale (Section 3(b)) is broader than GINA, covering all third-party transfers including to data brokers

### Emotion Recognition Prohibition Act

**Purpose**: Ban the use of emotion recognition technology in commercial and government contexts, reflecting the scientific consensus that facial expressions do not reliably indicate emotions.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Emotion Recognition
Prohibition Act of [Year]".

SEC. 2. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:

(1) EMOTION RECOGNITION TECHNOLOGY.--The term "emotion
    recognition technology" means any automated or
    semi-automated system that purports to detect, infer,
    classify, or predict the emotions, mental states,
    intentions, or internal psychological experiences of
    an individual based on--
    (A) facial expressions or facial movements;
    (B) voice characteristics or speech patterns;
    (C) gait, posture, or body movements;
    (D) physiological signals; or
    (E) any combination of the above.

SEC. 3. PROHIBITIONS.

(a) GOVERNMENT USE.--No Federal, State, or local government
    agency may use emotion recognition technology for any
    purpose, including law enforcement, border security,
    employment, or public services.

(b) EMPLOYMENT USE.--No employer may use emotion recognition
    technology for hiring, evaluation, promotion, discipline,
    monitoring, or any other employment-related purpose.

(c) COMMERCIAL USE.--No person may use emotion recognition
    technology for customer analysis, advertising targeting,
    pricing, service delivery, or any other commercial purpose.

(d) EDUCATIONAL USE.--No educational institution may use
    emotion recognition technology for student assessment,
    monitoring, or discipline.

SEC. 4. EXCEPTIONS.

(a) CLINICAL USE.--This Act does not apply to emotion
    recognition technology used by licensed healthcare
    providers in clinical settings with informed patient
    consent.

(b) ACADEMIC RESEARCH.--This Act does not apply to emotion
    recognition technology used in academic research approved
    by an institutional review board, with informed consent
    of research subjects.

SEC. 5. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) FTC ENFORCEMENT.--The Commission may enforce this Act
    with civil penalties of up to $50,000 per violation per
    day.

(b) PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.--An individual subjected to
    emotion recognition technology in violation of this Act
    may recover liquidated damages of $5,000 per violation,
    or actual damages, whichever is greater, plus reasonable
    attorneys' fees.

(c) STATE AG ENFORCEMENT.--State attorneys general may bring
    civil actions to enforce this Act.

SEC. 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 6 months after the date of
enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- The definition covers the full range of emotion recognition modalities (facial, voice, gait, physiological)
- The prohibition is comprehensive across government, employment, commercial, and educational contexts
- Narrow exceptions for clinical healthcare and approved academic research preserve legitimate scientific investigation
- The EU AI Act (2024) provides international precedent for banning emotion recognition in workplaces and educational settings
- The scientific basis is strong: Barrett et al. (2019) demonstrated that facial expressions do not reliably indicate emotions

## State Model Legislation

### Model State Biometric Information Privacy Act

**Purpose**: Provide a template for BIPA-style state legislation with a private right of action.

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Biometric Information
Privacy Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.

(a) "Biometric identifier" means a retina or iris scan,
    fingerprint, voiceprint, scan of hand or face geometry,
    or any other biological characteristic that can be used
    to identify a specific individual. Biometric identifier
    does not include writing samples, written signatures,
    photographs, demographic data, tattoo descriptions,
    or physical descriptions.

(b) "Biometric information" means any information, regardless
    of how it is captured, converted, stored, or shared,
    based on an individual's biometric identifier used to
    identify an individual.

(c) "Private entity" means any individual, partnership,
    corporation, limited liability company, association, or
    other group, however organized. "Private entity" does
    not include a State or local government agency.

(d) "Written release" means informed written consent.

SECTION 3. RETENTION; COLLECTION; DISCLOSURE; DESTRUCTION.

(a) A private entity in possession of biometric identifiers
    or biometric information shall develop a written policy,
    made available to the public, establishing a retention
    schedule and guidelines for permanently destroying
    biometric identifiers and biometric information when
    the initial purpose for collecting or obtaining such
    identifiers or information has been satisfied or within
    3 years of the individual's last interaction with the
    private entity, whichever occurs first.

(b) No private entity may collect, capture, purchase,
    receive through trade, or otherwise obtain a person's
    biometric identifier or biometric information, unless
    it first:
    (1) informs the subject in writing that a biometric
        identifier or biometric information is being
        collected or stored;
    (2) informs the subject in writing of the specific
        purpose and length of term for which a biometric
        identifier or biometric information is being
        collected, stored, and used; and
    (3) receives a written release executed by the subject.

(c) No private entity in possession of a biometric identifier
    or biometric information may sell, lease, trade, or
    otherwise profit from a person's biometric identifier
    or biometric information.

(d) No private entity in possession of a biometric identifier
    or biometric information may disclose, redisclose, or
    otherwise disseminate such data unless:
    (1) the subject or the subject's legally authorized
        representative consents;
    (2) the disclosure completes a financial transaction
        requested or authorized by the subject;
    (3) the disclosure is required by State or Federal law;
        or
    (4) the disclosure is required pursuant to a valid
        warrant or subpoena.

(e) A private entity in possession of biometric identifiers
    or biometric information shall store, transmit, and
    protect from disclosure all biometric identifiers and
    biometric information using the reasonable standard of
    care within the private entity's industry, and in a
    manner that is the same as or more protective than the
    manner in which the private entity stores, transmits,
    and protects other confidential and sensitive information.

SECTION 4. RIGHT OF ACTION.

(a) Any person aggrieved by a violation of this Act shall
    have a right of action in [State] court and may recover
    for each violation:
    (1) against a private entity that negligently violates
        this Act, liquidated damages of $1,000 or actual
        damages, whichever is greater;
    (2) against a private entity that intentionally or
        recklessly violates this Act, liquidated damages of
        $5,000 or actual damages, whichever is greater;
    (3) reasonable attorneys' fees and costs; and
    (4) other relief, including injunction, as the State
        court may deem appropriate.

(b) A person need not allege or prove actual injury or
    adverse effect beyond the violation itself.

(c) Each collection, sale, disclosure, or other violation
    constitutes a separate violation.

SECTION 5. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect on [date 6 months after enactment].
```

## Regulatory Framework

### FTC Rulemaking on Biometric Data

| Element | Proposed Rule |
|---------|--------------|
| **Scope** | All private entities collecting, processing, or storing biometric identifiers or biometric information |
| **Consent** | Informed, written consent required before collection; consent must be specific to purpose and duration |
| **Retention** | Maximum 3-year retention from last interaction; mandatory destruction schedule |
| **Sale prohibition** | Unfair practice to sell, lease, or trade biometric data |
| **Breach notification** | 48-hour notification for biometric data breaches (vs. standard 60-72 days) |
| **Security standards** | Minimum security requirements for biometric data storage and transmission; encryption mandatory |
| **Enforcement** | Unfair/deceptive practice finding; civil penalties; algorithmic disgorgement for systems trained on improperly collected biometric data |

### NIST Biometric Accuracy Standards

| Element | Proposed Standard |
|---------|------------------|
| **Scope** | All biometric identification and verification systems deployed by government or in commercial applications affecting public |
| **Accuracy threshold (1:1 verification)** | False acceptance rate no greater than 1:100,000; false rejection rate no greater than 1:1,000 |
| **Accuracy threshold (1:N identification)** | False positive identification rate no greater than 1:1,000,000 across all demographic groups |
| **Demographic parity** | Error rate for any demographic group may not exceed 3x the rate for the most accurate group |
| **Testing conditions** | Must include real-world conditions (variable lighting, angles, image quality), not solely laboratory conditions |
| **Certification** | NIST certification required before government deployment; recommended for commercial deployment |
| **Recertification** | Annual recertification with updated testing data |

### DHS Biometric Database Oversight

| Element | Proposed Regulation |
|---------|-------------------|
| **Scope** | HART/IDENT system, FBI NGI, and all federal biometric databases |
| **Privacy impact assessment** | Updated assessment required every 2 years; public comment period |
| **Retention limits** | Maximum retention periods by data type; mandatory destruction schedules |
| **Access controls** | Documented authorization required for each query; audit trail for all access |
| **Accuracy reporting** | Annual public reporting on accuracy, demographic performance, and misidentification rates |
| **Independent oversight** | Inspector General audit of database practices every 2 years |

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

| Issue | Analysis |
|-------|----------|
| **First Amendment** | Clearview AI has argued that facial recognition is protected speech; courts have generally rejected this, treating biometric data collection as conduct, not speech. BIPA has withstood First Amendment challenges. |
| **Fourth Amendment** | *Carpenter v. United States* (2018) expanded digital privacy protection; government facial recognition in public spaces may require warrant under evolving Fourth Amendment doctrine. |
| **Due Process** | Government use of biased facial recognition that leads to wrongful arrests raises due process concerns under the Fourteenth Amendment. |
| **Commerce Clause** | Federal authority to regulate commercial biometric data collection under the Commerce Clause is well-established. |
| **Takings** | Industry may argue that restrictions on existing biometric databases constitute regulatory takings; this argument is unlikely to succeed given the police power justification. |
| **Equal Protection** | Government use of facial recognition systems with documented racial accuracy disparities raises equal protection concerns under the Fourteenth Amendment. |

### Preemption

| Dynamic | Analysis |
|---------|----------|
| **Floor-not-ceiling approach** | Federal biometric law should establish minimum protections without preempting stronger state laws. BIPA is the most effective biometric privacy law in the world; preempting it would weaken protection. |
| **Industry preemption strategy** | Industry's primary legislative goal is a federal law that preempts BIPA. Every major industry-supported federal privacy bill has included BIPA preemption. |
| **CAN-SPAM precedent** | CAN-SPAM (2003) preempted stronger state anti-spam laws and became widely regarded as ineffective. This precedent argues strongly against preempting BIPA. |
| **State experimentation** | States serve as laboratories for policy innovation. BIPA was enacted by Illinois before any federal action; this experimentation should be preserved. |

### Enforcement Mechanism Comparison

| Mechanism | BIPA (Illinois) | CUBI (Texas) | HB 1493 (Washington) |
|-----------|----------------|--------------|---------------------|
| Private right of action | Yes ($1,000/$5,000) | No | No |
| Government enforcement | AG enforcement | AG enforcement only | AG enforcement only |
| Total enforcement (cumulative) | $6+ billion | $1.4B (single AG case) | Minimal |
| Lawsuits filed | 2,000+ | Minimal (pre-2024) | Near zero |
| Deterrence effect | Very high | Low | Very low |
| **Lesson** | PRA essential | AG enforcement alone insufficient | AG enforcement alone insufficient |

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| **On-device processing exemption abuse** | Companies process biometric data "on device" but transmit derived data (templates, embeddings, or hashes) to servers, claiming exemption from consent requirements | High |
| **Vendor-employer liability gap** | Biometric time clock vendors claim they are merely providing tools; employers claim vendors handle the data; neither takes full responsibility | High |
| **Consent through terms of service** | Companies bury biometric consent in lengthy terms of service or privacy policies rather than obtaining separate, informed written consent | High |
| **Government contractor exception** | Private companies collecting biometric data as government contractors may claim government immunity or argue they are not "private entities" under the law | Medium |
| **Biometric template vs. raw data** | Companies argue that biometric templates (mathematical representations) are not "biometric identifiers" because they cannot be reverse-engineered to the original biometric | Medium |
| **Research exception expansion** | Companies characterize commercial biometric data use as "research" to avoid consent requirements | Medium |
| **Subsidiary and affiliate sharing** | Companies share biometric data among corporate subsidiaries and affiliates, arguing this is not "disclosure" to a "third party" | Medium |
| **Implied consent through continued use** | Companies argue that individuals who continue using a service after being notified of biometric collection have impliedly consented | Low |
| **De-identification claims** | Companies claim biometric data has been "de-identified" when it retains identifying potential | Medium |

### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| **Government exemption** | Federal biometric privacy legislation covers private entities but not government agencies (which operate the largest biometric databases) | Separate legal frameworks for government and private sector; government self-exemption |
| **Cross-border enforcement** | Foreign companies (e.g., Clearview AI operating from outside Illinois) may be difficult to enforce against | Jurisdictional limitations of domestic law |
| **Rapidly evolving biometric modalities** | New biometric technologies (gait recognition, behavioral biometrics, heartbeat identification) may not be captured by definitions tied to current technologies | Technology outpaces legislative process |
| **Accuracy standards lag** | NIST standards development takes years; technology is deployed before standards exist | Institutional pace vs. technology pace |
| **Collective vs. individual harm** | Biometric surveillance harms communities and democracy even when no individual can demonstrate personal injury | Individual rights framework insufficient for collective harms |
| **Power imbalance in consent** | Workers, travelers, and people in surveilled communities cannot meaningfully refuse biometric collection | Structural power asymmetry makes consent framework insufficient |

### Rectification Procedures

1. **Close the on-device processing loophole**: Define "collection" to include the creation, transmission, or storage of any biometric template, embedding, hash, or other representation derived from a biometric identifier, regardless of where the initial processing occurs. Any data that can be used to identify or verify an individual's identity constitutes biometric information.

2. **Joint vendor-employer liability**: Establish joint and several liability for biometric technology vendors and the entities that deploy their systems. Both the vendor that provides the biometric time clock and the employer that requires workers to use it are responsible for consent, retention, and destruction compliance.

3. **Separate written consent requirement**: Specify that consent for biometric data collection must be obtained through a standalone written document, separate from any general terms of service, privacy policy, or employment agreement. The consent document must describe the specific biometric data being collected, the specific purpose, and the specific retention period.

4. **Government contractor coverage**: Explicitly include any private entity collecting biometric data under a government contract within the definition of "private entity." Government contractor status does not exempt a company from biometric privacy obligations.

5. **Functional definition of biometric data**: Define biometric identifiers and information functionally, based on whether the data can be used to identify or verify an individual, rather than by reference to specific technologies. This captures biometric templates, embeddings, and future biometric modalities.

6. **Anti-research-washing provision**: Require that any "research" use of biometric data be (a) conducted by a bona fide research institution, (b) approved by an institutional review board, and (c) not used for commercial purposes. Commercial entities may not characterize product development or improvement as "research."

7. **Affiliate sharing treated as disclosure**: Define "disclosure" to include any sharing of biometric data with any legally distinct entity, including subsidiaries, affiliates, and parent companies. Intra-corporate sharing requires the same consent as third-party sharing.

8. **Emerging biometric technology provision**: Grant the enforcing agency (FTC or state AG) authority to designate new biometric modalities as covered by the Act through rulemaking, without requiring additional legislation. This ensures that gait recognition, behavioral biometrics, heartbeat identification, and future technologies are covered as they emerge.

### General Implementation Concerns

- **Small business compliance**: Biometric time clock vendors should provide compliance tools (consent forms, retention schedules) to small business customers. Consider a brief notice-and-cure period for first-time technical violations by small businesses, while maintaining strict liability for substantive violations (collection without consent, sale of data)
- **Interaction with state laws**: Clear non-preemption language is essential. Federal law must not become a vehicle for eliminating BIPA. Companies subject to both federal and state biometric laws must comply with whichever is more protective
- **Government biometric databases**: The largest biometric databases are operated by the FBI (641+ million photos), DHS (260+ million identities), and state DMVs. Private-sector legislation alone does not address these databases. Separate government biometric oversight legislation is needed
- **International coordination**: Alignment with GDPR biometric data protections (Article 9) and EU AI Act biometric identification restrictions facilitates compliance for multinational companies and strengthens global biometric privacy norms
- **Enforcement resources**: Even with a private right of action, FTC and state AG enforcement funding is essential to address systematic violations. Adequate appropriations must accompany new authority
