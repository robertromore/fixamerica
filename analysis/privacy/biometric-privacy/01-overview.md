# Biometric Privacy: Overview

## Executive Summary

Biometric data -- fingerprints, facial geometry, iris patterns, voiceprints, gait analysis, and DNA -- is fundamentally different from other personal information. Unlike passwords, credit card numbers, or even Social Security numbers, biometric identifiers cannot be changed if compromised. They are immutable, uniquely identifying, and increasingly collected without individuals' knowledge or consent. Once a biometric database is breached or misused, the harm is permanent and irreversible.

The use of biometric technology has expanded rapidly across law enforcement, commercial applications, and workplace management. Facial recognition systems scan millions of faces without consent. Employers require fingerprint or face scans for time tracking. Retailers analyze customer emotions and demographics through in-store cameras. Social media platforms tag faces in photos automatically. Yet federal regulation of biometric data collection remains virtually nonexistent.

Only a handful of states have enacted meaningful biometric privacy laws. Illinois's Biometric Information Privacy Act (BIPA), enacted in 2008, remains the strongest biometric privacy law in the world, primarily because it includes a private right of action. BIPA has generated billions of dollars in settlements and judgments, demonstrating both the scale of unauthorized biometric data collection and the power of enforceable individual rights. Texas and Washington enacted biometric laws without private rights of action and have seen minimal enforcement. A growing number of states have included biometric data in broader comprehensive privacy laws.

Biometric privacy reform requires federal legislation establishing consent requirements, use limitations, and retention and destruction schedules for biometric data, backed by strong enforcement including a private right of action.

## Core Tensions

- **Security vs. privacy** - Biometrics can strengthen authentication but create permanent surveillance risk if compromised
- **Law enforcement utility vs. civil liberties** - Facial recognition aids investigations but enables mass surveillance and disproportionately misidentifies people of color
- **Commercial convenience vs. consent** - Biometric authentication is convenient but collection often occurs without meaningful consent
- **Immutability vs. breach risk** - Unlike passwords, compromised biometric data cannot be reissued
- **Accuracy disparities vs. equal treatment** - Facial recognition systems perform significantly worse on darker-skinned individuals and women, creating discriminatory outcomes
- **Innovation vs. precaution** - Industry argues regulation should wait for technology to mature; civil liberties advocates argue the opposite

## Key Questions

1. Should commercial collection of biometric data require affirmative opt-in consent?
2. Should government use of facial recognition in public spaces be banned, moratorium-subject, or regulated?
3. How should biometric data retention and destruction be regulated?
4. What enforcement mechanisms are most effective -- private right of action, government enforcement, or both?
5. How should accuracy and bias requirements apply to biometric systems?
6. Should there be a federal biometric privacy law, and should it preempt state laws like BIPA?
7. What role should biometric data play in immigration enforcement and border security?

## Vision of Success

A successful biometric privacy framework would:

- Require informed, written consent before any collection of biometric identifiers or information
- Prohibit the sale, lease, or trade of biometric data
- Mandate retention schedules and destruction timelines
- Provide a private right of action with meaningful statutory damages
- Impose a moratorium on government facial recognition in public spaces pending accuracy and bias standards
- Require bias auditing and minimum accuracy standards for all biometric systems
- Establish federal baseline protections without preempting stronger state laws (Illinois BIPA)
- Create accountability for biometric data breaches with heightened penalties reflecting immutability
- Ensure that biometric authentication technologies can be used securely without creating surveillance infrastructure
