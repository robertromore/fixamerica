# Biometric Privacy: Actions

## Overview

Biometric privacy is an area where individual actions -- from opting out of facial recognition to advocating for local bans -- can have meaningful impact. Unlike many privacy issues where individual action is insufficient, the local political landscape for biometric privacy is unusually favorable: 20+ jurisdictions have already banned government facial recognition, and BIPA-style legislation has genuine bipartisan appeal. This guide provides concrete steps for individuals, communities, and organizations.

## Individual Protection Actions

### Immediate Steps (Low Effort)

- **Disable facial recognition features on devices where possible**: Review settings on phones, laptops, and apps; use PIN or password alternatives if you prefer not to use biometric authentication
- **Opt out of facial recognition at airports**: U.S. citizens can opt out of TSA facial recognition screening (ask for manual document check); look for opt-out signage or ask the TSA agent
- **Limit social media photo uploads**: Reduce the number of facial images available for scraping; adjust privacy settings on existing photos
- **Disable face tagging on social media**: Turn off automatic face recognition/tagging in Facebook, Google Photos, and other platforms
- **Review employer biometric practices**: Ask your employer if they collect fingerprints, facial scans, or other biometric data; request their retention and destruction policy

### Moderate Steps (Some Effort)

- **Exercise state privacy rights for biometric data**: If you live in a state with a privacy law that covers biometric data, submit data access and deletion requests
- **File BIPA claims if applicable**: If you are an Illinois resident and your biometric data was collected without written consent, you may have legal claims; consult an attorney
- **Opt out of consumer DNA sharing**: If you have used 23andMe, AncestryDNA, or similar services, opt out of research participation and third-party sharing; request data deletion if desired
- **Use privacy-protecting tools**: Anti-facial recognition glasses, makeup patterns, or clothing (research stage; effectiveness varies)
- **Request information from employers**: Under BIPA (Illinois) or other state laws, request written disclosure of biometric data practices from your employer

### Advanced Steps (Higher Effort)

- **Participate in facial recognition audits**: Support researchers testing facial recognition accuracy by participating in studies or providing feedback
- **Document biometric surveillance in your community**: Note locations of facial recognition cameras, biometric access systems, and other biometric collection; share with advocacy organizations
- **Support BIPA litigation**: If you have experienced unauthorized biometric data collection, consult with a BIPA attorney about potential claims
- **Request your FBI/DHS biometric records**: File Privacy Act requests (FBI) or FOIA requests to understand what biometric data the government holds on you

## Advocacy Actions

### Local Action

- **Advocate for a local facial recognition ban**: Contact city council members and advocate for banning or restricting government use of facial recognition in your jurisdiction. 20+ cities have done this successfully
- **Attend city council meetings**: When surveillance technology is on the agenda, provide public comment opposing facial recognition deployment without community consent
- **Support community surveillance oversight**: Advocate for community control over police surveillance technology (CCOPS ordinances modeled on ACLU Community Control Over Police Surveillance model)
- **Engage school boards**: Advocate against facial recognition in schools and on school property

### State Action

- **Support BIPA-style legislation**: If your state does not have a dedicated biometric privacy law, contact state legislators to support BIPA-style legislation *with a private right of action*
- **Oppose BIPA weakening**: If you are in Illinois, oppose efforts to amend BIPA to reduce damages or eliminate the private right of action
- **Support comprehensive state privacy law**: If your state is considering a comprehensive privacy law, advocate for strong biometric data protections including opt-in consent for sensitive biometric data

### Federal Action

- **Contact your federal representatives**: Urge support for federal biometric privacy legislation and government facial recognition restrictions

### Template Email to Federal Legislators

> Subject: Support Federal Biometric Privacy Legislation
>
> Dear [Senator/Representative] [Last Name],
>
> I am writing as your constituent to urge you to support federal biometric privacy legislation that protects Americans from unauthorized collection and use of their biometric data -- fingerprints, facial geometry, iris scans, voiceprints, and DNA.
>
> Biometric data is fundamentally different from other personal information: it cannot be changed if compromised. A stolen password can be reset. A compromised fingerprint is compromised forever.
>
> Yet there is no federal law regulating biometric data collection. Facial recognition systems include photos of over half of American adults without their knowledge or consent. Employers collect fingerprints and facial scans from millions of workers. Data brokers sell biometric information. And facial recognition technology is significantly less accurate for Black Americans, leading to wrongful arrests.
>
> I urge you to support legislation that:
>
> - Requires informed, written consent before any biometric data collection
> - Prohibits the sale of biometric data
> - Mandates retention schedules and timely destruction
> - Provides a private right of action so individuals can enforce their rights
> - Establishes accuracy and bias standards for biometric systems
> - Imposes a moratorium on government facial recognition in public spaces
> - Does not preempt stronger state laws like Illinois's BIPA
>
> [Personal story if applicable -- e.g., employer collected fingerprints without consent, concern about facial recognition in your community]
>
> Thank you for your attention to this critical issue.
>
> Sincerely,
> [Name]
> [Address]

## Talking Points

### For Conversations with Friends and Family

- "Your fingerprint or face scan can never be changed if it's stolen in a data breach. That's why biometric data needs stronger protection than a password."
- "Facial recognition is significantly less accurate for Black people, especially Black women. People have been wrongfully arrested because of these errors."
- "Over half of American adults are in facial recognition databases used by law enforcement, and they never consented to being included."
- "Illinois's biometric privacy law has generated over $6 billion in accountability for unauthorized collection. That tells you how much biometric data has been collected without consent."
- "If your employer makes you scan your fingerprint to clock in, they should have to get your written consent first and tell you how long they'll keep that data."

### For Conversations with Policymakers

- "BIPA is the most effective privacy enforcement mechanism in the United States. It should be the model for federal legislation, not replaced by a weaker federal standard."
- "The comparison between Illinois (BIPA with private right of action) and Texas/Washington (biometric laws without PRA) is a natural experiment: private right of action is essential for enforcement."
- "A federal facial recognition moratorium would give Congress and NIST time to develop accuracy and bias standards before deployment continues expanding."
- "Every known wrongful arrest from facial recognition has involved a Black individual. This is a civil rights issue, not just a privacy issue."
- "Biometric data's immutability means that once it's compromised, the individual is permanently at risk. This warrants stronger protections than we give passwords or credit card numbers."

## Community Actions

- **Host a community screening**: Show "Coded Bias" (documentary on facial recognition bias) at a library, community center, or church, followed by discussion
- **Map local surveillance**: Document facial recognition cameras and biometric collection in your community; share findings publicly
- **Support affected workers**: If you know workers whose biometric data is being collected without consent, connect them with legal resources
- **Engage faith communities**: Frame biometric privacy as a dignity and justice issue; seek resolutions from faith organizations
- **Partner with civil rights organizations**: Biometric privacy is a racial justice issue; build coalitions with NAACP, Urban League, and other civil rights organizations
