# Biometric Privacy: Opposition

## Overview

Opposition to biometric privacy regulation comes from two distinct camps: law enforcement agencies that rely on biometric databases for identification and investigation, and the technology industry and employer groups that profit from biometric data collection. These camps deploy different arguments but share the goal of preventing enforceable biometric privacy legislation, particularly laws with a private right of action.

## Opposition Landscape

| Opponent | Primary Concern | Political Lever | Effectiveness |
|----------|----------------|-----------------|---------------|
| FBI / DHS / ICE | Unrestricted biometric database access | National security framing; executive branch influence | Very High |
| State/local law enforcement | Facial recognition for investigations | Public safety framing; police union lobbying | High |
| Clearview AI / biometric vendors | Revenue from facial recognition sales | Lobbying; First Amendment litigation | High |
| Employer associations (Chamber, NAM) | Biometric time clock liability | BIPA "litigation crisis" framing | High |

| Consumer DNA companies | Genetic data business model | Privacy policy claims; consumer choice framing | Moderate |

## Detailed Argument Analysis

### Argument 1: Facial Recognition Is Essential for Public Safety

**Claim**: Facial recognition technology is a critical tool for solving crimes, finding missing persons, and preventing terrorism. Banning or restricting it will make communities less safe.

**Evidence Cited**:

- Facial recognition has aided in solving child exploitation cases (NCMEC partnerships)
- Facial recognition helped identify Capitol rioters (January 6, 2021)
- Missing persons cases have been resolved using facial recognition
- Interpol and international partners share biometric data for counterterrorism

**Reality**:

- Law enforcement solved crimes before facial recognition and continues to solve the vast majority of cases without it
- No controlled study demonstrates that facial recognition reduces overall crime rates
- All documented wrongful arrests from facial recognition have involved Black individuals, creating a civil rights crisis that undermines the legitimacy of the technology
- NIST testing shows that one-to-many matching (the type used in investigations) has significantly higher error rates than one-to-one verification
- Many jurisdictions that have banned government facial recognition (San Francisco, Boston) have not experienced public safety deterioration
- The public safety argument is unfalsifiable: any restriction can be framed as potentially preventing a hypothetical future case

**Rebuttal**: Public safety does not require unrestricted, unregulated biometric surveillance. Warrant requirements, accuracy standards, bias auditing, and use limitations can preserve legitimate law enforcement tools while preventing mass surveillance and wrongful identification. The question is not whether facial recognition can ever be useful but whether unrestricted deployment is worth the civil rights costs.

### Argument 2: BIPA Creates a "Litigation Crisis"

**Claim**: Illinois's BIPA has created a flood of frivolous lawsuits that punish legitimate businesses, particularly after *Cothron v. White Castle* (2023) made each biometric scan a separate violation with potential per-scan damages.

**Evidence Cited**:

- 2,000+ BIPA lawsuits filed
- *Cothron* potentially exposes employers to massive per-scan damages
- BIPA compliance costs for small and medium businesses
- Some BIPA lawsuits target businesses with minimal biometric data practices
- BIPA settlements totaling $6+ billion

**Reality**:

- BIPA litigation is high because BIPA is the only biometric privacy law with a private right of action; the volume reflects the scale of unauthorized biometric collection, not frivolous litigation
- Companies that comply with BIPA (obtain written consent, publish retention schedules, do not sell data) do not face lawsuits
- The $6+ billion in settlements and judgments reflects genuine violations of consent requirements, not spurious claims
- Texas enacted CUBI without a private right of action in 2009; it produced near-zero enforcement until the AG brought the Meta case in 2024 -- demonstrating that without PRA, biometric laws go unenforced
- Washington's law (also no PRA) has produced even less enforcement than Texas
- Courts have tools to manage litigation volume (class certification standards, standing requirements, sanctions for frivolous claims)

**Rebuttal**: The "litigation crisis" is actually an "accountability crisis." BIPA lawsuits reflect the scale of unauthorized biometric data collection, not abuse of the legal system. The comparison between Illinois (strong enforcement with PRA) and Texas/Washington (minimal enforcement without PRA) is the most important evidence: enforcement mechanisms determine whether biometric privacy laws work. Companies that obtain consent face no liability.

### Argument 3: Biometric Technology Is Improving and Regulation Is Premature

**Claim**: Facial recognition accuracy is improving rapidly. Regulation should wait until the technology matures rather than restricting beneficial uses based on current limitations.

**Evidence Cited**:

- NIST FRVT shows significant accuracy improvements in top algorithms
- AI advances are reducing demographic accuracy disparities
- Technology will continue improving with more training data and better algorithms
- Premature regulation could lock in suboptimal approaches

**Reality**:

- Accuracy improvements in laboratory settings do not translate directly to real-world conditions (variable lighting, angles, image quality)
- Demographic disparities persist in NIST testing even as overall accuracy improves
- "Wait for improvement" arguments have been made for over a decade; they serve to delay regulation indefinitely
- Once biometric databases are created and facial recognition is deployed, the privacy harm has occurred and cannot be undone by later accuracy improvements
- The EU did not wait for technology to mature before regulating; the AI Act (2024) restricts real-time biometric identification now
- Wrongful arrests are happening now, not in some future state of imperfect technology

**Rebuttal**: The argument to wait for technology to improve while deploying it today creates a paradox: the people harmed by current inaccuracies are asked to bear the cost of industry's R&D process. Regulation should set accuracy and bias standards that technology must meet *before* deployment, not after. The appropriate response to imperfect technology is not no regulation but accuracy-based regulation.

---

### Argument 4: Consent Requirements Are Impractical for Real-Time Systems

**Claim**: Requiring consent before biometric collection is impractical for real-time applications like security cameras, stadium entry, and public-space monitoring.

**Evidence Cited**:

- Security cameras cannot stop to obtain consent from every individual
- Event venues cannot consent-process every attendee
- Real-time monitoring for missing persons or Amber Alerts requires continuous scanning
- Consent requirements would effectively ban facial recognition in public spaces

**Reality**:

- The impracticality of consent for mass surveillance is precisely the argument *for* restricting mass biometric surveillance, not for exempting it from consent requirements
- The GDPR and EU AI Act restrict real-time biometric identification in public spaces for exactly this reason: if consent cannot be meaningfully obtained, the practice should not occur
- Consent is perfectly practical for most biometric collection: employer time clocks, consumer device enrollment, access control, financial authentication
- The argument conflates targeted, warranted facial recognition (searching a suspect's image against a database) with mass real-time surveillance (scanning everyone in a crowd) -- only the latter is inherently consent-incompatible

**Rebuttal**: The impracticability of consent for mass surveillance is not an argument for eliminating consent -- it is an argument for restricting mass biometric surveillance. Consent works for almost all biometric applications. The narrow category of applications where consent is impractical (mass public surveillance) is precisely the category that should face the highest scrutiny.

### Argument 5: Biometric Data Is Already Protected by General Privacy Laws

**Claim**: Biometric data does not need dedicated legislation because it is covered by existing privacy frameworks, including CCPA/CPRA, state comprehensive privacy laws, and FTC enforcement.

**Evidence Cited**:

- 19 state comprehensive privacy laws classify biometric data as "sensitive" requiring opt-in consent
- California CPRA provides deletion, access, and correction rights for biometric data
- FTC has brought enforcement actions involving biometric data (Rite Aid, 2023)
- General data breach notification laws cover biometric data

**Reality**:

- State comprehensive privacy laws generally lack private right of action for biometric violations (except California for data breaches)
- "Sensitive data" classification in state laws provides opt-in consent requirements but does not include BIPA-specific protections: written consent, retention and destruction schedules, prohibition on sale, and meaningful private enforcement
- FTC has brought one biometric-specific action (Rite Aid); this is grossly insufficient for the scale of biometric data collection
- Biometric data's immutability makes it fundamentally different from other "sensitive data" categories and warrants dedicated protections
- BIPA has generated $6+ billion in accountability; all other state laws combined have generated a tiny fraction of that for biometric violations

**Rebuttal**: Biometric data is categorically different from other personal data because it cannot be changed. Including biometric data as one category among many in general privacy laws dilutes the unique protections needed. BIPA's success demonstrates that dedicated biometric legislation with a private right of action is far more effective than general privacy law classification.

---

### Argument 6: Federal Law Should Preempt BIPA

**Claim**: A federal biometric privacy standard should replace the patchwork of state laws, including BIPA, to create consistency for businesses operating nationwide.

**Evidence Cited**:

- Different states have different standards for biometric data
- Multistate employers face compliance complexity
- BIPA's private right of action creates disproportionate exposure for Illinois operations
- Uniform federal standard would benefit consumers and businesses

**Reality**:

- The "patchwork" argument is the same one industry uses to oppose all state privacy laws; the solution offered is always a weaker federal standard
- BIPA is the strongest biometric privacy law in the world; preempting it would eliminate the most effective enforcement mechanism for biometric privacy
- Federal preemption of BIPA would reward companies that violated Illinois residents' biometric rights by removing accountability going forward
- A federal floor (minimum standard without preempting stronger state laws) addresses the consistency concern without weakening the strongest protections
- Industry's actual goal is not consistency but weakness: a federal law without a private right of action would effectively deregulate biometric data collection

**Rebuttal**: Federal biometric legislation should establish a floor, not a ceiling. Preempting BIPA would eliminate the most effective biometric privacy enforcement mechanism in the world. Industry's preemption argument is designed to achieve deregulation, not consistency.

## Counter-Strategies

### For Public Safety Arguments

- Distinguish between targeted warranted searches and mass surveillance
- Cite wrongful arrest cases showing current accuracy is insufficient
- Point to jurisdictions with bans that maintain public safety
- Propose accuracy and bias standards as prerequisite for deployment

### For Litigation Crisis Arguments

- Compare BIPA enforcement to Texas/Washington non-enforcement
- Emphasize that compliant companies face no liability
- Note that settlements reflect genuine violations, not frivolous claims
- Propose reasonable safeguards (notice-and-cure for first-time violations) without eliminating PRA

### For Premature Regulation Arguments

- Cite decade of "wait for improvement" arguments without improvement sufficient to prevent wrongful arrests
- Point to EU AI Act as model for regulating imperfect technology now
- Emphasize that deployment creates irreversible privacy harms
- Propose accuracy standards that technology must meet before deployment

### For Consent Impracticality Arguments

- Distinguish consent-compatible uses (employer, consumer, access control) from mass surveillance
- Argue that consent-incompatible uses should be restricted, not exempted
- Cite GDPR/EU AI Act approach as viable model
- Emphasize that most biometric applications can accommodate consent

### For General Privacy Law Arguments

- Document BIPA's enforcement record vs. general privacy law enforcement of biometric provisions
- Emphasize immutability as distinguishing factor requiring dedicated protections
- Cite specific BIPA provisions (written consent, retention schedule, no sale) absent from general laws
- Argue for both dedicated biometric legislation and general privacy law inclusion

### For Federal Preemption Arguments

- Advocate federal floor without ceiling preemption
- Cite CAN-SPAM as cautionary example of preemption weakening protection
- Emphasize that BIPA is working and should be preserved
- Frame preemption as industry reward for non-compliance
