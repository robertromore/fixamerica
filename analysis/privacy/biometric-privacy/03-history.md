# Biometric Privacy: History

## Overview

The history of biometric identification in America spans from 19th-century fingerprinting through today's AI-powered facial recognition systems. Each technological advance has expanded the collection and use of biometric data while legal protections have lagged far behind. The key turning point came in 2008 when Illinois enacted BIPA, the first law to treat biometric data as a distinct category requiring specific protections. BIPA's private right of action has proven to be the most effective mechanism for enforcing biometric privacy rights.

---

## Era 1: Analog Biometrics (1880s-1990s)

### Context

Biometric identification began with fingerprinting, which moved from scientific curiosity to law enforcement tool in the late 19th century. For a century, biometric data collection was primarily a government function limited to criminal justice and immigration.

### Key Developments

- **1882** - Alphonse Bertillon develops anthropometric identification system in France; adopted by police departments
- **1892** - Sir Francis Galton publishes *Finger Prints*, establishing fingerprint classification
- **1903** - New York State prisons begin fingerprinting inmates
- **1924** - FBI establishes Identification Division; centralizes fingerprint records
- **1930s-1950s** - Fingerprinting expands to military service, government employment, some occupational licensing
- **1953** - FBI fingerprint collection reaches 100 million cards
- **1965** - Automated Fingerprint Identification Systems (AFIS) begin development
- **1984** - DNA fingerprinting discovered by Sir Alec Jeffreys
- **1990** - FBI begins deploying AFIS nationwide
- **1994** - **DNA Identification Act** establishes CODIS (Combined DNA Index System); FBI authorized to maintain national DNA database
- **1998** - FBI launches Integrated Automated Fingerprint Identification System (IAFIS)

### Lessons

- Government biometric collection began as limited criminal justice tool but steadily expanded scope
- No privacy legislation accompanied any expansion of biometric data collection
- DNA databases established through legislation but with minimal privacy debate
- The pattern of technology deployment first, regulation later, began in this era

## Era 2: Digital Biometrics and Post-9/11 Expansion (2001-2008)

### Context

The September 11 attacks dramatically accelerated government biometric collection. Border security, aviation security, and intelligence programs drove massive expansion of fingerprint, facial, and iris databases. Simultaneously, commercial biometric technology began emerging in consumer devices and workplace applications.

### Key Developments

- **2001** - September 11 attacks lead to immediate calls for biometric identification at borders
- **2002** - **Enhanced Border Security and Visa Entry Reform Act** mandates biometric identifiers for travel documents
- **2003** - DHS creates US-VISIT program (later OBIM); begins collecting fingerprints and photographs from foreign visitors at ports of entry
- **2004** - DHS IDENT database established; begins aggregating biometric data across immigration, border, and law enforcement
- **2004** - **Intelligence Reform and Terrorism Prevention Act** mandates biometric entry-exit system
- **2005** - FBI begins Next Generation Identification (NGI) program development to replace IAFIS
- **2005** - UK introduces biometric passports; U.S. follows
- **2006** - Commercial fingerprint sensors appear in laptops (Lenovo, HP)
- **2007** - Apple files patents for fingerprint authentication (leading to Touch ID in 2013)
- **2008** - **Illinois enacts Biometric Information Privacy Act (BIPA)** -- first dedicated biometric privacy law in the world

### Lessons

- Post-9/11 security imperatives overrode privacy concerns and drove massive biometric database expansion
- Government biometric collection expanded with minimal Congressional debate on privacy implications
- BIPA was enacted proactively, before widespread commercial biometric collection, showing foresight is possible
- BIPA was motivated in part by the bankruptcy of a fingerprint-scanning company (Pay By Touch), raising questions about what happens to biometric data when companies fail

## Era 3: Consumer Biometrics and Facial Recognition (2009-2017)

### Context

Biometric technology entered mainstream consumer devices and commercial applications. Smartphones with fingerprint sensors became ubiquitous. Facebook launched face recognition tagging for billions of users. Law enforcement agencies quietly deployed facial recognition with minimal oversight or public awareness.

### Key Developments

- **2009** - **Texas enacts Capture or Use of Biometric Identifier Act (CUBI)** -- second state biometric law, but without private right of action
- **2010** - Facebook launches facial recognition photo tagging globally (default: enabled); no opt-in consent
- **2011** - FBI deploys facial recognition pilot through NGI program
- **2011** - Samsung introduces face unlock in Android; early implementation unreliable
- **2013** - **Apple launches Touch ID** (fingerprint sensor on iPhone 5s) -- first mass-market biometric device authentication
- **2013** - FBI NGI system goes operational with facial recognition capability
- **2014** - FBI Interstate Photo System links DMV databases for facial recognition searches
- **2015** - FBI facial recognition database reaches 400+ million photos (GAO estimate)
- **2016** - Georgetown Law Center on Privacy and Technology publishes *The Perpetual Line-Up*, revealing that facial recognition databases include photos of 117 million American adults
- **2016** - Snapchat launches face filter technology (Looksery acquisition)
- **2017** - **Washington enacts biometric privacy law (HB 1493)** -- third state, no private right of action
- **2017** - **Apple launches Face ID** (iPhone X) -- mass-market facial recognition authentication
- **2017** - **Rapid DNA Act** authorizes use of rapid DNA analysis at booking stations

### Lessons

- Consumer adoption normalized biometric data collection (Touch ID, Face ID) even as privacy risks grew
- Facebook's unilateral deployment of facial recognition tagging for billions of users demonstrated corporate willingness to collect biometric data without meaningful consent
- Government facial recognition databases grew to include the majority of American adults without any specific legislation authorizing this expansion
- The gap between Texas/Washington laws (no private right of action, minimal enforcement) and Illinois BIPA (private right of action, massive enforcement) demonstrated that enforcement mechanism design determines whether biometric privacy laws work

## Era 4: BIPA Litigation Explosion and Facial Recognition Backlash (2018-Present)

### Context

Several converging developments made biometric privacy a major issue: BIPA litigation exploded after key court victories for plaintiffs; public awareness of facial recognition accuracy disparities and wrongful arrests grew; cities began banning facial recognition; and global regulatory efforts (GDPR, EU AI Act) created international frameworks for biometric data protection.

### Key Developments

- **2018** - **GDPR takes effect**; classifies biometric data as "special category" requiring explicit consent
- **2019** - *Rosenbach v. Six Flags* (IL Supreme Court): no need to show actual injury to sue under BIPA -- mere violation of consent requirement is sufficient
- **2019** - San Francisco becomes first city to ban government facial recognition use
- **2019** - Oakland, Berkeley, Somerville ban government facial recognition
- **2019** - Amazon shareholders vote on (and reject) limiting facial recognition sales to government; AI researchers and civil rights groups campaign
- **2019** - Robert Williams wrongfully arrested in Detroit based on facial recognition misidentification (public in 2020)
- **2020** - *Patel v. Facebook* (9th Cir.): upholds $650 million BIPA class action settlement; facial recognition tagging without consent violates BIPA
- **2020** - **Clearview AI exposed** by *New York Times*; company scraped 3+ billion images from social media for facial recognition database sold to law enforcement
- **2020** - Portland, OR bans both government and private facial recognition use (first private-use ban)
- **2020** - Amazon, IBM, Microsoft impose moratoriums or restrictions on selling facial recognition to law enforcement
- **2020** - Boston bans government facial recognition
- **2021** - Meta announces it will delete facial recognition data for 1 billion users and discontinue automatic face tagging on Facebook
- **2021** - King County, WA bans county facial recognition use
- **2022** - Facebook/Meta $650 million BIPA settlement finalized
- **2022** - Google $100 million BIPA settlement (Google Photos face grouping)
- **2022** - Clearview AI settles with ACLU; agrees to limit sales in Illinois and not sell to most private companies
- **2023** - *Cothron v. White Castle* (IL Supreme Court): **each biometric scan is a separate BIPA violation**, not just the first collection -- dramatically increases potential damages
- **2023** - FTC bans Rite Aid from using facial recognition for 5 years after inaccurate system flagged customers as shoplifters
- **2023** - *Tims v. Black Horse Carriers* (IL Supreme Court): 5-year statute of limitations applies to BIPA claims
- **2024** - Texas AG reaches $1.4 billion settlement with Meta over biometric data violations under Texas CUBI
- **2024** - EU AI Act adopted; bans real-time remote biometric identification in public spaces with limited exceptions
- **2024** - TikTok $228 million BIPA settlement (collecting face/voice data without consent)
- **2024** - DHS HART (Homeland Advanced Recognition Technology) system, replacing IDENT, begins deployment with expanded biometric capabilities
- **2025** - BIPA litigation continues at high volume; 19 states cover biometric data in comprehensive privacy laws

### Lessons

- BIPA's private right of action has been the single most effective mechanism for enforcing biometric privacy, generating billions in accountability
- Facial recognition accuracy disparities have real consequences: all known wrongful arrest cases involve Black individuals
- City-level bans on government facial recognition demonstrate local political viability but create patchwork
- Corporate retreats on facial recognition (Meta, IBM, Rite Aid) show that legal and reputational pressure works
- The gap between BIPA (with private right of action) and Texas/Washington laws (without) provides clear natural experiment: private right of action drives enforcement

## Key Lessons from History

1. **Technology deployment consistently precedes regulation** -- fingerprinting, DNA databases, facial recognition, and consumer biometrics were all deployed at scale before privacy laws addressed them
2. **Private right of action is essential** -- BIPA (with PRA) has generated billions in accountability; Texas and Washington laws (without PRA) have produced minimal enforcement
3. **Government databases expand without effective oversight** -- FBI, DHS, and state databases grew to include hundreds of millions of Americans without specific authorization from Congress
4. **Accuracy disparities create civil rights harms** -- facial recognition bias disproportionately harms Black Americans, as wrongful arrest cases demonstrate
5. **Corporate voluntary restraint is temporary and unreliable** -- Amazon, Microsoft, and IBM moratoriums on law enforcement facial recognition sales have quietly expired or been narrowed
6. **Local bans are significant but insufficient** -- city-level facial recognition bans create awareness and set precedent but do not address the broader problem of unregulated biometric data collection
