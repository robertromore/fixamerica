# Location Tracking: Resources

## Key Federal Laws and Regulations

| Law/Regulation | Citation | Relevance |
|---------------|----------|-----------|
| Fourth Amendment to the U.S. Constitution | U.S. Const. amend. IV | Protection against unreasonable searches; basis for *Carpenter* and *Jones* holdings on location data |
| Electronic Communications Privacy Act (ECPA) | 18 U.S.C. 2510-2522 (Wiretap Act); 18 U.S.C. 2701-2712 (Stored Communications Act) | Governs government access to stored communications including some location data; outdated (1986) |
| Pen Register Act | 18 U.S.C. 3121-3127 | Governs real-time collection of communications metadata; lower standard than warrant for some location-adjacent data |
| Communications Assistance for Law Enforcement Act (CALEA) | 47 U.S.C. 1001-1010 (1994) | Required carriers to build surveillance capabilities into networks; includes location capability provisions |
| FTC Act, Section 5 | 15 U.S.C. 45 | Authority over unfair and deceptive practices, including deceptive location data practices |
| California Consumer Privacy Act / CPRA | Cal. Civ. Code 1798.100-1798.199.100 | Classifies precise geolocation as sensitive personal information; consent required for sale/sharing |
| Utah Location Data Protection Act | Utah Code 77-23c (2023) | State warrant requirement for law enforcement access to location data |
| Illinois Location Privacy Protection Act | 740 ILCS 14.5 (2024) | Requires consent before collecting location data from apps; private right of action |
| Montana Electronic Data Privacy Act | SB 171 (2023) | Warrant requirement for government access to electronic data including location |

## Key Court Cases

| Case | Citation | Significance |
|------|----------|-------------|
| *Katz v. United States* | 389 U.S. 347 (1967) | Established "reasonable expectation of privacy" test; overturned trespass-only Fourth Amendment doctrine |
| *United States v. Jones* | 565 U.S. 400 (2012) | GPS tracker on vehicle is a Fourth Amendment search under trespass theory; Alito and Sotomayor concurrences foreshadowed *Carpenter* |
| *Riley v. California* | 573 U.S. 373 (2014) | Warrantless search of cell phone incident to arrest violates Fourth Amendment; recognized phones contain "privacies of life" |
| *Carpenter v. United States* | 585 U.S. 296 (2018) | Historical CSLI protected by Fourth Amendment; warrant required for 7+ days; limited but did not overturn third-party doctrine |
| *United States v. Chatrie* | 4th Cir. (2024) | First federal appellate court to hold geofence warrants violate Fourth Amendment's particularity requirement |
| *Smith v. Maryland* | 442 U.S. 735 (1979) | Third-party doctrine: no expectation of privacy in information voluntarily shared with third parties; partially limited by *Carpenter* |
| *FTC v. Kochava, Inc.* | No. 2:22-cv-00377 (D. Idaho, 2022) | FTC sued location data broker for selling data revealing visits to reproductive health clinics and other sensitive locations |

## Academic Research

### Location Data Privacy

- de Montjoye, Yves-Alexandre, et al. "Unique in the Crowd: The Privacy Bounds of Human Mobility." *Nature Scientific Reports* 3 (2013): 1376. -- Demonstrated 95% re-identification from 4 spatiotemporal data points.
- Englehardt, Steven, and Arvind Narayanan. "Online Tracking: A 1-Million-Site Measurement and Analysis." *ACM Conference on Computer and Communications Security* (2016). -- Large-scale measurement of web tracking including location data.
- Zang, Hui, and Jean Bolot. "Anonymization of Location Data Does Not Work: A Large-Scale Measurement Study." *ACM MobiCom* (2011). -- Confirmed that anonymization of location data is ineffective at scale.
- Xu, Fengli, et al. "Trajectory Recovery from Ash: User Privacy IS NOT Preserved in Aggregated Mobility Data." *WWW Conference* (2017). -- Showed individual trajectories recoverable from aggregate mobility data.
- Keegan, Matthew. "Location, Location, Location: The Proliferation of Geofence Warrants." *Stanford Technology Law Review* 25 (2022). -- Legal analysis of geofence warrant constitutional issues.

### Government Surveillance and Location Data

- Gidari, Albert. "Carpenter v. United States: The Fourth Amendment Gets a Much-Needed Update." *Stanford Law School CIS Blog* (2018). -- Analysis of Carpenter's implications and limitations.
- Freiwald, Susan. "Cell Phone Location Data and the Fourth Amendment." *Hastings Law Journal* 60 (2008): 1. -- Pre-*Carpenter* analysis of cell phone location data privacy.
- Kerr, Orin S. "The Case for the Third-Party Doctrine." *Michigan Law Review* 107 (2009): 561. -- Defense of third-party doctrine; useful for understanding counter-arguments.
- Ohm, Paul. "The Many Revolutions of Carpenter." *Harvard Journal of Law and Technology* 32 (2019): 357. -- Comprehensive analysis of *Carpenter*'s doctrinal implications.

### Connected Vehicle Privacy

- Kohler, Kathleen. "Driving Blind: Cars, Data, and Privacy." *Yale Journal on Regulation Bulletin* 40 (2023). -- Analysis of connected vehicle data privacy challenges.
- Collingwood, Lisa. "Privacy Implications and Liability Issues of Autonomous Vehicles." *Information and Communications Technology Law* 26 (2017): 32. -- Early examination of vehicle data privacy.
