# Location Tracking: Opposition Analysis

## Primary Opponents

### Location Data Broker Industry

**Who They Are**

- Companies that aggregate and sell location data: SafeGraph, Placer.ai, Babel Street, Gravy Analytics, Near Intelligence, Foursquare
- Trade associations and industry groups representing data analytics and location intelligence sectors
- Former Venntel (now Babel Street), X-Mode Social/Outlogic, and similar firms with government contracts

**Why They Oppose Reform**

- Core business model is collecting and selling location data; regulation requiring consent would collapse supply
- Government contracts (ICE, CBP, IRS, FBI) provide reliable revenue that warrant requirements would eliminate
- Transparency requirements would expose practices that rely on consumer ignorance
- Data minimization mandates conflict with comprehensive location tracking

**Opposition Intensity**: **Very High**

**Arguments Used**

| Argument | Counterpoint |
|----------|-------------|
| "Location data is anonymized and poses no privacy risk" | 95% of individuals identifiable from 4 data points (de Montjoye et al., *Nature*, 2013); Gravy Analytics breach (2025) confirmed re-identification |
| "Location intelligence powers economic growth and innovation" | EU GDPR requires consent for location data; European location analytics industry continues to function |
| "Businesses need foot traffic data for planning" | Aggregate, privacy-preserving analytics can serve business needs without tracking individuals |
| "Government purchases are not searches under the Fourth Amendment" | *Carpenter* recognized that location data reveals "the privacies of life"; purchasing what cannot be compelled is a constitutional end-run |
| "Regulation would eliminate free apps and services" | Apps can operate with location data limited to providing the requested service; advertising is not the only business model |

**Tactics:**

- Lobby through trade associations to avoid direct public identification as opponents
- Fund research emphasizing economic benefits of location data
- Maintain "anonymization" claims despite scientific consensus that location data cannot be meaningfully anonymized
- Support weak federal legislation with preemption of stronger state laws
- Frame regulation as anti-business and anti-innovation
- Argue for narrow definitions of "precise location data" to exclude certain collection methods

### Advertising Technology Industry

**Key Players**

| Organization | Role |
|-------------|------|
| Interactive Advertising Bureau (IAB) | Primary advertising industry trade association; lobbies against consent requirements |
| Network Advertising Initiative (NAI) | Self-regulatory body for online advertising; opposes prescriptive regulation |
| Google (advertising division) | Largest digital advertising company; location data central to ad targeting |
| Meta | Second-largest digital advertiser; collects location data through Facebook, Instagram, WhatsApp |
| The Trade Desk | Major demand-side platform; processes billions of location-enriched bid requests |

**Why They Oppose Reform**

- Location targeting generates $32+ billion in annual US advertising revenue (eMarketer, 2024)
- Real-time bidding infrastructure inherently exposes location data to hundreds of companies per ad auction
- Consent requirements would reduce available inventory and depress ad prices
- Privacy-by-default settings (like Apple ATT) have already reduced iOS tracking revenue

**Opposition Intensity**: **High**

**Tactics:**

- Lobby for self-regulation through NAI and IAB codes of conduct
- Argue that targeted advertising subsidizes free internet services
- Push for federal legislation that preempts stronger state privacy laws
- Develop alternative tracking methods (fingerprinting, cohort-based approaches) to circumvent privacy protections
- Fund academic research supporting the value of location-based advertising

### Wireless Carriers (via CTIA)

**Key Players**

- AT&T, Verizon, T-Mobile
- CTIA (Cellular Telecommunications Industry Association): $12+ million annual lobbying (OpenSecrets, 2024)

**Why They Oppose Reform**

- Carrier business models include data monetization from location analytics
- Government cooperation on location data requests maintains regulatory relationships
- Expanded warrant requirements for CSLI would increase compliance costs and reduce law enforcement cooperation
- ECPA reform would impose new obligations on carrier location data retention

**Opposition Intensity**: **Moderate**

**Arguments Used**

| Argument | Counterpoint |
|----------|-------------|
| "Carriers already comply with legal process for location data" | Legal process under ECPA is outdated and insufficient; *Carpenter* applied only to 7+ days of historical CSLI |
| "E911 requirements mandate location capabilities" | E911 location for emergency services does not require continuous tracking or third-party data sharing |
| "Consumers benefit from carrier location services" | Benefits can be delivered with data minimization and meaningful consent |

### Law Enforcement Agencies

**Who They Are**

- Federal: DOJ, FBI, ICE, CBP, DEA, IRS, Secret Service, DIA
- State and local police departments
- Law enforcement trade associations: Major Cities Chiefs Association, Fraternal Order of Police, National Sheriffs' Association

**Why They Oppose Reform**

- Location data purchases allow surveillance without warrants; warrant requirements would reduce investigative capability
- Geofence warrants identify suspects when police have no leads
- Immigration enforcement depends on commercial location data (Georgetown Law, "American Dragnet," 2022)
- National security agencies use commercial location data for intelligence purposes

**Opposition Intensity**: **High**

**Arguments Used**

| Argument | Counterpoint |
|----------|-------------|
| "Location data helps solve serious crimes and prevent terrorism" | Warrant requirements allow access for legitimate investigations; the question is judicial oversight, not prohibition |
| "Geofence warrants are essential when there are no suspects" | 4th Circuit in *Chatrie* (2024) held geofence warrants violate the Fourth Amendment's particularity requirement |
| "Purchasing commercially available data is not a 'search'" | *Carpenter* rejected mechanical application of third-party doctrine to digital data; government should not purchase what it cannot compel |
| "Warrant requirements will cost lives by slowing investigations" | Emergency exceptions in warrant requirements address time-sensitive cases; routine surveillance should require judicial oversight |
| "Immigration enforcement requires location tracking" | Using commercial data to circumvent constitutional protections for immigrants undermines rule of law |

**Tactics:**

- DOJ files amicus briefs opposing warrant requirements for commercial data
- Law enforcement associations testify against privacy legislation at state and federal hearings
- Frame opposition as "soft on crime" and "endangering national security"
- Cite specific cases solved using location data to generate political support
- Exploit Carpenter gaps through commercial data purchases rather than compulsory process

## Secondary Opponents

### Automakers and Vehicle Data Companies

**Key Players**

- Alliance for Automotive Innovation (representing GM, Ford, Toyota, and others)
- Connected vehicle data platforms: Otonomo, Wejo
- Insurance data partners: LexisNexis Risk Solutions, Verisk

**Why They Oppose Some Reforms**

- Connected vehicle data monetization is a growing revenue source
- Insurance data partnerships (GM/OnStar/LexisNexis) generate subscription and data-sharing revenue
- Autonomous driving development depends on extensive location and driving data
- Consent and transparency requirements would disrupt established data flows

**Opposition Intensity**: **Moderate**

- GM/LexisNexis scandal (2024) has increased public pressure, creating some industry willingness to accept limited reforms
- Automakers publicly claim to protect privacy while privately sharing data extensively

### Retail and Real Estate Industries

**Position:** Oppose restrictions on foot traffic data

- Retailers use location analytics for site selection, competitive intelligence, and marketing
- Real estate developers use foot traffic data for property valuation
- Oppose consent requirements that would reduce available aggregate data
- Argue that aggregate analytics do not identify individuals (contradicted by research)

### Private Investigation and Surveillance Firms

**Position:** Oppose restrictions on location data access

- Use commercial location data and license plate reader databases for investigations
- Serve insurance companies, attorneys, and private clients
- Oppose regulations that would limit access to people-search and location databases
- Industry is largely unregulated at the federal level

## Opposition Arguments and Counterpoints

### "Anonymized Location Data Poses No Privacy Risk"

**Argument:** Location data is stripped of personal identifiers before sale, making it anonymous and therefore not a privacy concern. Regulation of anonymous data is unnecessary.

**Counterpoint:**

- de Montjoye et al. (*Nature*, 2013) demonstrated that 95% of individuals can be uniquely identified from just 4 spatiotemporal data points
- The *New York Times* "One Nation, Tracked" (2019) investigation showed that "anonymized" location data trivially reveals home addresses, workplaces, and daily routines
- Princeton Web Transparency Project demonstrated that advertising IDs can be linked to real identities through home location inference
- The Gravy Analytics breach (January 2025) exposed precise movements of millions, confirming re-identification at scale
- The scientific consensus is that location data cannot be meaningfully anonymized; industry claims to the contrary are misleading

### "Location Data Powers Innovation and Economic Growth"

**Argument:** Location intelligence drives a $14+ billion industry that creates jobs, enables urban planning, and powers services consumers rely on daily. Regulation would stifle this economic activity.

**Counterpoint:**

- The EU's GDPR requires consent for location data processing; the European location analytics industry continues to operate and innovate
- Economic benefits are not distributed to the individuals whose data generates them; the current system extracts value from people without compensation or consent
- Privacy-preserving analytics methods (differential privacy, aggregation with k-anonymity) can serve legitimate business needs without tracking individuals
- The economic argument ignores the billions in externalized costs: stalking, surveillance, discrimination, and chilling effects on constitutional rights
- Apple's ATT demonstrated that privacy protections do not destroy the advertising economy; they shift power toward consumers

### "Warrant Requirements Will Hamper Law Enforcement"

**Argument:** Requiring warrants for government access to location data, including commercial purchases, will slow investigations, cost lives, and give criminals an advantage.

**Counterpoint:**

- Warrant requirements include emergency exceptions for imminent threats to life or safety
- The Fourth Amendment's warrant requirement has coexisted with effective law enforcement for over two centuries
- States with warrant requirements for location data (Utah, Montana) have not reported degraded law enforcement effectiveness
- The *Carpenter* court noted that the warrant requirement is "familiar" and "workable" for digital data
- Warrantless surveillance disproportionately targets marginalized communities, not serious criminals
- Multiple government agencies (ICE, IRS) have used purchased location data for routine enforcement, not emergency situations

### "Consumers Choose to Share Location Data"

**Argument:** When users allow location access on their phones, they consent to the data practices described in privacy policies. This voluntary sharing negates the need for regulation.

**Counterpoint:**

- Less than 9% of users read location-related privacy policies (Carnegie Mellon, 2023)
- Only 44% of Americans know apps share location data with third parties (Annenberg, 2023)
- Permission dialogs are designed to maximize "Allow" clicks through dark patterns
- Consent is bundled: users must accept location sharing or lose access to the app entirely
- Background collection occurs even when users believe location tracking is limited to active app use
- The *Carpenter* court explicitly rejected the argument that sharing data with a third party constitutes voluntary relinquishment of privacy

### "Federal Privacy Law Should Preempt State Laws"

**Argument:** A patchwork of state location privacy laws creates compliance burdens and inconsistency. Federal legislation should establish a single standard that preempts all state laws.

**Counterpoint:**

- State laws like California CCPA/CPRA, Utah's Location Data Protection Act, and Illinois' Location Privacy Protection Act fill critical gaps that federal law does not address
- Industry's preference for preemption is driven by wanting the weakest possible standard, not genuine concern about compliance complexity
- A federal floor that does not preempt stronger state protections is the proven model (HIPAA uses this approach)
- States serve as laboratories of democracy; preemption would eliminate innovation in location privacy protection
- Companies already comply with GDPR in Europe, CCPA in California, and other jurisdiction-specific rules; compliance complexity is manageable

## Counter-Strategy Recommendations

### Reframe the Debate

- Shift from "privacy vs. innovation" to "trust-based innovation" -- consumers who trust their data is protected are more willing to use location services
- Shift from "privacy vs. security" to "oversight vs. unchecked surveillance" -- the question is not whether law enforcement can access location data, but whether a judge must agree
- Use the Gravy Analytics breach (2025) and GM/LexisNexis scandal (2024) to demonstrate concrete, personal harms from the status quo
- Highlight that foreign adversaries can purchase the same commercial location data used to track Americans

### Demolish the Anonymization Myth

- Cite the scientific consensus (de Montjoye et al., 2013; Princeton Web Transparency Project, 2020) in every legislative and public communication
- Use the Gravy Analytics breach as a case study showing that centralized "anonymized" databases create catastrophic risk
- Advocate for statutory language that treats all location data as personal data, regardless of claimed de-identification

### Build Bipartisan Coalitions

- Emphasize national security risks (tracking of military personnel through commercial data) to engage defense hawks
- Frame warrant requirements for government purchases as a Fourth Amendment issue to engage libertarian-leaning conservatives
- Use the connected vehicle scandal (GM/LexisNexis) to engage consumer protection-oriented legislators on both sides
- Connect reproductive health location tracking to reproductive rights advocates and privacy-focused libertarians

### Leverage State Momentum

- Use Utah, Montana, Illinois, and California laws as proof that location privacy regulation is workable
- Support adoption of location-specific privacy laws in additional states to build pressure for federal action
- Coordinate state AG enforcement actions to demonstrate the need for federal standards

### Support Enforcement

- Advocate for FTC rulemaking on location data practices under existing Section 5 authority
- Support private right of action as the most effective enforcement mechanism
- Push for meaningful penalties that exceed the economic benefit of violations
- Encourage continued FTC enforcement actions against location data brokers
