# Location Tracking: History

## Overview

The history of location tracking in America reflects a recurring pattern: new technologies enable increasingly precise monitoring of physical movements, commercial applications drive mass adoption, legal frameworks fail to keep pace, and the resulting surveillance infrastructure is exploited by both private and government actors. From physical surveillance by private detectives to satellite-enabled tracking of every smartphone, each era has expanded the scope, precision, and permanence of location surveillance.

## Timeline

### Physical Surveillance Era (Before 1960)

| Year | Development | Significance |
|------|------------|--------------|
| 1850s | Pinkerton detective agency founded | Professional physical surveillance for hire; tracked union organizers, suspected criminals |
| 1908 | FBI established | Federal government begins systematic physical surveillance of individuals |
| 1920s-1960s | FBI COINTELPRO and predecessors | Physical surveillance of civil rights leaders, labor organizers, anti-war activists; J. Edgar Hoover's surveillance files |
| 1940s | Wiretapping of telephone networks | Extended surveillance beyond physical location to communications; location inferred from phone use |
| 1957 | First satellites launched (Sputnik) | Began the technological trajectory toward GPS |

### Electronic Surveillance Foundations (1960-1996)

| Year | Development | Significance |
|------|------------|--------------|
| 1965 | FCC requires wireline carriers to assist law enforcement | Established precedent for carrier cooperation with government surveillance |
| 1967 | *Katz v. United States* | Supreme Court established "reasonable expectation of privacy" test; overturned trespass-only doctrine |
| 1968 | Title III of Omnibus Crime Control Act | First comprehensive federal wiretap statute; established warrant requirement for electronic surveillance but did not address location |
| 1973 | Department of Defense launches NAVSTAR GPS program | Military satellite positioning system that would eventually enable civilian location tracking |
| 1978 | Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act (FISA) | Created FISA Court for foreign intelligence surveillance; location tracking within its scope for foreign targets |
| 1986 | Electronic Communications Privacy Act (ECPA) | Governed electronic surveillance including pen registers (which capture phone numbers, not content); location provisions were minimal and rapidly outdated |
| 1994 | Communications Assistance for Law Enforcement Act (CALEA) | Required carriers to build surveillance capabilities into telephone networks; later extended to broadband |
| 1996 | FCC E911 mandate | Required carriers to locate emergency callers; created infrastructure for cell-site location tracking |

### GPS Civilian Opening and Early Tracking (1996-2008)

| Year | Development | Significance |
|------|------------|--------------|
| 1996 | GPS made available for civilian use (Clinton executive order) | Satellite positioning became accessible for commercial applications |
| 1999 | First GPS-enabled cell phones | Consumer devices begin generating location data continuously |
| 2000 | Selective availability disabled | GPS accuracy improved from 100 meters to 10 meters for civilian users; tracking became precise |
| 2001 | Post-9/11 surveillance expansion | Patriot Act expanded government surveillance authority; location tracking became counterterrorism tool |
| 2003 | Cell tower location data retained by carriers | Major carriers began retaining CSLI indefinitely; created historical location databases |
| 2004 | First location-based mobile services | Google Maps, Foursquare predecessors began collecting user location data |
| 2005 | Real-time cell phone tracking by police | Law enforcement agencies began requesting real-time location data from carriers; no warrant standard |
| 2006 | *In re Application for Pen Register* | First federal magistrate to require probable cause for cell-site location data; other courts disagreed, creating circuit split |
| 2007 | iPhone launched | Smartphone revolution dramatically increased location data generation; GPS, WiFi, and cell data all collected |
| 2008 | App Store / Google Play launch | Mobile apps begin collecting and sharing location data at unprecedented scale |
| 2008 | Automated license plate readers proliferate | Police departments deploy ALPRs; scans stored in commercial databases (Vigilant Solutions / DRN) |

### Smartphone Tracking Era (2008-2018)

| Year | Development | Significance |
|------|------------|--------------|
| 2010 | *United States v. Maynard* (D.C. Circuit) | Court held that prolonged GPS tracking constitutes a Fourth Amendment search; presaged *Jones* |
| 2011 | "LocationGate" scandal | Researchers discovered iPhone and Android devices storing detailed location history locally; Apple and Google modified practices after public outcry |
| 2012 | *United States v. Jones* (Supreme Court) | Physical GPS tracker on vehicle is a Fourth Amendment search under trespass theory; Alito concurrence raised mosaic theory; Sotomayor concurrence questioned third-party doctrine |
| 2013 | de Montjoye et al. *Nature* paper | Demonstrated that anonymized location data can identify 95% of individuals from 4 data points; challenged "anonymization" claims |
| 2013 | NSA phone metadata program revealed (Snowden) | Bulk collection of call records including location-relevant metadata; demonstrated scale of government location surveillance |
| 2014 | Location data broker industry consolidates | Companies like PlaceIQ, Factual, and xAd aggregate billions of location data points from apps |
| 2015 | Beacon technology deployed in retail | Bluetooth beacons track shoppers' in-store movements; deployed in thousands of retail locations |
| 2016 | Pokemon Go controversy | Augmented reality game collected detailed location data on millions of users; raised awareness of app-based tracking |
| 2016 | FTC settles with InMobi | Mobile advertising company collected location data from children's apps without consent; $950,000 penalty (FTC, 2016) |
| 2017 | Geofence warrants emerge | Law enforcement begins requesting data on all devices in a geographic area from Google Sensorvault; requests grow exponentially |
| 2018 | **Carpenter v. United States** | Supreme Court held 5-4 that obtaining 7+ days of historical CSLI requires a warrant; recognized that "seismic shifts in digital technology" require updating Fourth Amendment doctrine |

### Post-Carpenter Era (2018-Present)

| Year | Development | Significance |
|------|------------|--------------|
| 2018 | Government agencies begin purchasing commercial location data | ICE, CBP, and other agencies use Venntel and similar brokers to acquire location data without warrants; *Carpenter* gap exploited |
| 2019 | *New York Times* "One Nation, Tracked" | Investigative series demonstrated that commercial location data could track individuals with precision, including tracking a senior government official; generated national attention |
| 2019 | Geofence warrants surge | Google received 8,396 geofence requests in 2019, up from 982 in 2018 (Google Transparency Report) |
| 2020 | COVID-19 contact tracing debate | Pandemic raised questions about government use of location data for public health; Apple and Google created privacy-preserving contact notification system |
| 2020 | Sen. Wyden investigation of government data purchases | Documented ICE, CBP, IRS, and other agencies purchasing commercial location data to circumvent warrant requirements |
| 2021 | Apple App Tracking Transparency (ATT) | iOS 14.5 required apps to ask permission before tracking across apps; 75% of users opted out; disrupted location advertising ecosystem |
| 2022 | *Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization* | Overturning *Roe v. Wade* created new risks for location tracking near abortion clinics; FTC and state AGs acted against location data brokers |
| 2022 | FTC sues Kochava | FTC sued location data broker for selling precise location data revealing visits to reproductive health clinics, places of worship, and other sensitive locations |
| 2022 | FTC settles with X-Mode/Outlogic | Consent order banned sale of sensitive location data |
| 2023 | Google stops storing location in Sensorvault | Google moved location data storage to individual devices, largely ending ability to respond to geofence warrants |
| 2024 | *United States v. Chatrie* (4th Circuit) | First federal appellate court to hold that geofence warrants violate the Fourth Amendment's particularity requirement |
| 2024 | GM/LexisNexis scandal | Investigation revealed General Motors shared OnStar driving data with LexisNexis, which sold it to insurers, affecting premiums without clear driver consent |
| 2024 | California, Utah, Montana pass location-specific laws | States begin enacting laws requiring consent for location data collection and warrants for government access |
| 2025 | Gravy Analytics data breach | Major location data broker breached; exposed precise location data of millions of individuals; demonstrated catastrophic risks of centralized collection |

## Key Patterns

### Recurring Dynamics

1. **Technology precedes law**: GPS was available for civilian use 22 years before *Carpenter* addressed location privacy; app tracking operated for 15+ years before meaningful regulation
2. **Commercial collection enables government surveillance**: Data collected for advertising purposes becomes government intelligence through purchase rather than legal compulsion
3. **"Anonymization" claims collapse**: Every generation of anonymization technology has been defeated by de-anonymization research, but claims persist as industry defense
4. **Narrow court rulings leave gaps**: *Jones* addressed trespassory GPS; *Carpenter* addressed CSLI; neither addressed the full scope of modern location tracking
5. **Crisis-driven expansion**: 9/11, COVID-19, and *Dobbs* each created new justifications for or heightened risks from location surveillance
6. **Industry growth outpaces regulation**: Location intelligence market grew from negligible to $14+ billion while regulatory frameworks remained largely unchanged
7. **Disproportionate impact on marginalized communities**: Immigration enforcement, protest surveillance, and reproductive health tracking disproportionately affect vulnerable populations

### The Carpenter Legacy

*Carpenter v. United States* (2018) was the most significant Fourth Amendment decision for the digital age, recognizing that:

- Historical location data reveals "the privacies of life" (quoting *Riley v. California*, 2014)
- The third-party doctrine cannot be mechanically applied to digital data
- The nature and quantity of information matters for Fourth Amendment analysis

But *Carpenter*'s limitations have allowed surveillance to continue and expand:

| What *Carpenter* Said | What It Left Open |
|----------------------|-------------------|
| CSLI requires a warrant | Does GPS, WiFi, or app data require a warrant? |
| 7+ days of records is protected | What about shorter periods? |
| Government compulsion triggers 4th Amendment | Do government purchases of commercial data? |
| Historical CSLI is covered | What about real-time tracking? |
| Cell carriers are regulated | What about data brokers? |

Source: *Carpenter v. United States*, 585 U.S. 296 (2018); scholarly analysis by Orin Kerr, Susan Freiwald, and Paul Ohm.

## Lessons

- **Court decisions are necessary but insufficient**: *Carpenter* was essential but did not solve location privacy; legislative action is required to close gaps
- **Commercial data is government surveillance data**: Any data collected commercially will eventually be available to government through purchase, subpoena, or breach
- **Privacy-by-design works**: Apple's ATT demonstrates that technical architecture changes can meaningfully reduce tracking; Google's Sensorvault decision shows companies can choose privacy-protective designs
- **Anonymization is not protection**: Location data cannot be meaningfully anonymized; legal frameworks must treat all location data as personal data
- **International models show alternatives**: GDPR's consent requirement for location data demonstrates that regulation is compatible with a functioning digital economy
