# Algorithmic Accountability: History

## Overview

The history of algorithmic accountability traces the evolution from simple rule-based automation to complex machine learning systems that now make consequential decisions affecting millions of Americans. Understanding this trajectory reveals how technological capability outpaced legal and ethical frameworks, creating the accountability vacuum that exists today.

## Timeline

### Pre-Digital Automation (Pre-1960s)

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 1936 | Alan Turing publishes "On Computable Numbers" | Theoretical foundation for automated computation |
| 1950s | Insurance actuarial tables automated | Early use of statistical models for consequential decisions |
| 1956 | Dartmouth Conference coins "artificial intelligence" | Launch of AI as a research field |

### Early Computerized Decision-Making (1960s-1980s)

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 1964 | Civil Rights Act prohibits employment discrimination | Legal framework predates algorithmic decisions but applies to their effects |
| 1968 | Fair Housing Act enacted | Prohibits discrimination in housing, including through automated tools |
| 1970 | Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA) enacted | First law regulating automated consumer data use; requires accuracy and dispute rights |
| 1974 | Equal Credit Opportunity Act (ECOA) | Prohibits lending discrimination; later applied to algorithmic underwriting |
| 1974 | Privacy Act | Governs federal agency record-keeping; early framework for automated government decisions |
| 1977 | Community Reinvestment Act | Addresses discriminatory lending patterns that later algorithms would replicate |
| 1978 | FICO score introduced | Standardized credit scoring; first widely used algorithmic assessment of individuals |
| 1986 | Expert systems peak in commercial AI | Rule-based systems used in medicine, finance; interpretable but limited |

### Rise of Data-Driven Systems (1990s-2000s)

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 1990s | Credit scoring becomes universal in lending | FICO scores used in virtually all consumer lending decisions |
| 1996 | Telecommunications Act | Deregulation accelerates data collection infrastructure |
| 1998 | Google founded; PageRank algorithm | Algorithmic ranking of information becomes foundational to internet |
| 2001 | Patriot Act | Expands government authority to use automated analysis for surveillance |
| 2002 | Total Information Awareness program proposed | DARPA program for mass automated surveillance; Congressional opposition led to defunding |
| 2005 | Applicant Tracking Systems (ATS) become widespread | Automated resume filtering becomes standard in hiring |
| 2006 | Netflix Prize competition | Demonstrates power of machine learning recommendation systems |
| 2008 | Predictive policing programs begin | CompStat-derived programs use data to direct police resources; PredPol founded |

### Machine Learning Revolution (2010-2016)

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 2010 | IBM Watson wins *Jeopardy!* | Public demonstration of AI capabilities increases commercial interest |
| 2011 | COMPAS risk assessment tool adopted widely | Northpointe's recidivism prediction tool used in sentencing and parole |

| 2012 | Obama campaign uses data analytics for voter targeting | Demonstrates algorithmic targeting in politics |

| 2020 | COVID-19 accelerates algorithmic deployment | Automated systems used for unemployment fraud detection, healthcare triage, remote proctoring |

## Key Themes in Historical Development

### Technological Capability Outpacing Regulation

Every major expansion of algorithmic decision-making has preceded regulatory response by years or decades:

- Credit scoring was deployed universally before any fairness analysis was required
- Predictive policing operated for years before independent audits revealed bias
- Healthcare algorithms affected hundreds of millions before the Obermeyer study exposed racial discrimination
- AI hiring tools screened millions of applicants before the EEOC issued guidance

### The Pattern of Scandal-Driven Reform

Algorithmic accountability advances have consistently followed the pattern of: deployment, harm, exposure (usually by journalists or researchers), public outcry, and incremental regulatory response.

| Scandal | Year | Response | Lag Time |
|---------|------|----------|----------|
| COMPAS racial bias (ProPublica) | 2016 | No federal action; some jurisdictions reconsidered use | Years, ongoing |
| Amazon hiring bias | 2018 | Internal action; no regulatory response | N/A (internal) |
| Gender Shades facial recognition bias | 2018 | Municipal bans; NIST study | 1-3 years |
| Healthcare algorithm racism | 2019 | HHS rule (2024) | 5 years |
| Michigan unemployment fraud system | 2020 | State-level reforms; no federal action | 1-2 years |
| UnitedHealth AI claim denials | 2023 | Congressional investigation; pending | Ongoing |

### Shift from Rule-Based to Opaque Systems

The accountability challenge has intensified as systems moved from interpretable rule-based decisions to opaque machine learning:

| Era | System Type | Interpretability | Accountability |
|-----|------------|-----------------|----------------|
| 1970s-1990s | Rule-based, expert systems | High: rules can be inspected | Possible but rarely required |
| 1990s-2010s | Statistical models (regression, FICO) | Moderate: factors known, weights interpretable | FCRA provides some transparency for credit |
| 2010s-present | Machine learning, neural networks | Low to none: "black box" decisions | No legal framework for accountability |
| 2020s-present | Large language models, generative AI | Very low: emergent behaviors, billions of parameters | Regulatory void |

## Lessons from History

1. **Self-regulation has consistently failed**: Companies have never voluntarily adopted meaningful algorithmic accountability without regulatory pressure or public scandal
2. **Existing civil rights laws are insufficient**: Laws designed for human decision-makers poorly address algorithmic discrimination
3. **Government is both regulator and offender**: Federal and state agencies deploy algorithmic systems with the same problems they should be regulating
4. **International examples prove regulation is feasible**: The EU's GDPR and AI Act demonstrate that algorithmic accountability requirements can coexist with innovation
5. **Marginalized communities bear disproportionate harm**: Historical pattern shows algorithmic harms concentrated on communities already facing discrimination
