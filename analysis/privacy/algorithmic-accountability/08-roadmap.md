# Algorithmic Accountability: Roadmap

## Overview

This roadmap outlines a phased strategy for establishing comprehensive algorithmic accountability in the United States. It recognizes that legislative, regulatory, and institutional reforms must advance simultaneously at federal, state, and local levels, and that coalition-building and public education are prerequisites for major federal legislation.

## Phase 1: Foundation Building (2025-2027)

### Objective

Establish transparency requirements, strengthen enforcement under existing law, and build technical and institutional capacity for algorithmic oversight.

### Federal Actions

| Action | Mechanism | Lead Actor | Timeline |
|--------|-----------|------------|----------|
| Codify OMB M-24-10 requirements | Legislation requiring AI impact assessments for federal agencies | Congress | 2025-2026 |
| Expand FTC authority for algorithmic enforcement | Statutory rulemaking authority for unfair algorithmic practices | Congress, FTC | 2025-2026 |
| EEOC algorithmic hiring guidance finalized | Binding guidance on Title VII application to AI hiring tools | EEOC | 2025 |
| CFPB adverse action enforcement | Enforcement actions against lenders failing to explain AI-driven denials | CFPB | Ongoing |
| Fund NIST algorithmic auditing standards | Appropriations for technical standard development | Congress, NIST | 2025-2026 |
| Executive order on government AI procurement | Require bias testing and transparency in all government algorithm acquisitions | White House | 2025 |

### State and Local Actions

| Action | Mechanism | Lead Actor | Timeline |
|--------|-----------|------------|----------|
| Adopt Colorado SB 24-205 model in additional states | State legislation requiring impact assessments for high-risk AI | State legislatures | 2025-2027 |
| Expand NYC Local Law 144 model | Additional cities require bias audits for automated hiring | City councils | 2025-2027 |
| State AG enforcement task forces | Coordinate state attorney general enforcement against algorithmic discrimination | State AGs (multistate) | 2025-2026 |
| Municipal algorithmic transparency ordinances | Local government disclosure requirements for government-deployed algorithms | City councils | 2025-2027 |

### Civil Society Actions

| Action | Mechanism | Lead Actor | Timeline |
|--------|-----------|------------|----------|
| Build unified advocacy coalition | Coordinate civil rights, consumer, labor, and privacy organizations | Civil society organizations | 2025 |
| Public education campaign | Educate public on algorithmic impacts through media, events, community engagement | Advocacy organizations, media | 2025-2027 |
| Document algorithmic harms | Systematic documentation and publicizing of specific harms | Researchers, journalists | Ongoing |
| Technical literacy programs | Train advocates, regulators, and legislators on algorithmic basics | Academic institutions, nonprofits | 2025-2027 |

### Milestones

- [ ] At least 5 additional states enact algorithmic accountability legislation
- [ ] EEOC issues binding guidance on algorithmic hiring
- [ ] NIST publishes auditing methodology standards
- [ ] FTC brings 5+ enforcement actions against algorithmic discrimination
- [ ] Federal government AI procurement standards established

## Phase 2: Comprehensive Federal Legislation (2027-2029)

### Objective

Enact comprehensive federal algorithmic accountability legislation establishing mandatory transparency, impact assessment, auditing, and enforcement with private right of action.

### Legislative Strategy

| Component | Priority | Coalition Support |
|-----------|----------|------------------|
| **Mandatory disclosure** | Highest | Bipartisan -- transparency is broadly popular |
| **Impact assessment requirement** | High | Moderate bipartisan potential; industry may accept as alternative to stronger measures |
| **Independent auditing** | High | Moderate -- industry prefers self-selected auditors |
| **Updated disparate impact standards** | High | Progressive + civil rights coalition; some bipartisan potential |
| **Private right of action** | High but contentious | Progressive + consumer coalition; strong industry opposition |
| **Federal accountability office** | Medium | Progressive priority; fiscal conservatives will oppose |
| **State law preservation** | Essential | Progressive + state-rights coalition; industry opposes |

### Federal Legislation Package

| Bill | Focus | Path |
|------|-------|------|
| **Algorithmic Accountability Act** | Comprehensive framework: transparency, impact assessment, auditing | Commerce and Judiciary committees |
| **AI Civil Rights Act** | Update anti-discrimination law for algorithmic context | Judiciary Committee |
| **Government Algorithm Transparency Act** | Mandatory disclosure and public auditing of all government algorithmic systems | Government Operations |
| **AI Auditing Standards Act** | Establish auditor certification and methodology standards | Commerce Committee |

### Key Political Challenges

| Challenge | Strategy |
|-----------|----------|
| Industry lobbying against binding requirements | Build public pressure through documented harms; frame as consumer protection |
| Preemption demands | Insist on floor-not-ceiling approach; preserve state authority |
| Private right of action opposition | Demonstrate inadequacy of agency-only enforcement; point to CFPB model |
| "Innovation" framing | Counter with evidence that accountability improves innovation quality |
| Partisan gridlock | Build cross-partisan coalition around shared interests (transparency, government accountability, children's protection) |

### Milestones

- [ ] Comprehensive algorithmic accountability bill introduced with bipartisan co-sponsors
- [ ] Congressional hearings on algorithmic harms in employment, credit, criminal justice, and healthcare
- [ ] Coalition of 50+ organizations supporting comprehensive legislation
- [ ] At least one major tech company publicly supports mandatory accountability
- [ ] Comprehensive federal algorithmic accountability law enacted

## Phase 3: Institutional Development (2029-2031)

### Objective

Build the institutional infrastructure needed for effective ongoing algorithmic oversight, including dedicated regulatory capacity, auditing ecosystem, and international coordination.

### Institutional Actions

| Action | Mechanism | Lead Actor | Timeline |
|--------|-----------|------------|----------|
| Establish Federal Algorithmic Accountability Office | New office within FTC or independent agency | Congress | 2029 |
| Fund regulatory technical capacity | Appropriations for hiring data scientists, engineers, auditors | Congress | 2029-2031 |
| Certify algorithmic auditing firms | Establish standards and certification process | NIST, new office | 2029-2030 |
| Build state and local capacity | Federal grants to state AGs and local agencies | Congress, new office | 2029-2031 |
| International harmonization | Align US standards with EU AI Act, OECD principles | State Department, new office | 2029-2031 |

### Enforcement Development

| Action | Mechanism | Timeline |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| First wave of enforcement under new law | Agency actions against non-compliant systems | 2029-2030 |
| Private right of action cases | Individuals and organizations bring claims under new law | 2029-2030 |
| Auditing ecosystem matures | Third-party auditing industry develops standards and capacity | 2029-2031 |
| Public algorithm registry operational | Registry of high-stakes algorithmic systems publicly accessible | 2030 |
| Periodic review and update cycle | Regular assessment of standards and requirements | 2031+ |

### Milestones

- [ ] Federal Algorithmic Accountability Office operational with adequate funding
- [ ] First successful enforcement actions under comprehensive law
- [ ] Certified auditing ecosystem serving all 50 states
- [ ] International mutual recognition agreements in place
- [ ] Public registry containing 1,000+ high-stakes algorithmic systems

## Phase 4: Maturation and Adaptation (2031-2035)

### Objective

Refine and strengthen the accountability framework based on implementation experience, technological evolution, and emerging challenges.

### Key Activities

| Activity | Description | Timeline |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| **Framework review** | Comprehensive assessment of accountability law's effectiveness, coverage, and enforcement | 2031 |
| **Technical standard updates** | Revise auditing methodologies and fairness metrics as technology evolves | Ongoing |
| **Scope expansion** | Extend requirements to emerging algorithmic applications (autonomous vehicles, generative AI in decisions, brain-computer interfaces) | As needed |
| **International leadership** | US participation in global AI governance frameworks | 2031-2035 |
| **Community oversight development** | Affected community participation in algorithmic oversight processes | 2031-2035 |

### Milestones

- [ ] Five-year review of comprehensive law completed with recommendations
- [ ] Documented reduction in algorithmic discrimination in regulated sectors
- [ ] Effective community participation in algorithmic governance
- [ ] US algorithmic accountability framework recognized as international model
- [ ] Emerging technologies covered by adaptive regulatory framework

## Risk Assessment

| Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation |
|------|-----------|--------|------------|
| Industry captures regulatory process | High | High | Strong civil society participation; revolving door restrictions; independent funding |
| Federal legislation preempts stronger state laws | Moderate | High | Insist on floor-not-ceiling in all legislation; preserve state authority |
| Regulatory capacity insufficient | Moderate | High | Adequate appropriations; technical hiring flexibility; academic partnerships |
| Technology evolves faster than regulation | High | Moderate | Principles-based standards; regular review cycles; adaptive framework |
| Political backlash / "anti-innovation" framing | Moderate | Moderate | Document consumer and civil rights benefits; build bipartisan coalition |
| Enforcement resources inadequate | High | High | Private right of action supplements agency enforcement; fee-shifting provisions |
