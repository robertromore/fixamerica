# Algorithmic Accountability: Stakeholders

## Overview

Algorithmic accountability involves a complex ecosystem of stakeholders with competing interests. Understanding who builds, deploys, profits from, regulates, and is harmed by algorithmic decision-making is essential for designing effective reforms and building viable political coalitions.

## Stakeholder Map

### Technology Developers

| Stakeholder | Role | Interest | Position on Accountability |
|-------------|------|----------|---------------------------|
| **Large tech companies** (Google, Microsoft, Amazon, Meta, Apple) | Develop foundational AI models; deploy algorithms across products | Maintain market dominance; protect IP; shape regulation to favor incumbents | Publicly support "responsible AI"; oppose binding regulation; lobby for voluntary frameworks |
| **Enterprise AI vendors** (Palantir, Workday, HireVue, Equivant) | Build domain-specific algorithmic tools for clients | Revenue from software sales/subscriptions; protect trade secrets | Resist transparency mandates that expose product details; prefer industry self-regulation |
| **AI startups** | Develop novel algorithmic applications | Rapid growth; fundraising; market entry | Concerned regulation will create compliance barriers favoring incumbents |
| **Open-source AI community** | Build publicly accessible models and tools | Democratize AI access; advance research | Generally support transparency; concerned about regulation that restricts open development |

### Deployers

| Stakeholder | Role | Interest | Position on Accountability |
|-------------|------|----------|---------------------------|
| **Employers** | Use hiring, scheduling, and performance algorithms | Cost reduction; legal compliance; workforce optimization | Want legal safe harbor for using vendor tools; resist disclosure requirements |
| **Financial institutions** | Use credit scoring, underwriting, fraud detection | Regulatory compliance; risk management; efficiency | Accept some regulation (ECOA, FCRA compliance); oppose expansions |
| **Healthcare organizations** | Deploy clinical decision support, prior authorization AI | Improve outcomes; reduce costs; manage liability | Support evidence-based AI; concerned about liability for algorithmic errors |
| **Insurance companies** | Use pricing, claims, and fraud detection algorithms | Actuarial accuracy; profitability; competitive advantage | Resist transparency in pricing algorithms; claim actuarial fairness |
| **Landlords and property managers** | Use tenant screening algorithms | Risk reduction; efficiency; legal compliance | Want protection from discrimination liability for vendor tool results |
| **Law enforcement** | Use predictive policing, facial recognition, risk assessment | Public safety; efficiency; intelligence capabilities | Resist oversight; claim tools are objective; resist disclosure |
| **Government agencies** | Use algorithms for benefits, fraud detection, resource allocation | Efficiency; cost savings; political accountability | Mixed -- want tools but face public accountability pressure |

### Affected Populations

| Stakeholder | How Affected | Scale | Vulnerability |
|-------------|-------------|-------|---------------|
| **Job seekers** | Screened out by resume algorithms; assessed by AI interviews | 160+ million workers | High -- no visibility into screening criteria or decisions |
| **Criminal defendants** | Risk-scored for bail, sentencing, parole | 10+ million annually | Very high -- liberty at stake; limited due process |
| **Loan and credit applicants** | Algorithmically scored and priced | 200+ million adults | High -- affects housing, transportation, economic mobility |
| **Renters** | Algorithmically screened by landlords | 44 million households | High -- housing access; racial disparities documented |
| **Insurance applicants** | Algorithmically priced and underwritten | Most Americans | Moderate -- affects costs; limited transparency |
| **Benefits recipients** | Eligibility and fraud determined algorithmically | 80+ million people | Very high -- basic needs at stake; limited appeal mechanisms |
| **Students** | Assessed by admissions, proctoring, plagiarism detection algorithms | 70+ million | Moderate to high -- educational opportunity affected |
| **Patients** | Clinical decisions assisted or made by algorithms | 330+ million | High -- health outcomes at stake |

### Disproportionately Affected Communities

| Community | Documented Disproportionate Harms | Source |
|-----------|----------------------------------|--------|
| **Black Americans** | Higher criminal risk scores; lower healthcare access scores; higher error rates in facial recognition; discriminatory credit scoring | ProPublica (2016); Obermeyer et al. (2019); NIST (2019) |
| **Latino Americans** | Discriminatory lending algorithms; immigration enforcement algorithms; language-based bias | CFPB enforcement actions; DOJ investigations |
| **Women** | Hiring algorithm bias; credit algorithm disparities; healthcare algorithm under-diagnosis | Amazon hiring (2018); Apple Card investigation (2019) |
| **People with disabilities** | ATS filtering out disability accommodations; inaccessible algorithmic interfaces; biased health assessments | EEOC guidance (2022); DOJ ADA enforcement |
| **Low-income communities** | Welfare fraud algorithms; predatory lending targeting; child welfare over-surveillance | Eubanks, *Automating Inequality* (2018) |
| **Older adults** | Age-discriminatory hiring algorithms; insurance pricing | EEOC v. iTutorGroup (2023) |
| **LGBTQ+ individuals** | Algorithmic outing; discriminatory ad targeting; facial recognition misidentification | Studies on ad targeting bias |

### Regulatory and Oversight Bodies

| Stakeholder | Authority | Current Activity | Effectiveness |
|-------------|----------|-----------------|---------------|
| **Federal Trade Commission (FTC)** | Unfair/deceptive practices; algorithmic disgorgement | Enforcement actions; guidance; rulemaking proposals | Moderate -- limited resources; constrained authority |
| **Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC)** | Employment discrimination | AI hiring guidance; enforcement actions | Limited -- small staff relative to scale of algorithmic hiring |
| **Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB)** | Consumer financial protection | Adverse action notice guidance; fair lending enforcement | Moderate -- authority challenged; political vulnerability |
| **Department of Justice (DOJ)** | Civil rights enforcement | Housing and lending discrimination cases | Limited -- resource-intensive cases; few algorithmic actions |
| **Department of Health and Human Services (HHS)** | Health discrimination | Section 1557 nondiscrimination rule for clinical algorithms | Emerging -- rule finalized 2024 |
| **National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST)** | Technical standards | AI RMF; facial recognition testing | Advisory only -- no enforcement authority |
| **State attorneys general** | State consumer protection and civil rights | Varied enforcement activity across states | Uneven -- depends on state resources and political will |
| **Congress** | Legislative authority | Multiple bills introduced; no comprehensive law passed | Ineffective -- unable to pass algorithmic accountability legislation |

### Civil Society and Advocacy

| Stakeholder | Focus | Activities | Influence |
|-------------|-------|-----------|-----------|
| **AI Now Institute** | Social implications of AI | Research, policy advocacy, public education | High in policy discourse; limited legislative impact |
| **ACLU** | Civil liberties and civil rights | Litigation, advocacy, public campaigns | High -- major lawsuits challenging algorithmic systems |
| **Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF)** | Digital rights | Technical analysis, advocacy, litigation | Moderate -- respected technical analysis |
| **Algorithmic Justice League** | Algorithmic bias, especially facial recognition | Research, public campaigns, congressional testimony | High visibility; influenced municipal facial recognition bans |
| **NAACP** | Racial justice | Advocacy against discriminatory algorithms | Growing engagement; coalition building |
| **National Consumer Law Center** | Consumer protection | Research on tenant screening, credit scoring | Moderate -- influential in financial regulation |
| **Center for Democracy and Technology (CDT)** | Technology policy | Policy analysis, advocacy, convening | Moderate -- bridge between industry and advocates |
| **Data & Society Research Institute** | Data-driven technologies and society | Research, public education | Academic influence; limited direct policy impact |
| **Partnership on AI** | Industry-civil society collaboration | Research, best practices | Limited -- industry-funded; perceived as toothless |

### Academic and Research Community

| Stakeholder | Contribution | Key Work |
|-------------|-------------|----------|
| **Fairness, Accountability, Transparency (FAccT) community** | Research on algorithmic bias detection and mitigation | Annual conference; technical fairness methods |
| **Law school clinics and programs** | Legal analysis and public interest litigation | Georgetown, NYU, Stanford, Harvard clinics |
| **Computer science researchers** | Technical auditing methodologies | External audits of commercial systems |
| **Social science researchers** | Empirical studies of algorithmic impacts | Field studies of algorithmic systems in practice |

## Power Analysis

### Power Dynamics

| Power Dimension | Who Holds Power | Who Lacks Power |
|-----------------|----------------|-----------------|
| **Economic** | Tech companies, deployers, investors | Affected individuals, advocacy organizations |
| **Informational** | Developers (understand systems), deployers (control data) | Individuals (no visibility), regulators (limited technical capacity) |
| **Political** | Industry lobbyists, campaign donors | Marginalized communities most affected by algorithmic harms |
| **Legal** | Companies (trade secret protection, arbitration clauses, legal teams) | Individuals (burden of proof, limited access, cost barriers) |
| **Technical** | Developers and data scientists | Everyone else -- legislators, judges, affected individuals |
| **Narrative** | Industry ("AI improves everything") | Critics (fighting techno-optimism and innovation framing) |

### Coalition Opportunities

| Coalition | Shared Interest | Potential |
|-----------|----------------|-----------|
| **Civil rights + consumer advocates + labor** | Protection from discrimination and exploitation | Strong -- aligned on mandatory accountability |
| **Libertarians + progressives** | Opposition to unaccountable government algorithmic surveillance | Moderate -- different reasons, same conclusion |
| **Small business + consumer groups** | Opposition to algorithmic systems controlled by large tech companies | Moderate -- alignment on market power concerns |
| **State AGs + federal regulators** | Enforcement of existing anti-discrimination law | Strong -- complementary authority |
| **Responsible AI industry + advocates** | Voluntary-then-mandatory accountability frameworks | Emerging -- some companies see competitive advantage in accountability |
