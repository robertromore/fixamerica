# State Privacy Laws: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

The state privacy law landscape requires legislative action on two fronts: strengthening state-level protections and establishing a federal floor that does not preempt stronger state laws. This document provides model state legislation incorporating the strongest provisions from existing state laws, a federal privacy floor bill designed to protect state innovation, targeted federal legislation for data broker regulation, and a regulatory framework for state enforcement coordination. The legislative approach prioritizes private right of action, data minimization, cure period elimination, and harmonized definitions -- the provisions most critical for transforming state privacy laws from paper rights into effective protections.

## Federal Legislation

### Federal Privacy Floor Act

**Purpose**: Establish comprehensive minimum privacy protections for all Americans while explicitly preserving the authority of states to provide stronger protections. This bill addresses the patchwork problem by ensuring that residents of all 50 states have baseline privacy rights, without creating the ceiling preemption that would weaken California, Illinois, and other states with strong protections.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Federal Privacy Floor Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSE.

(a) FINDINGS.—Congress finds that:
(1) Twenty states have enacted comprehensive consumer privacy
laws because of the absence of federal privacy legislation.
(2) Thirty states have no comprehensive privacy law, leaving
approximately 120 million Americans without basic data
privacy rights.
(3) The patchwork of inconsistent state laws creates compliance
complexity for businesses while failing to protect all
Americans.
(4) State innovation has driven every major advance in American
privacy protection, including the California Consumer Privacy
Act, the Illinois Biometric Information Privacy Act, and
state data broker registration laws.
(5) Federal preemption of stronger state protections would
weaken privacy rights for hundreds of millions of Americans
currently protected by state law.
(6) A federal floor that preserves state authority to provide
stronger protections serves both national consistency and
continued innovation.

(b) PURPOSE.—The purpose of this Act is to—
(1) establish minimum consumer data privacy rights for all
Americans;
(2) create enforceable federal privacy standards;
(3) preserve and protect the authority of States to enact and
enforce stronger privacy protections; and
(4) encourage continued State innovation in privacy protection.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:
(1) COVERED ENTITY.—The term "covered entity" means any
person or entity that, alone or jointly with others,
determines the purposes and means of collecting, processing,
or transferring covered data, and—
    (A) is subject to the Federal Trade Commission Act
    (15 U.S.C. 41 et seq.);
    (B) is a common carrier subject to the Communications
    Act of 1934 (47 U.S.C. 151 et seq.); or
    (C) is a nonprofit organization.
(2) COVERED DATA.—The term "covered data" means information
that identifies or is linked or reasonably linkable to an
individual or a device that identifies or is linked or
reasonably linkable to an individual, including derived data
and unique identifiers. The term does not include—
    (A) de-identified data that cannot reasonably be used
    to infer information about, or otherwise be linked to,
    a particular individual or device, provided the covered
    entity takes reasonable technical and organizational
    measures to ensure the data cannot be re-identified and
    commits not to re-identify the data; or
    (B) publicly available information lawfully made
    available from federal, state, or local government records.
(3) SENSITIVE COVERED DATA.—The term "sensitive covered data"
includes—
    (A) biometric information;
    (B) precise geolocation information;
    (C) health information not covered by HIPAA;
    (D) financial account information;
    (E) government-issued identifiers;
    (F) information revealing race, ethnicity, national
    origin, religion, sexual orientation, gender identity,
    or immigration status;
    (G) private communications content;
    (H) login credentials; and
    (I) information of individuals under 17 years of age.
(4) DATA BROKER.—The term "data broker" means a covered
entity whose primary revenue is derived from collecting,
processing, or transferring covered data of individuals
with whom the entity does not have a direct relationship.
(5) AFFIRMATIVE EXPRESS CONSENT.—The term "affirmative
express consent" means an affirmative act by an individual
that clearly communicates authorization, obtained after
the individual is presented with a clear and conspicuous
description of the data practice, and not obtained through
a dark pattern.
(6) DARK PATTERN.—The term "dark pattern" means a user
interface designed or manipulated with the substantial
effect of subverting or impairing user autonomy,
decision-making, or choice.
(7) SALE.—The term "sale" means the transfer of covered
data by a covered entity to a third party for monetary
or other valuable consideration.
(8) SHARE.—The term "share" means the transfer of covered
data by a covered entity to a third party for purposes
of targeted advertising, whether or not for monetary
consideration.

SEC. 4. CONSUMER DATA RIGHTS.

(a) RIGHT OF ACCESS.—A covered entity shall, upon verified
request by an individual, provide the individual with—
(1) the categories and specific pieces of covered data
collected about the individual;
(2) the purposes for which the data is processed;
(3) the categories of third parties with whom data has been
shared or sold; and
(4) the sources from which the data was obtained.

(b) RIGHT OF DELETION.—A covered entity shall, upon verified
request by an individual, delete all covered data pertaining
to the individual and shall direct any service provider or
third party to whom the data was transferred to delete such
data, unless retention is required by law or necessary for
the completion of a transaction requested by the individual.

(c) RIGHT OF CORRECTION.—A covered entity shall, upon verified
request by an individual, correct inaccurate covered data.

(d) RIGHT OF PORTABILITY.—A covered entity shall, upon
verified request, provide the individual's covered data in
a structured, commonly used, machine-readable format.

(e) RIGHT TO OPT OUT.—An individual shall have the right to
opt out of—
(1) the sale of covered data;
(2) the sharing of covered data for targeted advertising;
and
(3) profiling in furtherance of decisions that produce
legal or similarly significant effects.

(f) UNIVERSAL OPT-OUT.—Covered entities shall honor
universal opt-out signals, including the Global Privacy
Control specification or any successor standard designated
by the Commission.

(g) SENSITIVE DATA.—Sensitive covered data shall not be
collected or processed without affirmative express consent
obtained prior to collection.

(h) RESPONSE REQUIREMENTS.—Covered entities shall respond
to verified requests under this section within 45 days and
shall not charge a fee for the first two requests per
individual per 12-month period.

SEC. 5. DATA MINIMIZATION AND PURPOSE LIMITATION.

(a) COLLECTION LIMITATION.—A covered entity shall not
collect, process, or transfer covered data beyond what is
reasonably necessary and proportionate to provide or maintain
a specific product or service requested by the individual.

(b) PURPOSE LIMITATION.—Covered data shall not be processed
for a purpose materially different from the purpose disclosed
at collection without obtaining affirmative express consent.

(c) RETENTION LIMITATION.—Covered data shall not be retained
longer than reasonably necessary to fulfill the purpose for
which it was collected. Covered entities shall establish
and disclose data retention schedules.

SEC. 6. DARK PATTERNS PROHIBITION.

(a) PROHIBITION.—A covered entity shall not use a dark
pattern to obtain consent, prevent an individual from
exercising rights under this Act, or manipulate an
individual into providing more covered data than necessary.

(b) VOID CONSENT.—Any consent obtained through a dark
pattern shall not constitute affirmative express consent.

SEC. 7. DATA BROKER REQUIREMENTS.

(a) REGISTRATION.—Every data broker shall register annually
with the Commission, disclosing categories of data collected,
number of individuals profiled, categories of purchasers,
opt-out mechanisms available, and data breach history.

(b) CENTRALIZED REGISTRY.—The Commission shall maintain a
public registry of all registered data brokers with links
to their opt-out mechanisms.

(c) CENTRALIZED OPT-OUT.—The Commission shall establish a
centralized mechanism through which individuals may opt out
of all registered data brokers simultaneously.

SEC. 8. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) FTC ENFORCEMENT.—
(1) AUTHORITY.—A violation of this Act shall be treated
as a violation of a rule defining an unfair or deceptive
act or practice under Section 18(a)(1)(B) of the Federal
Trade Commission Act (15 U.S.C. 57a(a)(1)(B)).
(2) CIVIL PENALTIES.—The Commission may impose civil
penalties of not more than $50,000 per violation per day.
(3) RULEMAKING.—The Commission shall have authority to
issue rules implementing this Act under the Administrative
Procedure Act (5 U.S.C. 553).

(b) STATE ATTORNEY GENERAL ENFORCEMENT.—
(1) The attorney general of any State may bring a civil
action in Federal or State court to enforce this Act on
behalf of residents of the State.
(2) The attorney general shall notify the Commission prior
to filing suit, and the Commission may intervene.
(3) State attorneys general may obtain injunctive relief,
damages, civil penalties, and reasonable attorney's fees.

(c) PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.—
(1) STANDING.—Any individual whose rights under this Act
have been violated may bring a civil action in any court
of competent jurisdiction.
(2) STATUTORY DAMAGES.—An individual may recover the
greater of actual damages or statutory damages of not less
than $100 and not more than $750 per individual per
incident.
(3) NOTICE AND CURE.—Prior to filing suit, an individual
shall provide 30 days' written notice to the covered
entity. If the covered entity cures the violation within
30 days and provides written confirmation, no action may
be brought for that violation. This cure provision shall
not apply to—
    (A) repeated violations (two or more violations within
    a 12-month period);
    (B) willful or knowing violations; or
    (C) violations involving sensitive covered data.
(4) ATTORNEY'S FEES.—A court shall award reasonable
attorney's fees and costs to a prevailing plaintiff.
(5) CLASS ACTIONS.—Class actions may be maintained for
systematic violations affecting 50 or more individuals.

SEC. 9. SMALL BUSINESS PROVISIONS.

(a) EXEMPTION THRESHOLD.—Sections 4 and 5 of this Act
shall not apply to a covered entity that—
(1) has annual gross revenue of less than $15 million;
(2) collects, processes, or transfers covered data of
fewer than 35,000 individuals annually; and
(3) does not derive more than 25 percent of revenue from
transferring covered data.

(b) An entity that meets the exemption criteria under
subsection (a) shall nonetheless comply with Sections 6
(dark patterns), 7 (data broker requirements, if applicable),
and 8(c) (private right of action for violations of any
applicable provision).

(c) The Commission, in consultation with the Small Business
Administration, shall publish a compliance guide for small
entities within 6 months of enactment.

SEC. 10. PRESERVATION OF STATE LAW.

(a) FEDERAL FLOOR.—This Act establishes minimum protections
and shall not be construed to preempt, limit, or otherwise
affect any State law, regulation, or rule that provides
equal or greater protection to individuals with respect
to the collection, processing, transfer, or security of
covered data.

(b) SPECIFIC PRESERVATION.—Nothing in this Act shall be
construed to preempt, limit, or affect—
(1) the California Consumer Privacy Act (Cal. Civ. Code
Sec. 1798.100 et seq.) or the California Privacy Rights
Act;
(2) the Illinois Biometric Information Privacy Act
(740 ILCS 14);
(3) the Washington My Health My Data Act; or
(4) any other State law providing equal or greater
protection than this Act.

(c) STATE ENFORCEMENT PRESERVED.—Nothing in this Act shall
limit the authority of any State attorney general or other
State official to enforce State consumer protection laws,
including State privacy laws.

SEC. 11. EFFECTIVE DATE.

(a) This Act shall take effect 18 months after the date
of enactment.
(b) The Commission shall promulgate implementing regulations
within 12 months of enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- Section 4 establishes comprehensive consumer rights modeled on the strongest state laws (California, Maryland, Oregon)
- Section 5 requires data minimization and purpose limitation, addressing the fundamental flaw of notice-and-consent frameworks
- Section 6 prohibits dark patterns, which undermine meaningful consent
- Section 7 creates first federal data broker registration and centralized opt-out mechanism
- Section 8 provides three-tier enforcement: FTC, state AGs, and private right of action with reasonable safeguards (notice/cure, damage limits, anti-abuse provisions)
- Section 9 exempts genuine small businesses while using a lower threshold than most state laws ($15M revenue, 35,000 consumers)
- Section 10 is the critical non-preemption provision, explicitly preserving all state laws that provide equal or greater protection

**Potential Challenges**:

- Industry will lobby intensely against non-preemption, arguing patchwork persists
- Private right of action faces opposition from business groups and some moderate legislators
- Data minimization standard ("reasonably necessary") requires regulatory guidance to operationalize
- First Amendment challenges possible under *Sorrell v. IMS Health* (2011) for data transfer restrictions
- Commerce Clause authority for regulating nonprofits may face challenge
- Senate filibuster (60-vote threshold) may block passage

**Refinements**:

- Phased implementation: large companies (18 months), mid-size (24 months), small (36 months)
- FTC sector-specific guidance on data minimization within 18 months of enactment
- Annual Congressional reporting on enforcement activity and effectiveness
- 5-year review provision for threshold adjustments
- Safe harbor for companies adopting FTC-approved privacy management programs

### Federal Data Broker Transparency and Accountability Act

**Purpose**: Establish comprehensive federal regulation of data brokers as standalone legislation that can be enacted independently of comprehensive privacy reform. This targets the most opaque segment of the data economy where consumer data is collected and sold without any direct consumer relationship.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Federal Data Broker Transparency
and Accountability Act".

SEC. 2. DEFINITIONS.

(a) DATA BROKER.—The term "data broker" means any entity
that knowingly collects, processes, or transfers the personal
data of individuals with whom the entity does not have a
direct relationship, and for which data collection,
processing, or transfer constitutes a primary source of
revenue.

(b) PERSONAL DATA.—The term "personal data" means
information that identifies or is linked or reasonably
linkable to an individual or device associated with an
individual.

(c) SENSITIVE PERSONAL DATA.—The term "sensitive personal
data" includes biometric data, precise geolocation data,
health data, financial data, data revealing race or
ethnicity, sexual orientation, religion, or immigration
status, and data of individuals under 17.

SEC. 3. REGISTRATION REQUIREMENTS.

(a) ANNUAL REGISTRATION.—Every data broker shall register
annually with the Federal Trade Commission, providing—
(1) the categories of personal data collected;
(2) the number of individuals whose data is held;
(3) the sources from which data is obtained;
(4) the categories of purchasers and the purposes for
which data is sold;
(5) opt-out mechanisms available to consumers;
(6) data security measures employed; and
(7) data breach history for the preceding three years.

(b) REGISTRATION FEE.—The Commission shall establish an
annual registration fee sufficient to fund the registry
and enforcement.

(c) PUBLIC REGISTRY.—The Commission shall maintain a
searchable public registry of all registered data brokers.

SEC. 4. CONSUMER RIGHTS.

(a) ACCESS.—Upon verified request, a data broker shall
provide an individual with all personal data held about
that individual within 30 days.

(b) DELETION.—Upon verified request, a data broker shall
delete all personal data held about the individual within
30 days and shall not re-collect data about that individual
from any source for a period of 12 months following
deletion.

(c) CENTRALIZED OPT-OUT.—The Commission shall establish and
maintain a centralized mechanism through which an individual
may submit a single request to opt out of data collection,
processing, and transfer by all registered data brokers.

(d) PROHIBITION ON SENSITIVE DATA SALES.—A data broker
shall not sell or transfer sensitive personal data without
affirmative express consent from the individual to whom
the data pertains.

SEC. 5. PROHIBITED PRACTICES.

(a) A data broker shall not sell personal data to any
entity for the purpose of—
(1) determining eligibility for employment, credit,
insurance, housing, or education, unless the data broker
is registered as a consumer reporting agency under the
Fair Credit Reporting Act;
(2) stalking, harassment, or intimidation; or
(3) discriminating on the basis of race, ethnicity,
religion, sexual orientation, gender identity, or
disability.

(b) A data broker shall not sell precise geolocation data
of individuals visiting health care facilities, religious
institutions, political events, or domestic violence
shelters.

SEC. 6. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) FTC ENFORCEMENT.—Violations of this Act shall be
treated as unfair or deceptive acts under the FTC Act.
Civil penalties of up to $50,000 per violation per day.

(b) STATE ATTORNEY GENERAL.—State attorneys general may
enforce this Act in Federal or State court.

(c) PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.—Any individual may bring a
civil action for statutory damages of $500 to $5,000 per
violation, or actual damages, whichever is greater.

(d) OPERATING WITHOUT REGISTRATION.—Operating as a data
broker without registration shall constitute an unfair
trade practice subject to civil penalties of $10,000 per
day of non-registration.

SEC. 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 12 months after the date of
enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- Section 3 creates the first comprehensive federal data broker registry
- Section 4(c) establishes a centralized opt-out mechanism (modeled on California's Delete Act)
- Section 4(b) includes a 12-month re-collection prohibition to prevent brokers from immediately re-acquiring deleted data
- Section 5 prohibits the most harmful data broker practices, particularly sensitive location data sales
- Section 6 provides robust enforcement including private right of action with meaningful statutory damages

**Potential Challenges**:

- Defining "data broker" precisely enough to avoid both over- and under-inclusion
- First Amendment challenges to restrictions on data transfers
- Industry argues data broker regulation is part of comprehensive privacy law (uses this to delay)
- Enforcement of centralized opt-out mechanism is technically complex

**Refinements**:

- Phase registration fees by broker size to avoid burdening small operators
- Annual compliance audits for brokers handling sensitive data
- International data broker coverage for entities serving U.S. consumers

## State Model Legislation

### Model Strong State Consumer Privacy Act

**Purpose**: Provide a model comprehensive state privacy law incorporating the strongest provisions from existing state laws, designed to replace or strengthen Virginia-model state laws.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Consumer Privacy
Protection Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.

(a) "Personal data" means any information that is linked
or reasonably linkable to an identified or identifiable
individual, including derived data and unique identifiers.
The term does not include de-identified data or publicly
available information.

(b) "Sensitive personal data" means personal data that
includes biometric data, precise geolocation data, health
data, financial data, data revealing race, ethnicity,
religion, sexual orientation, gender identity, immigration
status, or data of individuals under 17 years of age.

(c) "Controller" means a person that, alone or jointly
with others, determines the purposes and means of
processing personal data.

(d) "Sale" means the exchange of personal data for monetary
or other valuable consideration.

(e) "Dark pattern" means a user interface designed or
manipulated with the substantial effect of subverting or
impairing user autonomy, decision-making, or choice.

(f) "Data broker" means a controller whose primary revenue
is derived from collecting, processing, or selling personal
data of individuals with whom it does not have a direct
relationship.

SECTION 3. APPLICABILITY.

(a) This Act applies to a person that conducts business
in [State] or produces products or services targeted to
residents of [State] and that during a calendar year—
(1) controls or processes the personal data of 35,000
or more consumers; or
(2) controls or processes the personal data of 10,000
or more consumers and derives more than 25 percent of
gross revenue from the sale of personal data.

(b) This Act applies to nonprofit organizations and
entities that process employee data in the course of
employment.

SECTION 4. CONSUMER RIGHTS.

(a) RIGHT OF ACCESS.—A consumer has the right to confirm
whether a controller is processing the consumer's personal
data and to access such data in a portable format.

(b) RIGHT OF DELETION.—A consumer has the right to
require a controller to delete personal data provided by
or obtained about the consumer.

(c) RIGHT OF CORRECTION.—A consumer has the right to
correct inaccurate personal data.

(d) RIGHT TO OPT OUT.—A consumer has the right to opt
out of—
(1) the sale of personal data;
(2) targeted advertising;
(3) profiling in furtherance of decisions that produce
legal or similarly significant effects; and
(4) the processing of personal data for any purpose not
reasonably necessary for the product or service requested.

(e) UNIVERSAL OPT-OUT MECHANISM.—Controllers shall
recognize and comply with universal opt-out signals,
including the Global Privacy Control specification.
Receipt of a universal opt-out signal shall be treated
as a valid opt-out request under subsection (d).

(f) A controller shall respond to consumer requests within
45 days and shall not charge a fee for the first two
requests per consumer per 12-month period.

SECTION 5. DATA MINIMIZATION.

(a) A controller shall not collect, process, or transfer
personal data beyond what is reasonably necessary and
proportionate to provide or maintain a specific product
or service requested by the consumer.

(b) A controller shall not process personal data for a
purpose materially different from the purpose disclosed
to the consumer at or before the time of collection
without obtaining affirmative express consent.

(c) A controller shall not retain personal data longer
than reasonably necessary to fulfill the purpose for
which it was collected.

SECTION 6. SENSITIVE DATA.

(a) A controller shall not process sensitive personal data
without obtaining the consumer's affirmative express
consent prior to processing.

(b) Consent obtained through a dark pattern is void and
shall not constitute affirmative express consent.

SECTION 7. DATA BROKER REGISTRATION.

(a) Every data broker operating in [State] shall register
annually with the [Attorney General/Secretary of State],
disclosing categories of data collected, number of
individuals profiled, and opt-out mechanisms.

(b) The [Attorney General/Secretary of State] shall
maintain a searchable public registry of registered data
brokers.

(c) Operating as a data broker without registration shall
constitute a violation subject to civil penalties of
$10,000 per day of non-registration.

SECTION 8. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) ATTORNEY GENERAL.—The Attorney General may bring an
action to enforce this Act and may seek injunctive relief,
civil penalties of up to $7,500 per intentional violation
and $2,500 per unintentional violation, and reasonable
attorney's fees and costs.

(b) PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.—
(1) Any consumer whose rights under this Act have been
violated may bring a civil action for statutory damages
of not less than $100 and not more than $750 per consumer
per incident, or actual damages, whichever is greater.
(2) A consumer shall provide 30 days' written notice
prior to filing suit. If the controller cures the
violation within 30 days and provides written confirmation,
no action may be brought for that violation.
(3) The notice and cure provision in paragraph (2) shall
not apply to—
    (A) repeated violations (two or more within 12 months);
    (B) willful or knowing violations; or
    (C) violations involving sensitive personal data.
(4) The notice and cure provision in paragraph (2) shall
expire and have no force or effect beginning [2 years
after effective date].
(5) A court shall award reasonable attorney's fees and
costs to a prevailing plaintiff.
(6) Class actions may be maintained for systematic
violations affecting 50 or more consumers.

(c) ENFORCEMENT FUND.—Civil penalties collected under this
Act shall be deposited into a [State] Privacy Enforcement
Fund, which shall be used exclusively to fund privacy
enforcement activities by the Attorney General.

SECTION 9. NO WAIVER OF RIGHTS.

Any provision of a contract or agreement that purports to
waive or limit a consumer's rights under this Act shall be
void and unenforceable.

SECTION 10. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 12 months after enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- Section 3 uses Maryland's lower threshold (35,000 consumers) and includes nonprofits and employee data (Oregon model)
- Section 4(e) requires universal opt-out signal recognition (California/Colorado model)
- Section 5 incorporates data minimization (Maryland model), shifting from notice-and-consent
- Section 8(b) includes private right of action with reasonable safeguards: notice and cure for first unintentional violations, but no cure for repeated, willful, or sensitive data violations
- Section 8(b)(4) includes a cure period sunset (Connecticut/Colorado model)
- Section 8(c) creates an enforcement fund from penalties (self-funding enforcement)
- Section 9 prevents contractual waiver of consumer privacy rights

**Adaptations**:

- States without ballot initiative processes may need to negotiate PRA provisions with industry
- States with existing Virginia-model laws can adopt this as an amendment package
- Threshold (35,000 consumers) can be adjusted for smaller states
- States may choose to establish a dedicated privacy agency (California model) instead of AG-only enforcement

### Model State Data Broker Registration Act

**Purpose**: Provide standalone data broker registration legislation for states that may not be ready for comprehensive privacy laws.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Data Broker
Registration Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.

(a) "Data broker" means any person or entity that
knowingly collects, processes, or transfers the personal
information of individuals with whom the person or entity
does not have a direct relationship, where such activity
constitutes a primary business purpose.

(b) "Personal information" means information that
identifies or is linked or reasonably linkable to an
identified or identifiable individual.

SECTION 3. REGISTRATION.

(a) Every data broker shall register annually with the
[Secretary of State/Attorney General] no later than
January 31 of each year.

(b) Registration shall include—
(1) the name, principal address, and contact information
of the data broker;
(2) the categories of personal information collected;
(3) the approximate number of individuals whose data
is held;
(4) the categories of purchasers;
(5) the methods by which consumers may opt out;
(6) a description of data security measures; and
(7) any data breaches in the preceding three years.

(c) A registration fee of [$500] shall accompany each
annual registration.

SECTION 4. CONSUMER RIGHTS.

(a) A data broker shall provide consumers with a clear
and accessible mechanism to—
(1) access all personal information held about them;
(2) request deletion of all personal information held
about them; and
(3) opt out of further data collection and sale.

(b) A data broker shall respond to consumer requests
within 30 days.

SECTION 5. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) A data broker operating without registration shall be
subject to civil penalties of $1,000 per day of
non-registration.

(b) The Attorney General may enforce this Act and seek
injunctive relief, civil penalties, and attorney's fees.

SECTION 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect 6 months after enactment.
```

## Regulatory Framework

### Multi-State Privacy Enforcement Compact

**Existing Authority**: State attorneys general enforce consumer protection and privacy laws under state constitutional and statutory authority. Interstate compacts are authorized under the Compact Clause (U.S. Const. art. I, sec. 10, cl. 3) and do not require Congressional consent when they do not increase state power at the expense of federal authority.

**Draft Compact Framework**:

```text
MULTI-STATE PRIVACY ENFORCEMENT COMPACT

ARTICLE I. PURPOSE.

The purpose of this Compact is to facilitate coordination
among member States in the enforcement of State consumer
data privacy laws.

ARTICLE II. MEMBERSHIP.

Any State that has enacted a comprehensive consumer data
privacy law may join this Compact by legislative enactment
or executive agreement.

ARTICLE III. COORDINATION.

(a) Member States shall designate privacy enforcement
liaisons within their attorney general offices.

(b) Liaisons shall share information about ongoing
investigations, enforcement targets, and emerging
privacy threats.

(c) Member States may conduct joint investigations and
coordinated enforcement actions.

ARTICLE IV. SHARED RESOURCES.

(a) Member States shall contribute to a shared technical
assistance fund for privacy enforcement, proportional to
State population.

(b) The Compact shall maintain shared technical resources
including forensic data analysis capabilities.

ARTICLE V. COMMON ENFORCEMENT PRIORITIES.

Member States shall annually adopt shared enforcement
priorities through majority vote.
```

### FTC State Privacy Coordination Rule

**Authority**: FTC Act Section 6 (investigation authority) and proposed Federal Privacy Floor Act

**Draft Rule Framework**:

```text
FTC STATE PRIVACY LAW COORDINATION RULE

1. INFORMATION SHARING
(a) The Commission shall share investigation findings and
enforcement intelligence with State attorneys general
upon request.
(b) State attorneys general shall notify the Commission
of significant enforcement actions under State privacy
laws.

2. NON-DUPLICATION
(a) Where a State enforcement action and a Commission
enforcement action address the same conduct, the
Commission and State shall coordinate to avoid duplicative
penalties while maintaining the deterrent effect.

3. TECHNICAL ASSISTANCE
(a) The Commission shall provide technical assistance to
State attorneys general for privacy investigations,
including data analysis, forensic auditing, and expert
testimony.

4. COMPLIANCE GUIDANCE
(a) The Commission shall issue coordinated compliance
guidance addressing the intersection of Federal and
State privacy law requirements.
```

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

- **Commerce Clause**: Federal authority to regulate interstate data collection and processing is well established. State privacy laws apply to in-state data processing activities and to companies doing business within the state
- **First Amendment**: *Sorrell v. IMS Health Inc.*, 564 U.S. 552 (2011) applied heightened scrutiny to restrictions on data transfer but did not hold that all data regulation violates the First Amendment. Data minimization and transfer restrictions serve substantial privacy interests
- **Supremacy Clause and Preemption**: The Federal Privacy Floor Act's explicit non-preemption provision preserves state authority. Congress has broad discretion to define the scope of preemption
- **Tenth Amendment**: State privacy laws are a valid exercise of state police power to protect residents' welfare
- **Standing (Article III)**: *TransUnion LLC v. Ramirez*, 594 U.S. 413 (2021) limits standing for statutory violations without concrete harm. Private right of action provisions should be drafted to require concrete injury (privacy invasion itself constitutes concrete injury under *Spokeo Inc. v. Robins*, 578 U.S. 330 (2016))

### Preemption Analysis

- **Federal floor approach**: Preserves all stronger state laws. This approach has precedent in federal consumer protection statutes that set minimum standards while allowing states to exceed them
- **Ceiling preemption risk**: Industry strongly prefers ceiling preemption to eliminate California and Illinois standards. CAN-SPAM (2003) preempted stronger state email laws, weakening overall protection -- a cautionary example
- **Express vs. implied preemption**: Federal Privacy Floor Act includes express non-preemption provision, eliminating ambiguity about Congressional intent
- **Field preemption**: Federal law does not occupy the entire field of privacy regulation; states retain concurrent authority

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- **Three-tier enforcement**: FTC (federal), state AGs (state), and private right of action (individual). This mirrors successful enforcement models in consumer protection (FDCPA), telecommunications (TCPA), and environmental law (citizen suits)
- **Private right of action safeguards**: Notice and cure (30 days, limited to first unintentional violations), statutory damage caps ($100-$750 per incident), heightened pleading for class actions, fee-shifting for frivolous claims
- **Enforcement fund**: Self-funding mechanism ensures enforcement capacity grows with violation frequency

### Sunset and Review Provisions

- **Cure period sunset**: 2-year sunset on notice-and-cure provisions in model state act
- **Threshold review**: 5-year review of small business exemption thresholds to account for changes in data processing economics
- **FTC reporting**: Annual reporting on enforcement activity, compliance costs, and effectiveness metrics

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Federal Privacy Floor Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Small business exemption gaming | Companies structure subsidiaries to stay below $15M revenue and 35,000 consumer thresholds | High |
| "Reasonably necessary" ambiguity | Data minimization standard subject to broad interpretation without sector-specific guidance | High |
| De-identification workaround | Companies claim data is de-identified when re-identification is feasible with available technology | High |
| Service provider pass-through | Companies classify data transfers as "service provider" relationships to avoid sale/share restrictions | Moderate |
| Consent manufacturing | New dark pattern techniques designed to circumvent the dark pattern prohibition | Moderate |
| Cross-device tracking gaps | Data collected across devices may evade individual-level opt-out mechanisms | Moderate |
| Non-preemption exploitation | Industry challenges state laws individually while accepting weaker federal floor | Low |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Enforcement resource gap | Federal and state agencies underfunded relative to data economy scale | Budget constraints and political resistance to appropriations |
| Technical complexity | Regulators lack technical capacity to audit complex data practices | Government hiring challenges; salary competition with private sector |
| Retroactivity gap | Existing data hoards not addressed unless individual deletion requested | Legal constraints on retroactive regulation |
| AI training data | Personal data already used to train AI models cannot be meaningfully "deleted" | Technical reality of model training |
| Consumer awareness | Rights are meaningless if consumers do not know they exist | Information asymmetry; no mandated public education budget |
| International data flows | Federal law may not reach foreign data processors handling American data | Jurisdictional limits of domestic legislation |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Anti-circumvention provision**: Prohibit structuring entities to avoid thresholds; apply look-through rules for affiliated entities and common ownership. Count data processing across all affiliated entities.

2. **Sector-specific data minimization guidance**: FTC shall issue sector-specific guidance on "reasonably necessary" data collection within 18 months of enactment, with annual updates. Guidance shall be binding and enforceable.

3. **Technical de-identification standards**: NIST shall develop standards for de-identification that account for re-identification risk using available technology. Data that can be re-identified with reasonable effort remains covered data.

4. **Service provider audit requirements**: Contracts with service providers must include data use restrictions; transfers to service providers that process data for their own purposes are treated as third-party transfers subject to consumer opt-out rights.

5. **Consumer awareness mandate**: Covered entities shall provide annual notice of consumer rights in clear, plain language, distinct from privacy policies. FTC shall fund public awareness campaigns from enforcement fund.

6. **AI training data disclosure**: Companies that used personal data to train AI models must disclose this use and provide mechanisms for individuals to request exclusion from future training.

7. **Enforcement funding mechanism**: Establish privacy enforcement fund capitalized by civil penalties, ensuring enforcement capacity scales with violation frequency.

### Model Strong State Consumer Privacy Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Out-of-state processing | Controllers route data processing to states without privacy laws | Moderate |
| Cure period exploitation | During the 2-year pre-sunset period, companies repeatedly "cure" violations without behavioral change | Moderate |
| Consent bundling | Controllers require consent to unnecessary data processing as condition of service | Moderate |
| Contractual arbitration | Companies include mandatory arbitration clauses limiting private right of action effectiveness | High |
| Threshold manipulation | Companies artificially limit consumer count to stay below 35,000 threshold | Moderate |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| AG enforcement capacity | Even with enforcement fund, AG offices may lack technical expertise | Government hiring challenges |
| Interstate inconsistency | Even the model act does not solve inconsistency with non-adopting states | Federalism; voluntary adoption model |
| Preemption vulnerability | Strong state laws remain subject to federal ceiling preemption | Congressional authority under Supremacy Clause |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Anti-arbitration provision**: Add provision voiding mandatory arbitration clauses for privacy claims, modeled on state consumer protection arbitration restrictions.

2. **Processing jurisdiction rule**: Data processing is deemed to occur where the consumer resides, regardless of server location.

3. **Technical hiring incentives**: Authorize AG offices to offer competitive salaries for technical privacy staff funded by enforcement fund.

4. **Interstate cooperation**: Multi-State Privacy Enforcement Compact addresses coordination gap.

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| Industry-captured legislative process | Ballot initiative strategy bypasses captured legislatures; model bill reduces dependence on industry-drafted text |
| Insufficient AG resources | Enforcement fund self-funding mechanism; multi-state resource sharing |
| Consumer awareness gap | Mandated annual notice; FTC public awareness campaigns; standardized privacy icons |
| Rapid technology change | Technology-neutral drafting; FTC and state agency rulemaking authority for emerging issues |
| International enforcement | Bilateral agreements; requirement that foreign processors contractually submit to U.S. jurisdiction |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- **Cure period sunset**: 2-year automatic sunset on notice-and-cure provisions
- **Threshold review**: 5-year review of exemption thresholds
- **Technology review**: 3-year review of de-identification standards and technical requirements
- **Enforcement effectiveness review**: Annual reporting on enforcement actions, penalties collected, and behavioral change metrics
