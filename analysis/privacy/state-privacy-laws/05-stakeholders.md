# State Privacy Laws: Stakeholders

## Overview

The state privacy law landscape involves a complex set of stakeholders at the intersection of state politics, federal policy, technology industry interests, and consumer advocacy. The balance of power among these stakeholders explains both why state privacy laws exist and why most are weaker than consumer advocates want. Understanding stakeholder positions, power, and strategies is essential for advancing stronger state protections.

## Primary Stakeholders

### State Legislators and Governors

| Role | Interest | Key Dynamics |
|------|----------|-------------|
| **Privacy champions** | Genuine consumer protection; political visibility | Often lack technical expertise; depend on outside drafts |
| **Committee chairs** | Agenda control; campaign contributions from interested parties | Gatekeeping power over which bills advance |
| **Leadership** | Manage competing priorities; maintain business climate | Balance constituent concerns with industry relationships |
| **Governors** | Sign or veto; set state policy direction | Can demand amendments or threaten veto for stronger/weaker provisions |

### Technology Industry

| Company/Group | State Privacy Strategy | Key Activities |
|--------------|----------------------|----------------|
| **Amazon** | Shaped Virginia model; promotes AG-only enforcement | Lobbied in 20+ state legislatures; major Virginia employer |
| **Google/Alphabet** | Supports federal preemption; shapes state bills | Active in all major state privacy debates |
| **Meta/Facebook** | Opposes PRA; supports broad exemptions | $650M BIPA settlement changed calculus |
| **Apple** | Supports privacy regulation (competitive advantage) | Selectively supports stronger state laws |
| **Microsoft** | Moderate; supports comprehensive frameworks | Active in Washington state debates |
| **TechNet** | Industry trade association coordinating state strategy | Provides model bill language; coordinates lobbying |
| **State Privacy and Security Coalition (SPSC)** | Industry coalition for state-level privacy lobbying | Provides template legislation used by 10+ states |
| **US Chamber of Commerce** | Opposes strong state laws; promotes federal preemption | Active in every state with pending privacy legislation |

### Affected Populations

| Group | Privacy Impact | Current Protection |
|-------|---------------|-------------------|
| **Consumers in enacting states** | Varying levels of data rights depending on state law strength | 20 states with some protections |
| **Consumers in non-enacting states** | No comprehensive privacy protections | 30 states unprotected |
| **Small business operators** | Compliance costs for multi-state operations | Varying exemptions across states |
| **Children** | Enhanced protections in some states; gaps in others | COPPA (federal) plus varying state provisions |
| **Workers** | Employee data excluded from most state laws | Minimal protections in most states |
| **Low-income consumers** | Data practices disproportionately affect; less ability to exercise rights | Rights exist on paper but exercising them is complex |
| **Biometric data subjects (Illinois)** | Strong protections under BIPA with PRA | Illinois only; other states much weaker |

## Power Analysis

### Who Has the Most Power

| Rank | Stakeholder | Source of Power | How Exercised |
|------|------------|----------------|---------------|
| 1 | Tech industry (Amazon, Google, Meta) | Lobbying resources; campaign contributions; economic leverage | Shape bill language; block PRA; promote Virginia model |
| 2 | State attorneys general | Enforcement authority; political visibility | Selective enforcement; public statements; multi-state actions |
| 3 | California (CPPA + AG) | First mover; dedicated agency; largest economy | Sets national standards through market power |
| 4 | Committee chairs | Agenda control; determine which bills advance | Can block or advance privacy bills |
| 5 | Industry trade associations | Coordinate multi-state strategy; provide model legislation | Ensure consistent industry-favorable outcomes across states |

### Who Has the Least Power

| Rank | Stakeholder | Barrier to Power | Consequence |
|------|------------|-------------------|-------------|
| 1 | Consumers in non-enacting states | No law to protect them; no organized voice | Completely unprotected |
| 2 | State-level privacy advocates | Out-funded and out-staffed by industry 10:1 | Cannot effectively counter industry influence in most states |
| 3 | Workers | Employee data excluded from most laws; union decline | Workplace privacy unprotected in most states |
| 4 | Small businesses | Must comply with patchwork without resources of large companies | Bear disproportionate compliance costs |
| 5 | Individual consumers in enacting states | Rights require individual action to exercise; no PRA in most states | Rights exist but are difficult to exercise effectively |

## Stakeholder Positions on Key Issues

| Issue | Tech Industry | Privacy Advocates | Enacting State AGs | Non-Enacting State AGs | Small Business |
|-------|--------------|-------------------|--------------------|-----------------------|---------------|
| Private right of action | Strongly oppose | Strongly support | Mixed (some support, some prefer exclusive authority) | Varies | Oppose |
| Federal preemption | Strongly support | Strongly oppose | Oppose (if preempts stronger state laws) | Mixed | Support (simplification) |
| Data minimization | Oppose | Strongly support | Support | N/A | Neutral |
| Cure periods | Support (permanent) | Oppose (want sunset or elimination) | Want discretion | N/A | Support |
| Dedicated privacy agency | Oppose (prefer AG) | Support | Mixed | N/A | Oppose (cost) |
| Universal opt-out (GPC) | Grudgingly accept | Strongly support | Support | N/A | Accept |
| Nonprofit coverage | Oppose | Support | Mixed | N/A | N/A |
| Employee data coverage | Oppose | Support | Mixed | N/A | Oppose |

## Coalition Dynamics

### Pro-Strong Privacy Law Coalition

| Members | Shared Goal | Tension Points |
|---------|------------|----------------|
| Privacy advocacy groups, consumer organizations, some AGs, academic researchers | Stronger consumer protections; PRA; no preemption | Prioritization of provisions; willingness to compromise |

### Pro-Industry Coalition

| Members | Shared Goal | Tension Points |
|---------|------------|----------------|
| Tech companies, trade associations, Chamber of Commerce, some business groups | Weaker laws; AG-only enforcement; federal preemption | Large companies can absorb compliance; small businesses struggle |

### Emerging Cross-Cutting Coalition

| Members | Shared Goal | Potential |
|---------|------------|-----------|
| Some conservative AGs, privacy advocates, parents' groups | Children's privacy; biometric protection | Could bridge partisan divide for targeted legislation |
