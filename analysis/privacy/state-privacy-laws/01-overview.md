# State Privacy Laws: Overview

## Executive Summary

In the absence of comprehensive federal privacy legislation, US states have become the primary laboratories for consumer privacy protection. Beginning with California's landmark Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA) in 2018, a wave of state privacy laws has spread across the country, with 20 states enacting comprehensive privacy legislation by early 2026. These laws vary significantly in scope, enforcement mechanisms, consumer rights, and industry obligations, creating a complex patchwork that both advances privacy protection and generates compliance challenges. This fragmented landscape represents both the greatest achievement and the most significant limitation of American privacy law.

The state privacy law landscape divides broadly into two models. The "California model" (CCPA/CPRA) provides stronger consumer rights, broader scope, private right of action for data breaches, and a dedicated enforcement agency (the California Privacy Protection Agency). The "Virginia model" (VCDPA), adopted by most other states, offers narrower consumer rights, no private right of action, exclusive attorney general enforcement, and broader business exemptions. Understanding the differences between these models -- and their respective strengths and weaknesses -- is essential for evaluating the current state of American privacy protection and designing effective reform strategies.

The patchwork itself creates systemic problems: businesses face 20+ different compliance frameworks with varying definitions, consumer rights, and enforcement mechanisms; consumers in non-enacting states receive no protection; enforcement is uneven depending on attorney general priorities and resources; and the strongest state protections are constantly threatened by federal preemption proposals that would replace robust state laws with weaker national standards. At the same time, state innovation has driven the conversation forward in ways that federal inaction could not, and the strongest state laws serve as models for future federal legislation.

### Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Comprehensive privacy law** | A state law providing broad consumer data rights across sectors, as opposed to laws targeting specific data types or industries |
| **Private right of action** | Legal provision allowing individual consumers to sue companies for privacy violations, rather than relying exclusively on government enforcement |
| **Data broker registration** | State requirement that data brokers register with a state agency and disclose their data practices |
| **Opt-out right** | Consumer right to direct companies not to sell or share their personal information |
| **Opt-in consent** | Requirement that companies obtain affirmative consent before collecting or processing certain categories of data |
| **Attorney general enforcement** | Privacy law enforcement by the state's chief legal officer rather than a dedicated privacy agency |
| **Federal preemption** | Federal law overriding state privacy protections, potentially replacing stronger state laws with weaker federal standards |
| **Cure period** | Time given to companies to fix violations before enforcement actions can proceed |
| **Universal opt-out mechanism** | Technology-based signal (such as Global Privacy Control) that communicates consumer opt-out preferences to all websites and services |

## Core Tensions

- **State innovation vs. national consistency**: State laws drive progress but create compliance complexity for interstate businesses
- **Enforcement strength vs. business burden**: Private right of action deters violations but may create litigation costs; AG-only enforcement is cheaper but underenforces
- **Consumer rights vs. business obligations**: Stronger rights (opt-in, deletion, portability) impose corresponding obligations on data processors
- **Federal floor vs. federal ceiling**: Federal law could set minimum standards without preempting stronger state protections, or it could cap protections at a lower level
- **Broad scope vs. targeted protection**: Comprehensive laws cover all data processing but may over-regulate low-risk activities; targeted laws focus resources but leave gaps
- **Cure periods vs. deterrence**: Cure periods give companies time to fix violations but reduce the deterrent effect of enforcement

## Vision of Success

A reformed state privacy landscape would feature:

- **Strong federal floor**: Comprehensive federal privacy law establishing minimum protections for all Americans, without preempting stronger state protections
- **State innovation preserved**: States free to exceed federal minimums and experiment with new approaches
- **Meaningful enforcement**: Private right of action and adequately resourced state enforcement agencies
- **Harmonized definitions**: Consistent core definitions across state laws to reduce compliance confusion
- **Universal rights**: Every American has at minimum the right to know, access, delete, correct, and port their personal data
- **Data broker accountability**: All states require data broker registration and meaningful opt-out mechanisms
- **Biometric protection**: All states protect biometric data with BIPA-strength laws
- **No cure period loopholes**: Companies cannot repeatedly violate and "cure" without consequence
