# State Privacy Laws: Roadmap

## Overview

This roadmap sequences state privacy law reforms across three phases: immediate strengthening of existing state laws and expansion to new states (Phase 1), federal floor legislation and institutional development (Phase 2), and long-term harmonization and structural transformation (Phase 3). Each phase builds political momentum and compliance infrastructure for the next. The roadmap operates on two parallel tracks -- state-level and federal-level -- recognizing that state progress drives federal action and that federal inaction necessitates continued state innovation.

---

## Phase 1: Strengthen and Expand State Laws (0-18 Months)

### Strengthen Existing State Laws

| Action | Actor | Timeline | Difficulty |
|--------|-------|----------|------------|
| Eliminate or sunset cure periods in states with permanent cure provisions | State legislatures | 6-12 months | Low |
| Add private right of action provisions to existing state laws | State legislatures | 12-18 months | High |
| Increase AG enforcement funding in enacting states | State legislatures | 6-12 months | Low-Moderate |
| Require recognition of Global Privacy Control in all enacting states | State legislatures | 6-12 months | Low |
| Extend coverage to nonprofit and employee data | State legislatures | 12-18 months | Moderate |
| Lower applicability thresholds (toward Maryland's 35,000 consumer model) | State legislatures | 12-18 months | Moderate |

### Expand to New States

| Action | Actor | Timeline | Difficulty |
|--------|-------|----------|------------|
| Enact comprehensive privacy laws in large non-enacting states (NY, PA, FL, OH, IL, GA) | State legislatures | 12-18 months | Moderate-High |
| Promote model strong state privacy act in receptive state legislatures | Privacy advocacy organizations | 6-12 months | Moderate |
| Launch ballot initiative campaigns in states with direct democracy (FL, OH, MI, AZ, MO) | Advocacy organizations | 12-18 months | High |
| Enact data broker registration laws in states without them | State legislatures | 6-12 months | Low |
| Pass Washington-style health data laws in additional states | State legislatures | 12-18 months | Moderate |

### Build Enforcement Infrastructure

| Action | Actor | Timeline | Difficulty |
|--------|-------|----------|------------|
| Establish dedicated privacy enforcement units in AG offices | State AGs | 12-18 months | Moderate |
| Create multi-state AG privacy enforcement consortium | NAAG + state AGs | 6-12 months | Low |
| Coordinate multi-state enforcement actions against major violators | State AGs | Ongoing | Moderate |
| Build consumer complaint portals in enacting states | State AGs | 12-18 months | Moderate |
| Hire technical staff (data scientists, engineers) for AG offices | State AGs | 12-18 months | Moderate |

### Advocacy and Education

| Action | Actor | Timeline | Difficulty |
|--------|-------|----------|------------|
| Launch public education campaigns on state privacy rights | Advocacy organizations | Ongoing | Low |
| Develop model strong state privacy act with stakeholder input | EPIC, EFF, Consumer Reports | 3-6 months | Low |
| Publish enforcement effectiveness data comparing states | IAPP, advocacy organizations | 6-12 months | Low |
| Support state-level privacy advocacy organizations | National privacy organizations | Ongoing | Low |

## Risk Mitigation

| Risk | Probability | Mitigation |
|------|-------------|------------|
| Federal preemption of stronger state laws | High | Design federal floor with explicit non-preemption; build California coalition to block ceiling preemption |
| Industry lobbying blocks state laws in key states | High | Use ballot initiative strategy in direct democracy states; build public pressure through education |
| Private right of action opposition blocks passage | High | Include safeguards (notice/cure, damage caps, anti-abuse); separate PRA from comprehensive bill if needed |
| Virginia model continues to dominate new state laws | Moderate | Provide model strong bill text; publicize enforcement failures of AG-only model |
| AG enforcement remains underfunded | Moderate | Dedicate penalty revenues to enforcement; create multi-state coordination |
| Industry compliance is superficial | Moderate | Strong enforcement; technical auditing capacity; private right of action as backstop |
| Court challenges to state privacy laws | Moderate | Draft with constitutional challenges in mind; build judicial precedent incrementally |
| Political leadership change reverses progress | Moderate | Embed reforms in statute; build bipartisan support through children's privacy and data broker regulation |
