# State Privacy Laws: Actions

## Overview

While systemic reform requires legislation and institutional change, individuals can take meaningful steps to exercise their state privacy rights, advocate for stronger state laws, and support organizations working on privacy reform. This guide provides concrete actions organized by effort level and impact, with specific guidance based on whether your state has a comprehensive privacy law.

## Know Your State's Privacy Law

### Step 1: Determine Your State's Status

Check the IAPP US State Privacy Legislation Tracker (iapp.org) or NCSL Privacy Legislation Database (ncsl.org) to determine whether your state has a comprehensive privacy law and what rights it provides.

### States with Comprehensive Privacy Laws (as of early 2026)

California, Virginia, Colorado, Connecticut, Utah, Iowa, Indiana, Tennessee, Montana, Oregon, Texas, Delaware, New Hampshire, New Jersey, Kentucky, Maryland, Minnesota, Nebraska, Rhode Island, Wisconsin.

### States Without Comprehensive Privacy Laws

All other states. If your state is not listed above, you currently have limited comprehensive privacy protections at the state level (though you may have sectoral protections such as data breach notification).

## Actions for Residents of States with Privacy Laws

### Exercise Your Rights (Immediate)

- **Submit data access requests**: Request a copy of personal data held by major companies (Google, Meta, Amazon, data brokers) using your state's access right. Most states require companies to respond within 45 days
- **Submit deletion requests**: Request deletion of personal data from companies you no longer use or do not want holding your data
- **Opt out of data sales**: Use the "Do Not Sell or Share My Personal Information" links required on company websites
- **Enable Global Privacy Control**: Use a browser that supports GPC (Firefox, Brave, DuckDuckGo) to automatically send opt-out signals. Companies in California, Colorado, Connecticut, Oregon, Montana, and other states are legally required to honor GPC
- **Correct inaccurate data**: If you find errors in data held about you, exercise your right to correction
- **Opt out of targeted advertising**: Use your state's opt-out right to stop targeted advertising from major platforms

### Report Violations (Moderate Effort)

- **Document violations**: If a company refuses to honor your privacy rights, ignores requests, or fails to respond within the legal timeframe, document the interaction
- **File complaints with your state AG**: Most state AG offices have online complaint forms for privacy violations. Your complaint helps build enforcement records
- **Report to the CPPA**: If you are a California resident, file complaints directly with the California Privacy Protection Agency (cppa.ca.gov)
- **Support private enforcement**: If your state has a private right of action (Illinois for biometric data, California for data breaches), contact a consumer rights attorney if you believe your rights have been violated

## Actions for Residents of States Without Privacy Laws

### Advocate for State Legislation (High Impact)

- **Contact your state legislators**: Urge them to introduce and support comprehensive consumer data privacy legislation. See template below
- **Testify at committee hearings**: When privacy bills are considered, provide constituent testimony. Your personal experience with data collection matters
- **Join or organize a state privacy coalition**: Connect with national organizations (EFF, EPIC, Consumer Reports) for local chapter support
- **Support ballot initiatives**: If your state has a ballot initiative process, support efforts to put privacy on the ballot

### Template Email to State Legislators (No Privacy Law)

> Subject: [State] Needs a Comprehensive Consumer Data Privacy Law
>
> Dear [Title] [Last Name],
>
> I am writing as your constituent to urge you to introduce or support comprehensive consumer data privacy legislation for [State].
>
> Currently, [State] residents have no state-level right to know what personal data companies collect about us, no right to delete that data, and no right to opt out of the sale of our personal information. Twenty states have already enacted comprehensive privacy laws. [State] residents deserve the same protections.
>
> I urge you to support legislation that includes:
>
> - Consumer rights to access, delete, correct, and port personal data
> - The right to opt out of data sales and targeted advertising
> - Data minimization requirements limiting collection to what is necessary
> - A private right of action so consumers can enforce their rights
> - Mandatory recognition of universal opt-out signals like Global Privacy Control
> - No permanent cure period that allows companies to violate the law without consequence
>
> Please do not accept a weak bill modeled on Virginia's law, which provides minimal enforcement and has not changed corporate behavior. Strong privacy legislation modeled on California's or Maryland's approach would genuinely protect [State] residents.
>
> [Personal story about data privacy concern if applicable]
>
> Sincerely,
> [Name]
> [Address]

### Template Email to State Legislators (Existing Weak Law)

> Subject: Strengthen [State]'s Privacy Law
>
> Dear [Title] [Last Name],
>
> I appreciate that [State] has enacted consumer data privacy legislation. However, I am writing to urge you to strengthen our law to provide more effective protection.
>
> Specifically, I urge you to support amendments that:
>
> - Add a private right of action so consumers can enforce their rights when companies violate the law
> - Eliminate or sunset the permanent cure period, which allows companies to repeatedly violate privacy requirements without penalty
> - Lower the applicability threshold so that more businesses are covered
> - Increase enforcement funding so our Attorney General can effectively enforce the law
> - Require recognition of universal opt-out signals like Global Privacy Control
>
> Our current law provides rights on paper, but without meaningful enforcement mechanisms, those rights are largely illusory. Strengthening [State]'s privacy law would provide real protection.
>
> Sincerely,
> [Name]
> [Address]

## Federal Advocacy Actions

### Contact Federal Legislators

Urge your U.S. senators and representative to support comprehensive federal privacy legislation with:

- A federal floor that does not preempt stronger state laws
- A private right of action for consumers
- Data minimization requirements
- Data broker regulation
- Adequate enforcement funding

### Template Email to Federal Legislators

> Subject: Support Federal Privacy Legislation That Preserves Strong State Laws
>
> Dear [Senator/Representative] [Last Name],
>
> I am writing as your constituent to urge you to support comprehensive federal privacy legislation. The United States is the only major democracy without a comprehensive federal data privacy law.
>
> Critically, any federal privacy law must establish a floor -- not a ceiling. Twenty states have enacted their own privacy laws because Congress has not acted. Federal legislation must not preempt these hard-won state protections. States should remain free to provide stronger privacy protections to their residents.
>
> I urge you to support legislation that includes:
>
> - Enforceable consumer rights to access, delete, correct, and port personal data
> - Data minimization requirements
> - A federal floor that does not preempt stronger state laws
> - A private right of action so consumers can enforce their rights
> - Regulation of data brokers
> - Adequate funding for federal enforcement
>
> Thank you for your attention to this critical issue.
>
> Sincerely,
> [Name]
> [Address]

### Template Phone Script

> "Hi, my name is [Name] and I am a constituent from [City/Town]. I am calling to urge [Senator/Representative] [Last Name] to support comprehensive federal privacy legislation that does not preempt stronger state privacy laws. Americans deserve the right to control their personal data, and we need a strong federal floor -- not a ceiling -- that includes data minimization, a private right of action, and preserves states' ability to provide stronger protections. Can I count on [Senator/Representative] [Last Name]'s support? Thank you."

## Individual Privacy Protection Steps

### Immediate Steps (Any State)

- **Install Global Privacy Control**: Use Firefox, Brave, or DuckDuckGo browser with GPC enabled
- **Use a privacy-focused search engine**: DuckDuckGo, Startpage, or Brave Search
- **Review app permissions**: Remove unnecessary permissions from mobile apps
- **Use a password manager**: Generates unique passwords; reduces breach exposure
- **Enable two-factor authentication**: Protects accounts from credential attacks
- **Review privacy settings**: Check and restrict privacy settings on Google, Facebook, Amazon, and other major platforms

### Moderate Steps (Any State)

- **Use a VPN for browsing**: Prevents ISP tracking and location data collection
- **Opt out of targeted advertising**: Use optout.aboutads.info and optout.networkadvertising.org
- **Delete unused accounts**: Remove accounts you no longer use
- **Use privacy-respecting email**: ProtonMail, Tutanota, or other encrypted providers
- **Limit social media data sharing**: Restrict profile visibility; disable third-party app access

## Talking Points

### For Conversations with Friends and Family

- "Did you know that 30 states still have no comprehensive privacy law? If you live in one of those states, companies can collect and sell your personal data with almost no restrictions."
- "The strongest state privacy law, Illinois BIPA, has produced over $1 billion in settlements because it lets consumers sue. Most state privacy laws do not let consumers sue, which is why companies ignore them."
- "Industry lobbyists have successfully spread a weak privacy law template to 15+ states. These laws look good on paper but provide minimal protection because they rely on understaffed attorney general offices for enforcement."
- "Your state attorney general likely has fewer than 5 staff members dedicated to privacy enforcement. That is supposed to protect millions of residents against every company that collects your data."

### For Conversations with Policymakers

- "The Virginia model has failed. Fifteen states adopted it and none have produced meaningful behavioral change by companies. Illinois BIPA, with its private right of action, produced more enforcement in one state than all Virginia-model states combined."
- "Cure periods allow companies to violate privacy laws indefinitely. They 'cure' each reported violation but face no penalty, creating a perpetual get-out-of-jail-free card."
- "Federal preemption that caps privacy protections at a lower level would take away rights that state residents already have. Any federal law should be a floor, not a ceiling."
- "Data minimization is more important than notice-and-consent. Consumers cannot meaningfully consent to practices they do not understand. Companies should simply not collect data they do not need."

## Community Actions

- **Host a privacy rights workshop**: Organize a session at a library, community center, or school explaining residents' rights under your state's privacy law (or the absence of such rights)
- **Testify at legislative hearings**: When state privacy bills are considered, provide constituent testimony about personal experiences with data collection
- **Engage local government**: Advocate for municipal privacy policies, limits on government data purchases, and surveillance technology oversight
- **Support local journalism**: Share reporting on state privacy law enforcement (or lack thereof) and corporate data practices
- **Join state-level privacy advocacy groups**: Connect with organizations tracking your state legislature's privacy legislation
