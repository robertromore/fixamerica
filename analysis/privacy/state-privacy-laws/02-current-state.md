|--------------------|-----------|----------------------|-----------------|
| **Enforcer** | CPPA + AG | AG only | Private plaintiffs |
| **Staffing** | 50+ CPPA staff | 2-5 AG staff typically | Plaintiff's bar (unlimited) |
| **Annual enforcement actions** | 10-20+ | 0-3 per state | 1,000+ lawsuits filed |
| **Average penalty** | $100K-$5M per action | $50K-$500K per action | $1,000-$5,000 per violation per person |
| **Deterrent effect** | Moderate-High | Low-Moderate | Very High |
| **Consumer compensation** | Minimal (fines go to state) | None | Direct damages to individuals |

### The Private Right of Action Gap

The most significant divide in state privacy law is between states with and without a private right of action (PRA):

| Metric | States with PRA (IL BIPA, CA breaches) | States without PRA (Virginia model) |
|--------|----------------------------------------|-------------------------------------|
| Total enforcement actions | Thousands (IL); hundreds (CA) | Dozens across all states combined |
| Average company behavioral change | Significant (pre-collection consent) | Minimal (post-hoc compliance) |
| Total penalties/settlements | $1B+ (IL BIPA alone) | $200M+ across all states |
| Consumer awareness of rights | Higher (driven by media coverage of lawsuits) | Lower |
| Compliance investment by companies | High (proactive) | Moderate (reactive) |

## The Patchwork Problem

### Compliance Complexity

| Challenge | Description | Impact |
|-----------|-------------|--------|
| **Varying definitions** | "Personal information," "sale," "consumer," and other key terms defined differently across states | Companies must apply different standards depending on consumer location |
| **Different rights frameworks** | Varying consumer rights, exceptions, and procedures across states | Multiple compliance workflows needed |
| **Inconsistent exemptions** | Employee data, B2B data, nonprofit coverage vary by state | Complex applicability analysis required |
| **Different cure periods** | Ranging from 0 to 60 days; some sunset, others permanent | Different enforcement timelines by state |
| **Varying enforcement** | AG-only vs. PRA; different penalty structures | Uneven deterrence across states |
| **Conflicting requirements** | Some state provisions may conflict with others | Legal uncertainty for multi-state businesses |

## Trends (2024-2026)

- **Continued state adoption**: 5-8 new comprehensive privacy laws expected annually through 2027
- **Convergence toward stronger models**: Newer laws increasingly adopt provisions from California rather than Virginia
- **Data minimization momentum**: Maryland's data minimization requirement influencing newer state proposals
- **Universal opt-out proliferation**: More states requiring recognition of Global Privacy Control and similar signals
- **Cure period elimination**: Trend toward removing or sunsetting cure periods for greater enforcement effectiveness
- **Health data expansion**: Multiple states considering Washington-style health data laws
- **AI and automated decision-making**: States beginning to integrate AI provisions into privacy laws
- **Federal preemption risk**: Industry pushing for federal law that preempts stronger state protections

## The Gap

| What Exists | What Is Needed | Current Status |
|-------------|----------------|----------------|
| 20 states with comprehensive laws | All 50 states (or strong federal floor) | 30 states unprotected |
| AG-only enforcement in most states | Private right of action in all states | Only IL (BIPA) and CA (breaches) have PRA |
| 1 dedicated privacy agency | Dedicated enforcement in every state | Only California has CPPA |
| Varying definitions and rights | Harmonized core definitions and minimum rights | No harmonization mechanism |
| Industry-favored cure periods | Meaningful deterrence without perpetual cure | Some states sunsetting; most retain |
| Small business exemptions | Protections proportional to data processing risk | Large gaps in coverage |
