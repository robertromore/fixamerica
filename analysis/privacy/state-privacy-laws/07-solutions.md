# State Privacy Laws: Solutions

## Overview

Reforming the state privacy law landscape requires a two-track strategy: strengthening and harmonizing state laws where they exist, while building toward a federal floor that protects all Americans without preempting stronger state protections. The solutions below address the core problems identified in the root causes analysis -- the weak Virginia model, the absence of private right of action, limited enforcement resources, the patchwork problem, and the federal preemption threat. They are sequenced from immediately achievable state-level reforms to structural changes requiring federal legislation.

## Solution 1: Model Strong State Privacy Act

### Description

Develop and promote a model state privacy law that combines the strongest provisions from existing state laws -- California's enforcement infrastructure, Illinois BIPA's private right of action, Maryland's data minimization requirements, Oregon's nonprofit coverage, and Connecticut's cure period sunset -- into a single model bill available to every state legislature.

### Key Provisions

- **Comprehensive consumer rights**: Access, deletion, correction, portability, opt-out of sale and targeted advertising, opt-out of profiling
- **Data minimization**: Collection limited to what is reasonably necessary for the service requested
- **Private right of action**: Consumers may sue for violations with statutory damages of $100-$750 per incident
- **Universal opt-out**: Mandatory recognition of Global Privacy Control and equivalent signals
- **No permanent cure period**: 30-day cure period for first-time unintentional violations only; sunsets after 2 years
- **Nonprofit and employee data coverage**: Extends protections beyond for-profit consumer relationships
- **Low applicability threshold**: Covers businesses processing data of 35,000+ consumers (Maryland model)
- **Dedicated enforcement funding**: Portion of penalties dedicated to AG enforcement capacity

### Evidence Base

- Maryland's 2024 law demonstrates that stronger provisions (data minimization, low thresholds) can pass state legislatures
- Oregon's 2024 law shows nonprofit coverage is politically viable
- Connecticut's and Colorado's cure period sunsets confirm that temporary cure periods can be enacted
- California's CPPA demonstrates that dedicated enforcement agencies improve compliance
- Illinois BIPA's $1B+ in settlements proves that private right of action drives behavioral change

### Costs

| Cost Category | Estimated Range | Bearer |
|---------------|----------------|--------|
| Model bill development and promotion | $1-3 million | Advocacy organizations |
| State legislative campaigns (per state) | $200K-$1M | Advocacy organizations |
| Business compliance (per company, per state) | $50K-$500K initial | Industry |
| AG enforcement capacity (per state) | $2-10M/year | State budgets (offset by penalties) |

### Timeline

- Model bill drafting: 3-6 months
- First wave of state introductions: Next legislative session
- Enactment in receptive states: 12-24 months
- Broader adoption: 2-5 years

### International Models

- **EU GDPR**: Comprehensive rights with data minimization; enforced by dedicated authorities across 31 countries
- **Brazil LGPD**: National framework with dedicated authority (ANPD); influenced by GDPR

## Solution 2: State Privacy Enforcement Infrastructure

### Description

Build dedicated privacy enforcement capacity in every state with a comprehensive privacy law, moving beyond the current model where 2-5 attorney general staff attempt to enforce complex privacy statutes.

### Key Provisions

- **Dedicated privacy enforcement units**: Minimum staffing of 10-20 privacy specialists within each state AG office
- **Dedicated privacy enforcement funding**: Earmarked budget line for privacy enforcement, not dependent on AG discretionary allocation
- **Technical staff**: Data scientists, engineers, and forensic analysts capable of auditing corporate data practices
- **Multi-state enforcement coordination**: Formalized multi-state AG enforcement consortium modeled on NAAG (National Association of Attorneys General) working groups
- **Penalty reinvestment**: Civil penalty revenues deposited into privacy enforcement fund
- **Consumer complaint systems**: Centralized portals for consumers to report privacy violations

### Evidence Base

- California's CPPA (50+ staff) produces 10-20+ enforcement actions per year; most Virginia-model states produce 0-3
- Texas AG's investment in privacy enforcement (growing staff) resulted in the $1.4B Meta biometric settlement
- Multi-state enforcement actions (Clearview AI, Google location) demonstrate the power of coordinated AG efforts
- EU data protection authorities with dedicated funding and staff enforce GDPR far more effectively than understaffed U.S. state offices

### Costs

| Cost Category | Estimated Range | Bearer |
|---------------|----------------|--------|
| Per-state enforcement unit (10-20 staff) | $2-5M/year | State budgets |
| Multi-state coordination infrastructure | $5-10M/year | Shared across states |
| Technical auditing capacity | $1-3M/year per state | State budgets |
| Consumer complaint system | $500K-$2M initial; $200K-$500K/year ongoing | State budgets |

### Timeline

- Legislative appropriation: 6-12 months
- Staffing and training: 12-18 months
- Full operational capacity: 2-3 years

## Solution 3: Private Right of Action Expansion

### Description

Enact private right of action provisions in existing and future state privacy laws, modeled on Illinois BIPA's success and incorporating safeguards against abusive litigation.

### Key Provisions

- **Standing**: Any consumer whose privacy rights are violated may bring civil action
- **Statutory damages**: $100-$750 per consumer per incident (CCPA model) for comprehensive law violations; $1,000-$5,000 per violation (BIPA model) for biometric data violations
- **Actual damages**: Available where provable
- **Notice and cure**: 30-day cure period for first-time unintentional violations by small businesses only
- **Attorney's fees**: Available to prevailing plaintiffs
- **Class action availability**: Class actions permitted for systematic violations affecting 50+ consumers
- **Anti-abuse safeguards**: Heightened pleading requirements; fee-shifting for frivolous claims; judicial screening

### Evidence Base

- Illinois BIPA's private right of action has produced $1B+ in settlements and industry-wide behavioral change for biometric data
- CCPA's limited private right of action (data breaches only) has produced meaningful enforcement without excessive litigation
- Consumer protection statutes with PRA (FDCPA, TCPA, state consumer protection laws) function effectively across the economy
- AG-only enforcement has demonstrably failed to change corporate behavior in most states (see enforcement comparison in 02-current-state.md)

### Costs

| Cost Category | Estimated Range | Bearer |
|---------------|----------------|--------|
| Corporate litigation exposure (large companies) | $1-10M/year | Industry |
| Consumer recoveries | Variable | Industry to consumers (benefit) |
| Court system burden | Moderate | Judicial budgets |
| Compliance investment (driven by PRA deterrence) | $500K-$5M per large company | Industry |

### Timeline

- State-level enactment possible within 12-18 months in favorable states
- Broader adoption: 2-5 years
- Federal PRA as part of comprehensive legislation: 2-4 years

## Solution 4: Harmonized Core Definitions

### Description

Develop a set of harmonized core definitions for key privacy law terms through an interstate compact or model law process, reducing compliance complexity without requiring federal preemption.

### Key Provisions

- **Harmonized terms**: Uniform definitions for "personal information/data," "sale," "share," "consumer," "business/controller," "service provider/processor," "sensitive data," "de-identified data," "dark pattern," and "profiling"
- **Interstate compact mechanism**: States voluntarily adopt harmonized definitions through model law or interstate compact
- **Uniform Law Commission process**: Use the Uniform Law Commission (ULC) to develop model definitions with stakeholder input
- **Flexibility preserved**: States retain authority over consumer rights, enforcement mechanisms, and exemptions while using common definitional framework

### Evidence Base

- The Uniform Commercial Code (UCC) demonstrates that interstate harmonization of commercial law definitions is achievable without federal legislation
- Compliance cost estimates from IAPP-EY (2024) identify inconsistent definitions as a primary cost driver
- Businesses already operating under CCPA have invested in compliance infrastructure that could serve as a common baseline
- The EU GDPR's uniform definitions across 31 countries demonstrate the compliance efficiency of consistent terminology

### Costs

| Cost Category | Estimated Range | Bearer |
|---------------|----------------|--------|
| ULC drafting process | $1-3M | ULC/states |
| State legislative adoption | Minimal (amendment to existing laws) | State legislatures |
| Business compliance savings | $100K-$1M per multi-state company | Industry (benefit) |

### Timeline

- ULC drafting: 12-24 months
- State adoption of harmonized definitions: 2-5 years
- Critical mass of adopting states: 3-5 years

## Solution 5: Federal Privacy Floor Without Preemption

### Description

Enact comprehensive federal privacy legislation that establishes minimum privacy protections for all Americans while explicitly preserving the authority of states to provide stronger protections.

### Key Provisions

- **Federal floor**: Minimum consumer rights (access, deletion, correction, portability, opt-out) for all Americans
- **No ceiling preemption**: Explicit non-preemption of state laws providing greater protection, including California CCPA/CPRA and Illinois BIPA
- **Data minimization**: Federal data minimization standard applicable to all covered entities
- **Enforcement**: FTC authority with civil penalty power; concurrent state AG enforcement; private right of action with safeguards
- **Universal opt-out**: Federal requirement to honor Global Privacy Control
- **Data broker regulation**: Federal registration and transparency requirements for data brokers
- **Small business scaling**: Graduated obligations based on data processing volume and revenue

### Evidence Base

- 20 state privacy laws demonstrate that privacy regulation is workable in the U.S. economy
- Federal consumer protection laws (FDCPA, TCPA, CAN-SPAM) demonstrate federal floor models
- GDPR compliance costs have been manageable for U.S. companies operating in Europe
- Cisco 2024 Privacy Benchmark Study: 95% of organizations see privacy investment as net positive
- The patchwork problem affects 120+ million Americans in non-enacting states who have no privacy protection

### Costs

| Cost Category | Estimated Range | Bearer |
|---------------|----------------|--------|
| Initial compliance (large companies) | $1-5M per company | Industry |
| Ongoing compliance | $500K-$2M/year per large company | Industry |
| Small business compliance | $5K-$50K | Industry (with scaling) |
| FTC enforcement expansion | $100-200M/year | Federal budget |
| State AG enforcement | $5-20M/year per state | State budgets |

### Timeline

- Drafting and introduction: 6 months
- Committee process: 6-12 months
- Floor consideration: 6 months
- Implementation: 18-24 months after enactment

## Solution 6: Cure Period Elimination

### Description

Eliminate or sunset permanent cure periods in all state privacy laws, removing the mechanism that allows companies to repeatedly violate privacy requirements without consequence.

### Key Provisions

- **Immediate sunset**: States with existing permanent cure periods enact sunset provisions (2-year phase-out)
- **First-offense limitation**: Cure periods apply only to first unintentional violation by a given entity
- **No cure for intentional violations**: Willful or knowing violations are not eligible for cure
- **No cure for sensitive data violations**: Violations involving biometric, health, or children's data are not eligible for cure
- **AG discretion**: Attorney general retains discretion to decline enforcement for good-faith compliance efforts, replacing rigid cure periods

### Evidence Base

- Colorado and Connecticut have already enacted cure period sunsets, demonstrating political viability
- Companies in cure-period states show lower compliance investment than companies in states without cure periods (IAPP surveys)
- The cure period functions as a de facto exemption from enforcement for all but the most egregious violations
- No state AG has reported that cure periods improved compliance; multiple AGs have reported they impede enforcement

### Costs

| Cost Category | Estimated | Bearer |
|---------------|-----------|--------|
| Legislative action (amendment to existing laws) | Minimal | State legislatures |
| Increased compliance investment by companies | $50K-$500K per company | Industry |
| Increased enforcement effectiveness | Significant benefit | Public benefit |

### Timeline

- Achievable in next legislative session in states with existing laws
- Cure period sunsets already in effect in Colorado and Connecticut by 2025

## Solution 7: Ballot Initiative Strategy

### Description

Use direct democracy mechanisms (ballot initiatives and referenda) in states where they are available to enact strong privacy laws, replicating the strategy that produced California's CCPA and CPRA.

### Key Provisions

- **Model ballot initiative text**: Develop model initiative language incorporating strongest provisions
- **Target states**: Focus on states with ballot initiative processes and large populations without comprehensive privacy laws (Florida, Ohio, Michigan, Arizona, Missouri)
- **Campaign infrastructure**: Build voter education and signature-gathering capacity
- **Industry defense**: Prepare for industry opposition campaigns (estimated $50-100M per major state)

### Evidence Base

- California's CCPA was enacted only because a ballot initiative threatened stronger provisions; the legislature passed the law to preempt the initiative
- California's CPRA passed directly as a ballot initiative (Proposition 24, 2020) with 56% voter support
- Polls consistently show 70-80%+ of Americans support stronger privacy protections across party lines
- Ballot initiatives bypass industry-captured legislative processes

### Costs

| Cost Category | Estimated Range | Bearer |
|---------------|----------------|--------|
| Signature gathering (per state) | $2-5M | Advocacy organizations |
| Campaign (per state) | $10-30M | Advocacy organizations/donors |
| Industry opposition campaign (per state) | $50-100M (estimated) | Industry |

### Timeline

- Initiative drafting: 6 months
- Signature gathering: 6-12 months
- Election cycle dependent: 1-3 years
- Implementation after passage: 12-24 months

## Solution 8: Data Broker Registration Expansion

### Description

Expand data broker registration requirements to all 50 states and strengthen existing requirements with centralized opt-out mechanisms modeled on California's Delete Act.

### Key Provisions

- **Universal registration**: All data brokers operating in any state must register with the state's designated agency
- **Centralized opt-out**: Single mechanism allowing consumers to opt out of all registered data brokers simultaneously (California Delete Act model)
- **Data category disclosure**: Brokers must disclose categories of data collected, number of individuals profiled, and categories of purchasers
- **Deletion rights**: Consumers may demand deletion from any registered data broker
- **Annual reporting**: Brokers report data practices, breach history, and government data requests
- **Penalties for non-registration**: Significant fines for operating as a data broker without registration

### Evidence Base

- Vermont's 2018 registration law identified 375+ brokers; California's identified 530+
- California's Delete Act (2023) centralized opt-out mechanism is under development and will serve as a national model
- FTC enforcement actions against X-Mode Social and InMarket (2024) demonstrate federal interest in data broker regulation
- Data broker registration is among the most bipartisan privacy proposals

### Costs

| Cost Category | Estimated Range | Bearer |
|---------------|----------------|--------|
| Registration system (per state) | $500K-$2M | State budgets |
| Broker compliance (per broker) | $50K-$200K/year | Industry |
| Centralized opt-out mechanism (per state) | $2-5M initial | State budgets |
| Consumer benefit | Meaningful opt-out capability | Consumers (benefit) |

### Timeline

- Model legislation available: 3-6 months
- State enactment: 12-24 months
- Centralized opt-out operational: 18-36 months

## Dependencies

- Solutions 1, 3, and 6 can be combined in a single model state bill
- Solution 2 (enforcement infrastructure) should accompany any new state law enactment
- Solution 4 (harmonized definitions) can proceed independently through the ULC process
- Solution 5 (federal floor) benefits from state-level progress that increases political pressure on Congress
- Solution 7 (ballot initiatives) is an alternative path when legislative routes are blocked by industry lobbying
- Solution 8 (data broker registration) can proceed as standalone legislation in any state
