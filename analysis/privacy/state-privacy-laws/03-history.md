# State Privacy Laws: History

## Overview

The history of state privacy legislation reflects the recurring pattern in American federalism: states act when the federal government does not. From early data breach notification laws in the 2000s through the transformative Illinois Biometric Information Privacy Act of 2008 to the landmark California Consumer Privacy Act of 2018, states have driven every major advance in American privacy protection. This history reveals that state action is not merely a substitute for federal law but a proving ground for privacy concepts that eventually reshape the national debate.

## Timeline

### Pre-2000: State Privacy Foundations

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 1974 | Federal Privacy Act covers government records only | Left private sector unregulated; set precedent of sectoral approach |
| 1988 | Video Privacy Protection Act | Congress acted after Judge Bork's video rental records were published; narrow sectoral response |
| 1999 | Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act financial privacy provisions | Federal law covered financial data; left other categories to states |
| 1999 | Minnesota passes first state internet privacy law | Required ISPs to get consent before disclosing subscriber data |

### 2002-2008: Data Breach Era

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 2002 | California SB 1386 (data breach notification) | First state data breach notification law; created template adopted nationwide |
| 2003-2010 | 46 states adopt breach notification laws | Demonstrated state capacity for privacy legislation; created first patchwork |
| 2005 | ChoicePoint data breach exposes 163,000 records | Major breach drove public support for state privacy action |
| 2008 | **Illinois Biometric Information Privacy Act (BIPA)** | Landmark law: private right of action for biometric data; would become most consequential state privacy law |
| 2008 | Texas Capture or Use of Biometric Identifier law | Second biometric privacy law; AG enforcement only |

### 2009-2017: Sectoral Expansion

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 2009-2015 | 45+ states pass student data privacy laws | FERPA gaps drove state action; demonstrated category-by-category approach |
| 2013 | California "eraser law" (SB 568) for minors | First state law requiring deletion of minors' online data |
| 2014 | *Riley v. California* (SCOTUS) | Supreme Court recognized digital privacy; influenced state legislators |
| 2015 | California Electronic Communications Privacy Act (CalECPA) | Strongest state digital warrant law; required warrants for digital communications |
| 2016 | EU GDPR adopted (effective 2018) | Created global privacy standard that influenced state law design |
| 2017 | Vermont Data Broker Registration Act | First state to require data broker registration; proved concept |
| 2017 | Washington state biometric privacy law (HB 1493) | Third state biometric law; AG enforcement |

### 2018: The California Watershed

| Month | Event | Significance |
|-------|-------|-------------|
| January 2018 | Alastair Mactaggart begins collecting signatures for California Privacy Rights Initiative (ballot measure) | Threat of ballot measure with stronger provisions drove legislative action |
| June 28, 2018 | California legislature passes CCPA in emergency session | Passed in 7 days to preempt stronger ballot measure; Mactaggart withdrew initiative |
| September 2018 | California Governor Brown signs CCPA | First comprehensive state consumer privacy law in US |

**Why California First**: Several factors converged:

- Direct democracy mechanism (ballot initiative) gave privacy advocates leverage
- Silicon Valley proximity created both awareness and political dynamics
- Alastair Mactaggart, a real estate developer motivated by privacy concerns, provided funding and organizing
- Cambridge Analytica scandal (March 2018) heightened public concern
- EU GDPR implementation (May 2018) provided regulatory model
- Democratic legislative supermajority removed partisan veto

### 2019-2020: CCPA Implementation and Early Spread

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 2019 | Seven CCPA amendments signed, narrowing scope | Industry lobbying weakened law before it took effect |
| 2019 | Washington Privacy Act fails in legislature | First attempt to replicate CCPA fails; industry opposition model tested |
| 2019 | New York Privacy Act introduced | Strong bill with private right of action; did not advance |
| Jan 2020 | **CCPA takes effect** | First comprehensive state privacy law becomes operational |
| Mar 2020 | COVID-19 pandemic begins | Diverted legislative attention but increased digital data collection |
| Nov 2020 | **California Proposition 24 (CPRA) passes** | Voters strengthen CCPA: create CPPA agency, add rights, restrict data use |

### 2021-2022: The Virginia Model Emerges

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| Mar 2021 | **Virginia Consumer Data Protection Act (VCDPA) signed** | Second comprehensive state law; created weaker alternative model |
| Jul 2021 | **Colorado Privacy Act (CPA) signed** | Third comprehensive law; closer to Virginia model with some California elements |
| 2021 | Washington Privacy Act fails again | Industry-backed version could not pass even in tech-heavy state |
| May 2022 | **Connecticut Data Privacy Act (CTDPA) signed** | Fourth comprehensive law; Virginia model with cure period sunset |
| Mar 2022 | **Utah Consumer Privacy Act (UCPA) signed** | Fifth comprehensive law; weakest to date (business-friendly) |
| Jul 2022 | **ADPPA passes House committee** | Federal bill with preemption provisions threatened state law progress |
| Sep 2022 | **Facebook/Meta BIPA settlement: $650 million** | Largest biometric privacy settlement demonstrated PRA power |

**The Virginia Model**: Virginia's law became a template for most subsequent state privacy laws because:

- Amazon, headquartered in Virginia, influenced the bill's design
- No private right of action reduced business opposition
- AG-only enforcement was palatable to business interests
- Broad exemptions (employee data, nonprofits) limited scope
- 30-day cure period reduced enforcement risk
- Industry lobbyists actively promoted the Virginia model in other states

### 2023-2024: Proliferation

| Year | States Enacting Laws | Key Developments |
|------|---------------------|------------------|
| 2023 | Iowa, Indiana, Tennessee, Montana, Oregon, Texas | 6 new laws; Oregon stronger (covers nonprofits); Texas strong biometric provisions |
| 2023 | Washington My Health My Data Act | First non-HIPAA health data law with private right of action |
| 2023 | California Delete Act (SB 362) | Centralized data broker opt-out mechanism |
| 2023 | ADPPA stalls in Senate | Federal preemption threat diminishes; state action continues |
| 2024 | Delaware, New Hampshire, New Jersey, Kentucky, Maryland, Minnesota, Nebraska, Rhode Island | 8 new laws; Maryland strongest of new wave (data minimization, low threshold) |
| 2024 | Meta Texas biometric settlement: $1.4 billion | Largest privacy settlement in US history |
| 2024 | CPPA ramps up enforcement | California agency begins active enforcement cycle |

### 2025-Present: Maturation

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 2025 | Wisconsin enacts comprehensive privacy law | 20th state |
| 2025 | Multiple states introduce bills with private right of action | PRA gaining momentum beyond Illinois |
| 2025 | CPRA regulations fully operational | California's regulatory framework complete |
| 2025 | Cure period sunsets take effect in Colorado, Connecticut | Enforcement strengthened in early-adopting states |
| 2026 | Indiana, Kentucky, Rhode Island laws take effect | Additional states begin enforcement |
| 2026 | Federal privacy legislation reintroduced | Preemption debate continues |

## Key Historical Patterns

### The Industry Playbook

A consistent industry strategy has shaped state privacy legislation:

| Phase | Strategy | Examples |
|-------|----------|---------|
| **1. Block** | Prevent any law from passing | Washington Privacy Act defeats (2019, 2021) |
| **2. Weaken** | If law is inevitable, shape it to industry preferences | CCPA amendments (2019); Virginia model design |
| **3. Replicate** | Promote weaker model in other states | Virginia model spread to 15+ states |
| **4. Preempt** | Seek federal law that caps protections at weaker level | ADPPA preemption provisions (2022) |

### The Enforcement Gap Pattern

| Phase | Characteristic | Impact |
|-------|---------------|--------|
| **Enactment** | Strong rhetoric about consumer protection | Public believes they are protected |
| **Implementation** | Limited AG staff; no dedicated funding | Enforcement capacity inadequate |
| **Early enforcement** | Focus on large, visible targets | Most violations unaddressed |
| **Maturation** | Cure periods enable violations without penalty | Deterrence weakened |
| **Result** | Laws on books but limited behavioral change | Consumer protection largely illusory in most states |

**Exception**: Illinois BIPA, with its private right of action, is the only state privacy law that has produced industry-wide behavioral change (companies now routinely obtain consent before biometric collection in Illinois).

### Innovation Adoption Curve

| Innovation | Origin | Subsequent Adoption | Federal Influence |
|-----------|--------|---------------------|-------------------|
| Data breach notification | California (2002) | 50 states by 2018 | Federal proposals modeled on state laws |
| Biometric privacy | Illinois (2008) | 5 states (varying strength) | Federal proposals introduced |
| Comprehensive privacy | California (2018) | 20 states by 2026 | ADPPA modeled on CCPA/Virginia blend |
| Data broker registration | Vermont (2018) | 5 states by 2025 | Federal proposals introduced |
| Centralized opt-out | California (2023) | Under consideration in other states | Concept adopted in federal proposals |
| Health data (non-HIPAA) | Washington (2023) | 3+ states | Federal discussion stage |

## Historical Lessons

1. **Ballot initiative leverage is the most effective driver**: California's CCPA passed only because a ballot initiative threatened stronger provisions. States with initiative processes are more likely to get strong laws.

2. **Industry shapes law design more than consumers do**: The Virginia model's spread demonstrates that industry preferences, not consumer needs, drive the design of most state privacy laws.

3. **Private right of action is the strongest enforcement mechanism**: Illinois BIPA's $1B+ in settlements versus the combined enforcement of all AG-only states demonstrates the PRA's deterrent effect.

4. **Cure periods undermine enforcement**: Permanent cure periods allow companies to repeatedly violate privacy laws with no consequences as long as they "fix" each reported violation.

5. **Federal preemption threatens state progress**: The most significant risk to state privacy innovation is federal legislation that caps protections at a lower level.

6. **State laws improve over time**: Newer state laws increasingly adopt stronger provisions from earlier laws, creating a ratchet effect toward better protections.
