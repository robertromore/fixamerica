# State Privacy Laws: Opposition

## Overview

Opposition to strong state privacy laws comes primarily from the technology industry and broader business community, which have developed a sophisticated multi-state lobbying strategy to weaken privacy legislation. Their opposition operates on two levels: at the state level, they work to shape individual state laws toward the weaker Virginia model; at the federal level, they promote preemptive legislation that would override stronger state protections. Understanding this opposition is essential for advocates seeking to pass and defend strong state privacy laws.

## Opposition Arguments and Counterarguments

### Argument 1: "The Patchwork Is the Problem"

**Claim**: Multiple inconsistent state privacy laws create an unworkable compliance burden. The solution is a single federal law that preempts state laws.

**Who Makes It**: Tech industry (universally), US Chamber of Commerce, some business groups.

**Counterarguments**:

- The patchwork exists because Congress has failed to act; industry cannot create the problem and then use it to justify weakening the solution
- Industry already complies with GDPR (one additional framework); compliance with multiple state laws is manageable
- Federal preemption proposals consistently propose weaker protections than the strongest state laws
- Companies can comply with the strongest state law (California) and be compliant everywhere
- The "patchwork" argument was used against state data breach notification laws; 50 states now have them, and businesses comply without serious hardship
- Industry prefers the patchwork to strong uniform federal standards; they promote preemption only when it caps protections at a lower level

### Argument 2: "Private Right of Action Creates Abusive Litigation"

**Claim**: PRA provisions lead to frivolous lawsuits, shakedown litigation by plaintiff's attorneys, and excessive costs that harm businesses without benefiting consumers.

**Who Makes It**: All industry groups, some defense attorneys, business associations.

**Counterarguments**:

- Illinois BIPA is the only state privacy law that has produced large-scale behavioral change by companies
- The $650M Meta settlement and $1.4B Texas biometric settlement demonstrate that violations are real and substantial, not frivolous
- PRA supplements limited AG enforcement capacity; AGs cannot police the entire data economy
- Safeguards against abuse exist: standing requirements, pleading standards, fee-shifting, and statutory damage caps
- Consumer protection statutes in other areas (FDCPA, TCPA) include PRA without destroying industries
- The alternative (AG-only enforcement) has demonstrably failed to change corporate behavior in most states

### Argument 3: "Compliance Costs Are Prohibitive for Small Businesses"

**Claim**: State privacy laws impose disproportionate costs on small businesses that lack the resources to track and comply with multiple frameworks.

**Who Makes It**: NFIB, state chambers of commerce, some legislators.

**Counterarguments**:

- Most state privacy laws include small business exemptions (processing thresholds)
- Basic privacy practices (security, transparency, consent) are good business regardless of law
- The cost argument is primarily advanced by large companies that would bear costs proportional to their data practices; small businesses collecting little data face minimal burdens
- Compliance tools and services have reduced costs significantly since CCPA
- The cost of not regulating falls on consumers through data breaches, discrimination, and loss of autonomy

### Argument 4: "Market Solutions Are Better Than Regulation"

**Claim**: Consumer choice, market competition, and voluntary industry standards will produce better privacy outcomes than prescriptive state laws.

**Who Makes It**: Libertarian think tanks (Cato, Mercatus), some industry groups, ALEC.

**Counterarguments**:

- Twenty years of self-regulation have produced the current surveillance economy, not privacy protection
- Information asymmetry prevents consumers from making informed choices (91% do not read privacy policies)
- Market concentration in tech means consumers lack meaningful alternatives
- Voluntary standards are unenforceable and routinely violated
- No major tech company voluntarily adopted GDPR-level protections without legal compulsion

### Argument 5: "State Laws Threaten Innovation and Competitiveness"

**Claim**: Restrictive state privacy laws will drive innovation offshore or to states with weaker protections, harming economic growth.

**Who Makes It**: Tech industry, venture capital, some state economic development agencies.

**Counterarguments**:

- California, the state with the strongest privacy law, remains the nation's technology innovation leader
- EU GDPR has not reduced European technology innovation
- Privacy-by-design can be a competitive advantage (Apple's example)
- Innovation that depends on unconsented surveillance is not socially valuable innovation
- States that pass privacy laws attract privacy-conscious companies and talent

### Argument 6: "AG Enforcement Is Sufficient"

**Claim**: Attorney general enforcement provides adequate deterrence without the costs and risks of private litigation.

**Who Makes It**: Industry groups promoting Virginia model, some AG offices.

**Counterarguments**:

- Most state AG offices have fewer than 5 staff dedicated to privacy enforcement
- AG enforcement actions number in single digits per state per year; violations number in millions
- AG offices face political pressures that can limit enforcement against major employers
- AG enforcement produces fines paid to the state, not compensation to affected consumers
- The data overwhelmingly shows that PRA produces more deterrence per dollar than AG enforcement (Illinois vs. Virginia comparison)

## Opposition Strategies

### The Virginia Model Campaign

| Phase | Activity | Timeline |
|-------|----------|----------|
| **Preparation** | Develop model bill text based on Virginia VCDPA; identify state legislative champions | 2020-2021 |
| **Deployment** | Provide model text to legislators in target states; testify in favor at hearings | 2021-present |
| **Defense** | Oppose amendments strengthening the model (PRA additions, cure period removal) | Ongoing |
| **Federal pivot** | Use prevalence of Virginia model to argue for federal preemption at that level | 2022-present |

### Lobbying Tactics

| Tactic | Description | Effectiveness |
|--------|-------------|--------------|
| **Bill substitution** | Replace strong bills with Virginia-model alternatives | High; successful in 10+ states |
| **Amendment flooding** | Introduce dozens of weakening amendments to strong bills | Moderate; dilutes and delays |
| **Committee blocking** | Prevent strong bills from reaching floor vote | High in some states |
| **Governor pressure** | Lobby governors to veto strong bills or demand weakening amendments | Moderate |
| **Misinformation** | Exaggerate compliance costs; overstate litigation risks | Moderate; effective with legislators lacking expertise |

### Legal Challenges

| Strategy | Basis | Current Status |
|----------|-------|---------------|
| **First Amendment challenges** | Data processing as protected speech | *NetChoice v. Paxton* and related cases; outcome uncertain |
| **Dormant Commerce Clause** | State laws burden interstate commerce | Potential challenge to state laws with extraterritorial effect |
| **BIPA damage calculations** | Challenge per-violation statutory damages as excessive | *Cothron v. White Castle*: IL Supreme Court upheld per-scan damages (2023) |
| **Standing challenges** | Contest consumer standing absent concrete injury | Ongoing in multiple federal and state courts |

## Assessment

The opposition to strong state privacy laws is well-organized, well-funded, and has succeeded in shaping the majority of state laws toward the weaker Virginia model. However, the opposition faces growing challenges:

1. **Public support for privacy is increasing**: Polls consistently show 70-80%+ of Americans support stronger privacy protections
2. **Enforcement successes build momentum**: High-profile settlements (Meta $650M, Texas $1.4B) demonstrate the value of strong laws
3. **California effect**: CCPA's influence continues to shape corporate behavior nationally
4. **Newer state laws are trending stronger**: Maryland, Oregon, and other recent laws include provisions beyond the Virginia model
5. **Bipartisan children's privacy support**: Growing support for children's privacy creates wedge issues

Effective advocacy strategies should:

- Frame privacy as a consumer protection issue (bipartisan appeal)
- Use enforcement successes to demonstrate PRA value
- Provide model legislation with strong provisions as alternative to Virginia model
- Build state-level advocacy capacity to match industry presence
- Defend against federal preemption that would weaken state protections
