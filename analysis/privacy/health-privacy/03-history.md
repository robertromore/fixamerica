# Health Privacy: History

## Origins of Medical Confidentiality

### The Hippocratic Tradition

The principle that physicians should protect patient confidences dates to ancient Greece. The Hippocratic Oath (c. 400 BCE) states: "Whatever I see or hear in the lives of my patients, whether in connection with my professional practice or not, which ought not to be spoken of outside, I will keep secret." For millennia, this ethical norm -- not law -- was the primary basis for health privacy.

**Key characteristics of the pre-modern era:**

- Privacy was an ethical obligation of individual physicians, not a legal right
- Paper records were difficult to aggregate or transmit
- Information stayed within the doctor-patient relationship
- No insurance companies, government payers, or electronic systems involved
- Breaches were local and limited in scope

### Early American Medical Confidentiality

In the United States, medical confidentiality developed as a professional norm enforced through state medical practice acts and malpractice law.

| Period | Development |
|--------|-------------|
| Colonial era | Physician-patient confidentiality recognized as professional duty |
| 1847 | American Medical Association adopts Code of Ethics incorporating confidentiality |
| Late 1800s | Some states create physician-patient privilege in evidence law |
| Early 1900s | Medical licensing boards begin enforcing confidentiality standards |
| 1920s-1960s | Group insurance and government programs begin creating third-party access to medical records |

## The Insurance and Government Era (1940s-1980s)

### Employer-Based Insurance and Data Sharing

The growth of employer-based health insurance after World War II fundamentally changed health privacy by creating third parties with legitimate access to medical information.

**Key developments:**

- Employers selected and administered health plans, gaining indirect access to employee health information
- Insurance companies required medical records to process claims and underwrite policies
- Actuarial analysis demanded aggregation of health data across populations
- The Medical Information Bureau (MIB), established in 1902 but growing rapidly in the postwar era, became a clearinghouse for life and health insurance underwriting data

### Medicare, Medicaid, and Government Data Systems

The creation of Medicare and Medicaid in 1965 gave the federal government a massive role in health data:

- CMS (then HCFA) collected claims data on millions of Americans
- Government databases linked diagnoses, treatments, and payments
- Research agencies (NIH, CDC) sought access to health data for public health purposes
- The Privacy Act of 1974 provided some protections for federal records but exemptions limited its effectiveness

### Early Privacy Concerns

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 1970 | Fair Credit Reporting Act | First federal law addressing data privacy, but limited to credit; excluded health data |
| 1974 | Privacy Act | Covered federal agency records including some health data, but exemptions weakened protections |
| 1974 | Comprehensive Alcohol Abuse and Alcoholism Prevention, Treatment, and Rehabilitation Act | Created confidentiality protections for substance abuse records, later codified as 42 CFR Part 2 |
| 1976 | *Whalen v. Roe*, 429 U.S. 589 | Supreme Court recognized a constitutional right to informational privacy but declined to define its scope, upholding a New York law requiring reporting of controlled substance prescriptions |

## The Pre-HIPAA Patchwork (1980s-1996)

### Growing Computerization

The shift from paper to electronic medical records accelerated privacy concerns:

- Hospital information systems centralized patient data
- Insurance claims processing became electronic
- Prescription databases enabled tracking of medication histories
- Employer wellness programs collected health data outside the treatment relationship

### State Law Responses

Without federal legislation, states developed their own approaches:

| State | Year | Provision |
|-------|------|-----------|
| California | 1981 | Confidentiality of Medical Information Act (CMIA) -- one of the strongest state health privacy laws |
| New York | 1988 | Public Health Law provisions on medical record confidentiality |
| Montana | 1987 | Comprehensive medical privacy statute |
| Texas | 1993 | Medical Records Privacy Act |

**The patchwork problem:**

- Different states had different definitions, protections, and enforcement mechanisms
- Multi-state health systems faced compliance challenges
- No uniform standard for electronic health information
- Some states had no health privacy statute at all

### Congressional Attempts

Congress debated health privacy legislation throughout the early 1990s but could not reach consensus on key issues:

- Whether to preempt state laws
- How to handle research access to health data
- What enforcement mechanisms to establish
- How to balance privacy with administrative efficiency

## HIPAA and the Privacy Rule (1996-2003)

### The 1996 Statute

The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act was signed by President Clinton on August 21, 1996. Its primary purpose was insurance portability -- ensuring workers could maintain coverage when changing jobs -- not privacy. However, Congress included a provision (Section 264) directing HHS to issue privacy regulations if Congress did not enact a comprehensive health privacy law within three years.

**Why privacy was included:**

- Congress recognized that electronic health data exchange required privacy safeguards
- The "Administrative Simplification" provisions of HIPAA standardized electronic transactions, creating both efficiencies and risks
- Advocates pushed for privacy protections as a condition of supporting electronic data standards
- Congress expected to pass a standalone privacy bill but did not meet its own deadline

### The Privacy Rule (2000-2003)

When Congress failed to act by the August 1999 deadline, HHS developed the HIPAA Privacy Rule:

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| November 1999 | HHS publishes proposed Privacy Rule |
| December 2000 | HHS publishes final Privacy Rule under Clinton administration |
| March 2002 | Bush administration modifies the rule, weakening some consent requirements |
| April 14, 2003 | Compliance deadline for most covered entities |
| April 2005 | Compliance deadline for small health plans |

**Key compromises in the Privacy Rule:**

- Limited to "covered entities" rather than covering all health data holders
- Allowed use and disclosure for "treatment, payment, and health care operations" without individual authorization
- Required only "notice" of privacy practices, not affirmative consent for most disclosures
- Created a "minimum necessary" standard but with broad exceptions
- Provided no private right of action for individuals
- Preempted state laws only when HIPAA was more protective (state laws providing greater protection survived)

## The HITECH Era (2009-2015)

### The HITECH Act

The Health Information Technology for Economic and Clinical Health (HITECH) Act, enacted as part of the American Recovery and Reinvestment Act of 2009, significantly expanded HIPAA in several ways:

**Key HITECH provisions:**

| Provision | Effect |
|-----------|--------|
| Direct business associate liability | Business associates became directly subject to HIPAA penalties |
| Breach notification | Required notification of individuals and HHS for PHI breaches |
| Enhanced penalties | Tiered penalty structure with maximum $1.5 million per violation category per year |
| Meaningful Use program | $27+ billion in incentives for EHR adoption (CMS data, 2009-2016) |
| Accounting of disclosures | Expanded patients' right to know who accessed their records |

### Electronic Health Record Expansion

The Meaningful Use program dramatically accelerated EHR adoption:

| Year | Hospital EHR Adoption | Office-Based Physician EHR Adoption |
|------|----------------------|--------------------------------------|
| 2008 | 9% (certified EHR) | 42% (any EHR) |
| 2012 | 44% | 72% |
| 2015 | 84% | 87% |
| 2021 | 96% | 89% |

*Source: ONC Data Briefs; NCHS National Electronic Health Records Survey*

**Privacy implications of rapid EHR adoption:**

- Massive increase in electronically stored and transmittable health data
- Interoperability initiatives made data more accessible but also more vulnerable
- Health information exchanges (HIEs) created new data aggregation points
- Cloud computing introduced new business associate relationships

## The App and Wearable Era (2014-Present)

### Rise of Digital Health

| Year | Development | Privacy Significance |
|------|-------------|---------------------|
| 2014 | Apple launches HealthKit / Health app | Mainstream health data collection on consumer devices begins |
| 2015 | Fitbit IPO | Wearable health tracking becomes mass market |
| 2017 | FDA Digital Health Innovation Action Plan | Regulatory framework for digital health tools evolves |
| 2018 | 23andMe receives FDA approval for health-related genetic reports | Consumer genetic testing expands beyond ancestry |
| 2020 | COVID-19 pandemic accelerates telehealth | Massive shift to digital health with relaxed privacy rules |
| 2021 | FTC enforcement against Flo Health | First major health app privacy enforcement |
| 2022 | *Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization* | Reproductive health data becomes a criminal evidence risk |
| 2022 | The Markup reveals hospital tracking pixel practices | Health data leakage to advertising platforms exposed |
| 2023 | FTC enforces against GoodRx, BetterHelp, Premom | Wave of health app enforcement actions |
| 2023 | Washington enacts My Health My Data Act | First state law covering all consumer health data |
| 2023 | 23andMe data breach (6.9 million users) | Consumer genetic data security failure |
| 2024 | HHS final rule on reproductive health PHI | HIPAA strengthened for reproductive data within covered entities |
| 2024 | 23andMe files for bankruptcy | Fate of millions of genetic profiles uncertain |

### COVID-19 and Health Privacy

The pandemic had lasting effects on health privacy:

- HHS waived HIPAA penalties for certain telehealth practices during the public health emergency
- Contact tracing apps raised surveillance concerns
- Vaccination status became a contested privacy issue
- Employer health screening expanded workplace health data collection
- Remote patient monitoring increased data flows outside traditional clinical settings

## The 42 CFR Part 2 Story

### Origins

Federal protections for substance abuse treatment records have followed a distinct trajectory:

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1970 | Comprehensive Alcohol Abuse and Alcoholism Prevention, Treatment, and Rehabilitation Act |
| 1972 | Drug Abuse Office and Treatment Act |
| 1975 | HHS issues 42 CFR Part 2 regulations |
| 1987 | Regulations significantly revised to provide stronger protections |

**Rationale:** Substance abuse treatment records receive heightened protection because disclosure could subject patients to criminal prosecution, discrimination, or social stigma, deterring people from seeking treatment.

### Erosion

| Year | Event | Effect |
|------|-------|--------|
| 2017 | 21st Century Cures Act | Required HHS to consider aligning Part 2 with HIPAA |
| 2020 | CARES Act amendments | Permitted Part 2 data to be disclosed for treatment, payment, and health care operations without individual consent once initial consent is given |
| 2024 | HHS final rule implementing CARES Act changes | Part 2 disclosures more closely aligned with HIPAA, weakening historical protections while adding anti-discrimination provisions |

**Controversy:** Addiction medicine specialists, patient advocates, and civil liberties organizations argue the alignment with HIPAA reduces privacy protections that are essential for encouraging people to seek treatment. Proponents argue interoperability is necessary for coordinated care.

## Key Lessons from History

1. **Privacy has always lagged technology** -- From paper records to EHRs to apps, privacy protections consistently arrive years or decades after the technology they should govern
2. **Sector-specific regulation creates gaps** -- HIPAA's limitation to "covered entities" was a compromise that has grown into a chasm as health data moved to non-covered platforms
3. **Industry framing shapes law** -- HIPAA's Privacy Rule was shaped as much by administrative efficiency goals as by patient privacy concerns
4. **Crisis drives change** -- The *Dobbs* decision and COVID-19 pandemic each forced rapid reassessment of health privacy frameworks
5. **Enforcement determines effectiveness** -- Even strong statutory protections matter little without adequate enforcement resources and meaningful penalties
