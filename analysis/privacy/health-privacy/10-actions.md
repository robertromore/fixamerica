# Health Privacy: Actions

## What Individuals Can Do

### Protect Your Own Health Data

**Audit Your Health Apps**

- Review all health and fitness apps on your phone and assess what data they collect
- Read privacy policies for apps that handle health information (use Mozilla's *Privacy Not Included* reviews as a guide)
- Delete apps that share data with advertisers or third parties without clear consent
- Prefer apps that store data locally on your device rather than in the cloud
- Use apps from companies subject to HIPAA (e.g., your provider's patient portal) when possible

**Manage Reproductive Health Data**

- Consider using period-tracking apps that store data only on your device (e.g., Drip, which stores data locally)
- Disable location services for health-related apps
- Use a VPN when searching for reproductive health information online
- Pay cash for reproductive health services if privacy is a concern
- Be cautious about sharing reproductive health information through messaging apps; use end-to-end encrypted messaging (Signal) for sensitive communications

**Protect Genetic Data**

- Before using consumer genetic testing services, read their privacy policies carefully
- Opt out of research sharing if available
- Download and delete your genetic data if your testing company allows it
- Be aware that your genetic data also reveals information about biological relatives
- Monitor news about your testing company's data practices and financial stability

**Secure Your Health Records**

- Request copies of your medical records from all providers to know what data exists
- Review your records for accuracy and request corrections for errors
- Ask providers about their privacy practices, especially regarding electronic systems
- Opt out of health information exchanges if your state allows and you are concerned about data sharing
- Use strong, unique passwords for patient portal accounts; enable multi-factor authentication

**Manage Mental Health Data**

- Be cautious about AI-based therapy apps; understand how your data is used
- Ask human therapists about their electronic record-keeping practices
- If using crisis hotlines, be aware that some jurisdictions share data with law enforcement dispatchers
- Consider whether your mental health app is HIPAA-covered (most are not)

## What Patients Can Do at the Provider Level

### Exercise Your HIPAA Rights

**Access your records:**

- You have the right to access your medical records within 30 days of request
- Providers may charge reasonable fees for copies but cannot deny access
- You can designate a personal representative to access records on your behalf
- File a complaint with HHS OCR if access is denied

**Request amendments:**

- You have the right to request correction of inaccurate information in your health records
- Providers must respond within 60 days
- If denied, your request and the denial become part of your record

**Request an accounting of disclosures:**

- You have the right to know who your health information has been disclosed to (with exceptions for treatment, payment, and operations)
- This can reveal unexpected sharing of your data

**Request restrictions:**

- You can ask providers to restrict disclosures of your PHI
- Providers are not required to agree, except for disclosures to health plans for services you paid for entirely out of pocket
- Self-pay patients have stronger restriction rights under HITECH

### Ask Questions

**When visiting a provider, ask:**

- "Does your website use tracking technologies like Meta Pixel or Google Analytics?"
- "Do you share my health data with any data brokers or marketing companies?"
- "How do you handle requests for my health data from law enforcement?"
- "Is your patient portal independently audited for security?"
- "What happens to my data if I leave this practice or health system?"

## What Communities Can Do

### Organize Locally

- Form or join community health privacy advocacy groups
- Host educational events about health data rights and risks
- Partner with local health clinics to improve their privacy practices
- Connect with legal aid organizations that handle health privacy complaints
- Engage faith communities, particularly around reproductive and mental health privacy

### Engage Local Government

- Advocate for local health departments to adopt strong privacy practices
- Push for municipal contracts with health technology vendors to include privacy requirements
- Support local government resolutions calling for state and federal health privacy reform
- Attend city and county health board meetings to raise privacy concerns

### Support Affected Communities

- Connect reproductive-age women with resources on protecting health data in restrictive states
- Help people with mental health conditions understand their data rights
- Assist immigrant communities in understanding health data protections (and limitations)
- Support people in recovery from substance use disorders in understanding 42 CFR Part 2 changes

## What Advocates Can Do

### Legislative Advocacy

**Federal Level**

- Contact your U.S. senators and representative to support:
    - My Body, My Data Act (reproductive health data protection)
    - Comprehensive health data privacy legislation covering non-HIPAA entities
    - GINA expansion to life insurance, disability insurance, and long-term care insurance
    - HIPAA modernization including private right of action
    - Increased funding for HHS OCR enforcement
- Provide testimony at congressional hearings on health privacy
- Submit detailed public comments on HHS and FTC health privacy rulemakings
- Meet with legislative staff to share constituent stories about health data misuse

**State Level**

- Support adoption of Washington's My Health My Data Act model in your state
- Advocate for reproductive health data shield laws
- Push for state genetic privacy legislation
- Support state AG enforcement of health data violations
- Testify at state legislative committee hearings

### Regulatory Engagement

- Submit public comments on all HHS and FTC health privacy rulemakings
- Participate in HHS OCR listening sessions on HIPAA modernization
- Engage with state AG offices on health privacy enforcement priorities
- Monitor FTC enforcement actions and publicize outcomes

### Legal Action

- Support organizations filing health privacy lawsuits (EFF, ACLU, EPIC)
- Report HIPAA violations to HHS OCR (complaints are free and can be filed online)
- Report health app deceptive practices to the FTC
- Support class action litigation against companies that misuse health data
- Volunteer with legal aid organizations providing health privacy assistance

## What Health Care Professionals Can Do

### In Practice

- Minimize health data collection to what is necessary for treatment
- Audit your practice's electronic systems for tracking technologies
- Remove tracking pixels from patient-facing websites and portals
- Ensure business associate agreements are current and comprehensive
- Train staff on health privacy best practices and breach prevention

### In Professional Organizations

- Advocate within AMA, specialty societies, and nursing associations for stronger health privacy positions
- Support professional standards that exceed HIPAA minimums
- Participate in developing clinical guidelines for digital health tool recommendations
- Push for professional education on health data privacy

### As Citizen-Advocates

- Use your expertise and credibility to advocate for health privacy reform
- Write op-eds and letters to the editor about health privacy risks
- Testify at legislative hearings as a health professional
- Educate patients about their health data rights during clinical encounters

---

## What Technology Professionals Can Do

### Build Privacy-Respecting Health Technology

- Implement privacy-by-design principles in health applications
- Use local data storage and on-device processing when feasible
- Implement end-to-end encryption for health data
- Conduct regular privacy impact assessments
- Publish transparent privacy policies in plain language

### Audit and Advocate

- Conduct security and privacy audits of health technology systems
- Report vulnerabilities responsibly
- Participate in open-source health privacy tool development
- Advocate within technology companies for privacy-respecting health data practices
- Refuse to implement surveillance features in health technology

---

## What Employers Can Do

- Ensure employee wellness programs comply with HIPAA when applicable
- Minimize health data collection in workplace wellness initiatives
- Use independent, HIPAA-covered vendors for employee health programs
- Firewall health data from employment decisions
- Educate employees about their health data rights

---

## What Researchers Can Do

- Follow robust informed consent practices for health data research
- Implement strong de-identification and re-identification risk assessment
- Advocate for privacy-preserving research methods (federated learning, differential privacy)
- Publish findings on health privacy risks to inform public and policy debate
- Support research exceptions in privacy legislation that include meaningful safeguards

---

## Quick Reference: Where to File Complaints

| Issue | Agency | How to File |
|-------|--------|-------------|
| HIPAA violation by a provider, insurer, or clearinghouse | HHS OCR | hhs.gov/hipaa/filing-a-complaint |
| Health app deceptive privacy practices | FTC | reportfraud.ftc.gov |
| Health data breach by non-HIPAA entity | FTC | reportfraud.ftc.gov |
| State health privacy law violation | State AG | Your state AG's consumer complaint portal |
| Genetic discrimination in employment or health insurance | EEOC | eeoc.gov/filing-charge-discrimination |
| Identity theft using health data | FTC | identitytheft.gov |
