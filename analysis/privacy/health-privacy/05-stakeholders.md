# Health Privacy: Stakeholders

## Who Benefits from the Status Quo

### Digital Health Companies

**Who They Are**

- Health and fitness app developers (Fitbit/Google, Apple Health, MyFitnessPal)
- Mental health platforms (BetterHelp, Talkspace, Cerebral, Calm, Headspace)
- Period-tracking and fertility apps (Flo, Clue, Natural Cycles, Ovia)
- Telehealth companies (Teladoc, Amwell, MDLive)
- Consumer genetic testing companies (23andMe, Ancestry, MyHeritage)
- Digital therapeutics companies (Pear Therapeutics, Akili Interactive)

**What They Gain**

| Benefit | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| Revenue from data monetization | Selling user health data or health-derived insights to advertisers, insurers, and data brokers |
| Product improvement | Using aggregate health data to improve algorithms and features |
| Lower compliance costs | Avoiding HIPAA compliance by operating outside the covered entity framework |
| Competitive advantage | Data-rich companies attract investment and partnerships |
| User acquisition | "Free" services attract users whose data is the actual product |

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **High**

- Comprehensive health privacy regulation would require consent, limit data sharing, and increase compliance costs
- Many business models depend on data monetization that would be restricted

### Health Data Brokers

**Who They Are**

- Companies that aggregate and sell health-related data and inferences
- Notable players: LexisNexis Risk Solutions, Experian Health, IQVIA, Optum (UnitedHealth Group), IBM Watson Health (now Merative)
- Specialty firms selling categories like "likely diabetic," "obesity concerns," or "depression indicators"

**What They Gain**

| Benefit | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| Multi-billion dollar revenue | Selling health data to insurers, marketers, pharmaceutical companies, and researchers |
| Regulatory arbitrage | Operating outside HIPAA by sourcing data from non-covered entities |
| Low acquisition costs | Purchasing health data cheaply from apps and web tracking, selling at markup |

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **Very High**

- Health data brokerage is a core business model
- Regulation would require consent, transparency, and limits on data sales

### Pharmaceutical and Life Sciences Industry

**Who They Are**

- Pharmaceutical companies (Pfizer, Johnson & Johnson, Merck, Eli Lilly)
- Clinical research organizations
- Health analytics firms

**What They Gain**

- Real-world evidence from health data for drug development and marketing
- Targeted advertising to patients with specific conditions
- Market research based on prescription and diagnostic data
- Post-market surveillance data

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **Moderate to High**

- Industry supports research access to health data
- Opposes restrictions that would limit real-world evidence or direct-to-consumer marketing data

### Advertising and Technology Platforms

**Who They Are**

- Meta (Facebook, Instagram) -- tracking pixels on health websites
- Google (Search, YouTube, Android, Fitbit) -- health-related search and device data
- Amazon -- health products purchasing data, One Medical acquisition
- Apple -- Health app, HealthKit, Apple Watch health monitoring

**What They Gain**

- Health-related behavioral data improves advertising targeting
- Health data enhances platform value and user engagement
- Health device ecosystem creates sticky user relationships

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **Moderate**

- Large platforms publicly support some privacy regulation (preferring federal preemption of state laws)
- Privately resist requirements that would limit data collection or advertising practices

## Who Is Harmed by the Status Quo

### Patients and Consumers

**Scale:** 330+ million Americans generate health-related data

**Harms:**

| Harm | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Loss of control | Health data collected, shared, and sold without meaningful consent |
| Discrimination | Health inferences used in employment, insurance, housing, and lending decisions |
| Financial harm | Data breaches expose sensitive information; identity theft using health records costs an average of $13,500 per victim (Ponemon Institute, 2023) |
| Chilling effect on care | Patients avoid seeking treatment due to privacy concerns (KFF survey, 2023: 1 in 5 reproductive-age women withheld health information post-*Dobbs*) |
| Legal jeopardy | Health data used for criminal prosecution (reproductive care in restrictive states) |
| Stigma | Mental health, substance abuse, HIV, and reproductive data exposed |

### Reproductive-Age Women and Pregnant People

**Scale:** Approximately 72 million women of reproductive age in the U.S. (CDC, 2023)

**Specific harms:**

- Period-tracking app data could reveal pregnancies, miscarriages, or abortions
- Location data near reproductive health clinics can be subpoenaed
- Pharmacy records for reproductive medications subject to law enforcement access
- Crisis pregnancy centers collect health data with no HIPAA protections
- Post-*Dobbs*, health data is a tool for criminal enforcement in at least 14 states with total or near-total abortion bans

### People with Mental Health Conditions

**Scale:** 57.8 million adults (23.1%) experienced any mental illness in 2021 (SAMHSA, 2023)

**Specific harms:**

- Therapy app data shared with advertisers and data brokers
- Mental health diagnoses used in employment and insurance discrimination
- Crisis hotline data accessible to law enforcement in some jurisdictions
- Stigma from mental health data exposure deters treatment-seeking

### People with Substance Use Disorders

**Scale:** 46.3 million Americans aged 12+ had a substance use disorder in 2021 (SAMHSA, 2023)

**Specific harms:**

- 42 CFR Part 2 protections weakened by CARES Act amendments
- Treatment records used in criminal proceedings
- Stigma from exposure deters treatment
- Employment discrimination based on treatment history

### Marginalized Communities

**Who is disproportionately affected:**

| Group | Specific Risk |
|-------|---------------|
| Immigrants | Health data sharing with ICE/DHS deters medical care |
| LGBTQ+ individuals | Gender-affirming care records in states restricting such care |
| People with HIV/AIDS | HIV status exposure despite state confidentiality laws |
| Low-income populations | Dependence on public programs that collect extensive data |
| Communities of color | Algorithmic health scoring reflects and reinforces health disparities |

## Institutional Stakeholders

### Health Care Providers

**Position:** Mixed

- Support patient privacy in principle but face compliance costs
- Want clear, consistent rules rather than patchwork
- Prefer HIPAA modernization over new regulatory frameworks
- Concerned about information blocking rules conflicting with privacy
- Some have adopted tracking pixels on websites, exposing patient data

### Health Insurance Companies

**Position:** Generally oppose stronger regulation

- Use health data for underwriting, claims management, and cost control
- Benefit from data broker purchases for risk assessment
- Support interoperability for administrative efficiency
- Oppose private right of action for privacy violations

### Hospitals and Health Systems

**Position:** Cautiously supportive of some reform

- Face significant liability from tracking pixel lawsuits
- Want regulatory clarity on web tracking and data sharing
- Concerned about compliance costs from new requirements
- American Hospital Association generally supports HIPAA but opposes expansive new obligations

### Researchers and Academic Institutions

**Position:** Support privacy with research exceptions

- Depend on access to health data for medical research
- Support de-identification and limited data sets for research
- Oppose restrictions that would make research data access too difficult
- NIH, CDC, and academic medical centers advocate for balanced approaches

## Government Stakeholders

### HHS Office for Civil Rights (OCR)

**Role:** Primary HIPAA enforcement

**Position:**

- Supports strengthening HIPAA but limited by statutory scope and budget
- Has issued guidance on reproductive health data and tracking technologies
- Advocates for increased enforcement resources
- Cannot address health data outside HIPAA's covered entity framework

### Federal Trade Commission

**Role:** Emerging health privacy enforcer

**Position:**

- Aggressively expanding enforcement against health apps using Section 5 authority
- Revised Health Breach Notification Rule to cover health apps more broadly (2023)
- Advocates for comprehensive federal privacy legislation
- Limited by need to prove deception or unfairness rather than having prescriptive rules

### State Attorneys General

**Role:** State-level enforcement

**Position:**

- Several (California, Washington, Illinois, New York) actively enforce health privacy
- Support state authority and oppose federal preemption
- Resource constraints limit enforcement capacity
- Coalition letters and multistate investigations have targeted health data practices

### Law Enforcement

**Position:** Generally opposes stronger health privacy protections

- Values access to health records for criminal investigations
- Opposes warrant requirements for health data held by third parties
- Uses genetic databases for cold case investigations
- Post-*Dobbs*, some state law enforcement seeks reproductive health data

## Advocacy Organizations

### Privacy and Civil Liberties

| Organization | Focus | Position |
|-------------|-------|----------|
| Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF) | Digital rights | Strong advocacy for health data privacy |
| ACLU | Civil liberties broadly | Focuses on reproductive health data post-*Dobbs* |
| Electronic Privacy Information Center (EPIC) | Privacy policy | Advocates for comprehensive health privacy legislation |
| Center for Democracy and Technology (CDT) | Technology policy | Supports data minimization and consent requirements |
| Patient Privacy Rights | Health privacy specifically | Advocates for stronger patient control over health data |

### Health-Specific Advocacy

| Organization | Focus |
|-------------|-------|
| National Health Law Program | Health access and privacy for low-income populations |
| Planned Parenthood | Reproductive health data protection |
| National Alliance on Mental Illness (NAMI) | Mental health data privacy |
| Faces & Voices of Recovery | Substance abuse record protections |

### Industry Associations

| Organization | Position on Health Privacy Reform |
|-------------|----------------------------------|
| American Health Information Management Association (AHIMA) | Supports HIPAA modernization |
| Healthcare Information and Management Systems Society (HIMSS) | Supports interoperability with privacy safeguards |
| Connected Health Initiative | Opposes regulation of health apps as overly burdensome |
| App Association | Opposes extending HIPAA-like obligations to app developers |
| Biotechnology Innovation Organization (BIO) | Supports research access, opposes restrictions on data use |

## Stakeholder Power Map

| Stakeholder | Power Level | Alignment with Reform | Key Leverage |
|-------------|-------------|----------------------|--------------|
| Tech platforms | Very high | Against | Lobbying, market dominance, political donations |
| Health data brokers | High | Strongly against | Lobbying, industry associations |
| Pharmaceutical industry | High | Mostly against | Lobbying, campaign contributions |
| Health insurers | High | Against | Lobbying, employer relationships |
| Health care providers | Moderate | Mixed | Professional associations, patient trust |
| FTC | Moderate | Supportive | Enforcement authority, public visibility |
| HHS OCR | Moderate | Supportive | HIPAA enforcement, rulemaking |
| Privacy advocates | Moderate | Strongly supportive | Public opinion, litigation, media |
| State AGs | Moderate | Mostly supportive | Enforcement, multistate coalitions |
| Patients/consumers | Low individually, high collectively | Strongly supportive | Votes, public opinion, consumer behavior |
| Researchers | Low-moderate | Mixed | Expert credibility, institutional support |
