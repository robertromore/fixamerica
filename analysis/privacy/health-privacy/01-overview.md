# Health Privacy: Overview

## Executive Summary

Health privacy in America rests on a foundation that was built for the paper-record era and has never been adequately updated for the digital age. The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) of 1996 remains the primary federal framework, but its protections apply only to "covered entities" -- health plans, health care clearinghouses, and health care providers who transmit claims electronically -- and their "business associates." This leaves an enormous and growing volume of health-related data entirely outside HIPAA's reach: fitness trackers, period-tracking apps, mental health chatbots, genetic testing services, health-related internet searches, pharmacy loyalty programs, and the data brokers who aggregate and sell health inferences.

The consequences of this gap are far-reaching. After the Supreme Court's *Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization* decision in 2022, reproductive health data became a potential tool for criminal prosecution in states that banned or restricted abortion. Mental health data collected by apps has been sold to data brokers and shared with advertisers. Genetic testing companies have provided data to law enforcement and insurers despite privacy promises. Hospitals and health systems have embedded tracking pixels from Meta and Google on patient-facing web pages, transmitting diagnosis and appointment information to advertising platforms. Even within HIPAA's scope, enforcement has been inconsistent, penalties have been modest relative to the value of the data, and patients have no private right of action.

Health privacy reform requires both strengthening HIPAA and extending privacy protections to the vast universe of health-related data that currently falls outside any federal health privacy law. Without such reform, Americans face a landscape in which their most sensitive personal information -- conditions, treatments, medications, genetic predispositions, reproductive choices, and mental health status -- is collected, sold, and used against their interests with minimal legal constraint.

## Scope

### What This Subtopic Covers

- **HIPAA and its limitations** -- Coverage gaps, enforcement weaknesses, and modernization needs
- **Health apps and digital health data** -- Fitness trackers, mental health apps, telehealth platforms, and wearables
- **Reproductive health data** -- Period trackers, location data near clinics, prescription records, and post-*Dobbs* risks

### Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Covered entity** | Under HIPAA, a health plan, health care clearinghouse, or health care provider that transmits health information electronically |
| **Business associate** | A person or entity that performs functions on behalf of a covered entity involving the use or disclosure of protected health information (PHI) |
| **Protected health information (PHI)** | Individually identifiable health information held or transmitted by a covered entity or its business associate |
| **De-identification** | The process of removing identifiers from health data so it no longer identifies an individual; HIPAA provides two methods (Safe Harbor and Expert Determination) |
| **Health data broker** | An entity that collects, aggregates, and sells health-related data or inferences about individuals |
| **Minimum necessary standard** | HIPAA requirement that covered entities limit PHI disclosures to the minimum necessary for the intended purpose |
| **42 CFR Part 2** | Federal regulation providing heightened privacy protections for substance use disorder treatment records |

## Core Tensions

- **Innovation vs. protection** -- Digital health tools improve outcomes but collect sensitive data outside HIPAA's reach
- **Interoperability vs. privacy** -- Making health records portable increases both access and exposure risk
- **Law enforcement access vs. patient trust** -- Compelled disclosure of health records deters people from seeking care
- **Research value vs. consent** -- De-identified health data enables medical research but re-identification risks are growing
- **Federal uniformity vs. state experimentation** -- A federal floor helps consistency but must not preempt stronger state protections
- **Reproductive autonomy vs. state criminal law** -- Post-*Dobbs*, health data can be used to enforce abortion restrictions

## Key Questions

1. How much health-related data falls outside HIPAA's protections, and what are the real-world consequences of this gap?
2. What reforms would bring digital health tools -- apps, wearables, telehealth -- under meaningful privacy regulation?
3. How should reproductive health data be protected in a post-*Dobbs* legal landscape?
4. What is the appropriate scope of law enforcement access to health records?
5. Can health data de-identification remain effective given advances in re-identification techniques?
6. How should genetic and genomic data be regulated as consumer testing and research databases grow?
7. What enforcement mechanisms are needed to make health privacy protections meaningful?

## Vision of Success

A reformed health privacy landscape would feature:

- **Comprehensive coverage** -- All health-related data, regardless of who collects it, subject to privacy protections
- **Meaningful consent** -- Patients and consumers give informed, specific consent before health data is shared for non-treatment purposes
- **Data minimization** -- Entities collect only the health data necessary for the stated purpose and delete it when no longer needed
- **Robust enforcement** -- Significant penalties for violations, a private right of action, and adequately funded oversight
- **Reproductive data shields** -- Federal protections preventing health data from being used to enforce state abortion restrictions
- **Genetic data protections** -- Comprehensive limits on collection, use, and sharing of genetic information
- **Research access with safeguards** -- De-identified data available for medical research with strong re-identification penalties
- **Patient rights** -- Enforceable rights to access, correct, and delete health data across all holders
