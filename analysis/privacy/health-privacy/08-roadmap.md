# Health Privacy: Roadmap

## Phase 1: Immediate Actions (0-12 Months)

### Build Awareness and Coalitions

**Education**

- Educate the public about the gap between HIPAA and actual health data protection
- Publicize concrete examples of health data misuse (BetterHelp, GoodRx, hospital tracking pixels, Nebraska Facebook messages case)
- Distribute guides on protecting personal health data in the app era
- Engage medical professionals as privacy advocates through AMA and specialty society channels

**Coalition Building**

- Unite privacy advocates (EFF, EPIC, ACLU), health organizations (AMA, patient advocacy groups), and consumer groups
- Recruit bipartisan congressional champions for health privacy legislation
- Engage state AGs who have been active on health privacy enforcement
- Connect reproductive rights organizations with privacy advocates on data shield legislation
- Build alliances with mental health and addiction treatment communities on data protection

### Support Active Efforts

**Federal**

- Advocate for reintroduction and passage of the My Body, My Data Act
- Support FTC rulemaking on health apps and data brokers
- Push for increased appropriations for HHS OCR enforcement
- Encourage FTC to continue enforcement actions against health apps

**State**

- Support adoption of Washington-style My Health My Data Act in additional states
- Push for reproductive health data shield laws in states without them
- Advocate for genetic privacy legislation in response to 23andMe bankruptcy
- Support state AG enforcement actions against health data violations

### Advocate

- Submit public comments on any HHS or FTC health privacy rulemakings
- Testify at state legislative hearings on health data privacy bills
- Contact members of Congress about health privacy legislation
- Organize constituent meetings with legislators focused on health privacy stories

## Phase 2: Short-Term Goals (1-2 Years)

### Federal Legislative Priorities

**Priority 1: Reproductive Health Data Shield**

- Pass federal legislation prohibiting use of health data to investigate or prosecute lawful reproductive health care
- Extend protections beyond HIPAA covered entities to apps, search engines, and location data providers
- Create federal testimonial privilege for reproductive health communications
- Target: Introduction in first session of Congress, committee passage within 12 months

**Priority 2: Mental Health Data Protection**

- Enact targeted protections for mental health app data, therapy platform records, and crisis hotline data
- Prohibit use of mental health data for advertising
- Require disclosure and consent for AI use in mental health services
- Target: Introduction within 6 months, passage within 18 months

**Priority 3: Tracking Technology Ban**

- Prohibit health websites and apps from embedding advertising tracking technologies that transmit health information
- Apply to hospitals, providers, telehealth platforms, and health insurers
- Provide safe harbor for organizations that conduct regular audits
- Target: FTC rulemaking within 12 months; legislative codification within 24 months

### State Legislative Priorities

| Priority | Target States | Model |
|----------|--------------|-------|
| My Health My Data Act adoption | New York, Illinois, Massachusetts, New Jersey, Oregon | Washington State SB 5396 (2023) |
| Reproductive health data shields | Pennsylvania, Michigan, Minnesota, Arizona, Virginia | California AB 352 (2022) |
| Genetic privacy laws | All states without genetic privacy statutes | Illinois Genetic Information Privacy Act |
| Health data breach notification enhancement | States with weak notification laws | California CMIA breach provisions |

### Enforcement Actions

- Push for HHS OCR to increase enforcement actions to 50+ per year
- Support FTC in expanding Health Breach Notification Rule enforcement
- Encourage state AG coalitions on multistate health data investigations
- Advocate for DOJ criminal prosecution of egregious HIPAA violations

## Phase 3: Medium-Term Goals (2-4 Years)

### Comprehensive Federal Health Privacy Law

**Core legislation:**

- Enact a comprehensive health data protection law covering all entities that collect, process, or share health-related data
- Include: broad health data definition, consent requirements, data minimization, purpose limitation, deletion rights, private right of action
- Establish federal floor without preempting stronger state protections
- Include research exceptions with IRB oversight and strengthened de-identification requirements

**Target timeline:**

| Milestone | Target Date |
|-----------|-------------|
| Committee hearings | Year 2, first session |
| Committee markup | Year 2, second session |
| Floor votes | Year 3 |
| Enactment | Year 3-4 |
| Compliance deadline (large entities) | 12 months after enactment |
| Compliance deadline (small entities) | 24 months after enactment |

### HIPAA Modernization

- Amend HIPAA to include private right of action
- Increase penalty caps to revenue-based levels (2-4% of annual revenue)
- Strengthen de-identification requirements
- Update cybersecurity standards
- Expand patient access rights and reduce fees

### Genetic Data Protection

- Expand GINA to cover life insurance, disability insurance, long-term care insurance
- Enact bankruptcy protections for genetic data (cannot be transferred as business asset without consent)
- Require warrants for law enforcement access to consumer genetic databases
- Create right to deletion for genetic testing company data

### Health Data Broker Registration

- Require all entities selling health data or inferences to register with the FTC
- Mandate transparency about data sources, categories, and customers
- Require affirmative consent for health data sales
- Prohibit sale of sensitive health categories without explicit consent
- Restrict government purchases of health data from brokers to warrant-based access

## Phase 4: Long-Term Goals (4-8 Years)

### Institutional Reform

**Dedicated Health Data Protection Authority**

- Establish a division within FTC or a new independent agency focused on health data privacy
- Fund with adequate resources (target: $100+ million annual budget)
- Grant rulemaking, enforcement, and audit authority
- Staff with health privacy expertise and technical specialists

**International Harmonization**

- Negotiate health data adequacy agreements with EU under GDPR framework
- Align U.S. health privacy standards with international best practices
- Facilitate cross-border health data flows for research with privacy safeguards
- Participate in global health data governance initiatives

### Technology-Forward Protections

- Establish privacy-by-design requirements for health technologies
- Require privacy impact assessments for new health data systems
- Create standards for privacy-preserving health data analytics (federated learning, differential privacy)
- Address emerging technologies: AI-driven health diagnostics, brain-computer interfaces, continuous health monitoring

### Cultural Shift

- Normalize privacy as a component of quality health care
- Train health care professionals on digital health privacy
- Integrate health data literacy into patient education
- Establish industry standards for privacy-respecting health technology design

## Risk Factors

| Risk | Likelihood | Mitigation |
|------|-----------|------------|
| Federal legislation stalls due to partisan division | High | Continue state-level action; use FTC rulemaking as interim measure |
| Industry lobbying weakens legislation | High | Build strong public awareness campaign; engage bipartisan champions |
| Federal preemption eliminates stronger state laws | Moderate | Insist on floor-not-ceiling approach in any federal legislation |
| Technology evolves faster than regulation | High | Build technology-neutral principles into legislation; require regular review |
| Enforcement resources remain inadequate | High | Tie enforcement funding to fee structures; enable private right of action |
| Supreme Court invalidates reproductive data shields | Moderate | Build multiple legal theories; use spending power and preemption |
