# Law Enforcement Access to Data: Stakeholders

## Who Benefits from the Status Quo

### Federal Law Enforcement Agencies

**Who They Are**

- Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI)
- Drug Enforcement Administration (DEA)
- Department of Homeland Security (DHS), including Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) and Customs and Border Protection (CBP)
- Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives (ATF)
- Internal Revenue Service Criminal Investigation (IRS-CI)
- U.S. Secret Service
- U.S. Marshals Service

**What They Gain**

| Benefit | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| Broad data access without warrants | ECPA's outdated standards, third-party doctrine, and data purchase loophole allow access to vast digital records |
| Reduced investigative costs | Purchasing commercially available data is cheaper and faster than traditional surveillance |
| Operational secrecy | Non-disclosure agreements and parallel construction shield methods from scrutiny |
| Expanded surveillance capability | Emerging technologies (facial recognition, geofence warrants, AI analytics) provide new investigative tools without corresponding legal constraints |
| Minimal accountability | Limited congressional oversight, FOIA resistance, and lack of transparency requirements reduce external checks |

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **Very High**

- ECPA modernization and warrant requirements would constrain current practices
- Data purchase restrictions would eliminate a key intelligence source
- Technology oversight requirements would slow adoption of new surveillance tools

### State and Local Law Enforcement

**Who They Are**

- State police and highway patrol agencies
- Municipal police departments (18,000+ agencies nationwide)
- County sheriffs' offices
- State bureaus of investigation
- Fusion centers (80 federal-state-local intelligence hubs)

**What They Gain**

| Benefit | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| Access to federal data programs | Fusion centers and federal partnerships provide access to databases and tools otherwise unavailable |
| Surveillance technology adoption | Federal grants fund Stingrays, ALPRs, facial recognition, and predictive policing without local oversight |
| Data sharing networks | Participation in ALPR networks, Real-Time Crime Centers, and other shared surveillance infrastructure |
| Discretion in deployment | No federal standards govern local use of surveillance technology |

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **High**

- Local agencies resist oversight requirements that constrain investigative discretion
- Police unions actively lobby against warrant requirements and transparency mandates

### Surveillance Technology Vendors

**Who They Are**

- L3Harris Technologies (cell-site simulators)
- Clearview AI (facial recognition)
- Flock Safety, Motorola Solutions / Vigilant Solutions (ALPRs)
- Palantir Technologies (data analytics)
- Babel Street (location data, social media monitoring)
- ShadowDragon (social media analysis)
- SoundThinking / ShotSpotter (acoustic gunshot detection)
- Cellebrite, Grayshift (mobile device forensics)

**What They Gain**

| Benefit | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| Government contracts | Federal, state, and local agencies spend hundreds of millions annually on surveillance technology |
| Regulatory-free environment | No federal standards for accuracy, bias testing, or deployment conditions |
| NDA-protected market | Non-disclosure agreements with law enforcement clients prevent competitive scrutiny and public accountability |
| Expanding market | Each new technology deployment creates demand for upgrades, training, and maintenance |

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **Very High**

- Regulation would impose testing, accuracy, and transparency requirements
- Technology moratoriums would directly threaten revenue
- Oversight boards would create barriers to sales

### Data Brokers and Aggregators

**Who They Are**

- Babel Street (formerly Venntel) -- location data
- Locate X -- location data
- LexisNexis Risk Solutions -- comprehensive personal data
- Thomson Reuters CLEAR -- law enforcement data platform
- TLO (TransUnion) -- person search and skip tracing

**What They Gain**

| Benefit | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| Government revenue stream | Federal contracts for commercial surveillance data total $200+ million annually |
| Regulatory arbitrage | Selling data to government avoids warrant requirements that would apply to direct collection |
| Low legal risk | No federal law restricts government purchase of commercially available data |

**Level of Opposition to Reform**: **Very High**

- The *Fourth Amendment Is Not For Sale Act* would eliminate a core revenue stream
- Warrant requirements for data purchases would dramatically reduce government demand

## Who Is Harmed by the Status Quo

### Communities of Color

**Scale:** Surveillance technologies are deployed disproportionately in Black and Latino neighborhoods

**Harms:**

| Harm | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Discriminatory surveillance | Predictive policing algorithms concentrate enforcement in communities of color based on biased historical data |
| Facial recognition errors | NIST-documented higher false positive rates for darker-skinned individuals; 7+ documented wrongful arrests |
| Immigration enforcement | ICE uses commercial data, ALPR data, and social media monitoring to target immigrant communities |
| Chilling effect on speech and assembly | Social media monitoring and protest surveillance deter First Amendment activity |
| Over-policing feedback loop | Surveillance-driven policing generates more arrests in surveilled communities, justifying further surveillance |

### Immigrant Communities

**Scale:** 44+ million foreign-born residents in the United States (Census Bureau, 2023)

**Specific harms:**

- ICE purchases commercial location data, utility records, and DMV photos to locate undocumented individuals
- Fusion centers share local law enforcement data with federal immigration authorities
- Fear of surveillance deters immigrants from accessing health care, education, and social services
- Sanctuary city protections are undermined by federal data purchases that bypass local non-cooperation policies

### Low-Income Communities

**Scale:** Approximately 37 million Americans live below the poverty line (Census Bureau, 2023)

**Specific harms:**

- Higher surveillance density in low-income neighborhoods (more ALPRs, more cameras, more police presence)
- Less ability to adopt privacy-protective technologies (VPNs, encrypted services, privacy-focused devices)
- Greater reliance on public services that collect extensive data (benefits programs, public transit, public housing)
- Inability to afford legal counsel to challenge surveillance practices

### Political Activists and Protesters

**Scale:** Millions of Americans participate in protests and political organizing

**Specific harms:**

- Documented social media monitoring of racial justice protests (2020)
- Geofence warrants used to identify individuals present at protest locations
- Aerial drone surveillance of protests by CBP and local agencies
- Chilling effect on First Amendment-protected activity

### Criminal Defendants

**Scale:** 10+ million arrests annually in the United States

**Specific harms:**

| Harm | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Parallel construction | Evidence laundering conceals surveillance methods, denying defendants the ability to challenge evidence |
| NDA-concealed evidence | Non-disclosure agreements between agencies and vendors prevent disclosure of surveillance methods in court |
| Algorithmic risk assessment | Opaque predictive tools influence bail, sentencing, and parole decisions |
| Inadequate defense resources | Public defenders lack technical expertise and resources to challenge digital surveillance evidence |

### All Americans

**Scale:** 330+ million people

**Harms:**

- Pervasive data collection creates comprehensive digital profiles of every person with a cell phone or internet connection
- No meaningful ability to opt out of commercial data collection that feeds law enforcement databases
- Erosion of Fourth Amendment protections through the third-party doctrine and data purchase loophole
- Government surveillance capability far exceeds what any democratic society has previously permitted

## Institutional Stakeholders

### Technology Companies

**Position:** Mixed and evolving

- Some companies have pushed back on law enforcement demands (Apple encryption, Google ending geofence warrants)
- Others cooperate extensively through legal process and voluntary partnerships (Amazon Ring, Meta)
- Companies face legal compulsion through warrants and subpoenas and political pressure to cooperate
- Business models built on data collection create the repositories that law enforcement accesses
- Industry trade groups generally support ECPA reform but oppose broader regulation

### The Judiciary

**Position:** Gradually supportive of stronger protections

- The Supreme Court has moved toward digital privacy protections (*Riley*, *Carpenter*) but slowly and narrowly
- Lower courts are split on geofence warrants, keyword warrants, and the scope of *Carpenter*
- Magistrate judges who review warrant applications often lack technical expertise to evaluate digital search requests
- Federal courts are more protective than many state courts

### Congress

**Position:** Aware but inactive

- Broad bipartisan agreement that ECPA needs updating (Email Privacy Act passed House unanimously multiple times)
- Senate has repeatedly failed to advance ECPA reform
- The *Fourth Amendment Is Not For Sale Act* has bipartisan sponsors but has not passed
- Congressional dysfunction, competing priorities, and law enforcement lobbying prevent action
- Intelligence and judiciary committees receive briefings but rarely constrain agencies

### Inspectors General

**Position:** Supportive of oversight within institutional constraints

- DOJ Inspector General and DHS Inspector General have issued reports identifying surveillance problems
- Recommendations are advisory, not binding
- Reports are often delayed and heavily redacted
- IGs provide the most consistent institutional oversight but lack enforcement power

## Government Stakeholders

### Department of Justice

**Role:** Primary federal law enforcement authority; sets DOJ policy on data access

**Position:**

- Has voluntarily adopted warrant-for-email-content policy since 2017, but this is policy, not law
- Opposes statutory warrant requirements that would constrain prosecutorial discretion
- Resists data purchase restrictions
- Supports broad interpretation of existing authorities

### Federal Trade Commission

**Role:** Consumer protection enforcement; emerging privacy regulator

**Position:**

- Has taken enforcement action against companies that mislead consumers about data sharing with law enforcement
- Limited authority to regulate law enforcement data access directly
- Advocates for comprehensive privacy legislation that would include data broker restrictions

### State Attorneys General

**Role:** State-level enforcement and advocacy

**Position:**

- Several state AGs (California, New York, Illinois) actively enforce state electronic privacy laws
- Bipartisan coalition has supported federal ECPA reform
- State AGs have investigated surveillance technology vendors
- Resource constraints limit enforcement capacity

## Advocacy Organizations

### Privacy and Civil Liberties

| Organization | Focus | Position |
|-------------|-------|----------|
| American Civil Liberties Union (ACLU) | Civil liberties broadly | Leading advocate for warrant requirements, facial recognition bans, and surveillance technology oversight |
| Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF) | Digital rights | Comprehensive advocacy for ECPA reform, data purchase restrictions, and technology accountability |
| Electronic Privacy Information Center (EPIC) | Privacy policy | Advocates for comprehensive law enforcement data access reform |
| Brennan Center for Justice | Democracy and justice | Research and advocacy on police surveillance and racial disparities |
| Center for Democracy and Technology (CDT) | Technology policy | Supports warrant requirements and surveillance technology standards |
| Georgetown Law Center on Privacy and Technology | Research and policy | "American Dragnet" report documenting government data purchases |

### Criminal Justice Reform

| Organization | Focus |
|-------------|-------|
| National Association of Criminal Defense Lawyers (NACDL) | Defense attorney advocacy for disclosure and warrant requirements |
| Innocence Project | Challenges convictions obtained through unreliable surveillance evidence |
| Leadership Conference on Civil and Human Rights | Coalition advocacy on surveillance and racial justice |
| Color of Change | Racial justice advocacy including surveillance reform |

### Law Enforcement Associations (Opposing Reform)

| Organization | Position on Reform |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Fraternal Order of Police (FOP) | Opposes warrant requirements and oversight mandates |
| National Association of Police Organizations (NAPO) | Opposes restrictions on surveillance technology |
| International Association of Chiefs of Police (IACP) | Opposes ECPA reform that would slow investigations |
| Major Cities Chiefs Association (MCCA) | Supports some technology oversight but opposes federal mandates |
| National Sheriffs' Association | Opposes data purchase restrictions and facial recognition bans |

## Stakeholder Power Map

| Stakeholder | Power Level | Alignment with Reform | Key Leverage |
|-------------|-------------|----------------------|--------------|
| Federal law enforcement agencies | Very high | Strongly against | Congressional relationships, national security framing, secrecy authorities |
| Surveillance technology vendors | High | Strongly against | Government contracts, lobbying, NDA leverage |
| Law enforcement unions/associations | High | Strongly against | Political donations, endorsements, public safety narrative |
| Data brokers | High | Strongly against | Lobbying, industry associations, revolving door |
| Technology companies | High | Mixed | Lobbying, transparency reports, platform control |
| Congress | High | Divided | Legislative power, oversight authority |
| Federal judiciary | Moderate-high | Gradually supportive | Constitutional interpretation, warrant review |
| State/local oversight boards | Moderate | Supportive | Local democratic authority, CCOPS ordinances |
| Privacy advocates | Moderate | Strongly supportive | Litigation, public opinion, media, research |
| State AGs | Moderate | Mostly supportive | Enforcement authority, multistate coalitions |
| Inspectors General | Low-moderate | Supportive | Audit authority, public reports |
| Affected communities | Low individually, high collectively | Strongly supportive | Votes, public opinion, moral authority |
| Criminal defendants | Very low | Strongly supportive | Constitutional rights, but limited resources and political voice |
