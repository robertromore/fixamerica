# Law Enforcement Access to Data: Roadmap

## Phase 1: Immediate Actions (0-12 Months)

### Build Awareness and Coalitions

**Education**

- Publicize the scope of law enforcement data access through media engagement, op-eds, and social media campaigns
- Educate the public about the data purchase loophole, using concrete examples (ICE purchasing location data, IRS purchasing phone records)
- Distribute guides on digital privacy protection and surveillance self-defense
- Engage the legal community (defense attorneys, law professors, bar associations) on parallel construction and digital evidence challenges
- Use documented wrongful arrests from facial recognition to illustrate surveillance harms

**Coalition Building**

- Unite privacy advocates (ACLU, EFF, EPIC, CDT, Brennan Center) with criminal justice reform organizations
- Recruit bipartisan congressional champions for ECPA reform and the *Fourth Amendment Is Not For Sale Act*
- Engage conservative organizations that oppose government overreach (Cato Institute, R Street Institute, FreedomWorks)
- Connect communities of color affected by surveillance with privacy advocacy organizations
- Build alliances between technology company advocates for warrant requirements and civil liberties groups
- Coordinate with defense attorney organizations (NACDL) on parallel construction challenges

### Support Active Efforts

**Federal**

- Advocate for Senate action on ECPA reform (Email Privacy Act has passed the House multiple times)
- Support reintroduction and passage of the *Fourth Amendment Is Not For Sale Act*
- Push for congressional hearings on law enforcement data purchases and surveillance technology
- Encourage DOJ Inspector General audits of agency data purchase practices
- Support appropriations riders restricting use of funds for warrantless data purchases

**State and Local**

- Support adoption of CCOPS (Community Control Over Police Surveillance) ordinances in additional cities
- Advocate for state-level electronic privacy laws modeled on CalECPA
- Push for state facial recognition moratoriums and restrictions
- Support state legislation requiring warrants for cell-site simulator use
- Encourage local police oversight boards to review surveillance technology deployments

### Advocate

- Submit public comments on any DOJ or DHS rulemaking affecting data access policies
- Testify at state and local hearings on surveillance technology ordinances
- Contact members of Congress about ECPA reform and the data purchase loophole
- File FOIA requests to document law enforcement data access practices
- Support litigation challenging warrantless surveillance practices

## Phase 2: Short-Term Goals (1-2 Years)

### Federal Legislative Priorities

**Priority 1: ECPA Modernization**

- Pass the Email Privacy Act (or equivalent) requiring warrants for all stored communications content regardless of age
- Extend warrant requirements to sensitive metadata (location data, browsing history, IP address logs)
- Establish notification requirements with limited judicial exceptions
- Mandate annual transparency reporting by DOJ on data requests
- Target: Committee markup within first session; floor vote within 18 months

**Priority 2: Close the Data Purchase Loophole**

- Pass the *Fourth Amendment Is Not For Sale Act* requiring warrants for government purchase of personal data from data brokers
- Prohibit bulk data purchases without individualized suspicion
- Establish suppression remedy for evidence derived from unlawful purchases
- Target: Introduction within 6 months; committee passage within 12 months

**Priority 3: Surveillance Technology Transparency**

- Require all federal agencies to disclose surveillance technologies in use
- Mandate annual reporting on data requests, technology deployments, and data purchases
- Restrict NDA provisions that prevent judicial or defense attorney disclosure of surveillance methods
- Target: Attach to appropriations or defense authorization within 12 months

### State and Local Priorities

| Priority | Target Jurisdictions | Model |
|----------|---------------------|-------|
| Electronic privacy laws | New York, Massachusetts, Illinois, Oregon, Washington | CalECPA (California SB 178, 2015) |
| CCOPS ordinances | 10+ additional cities | Oakland Privacy Advisory Commission model |
| Facial recognition restrictions | Statewide in 10+ states | San Francisco / Massachusetts models |
| ALPR data retention limits | States without restrictions | Montana, Maine data retention laws |
| Cell-site simulator warrant requirements | States without specific statutes | Washington State Privacy Act |

### Litigation Priorities

- Support Supreme Court cases extending *Carpenter* to additional data categories
- Challenge geofence and keyword warrants in multiple circuits to develop favorable precedent
- File civil rights challenges to racially disparate surveillance technology deployment
- Challenge NDA provisions that prevent defense attorney access to surveillance evidence
- Support defense attorneys raising parallel construction challenges in criminal cases

## Phase 3: Medium-Term Goals (2-4 Years)

### Comprehensive Surveillance Reform Legislation

**Core federal legislation:**

- Establish federal standards for law enforcement surveillance technology (accuracy testing, bias auditing, independent evaluation)
- Impose moratorium on real-time facial recognition by law enforcement until standards are met
- Require community oversight processes for surveillance technology acquisition
- Prohibit parallel construction and require disclosure of all surveillance methods
- Create private right of action for individuals subjected to unlawful surveillance
- Establish federal grants for local surveillance technology oversight boards

**Target timeline:**

| Milestone | Target Date |
|-----------|-------------|
| Committee hearings | Year 2, first session |
| Committee markup | Year 2, second session |
| Floor votes | Year 3 |
| Enactment | Year 3-4 |
| Agency rulemaking | 12-18 months after enactment |
| Compliance deadline | 24 months after enactment |

### Digital Warrant Process Reform

- Enact restrictions on geofence warrants (require particularity, mandate minimization, require subsequent judicial review)
- Prohibit keyword warrants that seek to identify unknown individuals by their search terms
- Strengthen particularity requirements for all digital search warrants
- Authorize courts to appoint independent technical advisors for digital warrant review
- Require law enforcement to file returns describing all data obtained from digital searches

### Disparate Impact Protections

- Require racial and socioeconomic impact assessments before surveillance technology deployment
- Mandate independent bias audits of all algorithmic tools used in law enforcement
- Create federal oversight mechanism for surveillance equity
- Establish private right of action for communities subjected to discriminatory surveillance
- Condition federal law enforcement grants on compliance with equity standards

### Oversight Infrastructure

- Establish or strengthen an independent federal oversight body for law enforcement surveillance (potentially within DOJ Inspector General or as a new entity)
- Fund state and local surveillance technology oversight boards through federal grants
- Create a national database of surveillance technology deployments accessible to researchers and the public
- Mandate regular GAO audits of federal agency surveillance practices

## Phase 4: Long-Term Goals (4-8 Years)

### Institutional Reform

**Federal Data Access Standards Board**

- Establish a permanent federal body to develop and update standards for law enforcement data access
- Include representatives from law enforcement, privacy advocates, technology experts, affected communities, and the judiciary
- Grant authority to issue binding standards for surveillance technology, data access procedures, and privacy protections
- Fund with adequate resources (target: $50+ million annual budget)

**Fourth Amendment Modernization**

- Support litigation and advocacy to extend *Carpenter* principles to all categories of sensitive digital data
- Advocate for Supreme Court reconsideration of the third-party doctrine as applied to modern digital records
- Support state constitutional amendments strengthening digital privacy protections
- Build case law through strategic litigation that progressively narrows the scope of warrantless data access

### Technology-Forward Protections

- Establish privacy-by-design requirements for surveillance technology vendors seeking government contracts
- Require privacy impact assessments for all new law enforcement data systems
- Create standards for data minimization, retention limits, and deletion in law enforcement databases
- Address emerging technologies: AI-powered surveillance, real-time social media analysis, biometric systems beyond facial recognition

### Cultural Shift

- Normalize warrant requirements and oversight as essential components of professional policing
- Train law enforcement officers on constitutional data access requirements as part of academy curricula
- Establish professional standards that reward compliance and penalize surveillance abuse
- Build public expectation that surveillance practices are transparent and accountable

## Risk Factors

| Risk | Likelihood | Mitigation |
|------|-----------|------------|
| Major terrorist attack or crime wave generates pressure for expanded surveillance | High | Pre-build coalition commitment to maintaining oversight standards; demonstrate that oversight does not prevent effective response |
| Federal legislation stalls due to law enforcement lobbying | High | Continue state and local action; use litigation as alternative pathway; support appropriations riders |
| Supreme Court declines to extend *Carpenter* | Moderate | Pursue statutory protections independent of judicial action; build state constitutional protections |
| Technology evolves faster than regulation | High | Build technology-neutral principles into legislation; require regular review cycles |
| Industry lobbying weakens legislation with loopholes | High | Engage civil society in drafting; resist exemptions and carve-outs; insist on strong enforcement mechanisms |
| Partisan polarization prevents bipartisan coalition | Moderate | Frame as government overreach (appeals to right) and civil rights (appeals to left); maintain bipartisan sponsor model |
| Local jurisdictions resist federal oversight | Moderate | Tie compliance to federal funding eligibility; provide technical assistance and transition support |
