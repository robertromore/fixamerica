# Law Enforcement Access to Data: Actions

## What Individuals Can Do

### Protect Your Own Digital Privacy

**Reduce Your Data Footprint**

- Use end-to-end encrypted messaging apps (Signal) for sensitive communications
- Use a VPN (Virtual Private Network) to encrypt internet traffic and obscure browsing activity from your ISP
- Disable location services on your phone for apps that do not require it
- Review and restrict app permissions regularly (especially location, contacts, microphone, camera)
- Use privacy-focused browsers (Tor, Brave, Firefox with privacy extensions) and search engines (DuckDuckGo)
- Opt out of data broker listings where possible (Privacy Rights Clearinghouse maintains opt-out guides)

**Secure Your Devices**

- Use strong passcodes on all devices (not biometric unlock, which may receive less legal protection than passcodes)
- Enable full-disk encryption on phones and computers
- Keep software and operating systems updated
- Use a password manager with unique passwords for each account
- Enable multi-factor authentication on all accounts

**Limit Commercial Data Collection**

- Be aware that data you share with apps and services may be purchased by law enforcement
- Read privacy policies for services that collect sensitive data (location, health, financial)
- Delete accounts and data from services you no longer use
- Opt out of advertising tracking (Apple: Settings > Privacy > Tracking; Google: myaccount.google.com/data-and-privacy)
- Use cash or privacy-focused payment methods for sensitive purchases

**Know Your Rights During Police Encounters**

- You are not required to unlock your phone for police without a warrant (*Riley v. California*, 2014)
- You have the right to refuse consent to a search of your device
- If police have a warrant, ask to see it and note its scope -- warrants must specify what they authorize searching
- You do not have to provide passwords or passcodes without a court order (Fifth Amendment protections may apply, though case law is evolving)
- Say clearly: "I do not consent to a search of my device"

## What Communities Can Do

### Organize Locally

- Form or join community surveillance oversight coalitions
- Attend city council meetings when surveillance technology purchases or policies are on the agenda
- Request public records about local police surveillance technology inventory and data access practices
- Partner with local ACLU, EFF, or NACDL chapters on surveillance reform campaigns
- Host "know your rights" events focused on digital privacy and police encounters
- Connect with national organizations like the Surveillance Technology Oversight Project (S.T.O.P.)

### Advocate for Local Surveillance Oversight

**Push for a CCOPS Ordinance**

The Community Control Over Police Surveillance (CCOPS) model requires:

- Public disclosure of all surveillance technologies before acquisition
- City council approval of new surveillance technology purchases
- Public comment period and community impact assessment
- Annual reporting on how technologies are used and their outcomes
- Sunset provisions requiring periodic reauthorization

20+ cities have adopted CCOPS-style ordinances; the ACLU provides model legislation and organizing guides.

**Engage Local Government**

- Advocate for police oversight boards with authority to review surveillance practices
- Push for local ALPR data retention limits (many jurisdictions have none)
- Support municipal facial recognition bans or moratoriums
- Request that local governments audit police use of data brokers and commercial surveillance tools
- Demand transparency in police department budgets regarding surveillance technology spending

### Support Affected Communities

- Volunteer with organizations providing legal support to individuals targeted by surveillance
- Support immigrant rights organizations that challenge ICE surveillance practices
- Amplify the voices of communities of color who experience disproportionate surveillance
- Connect affected individuals with legal resources when surveillance practices violate their rights
- Support bail funds and legal defense organizations that can challenge surveillance evidence

## What Advocates Can Do

### Legislative Advocacy

**Federal Level**

- Contact your U.S. senators and representative to support:
    - ECPA reform (Email Privacy Act or equivalent)
    - The *Fourth Amendment Is Not For Sale Act* (data purchase loophole)
    - The Facial Recognition and Biometric Technology Moratorium Act
    - Surveillance technology oversight legislation
    - Increased funding for public defenders to challenge digital evidence
- Provide testimony at congressional hearings on surveillance reform
- Submit public comments on DOJ and DHS rulemaking affecting data access
- Meet with legislative staff to share constituent stories about surveillance impact

**State Level**

- Support adoption of CalECPA-style electronic privacy laws in your state
- Advocate for state facial recognition moratoriums or restrictions
- Push for state legislation requiring warrants for cell-site simulators
- Support state data broker registration and disclosure laws
- Testify at state legislative committee hearings on surveillance reform

### Regulatory Engagement

- Submit FOIA requests to document law enforcement data access practices in your jurisdiction
- File public records requests for surveillance technology contracts and purchase orders
- Monitor and publicize Inspector General reports on surveillance practices
- Engage with local police oversight boards on surveillance policy
- Participate in public comment processes for surveillance technology deployments

### Legal Action

- Support organizations filing surveillance challenges (ACLU, EFF, EPIC, NACDL)
- If you believe your data was accessed unlawfully, consult with a privacy attorney
- Report suspected surveillance abuse to:
    - DOJ Inspector General (oig.justice.gov)
    - DHS Inspector General (oig.dhs.gov)
    - Your state Attorney General
    - The ACLU (aclu.org/report-violation)
- Support class action litigation against surveillance practices that violate constitutional rights
- Volunteer with legal aid organizations that represent individuals challenging surveillance evidence

## What Legal Professionals Can Do

### Defense Attorneys

- Challenge parallel construction in every case where it may be present
- File motions to compel disclosure of surveillance methods used to develop evidence
- Challenge NDA provisions that prevent disclosure of surveillance technology in court proceedings
- Request Franks hearings to examine the accuracy of warrant applications for digital evidence
- Object to geofence and keyword warrant evidence as insufficiently particularized
- Educate yourself on surveillance technology capabilities through EFF and NACDL resources
- Share successful challenge strategies with the defense bar through CLE presentations and publications

### Prosecutors

- Adopt internal policies requiring warrants for all digital data access, including data purchases
- Refuse to use evidence obtained through parallel construction
- Require disclosure of all surveillance methods in discovery
- Decline to prosecute cases built on unreliable surveillance technology (especially facial recognition without corroboration)
- Advocate within your office for ethical data access practices

### Judges and Magistrates

- Scrutinize digital search warrant applications for specificity and particularity
- Require law enforcement to explain the scope and sensitivity of data sought in warrant applications
- Question whether geofence warrants and keyword warrants satisfy the Fourth Amendment's particularity requirement
- Seek independent technical advice when evaluating complex digital surveillance requests
- Appoint special masters for digital evidence review where appropriate

## What Technology Professionals Can Do

### Build Privacy-Respecting Tools

- Develop and contribute to end-to-end encryption tools and privacy-preserving technologies
- Implement data minimization in services you build -- collect less, retain less
- Design systems that store data on users' devices rather than centralized servers where possible
- Support open-source alternatives to commercial surveillance tools
- Conduct and publish security audits of surveillance technology

### Advocate Within Companies

- Push back against voluntary data sharing partnerships with law enforcement (e.g., Ring-style partnerships)
- Advocate for narrow responses to law enforcement data requests
- Support transparency reporting and detailed publication of data request statistics
- Refuse to build surveillance tools designed to circumvent warrant requirements
- Whistleblow on illegal or unconstitutional government data access (protections exist under federal and state whistleblower laws)

### Research and Publish

- Study and document the accuracy and bias of surveillance technologies
- Publish findings on algorithmic bias in predictive policing and facial recognition
- Develop and share tools for auditing surveillance technology
- Contribute to academic and public understanding of surveillance capabilities and limitations

---

## What Journalists Can Do

- Investigate and document surveillance technology deployments in your community
- File FOIA and public records requests for law enforcement surveillance contracts and policies
- Report on wrongful arrests and civil rights violations attributable to surveillance technology
- Examine local police department data purchase contracts using USASpending.gov and procurement records
- Cover local CCOPS campaigns and surveillance oversight debates
- Protect sources by using encrypted communications and secure drop systems (SecureDrop)

---

## What Elected Officials Can Do

### At the Federal Level

- Co-sponsor ECPA reform legislation
- Support the *Fourth Amendment Is Not For Sale Act*
- Hold oversight hearings on law enforcement data purchases and surveillance technology
- Request GAO audits of federal agency surveillance practices
- Attach appropriations riders restricting warrantless data purchases
- Increase funding for public defenders to challenge digital surveillance evidence

### At the State Level

- Introduce CalECPA-style electronic privacy legislation
- Enact state facial recognition moratoriums
- Require state agencies to disclose surveillance technology inventories
- Fund state-level surveillance technology oversight mechanisms

### At the Local Level

- Enact CCOPS ordinances requiring community oversight of surveillance technology
- Adopt ALPR data retention limits
- Establish police surveillance oversight boards with real authority
- Require disclosure of all law enforcement data sharing agreements

| Issue | Agency/Organization | How to Report |
|-------|-------------------|---------------|
| Federal agency surveillance abuse | DOJ Inspector General | oig.justice.gov/hotline |
| DHS/ICE/CBP surveillance concerns | DHS Inspector General | oig.dhs.gov/hotline |
| Police misconduct involving surveillance | Local civilian oversight board | Contact your city/county oversight body |
| Civil rights violation from surveillance | ACLU | aclu.org/report-violation |
| Digital rights violation | EFF | eff.org/pages/legal-assistance |
| Privacy violation by data broker | FTC | reportfraud.ftc.gov |
| State law violation | State Attorney General | Your state AG's consumer complaint portal |
| Constitutional violation in criminal case | NACDL / defense attorney | nacdl.org; contact a criminal defense lawyer |
