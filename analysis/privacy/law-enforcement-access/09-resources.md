# Law Enforcement Access to Data: Resources

## Key Federal Laws and Regulations

| Law/Regulation | Citation | Relevance |
|---------------|----------|-----------|
| Fourth Amendment to the U.S. Constitution | U.S. Const. amend. IV | Foundational protection against unreasonable searches and seizures |
| Wiretap Act (Title III) | 18 U.S.C. 2510-2522 | Governs real-time interception of communications content; requires super-warrant |
| Electronic Communications Privacy Act (ECPA) / Stored Communications Act | 18 U.S.C. 2701-2712 | Primary federal statute governing law enforcement access to stored electronic communications |
| Pen Register Act | 18 U.S.C. 3121-3127 | Governs real-time collection of communications metadata |
| USA PATRIOT Act | Pub. L. 107-56 (2001) | Expanded surveillance authorities for law enforcement and intelligence |
| CLOUD Act | Pub. L. 115-141, Division V (2018) | Governs cross-border law enforcement access to data stored overseas |
| National Security Letter statutes | 18 U.S.C. 2709 (FBI); 12 U.S.C. 3414 (financial); 15 U.S.C. 1681u-v (credit) | Administrative subpoenas for communications and financial metadata without judicial review |
| FISA Amendments Act, Section 702 | 50 U.S.C. 1881a | Authorizes programmatic surveillance of non-US persons; incidental collection of domestic communications |
| CalECPA (California Electronic Communications Privacy Act) | Cal. Penal Code 1546-1546.4 (2015) | State model requiring warrants for all electronic communications content and metadata |
| Illinois Biometric Information Privacy Act (BIPA) | 740 ILCS 14 (2008) | Strongest state biometric privacy law with private right of action |

## Key Court Cases

| Case | Citation | Significance |
|------|----------|-------------|
| *Katz v. United States* | 389 U.S. 347 (1967) | Established reasonable expectation of privacy test; required warrants for wiretapping |
| *United States v. Miller* | 425 U.S. 435 (1976) | Established third-party doctrine for bank records |
| *Smith v. Maryland* | 442 U.S. 735 (1979) | Extended third-party doctrine to phone number metadata (pen registers) |
| *United States v. Warshak* | 631 F.3d 266 (6th Cir. 2010) | Warrant required for email content regardless of storage duration (circuit-level) |
| *United States v. Jones* | 565 U.S. 400 (2012) | GPS tracking device is a Fourth Amendment search; five justices signaled concern about long-term location monitoring |
| *Riley v. California* | 573 U.S. 373 (2014) | Warrant required to search cell phone incident to arrest |
| *Carpenter v. United States* | 585 U.S. 296 (2018) | Warrant required for 7+ days of cell-site location information; narrowed third-party doctrine |
| *United States v. Chatrie* | No. 3:19-cr-130 (E.D. Va. 2022) | District court found geofence warrants raise serious Fourth Amendment concerns |
| *Olmstead v. United States* | 277 U.S. 438 (1928) | Held wiretapping was not a "search" (overruled by *Katz*); Brandeis dissent articulated right to privacy |
| *Brady v. Maryland* | 373 U.S. 83 (1963) | Prosecution must disclose exculpatory evidence; relevant to parallel construction challenges |

## Academic Research

### Fourth Amendment and Digital Privacy

- Kerr, Orin S. "The Fourth Amendment and New Technologies: Constitutional Myths and the Case for Caution." *Michigan Law Review* 102 (2004): 801-888. -- Foundational analysis of Fourth Amendment application to technology.
- Ohm, Paul. "The Fourth Amendment in a World Without Privacy." *Mississippi Law Journal* 81 (2012): 1309-1358. -- Analyzed the collapse of privacy-protective Fourth Amendment doctrines in the digital age.
- Gray, David, and Danielle Citron. "The Right to Quantitative Privacy." *Minnesota Law Review* 98 (2013): 62-144. -- Proposed a framework for limiting mass surveillance.
- Solove, Daniel J. "Digital Dossiers and the Dissipation of Fourth Amendment Privacy." *Southern California Law Review* 75 (2002): 1083-1168. -- Analyzed the inadequacy of the third-party doctrine for digital records.
- Henderson, Stephen E. "Beyond the (Current) Fourth Amendment: Protecting Third-Party Information, Third Parties, and the Rest of Us Too." *Pepperdine Law Review* 34 (2007): 975-1032. -- Argued for reform of the third-party doctrine.

### Surveillance Technology and Policing

- Ferguson, Andrew Guthrie. *The Rise of Big Data Policing: Surveillance, Race, and the Future of Law Enforcement.* New York: NYU Press, 2017. -- Comprehensive analysis of data-driven policing.
- Brayne, Sarah. *Predict and Surveil: Data, Discretion, and the Future of Policing.* New York: Oxford University Press, 2021. -- Ethnographic study of predictive policing in Los Angeles.
- Lum, Kristian, and William Isaac. "To Predict and Serve?" *Significance* 13.5 (2016): 14-19. -- Demonstrated racial bias in predictive policing algorithms.
- Richardson, Rashida, Jason Schultz, and Kate Crawford. "Dirty Data, Bad Predictions: How Civil Rights Violations Impact Police Data, Predictive Policing Systems, and Justice." *New York University Law Review Online* 94 (2019): 192-233. -- Showed how biased historical data perpetuates bias in policing algorithms.

### Facial Recognition and Bias

- Buolamwini, Joy, and Timnit Gebru. "Gender Shades: Intersectional Accuracy Disparities in Commercial Gender Classification." *Proceedings of Machine Learning Research* 81 (2018): 1-15. -- Foundational study of racial and gender bias in facial recognition.
- Garvie, Clare, Alvaro Bedoya, and Jonathan Frankle. "The Perpetual Line-Up: Unregulated Police Face Recognition in America." Georgetown Law Center on Privacy and Technology (2016). -- First comprehensive study of law enforcement facial recognition.

### Data Brokers and Government Purchases

- Slobogin, Christopher. "Panvasive Surveillance, Political Process Theory, and the Nondelegation Doctrine." *Georgetown Law Journal* 102 (2014): 1721-1776. -- Proposed constitutional limits on mass surveillance.
- Stanley, Jay. "The Dawn of Robot Surveillance." ACLU (2019). -- Analyzed the expanding capabilities of automated surveillance systems.
