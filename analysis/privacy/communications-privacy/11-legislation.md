# Communications Privacy: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Communications privacy legislation in the United States rests on a patchwork of outdated federal statutes, classified executive orders, and inconsistent state laws. The primary federal framework -- the Electronic Communications Privacy Act of 1986 -- predates the internet and has not been comprehensively updated despite nearly universal recognition of its inadequacy. This document provides draft federal legislation modernizing communications privacy, reforming surveillance authorities, protecting encryption, and establishing oversight mechanisms. The legislative approach prioritizes warrant protection for all communications, encryption as essential infrastructure, meaningful oversight of surveillance, and technology-neutral standards that will not become obsolete as communications evolve.

## Federal Legislation

### Communications Privacy Modernization Act

**Purpose**: Comprehensively update the Electronic Communications Privacy Act to require warrants for all communications content regardless of storage duration, strengthen metadata protections, reform National Security Letter authority, protect encryption, and establish technology-neutral standards for communications privacy.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Communications Privacy Modernization Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSE.

(a) FINDINGS.—Congress finds that:
(1) The Electronic Communications Privacy Act was enacted in
1986, before the development of the World Wide Web, smartphones,
cloud computing, social media, or encrypted messaging.
(2) Under current law, the government may access stored
communications content older than 180 days with a standard
below that of a probable cause warrant, a distinction that
reflected 1986 technology assumptions and is now indefensible.
(3) Communications metadata—including records of who
communicated with whom, when, where, and for how long—can
be as revealing as communications content but receives
substantially less legal protection.
(4) The Supreme Court recognized in Carpenter v. United
States (2018) that digital information can reveal the
"privacies of life" and requires warrant protection, yet
most communications metadata remains accessible without
a warrant.
(5) National Security Letters allow the FBI to compel
communications records without any judicial approval,
accompanied by indefinite gag orders.
(6) The legal framework governing communications privacy
should be technology-neutral so that protections extend to
new communications platforms without requiring further
legislative action.

(b) PURPOSE.—The purpose of this Act is to—
(1) require a probable cause warrant for government access to
all stored communications content regardless of age;
(2) strengthen legal protections for communications metadata;
(3) reform National Security Letter authority;
(4) prohibit government mandates to weaken or circumvent
encryption;
(5) regulate the use of cell-site simulators;
(6) establish technology-neutral communications privacy
standards; and
(7) strengthen oversight of communications surveillance.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:
(1) COMMUNICATIONS CONTENT.—The term "communications content"
means the substance, purport, or meaning of a communication,
including the text of an email, the audio of a voice call, the
content of a text message, and the content of any other form
of electronic communication.
(2) COMMUNICATIONS METADATA.—The term "communications metadata"
means information about a communication other than its content,
including the identity of the parties, the time and duration
of the communication, the location of the parties, routing
information, email headers, IP addresses, and any other
addressing or signaling information.
(3) ELECTRONIC COMMUNICATION SERVICE.—The term "electronic
communication service" means any service that provides to users
thereof the ability to send or receive wire or electronic
communications, including email, messaging, voice over internet
protocol, video conferencing, and social media messaging.
(4) CELL-SITE SIMULATOR.—The term "cell-site simulator" means
any device or software that simulates a cell tower or other
wireless access point to intercept communications, identify
wireless devices, or determine the location of wireless devices.
(5) ENCRYPTION.—The term "encryption" means the conversion of
data into a form that cannot be readily understood without
access to a decryption key, including end-to-end encryption
in which the service provider does not possess the ability to
decrypt communications.
(6) COVERED GOVERNMENT ENTITY.—The term "covered government
entity" means any department, agency, bureau, or office of the
Federal Government, or any State or local government.

SEC. 4. WARRANT REQUIREMENT FOR STORED COMMUNICATIONS CONTENT.

(a) AMENDMENT OF STORED COMMUNICATIONS ACT.—Section 2703 of
title 18, United States Code, is amended to provide that:
(1) A covered government entity may require the disclosure of
the contents of a wire or electronic communication that is in
electronic storage or held by an electronic communication
service or remote computing service only pursuant to a warrant
issued using the procedures described in the Federal Rules of
Criminal Procedure (or, in the case of a State court, issued
using State warrant procedures) by a court of competent
jurisdiction.
(2) The distinction in subsections (a) and (b) of Section
2703 between communications stored for 180 days or less and
those stored for more than 180 days is eliminated. A warrant
based on probable cause shall be required for all stored
communications content regardless of the duration of storage.

(b) EMERGENCY EXCEPTION.—A covered government entity may access
stored communications content without a warrant if—
(1) there is an emergency involving danger of death or
serious physical injury to any person that requires access
to the content without delay;
(2) the covered government entity applies for a warrant
within 48 hours of access; and
(3) any content obtained pursuant to this exception is
suppressed if a warrant is not obtained within 48 hours.

SEC. 5. ENHANCED PROTECTION FOR COMMUNICATIONS METADATA.

(a) WARRANT STANDARD FOR METADATA.—A covered government entity
may require the disclosure of communications metadata from an
electronic communication service only pursuant to—
(1) a warrant issued based on probable cause; or
(2) a court order issued under subsection (b).

(b) COURT ORDER STANDARD.—A court may issue an order requiring
disclosure of communications metadata only upon a showing by
the covered government entity that—
(1) there are specific and articulable facts showing that the
metadata sought is relevant and material to an ongoing criminal
investigation;
(2) the metadata cannot reasonably be obtained by other means
less intrusive to privacy; and
(3) the scope of the request is narrowly tailored to the
investigation.

(c) PEN REGISTER AND TRAP AND TRACE REFORM.—Chapter 206 of
title 18, United States Code, is amended to require that
applications for pen register and trap and trace orders meet
the standard set forth in subsection (b) of this section.

(d) METADATA MINIMIZATION.—Communications metadata obtained
pursuant to this section shall be—
(1) retained only for the duration necessary for the
investigation for which it was obtained;
(2) used only for the purpose for which it was obtained;
and
(3) destroyed within 90 days unless a court extends
retention for good cause shown.

SEC. 6. NATIONAL SECURITY LETTER REFORM.

(a) JUDICIAL APPROVAL.—A National Security Letter may be
issued under section 2709 of title 18, United States Code,
or any other statutory authority, only upon approval of the
Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court or, in exigent
circumstances, a federal magistrate judge.

(b) STANDARD.—The issuing court shall approve a National
Security Letter only upon a showing that—
(1) there are specific and articulable facts giving reason
to believe that the records sought are relevant to an
authorized investigation to protect against international
terrorism or clandestine intelligence activities; and
(2) the scope of the request is narrowly tailored.

(c) GAG ORDER LIMITATIONS.—
(1) Any nondisclosure requirement accompanying a National
Security Letter shall expire after one year unless renewed
by the issuing court.
(2) The recipient of a National Security Letter may
challenge the nondisclosure requirement before the issuing
court at any time.
(3) The government bears the burden of demonstrating that
continued nondisclosure is necessary.

(d) REPORTING.—The Director of National Intelligence shall
publish annually—
(1) the total number of National Security Letters issued;
(2) the number approved and denied by the issuing court;
(3) the number subject to nondisclosure requirements and
the average duration of such requirements; and
(4) the number challenged by recipients.

SEC. 7. ENCRYPTION PROTECTION.

(a) PROHIBITION ON BACKDOOR MANDATES.—No covered government
entity may—
(1) require, request by order, or otherwise compel any person
to design, build, maintain, or provide any technical
capability that would allow the decryption of end-to-end
encrypted communications by anyone other than the
communicating parties;
(2) require any electronic communication service to maintain
the ability to decrypt communications that it has designed
to be decryptable only by the communicating parties; or
(3) condition any license, contract, grant, or other
government benefit on the inclusion of a decryption
capability in any product or service.

(b) CALEA CLARIFICATION.—Section 103(b) of the Communications
Assistance for Law Enforcement Act (47 U.S.C. 1002(b)) is
amended to provide that—
(1) an information service, including any internet-based
communications service, is not a telecommunications carrier
for purposes of CALEA; and
(2) no information service shall be required to build,
maintain, or provide any wiretap capability in its products
or services.

(c) COMPELLED DECRYPTION LIMIT.—No covered government entity
may compel an electronic communication service to decrypt
communications that the service has designed so that the
service is unable to decrypt.

(d) ENCRYPTION PROMOTION.—The Director of the National
Institute of Standards and Technology shall—
(1) develop and promote encryption standards for
communications services;
(2) publish guidance on best practices for deployment of
end-to-end encryption; and
(3) fund research into privacy-preserving communications
technologies.

SEC. 8. CELL-SITE SIMULATOR REGULATION.

(a) WARRANT REQUIREMENT.—A covered government entity may not
use a cell-site simulator except—
(1) pursuant to a warrant issued based on probable cause by
a court of competent jurisdiction; or
(2) in an emergency involving danger of death or serious
physical injury, provided that a warrant is obtained within
48 hours.

(b) MINIMIZATION.—
(1) Data collected from devices other than the target device
shall be immediately deleted.
(2) Data collected from the target device shall be retained
only as authorized by the warrant.

(c) NON-DISCLOSURE AGREEMENTS.—
(1) No covered government entity may enter into any
agreement with a cell-site simulator manufacturer or other
party that prevents disclosure of the use of a cell-site
simulator in any judicial proceeding.
(2) Any existing agreement purporting to prevent such
disclosure is void and unenforceable.

(d) ENCRYPTION INTEGRITY.—A cell-site simulator shall not be
used to downgrade the encryption of any device to which it
connects.

(e) REPORTING.—Each covered government entity that uses a
cell-site simulator shall publish an annual report including—
(1) the number of times cell-site simulators were deployed;
(2) the number of warrants obtained;
(3) the number of emergency deployments; and
(4) the types of investigations in which they were used.

SEC. 9. CORPORATE COMMUNICATIONS SURVEILLANCE LIMITS.

(a) CONTENT SCANNING.—An electronic communication service
shall not scan or analyze the content of private communications
for advertising or marketing purposes.

(b) METADATA MONETIZATION.—An electronic communication service
shall not use communications metadata for advertising or
marketing purposes, or transfer such metadata to third parties
for such purposes, without the affirmative express consent of
the user.

(c) EMPLOYER MONITORING NOTICE.—
(1) An employer that monitors employee electronic
communications shall provide clear written notice to
employees prior to commencing monitoring.
(2) The notice shall specify the types of communications
monitored, the technology used, and the purposes of
monitoring.

(d) RETENTION LIMITS.—An electronic communication service
shall not retain communications metadata for longer than 18
months after the communication occurred, unless—
(1) retention is required by valid legal process; or
(2) the user has affirmatively requested longer retention
of their own metadata.

SEC. 10. OVERSIGHT AND TRANSPARENCY.

(a) PCLOB EXPANSION.—The Privacy and Civil Liberties Oversight
Board shall—
(1) be authorized as a permanent independent agency;
(2) have authority to issue subpoenas for documents and
testimony;
(3) conduct annual reviews of all communications surveillance
programs operated by the Federal Government;
(4) publish annual reports on compliance with this Act; and
(5) receive an annual appropriation of not less than
$50,000,000.

(b) TRANSPARENCY REPORTS.—
(1) Each electronic communication service with more than
100,000 users shall publish a semiannual transparency report
detailing government requests for communications content and
metadata.
(2) Transparency reports shall include the number of
requests received, the number complied with, and the legal
authority cited.
(3) Government-imposed restrictions on the specificity of
transparency reports shall expire after two years unless
renewed by a court.

(c) FISA COURT TRANSPARENCY.—
(1) All FISA Court opinions shall be reviewed for
declassification within 180 days of issuance.
(2) FISA Court opinions interpreting the legal authority
for surveillance programs shall be declassified to the
maximum extent consistent with national security, with
a presumption of declassification.

SEC. 11. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) SUPPRESSION.—Evidence obtained in violation of this Act
shall be inadmissible in any trial, hearing, or other
proceeding in or before any court, grand jury, department,
officer, agency, regulatory body, legislative committee,
or other authority of the United States, a State, or a
political subdivision thereof.

(b) CIVIL LIABILITY.—Any person whose communications privacy
is violated in violation of this Act may bring a civil action
in Federal court for—
(1) actual damages or statutory damages of $10,000 per
violation, whichever is greater;
(2) punitive damages in cases of willful violation;
(3) reasonable attorney's fees and costs; and
(4) such equitable relief as the court determines appropriate.

(c) GOVERNMENT LIABILITY.—The United States and its agencies
shall be liable for violations of this Act to the same extent
as a private person, and sovereign immunity is waived for
civil actions under this section.

(d) DISCIPLINARY ACTION.—A covered government entity employee
who willfully violates this Act shall be subject to
disciplinary action up to and including termination.

SEC. 12. PREEMPTION.

(a) This Act establishes minimum protections and shall not be
construed to preempt, limit, or otherwise affect any State law
that provides greater protection to the privacy of
communications.

(b) Nothing in this Act shall be construed to preempt
California's CalECPA or any State law providing protections
equal to or greater than those provided by this Act.

SEC. 13. EFFECTIVE DATE.

(a) This Act shall take effect 12 months after the date of
enactment.
(b) The Department of Justice shall promulgate implementing
regulations within 9 months of enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- Section 4 eliminates the indefensible 180-day rule and requires warrants for all stored communications content
- Section 5 strengthens metadata protection, recognizing that metadata can be as revealing as content
- Section 6 requires judicial approval for National Security Letters and limits gag orders
- Section 7 prohibits encryption backdoor mandates and clarifies that CALEA does not apply to internet services
- Section 8 establishes warrant requirements and transparency for cell-site simulators
- Section 9 limits corporate surveillance of private communications
- Section 10 strengthens oversight through PCLOB expansion and transparency requirements
- Section 11 provides enforcement through suppression, civil liability, and government accountability
- Section 12 preserves stronger state laws

**Potential Challenges**:

- National security establishment will oppose Sections 6 and 7 as threatening intelligence capabilities
- Law enforcement will oppose warrant requirements for metadata and cell-site simulators
- Technology companies may resist content scanning prohibition if it conflicts with content moderation practices
- First Amendment arguments may be raised against corporate communications surveillance limits

**Refinements**:

- Phased implementation for metadata warrant requirement (federal agencies first, then state and local)
- Safe harbor for communications services that adopt privacy-by-design principles
- Technical advisory board to update definitions as technology evolves
- Annual report to Congress on implementation and impact

### Section 702 Reform Act

**Purpose**: Reform Section 702 of the FISA Amendments Act to require warrants for queries of Americans' communications, strengthen minimization procedures, reform the expanded definition of electronic communications service provider, and enhance FISA Court oversight.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Protecting Americans'
Communications Act".

SEC. 2. WARRANT REQUIREMENT FOR US PERSON QUERIES.

(a) Section 702(f) of the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance
Act, as amended, is further amended to provide that:
(1) A query of communications collected under this section
using a United States person identifier may be conducted only
pursuant to an order of the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance
Court based on a finding of probable cause to believe that the
communications sought are relevant to a criminal investigation
or relate to a foreign intelligence threat.
(2) EXCEPTION.—The warrant requirement of paragraph (1) shall
not apply if—
    (A) the query is reasonably designed to find and extract
    foreign intelligence information and does not seek evidence
    of a crime; or
    (B) there is a reasonable belief that the life or safety
    of a person is threatened and the information is necessary
    to respond to such threat, provided that a warrant is sought
    within 72 hours.

SEC. 3. MINIMIZATION REFORM.

(a) Incidentally collected communications of United States
persons shall be—
(1) segregated from other collected communications;
(2) destroyed within 90 days of collection unless a warrant
is obtained under Section 2; and
(3) not used as evidence in any criminal prosecution unless
a warrant has been obtained.

(b) The Attorney General shall submit to the FISA Court
annually a report on the volume of incidentally collected
United States person communications and the disposition of
such communications.

SEC. 4. ELECTRONIC COMMUNICATIONS SERVICE PROVIDER
DEFINITION.

(a) The expanded definition of "electronic communications
service provider" enacted in the Reforming Intelligence and
Securing America Act (2024) is narrowed to exclude—
(1) entities whose primary business is not the provision
of electronic communications services;
(2) hotels, restaurants, libraries, and other entities
that provide incidental internet access; and
(3) any entity that would be required to provide novel
surveillance assistance beyond existing technical
capabilities.

SEC. 5. FISA COURT REFORM.

(a) AMICI CURIAE.—The FISA Court shall appoint amici curiae
in—
(1) any case presenting a novel or significant interpretation
of law;
(2) any case involving a certification or directive under
Section 702; and
(3) any case in which the court determines that appointment
would assist in the consideration of the matter.

(b) The amicus shall have access to all materials submitted
to the court and may submit briefing, question witnesses,
and appeal decisions to the FISA Court of Review.

(c) ANNUAL REPORTING.—The FISA Court shall publish annually—
(1) the number of applications received and approved;
(2) the number of amici appointed;
(3) a summary of significant legal interpretations; and
(4) the number of compliance violations reported.

SEC. 6. PROHIBITION ON BACKDOOR SEARCHES FOR LAW ENFORCEMENT.

(a) Communications collected under Section 702 may not be
introduced as evidence in any criminal prosecution of a
United States person unless—
(1) a warrant has been obtained under Section 2 of this Act;
and
(2) the communications were not the initial basis for the
criminal investigation.

(b) An agent or employee of the Federal Government may not
conduct a query of Section 702 data using a United States
person identifier for the purpose of investigating a crime
unrelated to foreign intelligence.

SEC. 7. SUNSET.

The authorities granted under Section 702 of the Foreign
Intelligence Surveillance Act, as reformed by this Act, shall
expire 4 years after the date of enactment, unless
reauthorized by Act of Congress.
```

**Explanation**:

- Section 2 requires warrants for US person queries while preserving foreign intelligence collection
- Section 3 strengthens minimization to ensure incidentally collected American communications are not retained without judicial oversight
- Section 4 rolls back the sweeping 2024 expansion of which entities can be compelled to assist with surveillance
- Section 5 requires adversarial process in FISA Court proceedings
- Section 6 prevents "backdoor searches" using intelligence data for criminal investigations
- Section 7 establishes a 4-year sunset to ensure regular congressional review

**Potential Challenges**:

- Intelligence community and administration opposition to warrant requirement
- Claims that warrant requirement will create intelligence gaps (contested)
- Definition of "foreign intelligence" exception in Section 2(2)(A) may be interpreted broadly
- FISA Court capacity to process warrant applications

**Refinements**:

- Emergency procedures allowing immediate queries with 72-hour warrant application
- Training requirements for FBI agents conducting 702 queries
- Compliance auditing by PCLOB with public reporting
- Technical assistance to FISA Court for evaluating surveillance applications

## State Model Legislation

### Model State Electronic Communications Privacy Act

**Purpose**: Provide comprehensive communications privacy protections at the state level, requiring warrants for all government access to electronic communications content and metadata, modeled on California's CalECPA.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Electronic Communications
Privacy Act".

SECTION 2. WARRANT REQUIRED.

(a) A government entity shall not do any of the following
without a warrant:
(1) Compel the production of or access to electronic
communication information from a service provider.
(2) Compel the production of or access to electronic
device information from any person or entity other than
the authorized possessor of the device.
(3) Access electronic device information by means of
physical interaction or electronic communication with
the device.
(4) Use a cell-site simulator.

(b) "Electronic communication information" means the content
of an electronic communication, as well as any metadata
associated with such communication, including the identity of
the parties, time, duration, location, and routing information.

SECTION 3. EXCEPTIONS.

(a) A government entity may access electronic communication
information without a warrant if—
(1) the owner or user of the information consents;
(2) an emergency involving danger of death or serious
physical injury exists, provided a warrant is sought
within 48 hours; or
(3) the device has been reported as lost or stolen and
the entity seeks only to identify, verify, or locate
the device.

SECTION 4. CELL-SITE SIMULATOR REGULATION.

(a) A government entity shall not use a cell-site simulator
without a warrant based on probable cause.
(b) Data collected from non-target devices shall be deleted
within 24 hours.
(c) The government entity shall disclose the use of a
cell-site simulator in any judicial proceeding.

SECTION 5. NOTIFICATION.

(a) A government entity that obtains electronic communication
information pursuant to a warrant shall notify the target of
the warrant within 90 days, unless a court grants a delay for
good cause.
(b) Delayed notification shall not exceed 180 days total.

SECTION 6. SUPPRESSION.

Evidence obtained in violation of this Act shall be
inadmissible in any state court proceeding.

SECTION 7. CIVIL ACTION.

Any person whose electronic communications privacy is
violated in violation of this Act may bring a civil action
for actual damages or statutory damages of $5,000 per
violation, whichever is greater, plus reasonable attorney's
fees.

SECTION 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act shall take effect [6 months] after enactment.
```

## Regulatory Framework

### DOJ Communications Privacy Compliance Guidelines

**Authority**: Attorney General authority under 28 U.S.C. SS 509-510

**Draft Framework**:

```text
COMMUNICATIONS PRIVACY COMPLIANCE GUIDELINES

1. WARRANT REQUIREMENT
(a) All Department components shall obtain a warrant based
on probable cause before compelling disclosure of stored
communications content, regardless of the age of such content.
(b) This requirement applies to requests made under 18 U.S.C.
Section 2703 and all other legal authorities.

2. METADATA STANDARDS
(a) Requests for communications metadata shall be supported
by specific and articulable facts demonstrating relevance and
materiality to an ongoing investigation.
(b) Metadata requests shall be narrowly tailored in scope
and duration.

3. CELL-SITE SIMULATOR USE
(a) All Department components shall obtain a warrant before
using a cell-site simulator.
(b) This requirement extends to all federal law enforcement
agencies, not only DOJ components.

4. ENCRYPTION POLICY
(a) Department components shall not seek court orders
compelling providers to decrypt end-to-end encrypted
communications that the provider is unable to decrypt.
(b) Department components shall develop and employ
alternative investigative techniques when encryption
prevents access to communications content.

5. NATIONAL SECURITY LETTER USE
(a) NSLs shall be approved by a senior Department official
(Deputy Assistant Attorney General or above).
(b) NSL requests shall be documented and subject to
quarterly review by the DOJ Inspector General.

6. TRANSPARENCY
(a) The Department shall publish semiannual reports on
the volume and type of communications data requests made
to electronic communication services.
```

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Authority

- **Fourth Amendment**: Warrant requirements for communications content and metadata rest squarely within Fourth Amendment protections, as recognized in *Katz v. United States* (1967), *Carpenter v. United States* (2018), and *Riley v. California* (2014)
- **Commerce Clause**: Federal authority to regulate electronic communication services operating in interstate commerce
- **First Amendment**: Communications privacy protections serve First Amendment values by protecting freedom of speech and association; corporate communications surveillance limits must be narrowly tailored to survive commercial speech scrutiny
- **Necessary and Proper Clause**: Authority to implement Fourth Amendment protections through statutory mechanism

### Preemption Analysis

- **Federal floor approach**: Preserves stronger state laws (CalECPA, state constitutional provisions); avoids weakening existing protections
- **Consistency with precedent**: USA FREEDOM Act (2015) established the principle of federal reform without state preemption
- **State innovation value**: States serve as laboratories for communications privacy reform; CalECPA (2015) demonstrated feasibility of warrant requirements before federal adoption

### Enforcement Mechanisms

- **Suppression remedy**: Most powerful enforcement tool; excludes evidence obtained in violation of the Act
- **Civil liability**: Provides individual enforcement mechanism; statutory damages ensure meaningful recovery
- **Government accountability**: Waiver of sovereign immunity ensures government violations have consequences
- **Disciplinary action**: Internal enforcement mechanism for government employees

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Emergency exception abuse | Government entities may invoke emergency exception to bypass warrant requirement, then fail to obtain warrant within 48 hours | High |
| Third-party purchase workaround | Government purchases communications data from data brokers instead of compelling disclosure from providers | High |
| Foreign intelligence exception breadth | Section 702 reform's foreign intelligence exception may be interpreted broadly to avoid warrant requirement | High |
| Parallel construction | Law enforcement uses intelligence-derived communications to develop independent evidence chain, concealing surveillance source | High |
| Metadata aggregation gap | Individual metadata requests may each be narrow, but aggregated metadata creates comprehensive surveillance | Moderate |
| Corporate scanning for "security" purposes | Content scanning prohibition exempts security scanning; companies may characterize advertising-related scanning as security | Moderate |
| Classified legal interpretations | FISA Court may issue classified interpretations that effectively expand surveillance authority beyond statutory text | Moderate |
| Cross-border communications gap | Communications routed through foreign infrastructure may be collected under Executive Order 12333 without statutory constraints | High |

### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Executive Order 12333 not addressed | Significant foreign intelligence collection occurs under EO 12333 with minimal statutory constraint | EO 12333 is executive authority, not statutory; legislative override is constitutionally complex |
| Retroactivity gap | Data already collected under current authorities is not required to be destroyed | Legal constraints on retroactive application |
| State and local compliance | Federal warrant requirements may not reach all state and local agencies effectively | Federalism constraints; enforcement depends on suppression remedy |
| Technical evolution | Statutory definitions may not capture future communications technologies | Inherent limitation of statutory drafting |
| Classified program oversight | Oversight reforms depend on willingness of oversight bodies to exercise authority | Institutional culture and political dynamics |
| Private communications platforms | Platforms designed for criminal use may be treated differently than general-purpose platforms | Difficult to distinguish by statute |

### Rectification Procedures

1. **Emergency exception safeguards**: Require suppression of any evidence obtained under emergency exception if warrant is not obtained within 48 hours; require annual reporting on emergency exception use; authorize PCLOB audits of emergency exception invocations.

2. **Data purchase prohibition**: Explicitly prohibit government entities from purchasing communications data, metadata, or location data from data brokers or other commercial sources as a means of circumventing warrant requirements. Model on the Fourth Amendment Is Not For Sale Act.

3. **Foreign intelligence exception narrowing**: Define "foreign intelligence information" narrowly to exclude ordinary criminal matters; require FISA Court review of all queries relying on the foreign intelligence exception; annual reporting on exception use.

4. **Parallel construction prohibition**: Require disclosure of the initial investigative basis for any communications-related evidence introduced in criminal proceedings; prohibit concealment of surveillance sources through parallel construction.

5. **Metadata aggregation controls**: Require separate judicial authorization for metadata requests that, in combination with prior requests about the same target, would produce a comprehensive surveillance profile; mandatory minimization of aggregated metadata.

6. **Security scanning transparency**: Require annual reporting on the volume and nature of content scanning conducted for "security" purposes; FTC authority to investigate whether security scanning is used as pretext for advertising-related analysis.

7. **Declassification mandate**: Require that all FISA Court opinions interpreting legal authority be declassified within 1 year of issuance, with only specific operational details redacted; create presumption of declassification.

8. **EO 12333 statutory framework**: Enact statutory framework governing foreign intelligence collection under Executive Order 12333, applying minimization and oversight requirements comparable to those in FISA.

### General Implementation Concerns

- Intelligence community resistance to transparency and oversight reforms may delay or weaken implementation
- FISA Court's institutional culture of deference to government may limit the effectiveness of amici curiae and transparency reforms
- Suppression remedy depends on defendants knowing their communications were obtained through surveillance, which classification may prevent
- Technology companies face competing pressures from government demands for access and user demands for privacy
- International coordination is necessary because communications cross borders, but international agreements are slow to negotiate
