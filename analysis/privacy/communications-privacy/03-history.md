# Communications Privacy: History

## Overview

The history of communications privacy in America reveals a recurring pattern: new communications technologies are developed, government surveillance of those technologies expands, abuses are exposed, legal protections are enacted, and then new technologies and new surveillance methods once again outpace the law. Understanding this cycle is essential for designing reforms that are durable rather than merely reactive.

## Timeline

### Founding Era and Postal Privacy (1789-1876)

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 1789 | Fourth Amendment ratified | Protects "persons, houses, papers, and effects" against unreasonable search; foundational privacy provision |
| 1792 | Post Office Act | Established federal postal system; postal employees prohibited from opening mail |
| 1825 | Postal reform legislation | Criminal penalties for unauthorized opening of sealed mail |
| 1844 | Telegraph invented (Morse) | First electronic communication; no legal framework for privacy |
| 1860s | Civil War telegraph censorship | Government monitored telegraph communications during wartime; no legal challenge |

### Telephone Era (1876-1967)

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 1876 | Telephone patented (Bell) | New communications technology; no privacy protections |
| 1890 | Warren and Brandeis, "The Right to Privacy" | Seminal law review article arguing for legal right to privacy |
| 1918 | Wartime espionage wiretapping | Government wiretapping during WWI; no judicial oversight |
| 1928 | *Olmstead v. United States* | Supreme Court holds wiretapping is not a "search" under Fourth Amendment because no physical trespass; Brandeis dissent argues Constitution must adapt to technology |
| 1934 | Federal Communications Act ss 605 | Prohibited interception of wire communications; first statutory communications privacy protection |
| 1937 | *Nardone v. United States* | Supreme Court applies ss 605 to exclude wiretap evidence in federal court |
| 1940s-1960s | FBI COINTELPRO | FBI wiretapped civil rights leaders, political organizations, journalists without warrants |
| 1967 | *Katz v. United States* | Supreme Court overturns *Olmstead*; Fourth Amendment protects "people, not places"; establishes reasonable expectation of privacy test |
| 1967 | *Berger v. New York* | Court strikes down New York wiretap law; establishes requirements for constitutionally valid wiretap orders |

### First Statutory Framework (1968-1985)

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 1968 | Omnibus Crime Control and Safe Streets Act (Title III) | First comprehensive federal wiretap law; requires judicial order based on probable cause for interception |
| 1971 | *United States v. US District Court* (Keith case) | Supreme Court rejects warrantless domestic security wiretapping; acknowledges possible different standard for foreign intelligence |
| 1972 | Watergate scandal | Nixon administration used wiretapping against political opponents; catalyzed reform |
| 1975-1976 | Church Committee investigations | Senate committee exposed FBI, CIA, NSA surveillance abuses spanning decades |
| 1978 | Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act (FISA) | Created FISA Court for intelligence surveillance; warrant-like process for domestic targets |
| 1979 | *Smith v. Maryland* | Supreme Court establishes third-party doctrine: phone numbers dialed have no Fourth Amendment protection because voluntarily conveyed to phone company |

### Digital Communications Era (1986-2000)

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 1986 | Electronic Communications Privacy Act (ECPA) | Extended wiretap protections to electronic communications; created Stored Communications Act and Pen Register Act |
| 1991 | Phil Zimmermann releases PGP | First widely available email encryption; government attempted prosecution under export controls |
| 1993 | Clipper Chip proposal | Clinton administration proposes key escrow system for all encryption; industry and public opposition leads to abandonment |
| 1994 | Communications Assistance for Law Enforcement Act (CALEA) | Required telecom carriers to build wiretap capabilities into networks |
| 1995-2000 | First Crypto War | Government relaxes encryption export controls after industry lobbying and legal challenges; encryption becomes widely available |
| 1996 | Telecommunications Act | Major telecom deregulation; did not update ECPA privacy provisions |
| 1999 | EU Data Protection Directive | European framework for communications privacy; highlighted US gaps |

### Post-9/11 Surveillance Expansion (2001-2012)

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 2001 | USA PATRIOT Act enacted | Dramatically expanded surveillance authority; roving wiretaps; business records; "sneak and peek" searches; lowered FISA standards |
| 2001-2007 | NSA warrantless wiretapping (Stellar Wind) | Bush administration authorized warrantless interception of international communications without FISA orders |
| 2004 | *Stellar Wind* DOJ internal crisis | Acting AG Comey and FBI Director Mueller threatened resignation over legal concerns with warrantless surveillance |
| 2005 | *New York Times* reveals NSA warrantless wiretapping | Public exposure of Stellar Wind program; political firestorm |
| 2006 | *USA Today* reveals NSA bulk phone metadata collection | Disclosed government collection of calling records of millions of Americans |
| 2007 | Protect America Act | Temporary legislation authorizing warrantless surveillance of foreign targets; precursor to FISA Amendments Act |
| 2008 | FISA Amendments Act (Section 702) | Codified warrantless surveillance of foreign targets; retroactive telecom immunity; PRISM and upstream programs |
| 2010 | *United States v. Warshak* (6th Cir.) | Sixth Circuit holds that email content is protected by Fourth Amendment regardless of storage duration; ECPA 180-day rule unconstitutional as applied |
| 2011 | PATRIOT Act reauthorization | Extended key surveillance provisions with minimal reform debate |

### Snowden Era and Reform Attempts (2013-2019)

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 2013 | Edward Snowden disclosures begin | Revealed scope of NSA surveillance programs: PRISM, upstream collection, bulk metadata, XKeyscore, and more |
| 2013 | PRISM program disclosed | NSA collecting communications from Google, Facebook, Apple, Microsoft, Yahoo, and others under Section 702 |
| 2013 | Upstream collection revealed | NSA scanning internet backbone traffic for selectors |
| 2013 | *Klayman v. Obama* | Federal judge calls NSA metadata collection "almost Orwellian"; preliminary injunction (later reversed) |
| 2014 | PCLOB Section 215 report | Privacy and Civil Liberties Oversight Board finds bulk metadata program illegal |
| 2014 | USA FREEDOM Act introduced | Reform bill to end bulk collection and add transparency |
| 2015 | USA FREEDOM Act enacted | Ended bulk telephony metadata collection; reformed FISA Court (amicus); transparency provisions |
| 2015 | *Apple v. FBI* (San Bernardino) begins | FBI demands Apple create tool to bypass iPhone encryption; Apple refuses |
| 2016 | FBI drops Apple case | FBI finds alternative access method; no legal precedent set |
| 2016 | FISA Court opinions declassified | Revealed significant compliance violations in Section 702 programs |
| 2018 | *Carpenter v. United States* | Supreme Court holds cell-site location information requires warrant; narrows third-party doctrine but does not overrule *Smith v. Maryland* |
| 2018 | CLOUD Act enacted | US law enforcement can compel US companies to produce data stored abroad |
| 2018 | FISA Amendments Reauthorization Act | Section 702 reauthorized for 6 years; minor reforms |

### Current Era (2020-Present)

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 2020 | EARN IT Act introduced | Threatens encryption by linking Section 230 immunity to content scanning; reintroduced multiple sessions |
| 2020 | FBI revealed querying 702 database for BLM protestors | Demonstrated abuse of incidental collection for domestic purposes |
| 2021 | Pegasus spyware revelations | NSO Group spyware used to hack journalists, activists, and officials worldwide; raised awareness of communications vulnerability |
| 2022 | *Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health* | Overturning *Roe v. Wade* intensified concerns about communications surveillance for reproductive health |
| 2023 | FBI revealed massive overcounting of 702 US person queries | FBI acknowledged 204,090 figure was substantially inflated; actual number still in tens of thousands |
| 2023 | Meta begins default E2EE rollout for Messenger | Major platform shift toward encrypted communications by default |
| 2024 | Section 702 reauthorized without warrant requirement | Two-year extension passed after contentious debate; warrant amendment narrowly defeated |
| 2024 | Section 702 reauthorization expanded definition of "electronic communications service provider" | Potentially sweeping new authority to compel surveillance assistance |
| 2025 | State communications privacy bills proliferate | Multiple states considering enhanced electronic privacy protections |

## Recurring Patterns

### The Surveillance Cycle

Every major communications technology has followed the same pattern:

1. **Technology deployed** with no specific privacy framework
2. **Government surveillance expands** to exploit new technology
3. **Abuses occur** and are eventually exposed (often decades later)
4. **Public outcry** drives political pressure for reform
5. **Legal protections enacted** -- often years after abuses began
6. **New technology emerges**, and the cycle repeats

| Technology | Unregulated Surveillance Period | Abuse Exposed | Reform Enacted | Time Lag |
|-----------|-------------------------------|---------------|----------------|----------|
| Telegraph | 1844-1934 | Various; wartime censorship | FCA ss 605 (1934) | ~90 years |
| Telephone | 1876-1967 | FBI COINTELPRO; warrantless wiretapping | *Katz* (1967); Title III (1968) | ~90 years |
| Email | 1971-1986 | Limited at time of legislation | ECPA (1986) | ~15 years |
| Internet | 1990s-present | NSA mass surveillance (Snowden, 2013) | USA FREEDOM Act (2015) -- partial | ~20 years |
| Encrypted messaging | 2010s-present | Government backdoor pressure | No protection enacted | Ongoing |

### The Pendulum

Communications privacy policy swings between expansion and contraction of government power:

- **Post-Church Committee (1978)**: FISA creates judicial oversight of intelligence surveillance
- **Post-9/11 (2001)**: PATRIOT Act and warrantless wiretapping dramatically expand surveillance
- **Post-Snowden (2015)**: USA FREEDOM Act modestly constrains bulk collection
- **Post-reauthorization (2024)**: Section 702 continues with expanded authority
