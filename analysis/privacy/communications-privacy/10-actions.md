# Communications Privacy: Actions

## Overview

While structural reform of communications privacy law requires legislative and institutional change, individuals can take meaningful steps to protect their own communications, advocate for reform, and support organizations working to advance communications privacy. This guide provides concrete actions organized by effort level and impact.

## Individual Communications Protection Actions

### Immediate Steps (Low Effort)

- **Switch to an encrypted messaging app**: Use Signal for sensitive communications. Signal uses end-to-end encryption by default, retains minimal metadata, and is free and open-source. WhatsApp also provides E2EE but collects metadata
- **Enable encryption on email**: Use ProtonMail or Tutanota for encrypted email. If using Gmail or Outlook, install the Mailvelope browser extension for PGP encryption of individual messages
- **Disable SMS for sensitive conversations**: Standard SMS and MMS are unencrypted and fully accessible to carriers and law enforcement. Use encrypted messaging instead
- **Review messaging app settings**: Ensure that disappearing messages are enabled in Signal for sensitive conversations; enable E2EE in apps that offer it as an option (such as Telegram Secret Chats)
- **Use a VPN**: A virtual private network encrypts internet traffic and prevents ISPs from monitoring communications destinations. Choose a VPN provider with a no-logs policy
- **Enable two-factor authentication on all communications accounts**: Prevents unauthorized access to email, messaging, and social media accounts. Use an authenticator app rather than SMS for 2FA

### Moderate Steps (Some Effort)

- **Audit your communications footprint**: Identify all platforms through which you communicate (email, messaging, social media, voice). Assess which are encrypted and which expose content or metadata
- **Minimize metadata exposure**: Use Signal's sealed sender feature; use a VPN to prevent IP address tracking; disable location services for communications apps when not needed
- **Review employer monitoring policies**: Understand whether your employer monitors email, messaging, or other communications. Use personal devices and accounts for personal communications
- **Use encrypted voice and video calls**: Signal and FaceTime offer end-to-end encrypted voice and video calls. Avoid standard phone calls for sensitive conversations
- **Secure your devices**: Enable full-disk encryption on computers (FileVault for Mac, BitLocker for Windows); set strong device passcodes; enable remote wipe capability
- **Use Tor for anonymous browsing**: The Tor Browser routes internet traffic through multiple relays, preventing surveillance of browsing activity. Use for sensitive research or communications

### Advanced Steps (Higher Effort)

- **Host your own email server**: Self-hosting email eliminates third-party access to communications content and metadata, though it requires technical expertise
- **Use SecureDrop for sensitive communications with journalists**: If you need to communicate sensitive information to journalists, use SecureDrop (securedrop.org) or Signal
- **Run a privacy-focused mobile operating system**: GrapheneOS or CalyxOS reduce data collection by mobile operating systems
- **Use hardware security keys**: YubiKey or similar hardware keys provide the strongest authentication protection for communications accounts
- **Contribute to open-source privacy tools**: Support Signal, Tor, and other privacy-preserving communications tools through code contributions, bug reports, or financial support

## Advocacy Actions

### Contact Elected Officials

**Federal**: Urge your U.S. senators and representative to support:

- ECPA modernization eliminating the 180-day rule and requiring warrants for all communications content and metadata
- Section 702 reform requiring warrants for queries of Americans' communications
- Encryption protection legislation prohibiting government-mandated backdoors
- Cell-site simulator warrant requirements
- Strengthened oversight of surveillance programs (PCLOB expansion, FISA Court reform)

**State**: If your state does not have a CalECPA-equivalent law requiring warrants for electronic communications, contact your state legislators to support enactment.

### Template Email to Federal Legislators (Section 702)

> Subject: Support Section 702 Reform -- Require Warrants for Americans' Communications
>
> Dear [Senator/Representative] [Last Name],
>
> I am writing as your constituent to urge you to support meaningful reform of Section 702 of the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act before its next reauthorization.
>
> Under current law, the government collects vast quantities of Americans' private communications through Section 702 surveillance of foreign targets. The FBI then queries this database for Americans' communications tens of thousands of times per year -- without a warrant. This is a backdoor around the Fourth Amendment's warrant requirement.
>
> The warrant amendment for Section 702 US person queries nearly passed the House in 2024, falling just one vote short in a 212-212 tie. The American people deserve this protection.
>
> I urge you to support Section 702 reform that includes:
>
> - A probable cause warrant requirement before the FBI queries the 702 database for Americans' communications
> - Stronger minimization procedures requiring destruction of incidentally collected US person data unless a warrant is obtained
> - Meaningful oversight including mandatory amici curiae in FISA Court proceedings
> - Transparency reporting on the scope of incidental collection
>
> Requiring warrants for queries of Americans' communications does not end foreign intelligence collection. It simply applies the same judicial oversight that the Fourth Amendment requires for domestic searches.
>
> [Personal story about communications privacy concern if applicable]
>
> Thank you for your attention to this critical civil liberties issue.
>
> Sincerely,
> [Name]
> [Address]

### Template Email to Federal Legislators (ECPA Reform)

> Subject: Modernize ECPA -- Require Warrants for Email and Digital Communications
>
> Dear [Senator/Representative] [Last Name],
>
> I am writing to urge you to support modernization of the Electronic Communications Privacy Act (ECPA), which was enacted in 1986 -- before the World Wide Web existed.
>
> Under ECPA's outdated text, the government can access stored emails older than 180 days with a mere subpoena rather than a warrant. This distinction made limited sense in 1986 but is indefensible in an era of cloud storage and permanent email archives. The Department of Justice already requires its attorneys to obtain warrants for stored email content, but the statute has not been updated.
>
> I urge you to support legislation that:
>
> - Eliminates the 180-day rule and requires warrants for all stored communications content
> - Strengthens protections for communications metadata (who communicated with whom, when, and where)
> - Reforms National Security Letter authority to require judicial approval
> - Applies protections equally regardless of communications technology
>
> Every other form of private content -- physical mail, in-home conversations, personal papers -- requires a warrant. Digital communications deserve the same protection.
>
> Sincerely,
> [Name]
> [Address]

### Template Phone Script

## Talking Points

### For Conversations with Friends and Family

- "The main federal law protecting your email privacy was written in 1986 -- before the internet existed. Under the text of that law, the government can access your old emails with less protection than a physical letter in your mailbox."
- "The FBI searched a database of Americans' private communications tens of thousands of times in a single year -- without a warrant. This is the equivalent of the government reading your mail without going to a judge."
- "Security experts unanimously agree that encryption backdoors would make everyone less safe. There is no way to create a door that only the 'good guys' can use."
- "Your phone's metadata -- who you call, when, for how long, and from where -- can reveal as much about you as the content of your calls. A former NSA director said, 'We kill people based on metadata.'"
- "Using encrypted messaging like Signal is not about having something to hide. It is about having the right to a private conversation, just as you have the right to close your front door."

### For Conversations with Policymakers

- "The Section 702 warrant amendment was one vote short of passing in 2024 (212-212). This is a bipartisan reform with overwhelming public support."
- "Even the DOJ requires its attorneys to get warrants for stored email content. The statute should catch up to the DOJ's own policy."
- "Encryption protects Americans' financial transactions, critical infrastructure, and government communications. Weakening it to pursue a small number of criminal cases would compromise the security of the entire nation."
- "CalECPA has been in effect in California since 2015. Law enforcement has not been unable to investigate crimes. Warrants work."
- "The FISA Court approves over 99% of government surveillance requests. Adding adversarial process and transparency would strengthen legitimacy, not prevent legitimate surveillance."

## Community Actions

- **Organize a CryptoParty**: Host a community event teaching practical communications security (encrypted messaging, email encryption, secure browsing). CryptoParty.in provides resources and guides
- **Support journalism on surveillance**: Share and amplify investigative reporting on government and corporate surveillance of communications
- **Engage local government**: Advocate for municipal policies requiring warrants for surveillance technology use; support local surveillance oversight ordinances modeled on ACLU's Community Control Over Police Surveillance (CCOPS) model
- **Testify at state legislature hearings**: When electronic privacy bills are considered, provide constituent testimony about the importance of communications privacy
- **Join advocacy campaigns during Section 702 reauthorization**: The reauthorization process is the most important near-term opportunity for reform; coordinate with EFF, ACLU, and other organizations
- **Educate local organizations**: Offer communications security training to journalists, lawyers, activists, domestic violence shelters, and other groups that depend on secure communications
