# Communications Privacy: Stakeholders

## Overview

Communications privacy involves a complex web of stakeholders including government surveillance agencies, technology companies, telecommunications carriers, civil society organizations, and the general public. Each stakeholder has distinct interests that shape the policy landscape.

## Stakeholder Map

### Government Surveillance Agencies

| Stakeholder | Role | Interest | Position on Reform |
|-------------|------|----------|-------------------|
| **National Security Agency (NSA)** | Signals intelligence collection | Maximize foreign intelligence collection; maintain Section 702 and EO 12333 authority | Opposes warrant requirements; supports encryption backdoors |
| **Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI)** | Domestic intelligence and law enforcement | Access communications for investigations; query 702 database for US persons | Opposes 702 warrant requirement; leads going-dark campaign |
| **Department of Justice (DOJ)** | Federal prosecution; FISA applications | Legal authority to compel communications disclosure | Defends broad surveillance authority; opposes ECPA reform that constrains access |
| **Central Intelligence Agency (CIA)** | Foreign intelligence | Communications intelligence for foreign operations | Supports broad collection authority |
| **Department of Homeland Security (DHS)** | Immigration and cybersecurity | Communications monitoring for border security and cyber threats | Supports surveillance authority expansion |
| **State and local law enforcement** | Criminal investigation | Access to communications for local investigations | Want expanded access; use cell-site simulators; request data from tech companies |

### Technology Companies

| Stakeholder | Role | Interest | Position on Reform |
|-------------|------|----------|-------------------|
| **Apple** | Device and services provider | User trust; product differentiation through privacy; legal compliance | Supports encryption; resisted FBI in San Bernardino case; opposes backdoor mandates |
| **Google/Alphabet** | Communications platforms (Gmail, Messages, Meet) | Advertising revenue from data; user trust; government relations | Mixed: supports ECPA reform; complies with government orders; adopted E2EE for Messages |
| **Meta** | Messaging platforms (WhatsApp, Messenger, Instagram DM) | User engagement; advertising; regulatory compliance | Adopted E2EE for Messenger (2023-2024); complies with government orders where legally required |
| **Microsoft** | Email, communications (Outlook, Teams) | Enterprise market; government contracts; user trust | Supports ECPA reform; challenged government gag orders; large government customer |
| **Signal Foundation** | Encrypted messaging | Privacy; open-source technology; user trust | Strongest privacy position; minimal data collection; would exit markets rather than compromise encryption |
| **Telecommunications carriers** (AT&T, Verizon, T-Mobile) | Carry voice and data communications | Regulatory compliance; government relations; liability protection | Comply with government requests; supported telecom immunity; built CALEA capabilities |

### Civil Society and Advocacy

| Stakeholder | Focus | Activities | Influence |
|-------------|-------|-----------|-----------|
| **Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF)** | Digital rights, encryption, surveillance reform | Litigation, advocacy, technical analysis, public education | High -- leading voice on communications privacy |
| **American Civil Liberties Union (ACLU)** | Civil liberties, surveillance reform | Litigation (*Clapper v. Amnesty International*), advocacy, FOIA | High -- major litigation and advocacy organization |
| **Brennan Center for Justice** | Democracy, justice, surveillance | Research, policy analysis, advocacy | High in policy discourse |
| **Center for Democracy and Technology (CDT)** | Technology policy | Policy analysis, advocacy, coalition building | Moderate -- bridge between industry and advocates |
| **Access Now** | Digital rights internationally | Advocacy, RightsCon convening, Helpline | Growing influence on international dimensions |
| **Reporters Committee for Freedom of the Press** | Press freedom, source protection | Litigation, advocacy for journalist communications privacy | High on reporter-specific issues |
| **Freedom of the Press Foundation** | Press freedom, secure communications | SecureDrop development, technical assistance to journalists | Moderate -- technical focus |

### Affected Populations

| Population | How Affected | Vulnerability |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| **General public** | All electronic communications subject to potential surveillance; metadata collected by companies and accessible to government | Moderate -- unaware of surveillance scope |
| **Journalists** | Source communications subject to surveillance; potential identification of confidential sources | High -- chilling effect on investigative journalism |
| **Lawyers** | Attorney-client communications may be intercepted through incidental collection | High -- privilege undermined |
| **Political activists** | Communications monitored for intelligence purposes; documented surveillance of protest movements | Very high -- targeted surveillance history |
| **Religious minorities** | Mosque and Muslim community communications surveilled post-9/11 | High -- documented targeting |
| **Immigrants** | Communications monitored for immigration enforcement | Very high -- ICE surveillance documented |
| **Whistleblowers** | Communications used to identify and prosecute leakers | Very high -- Espionage Act prosecutions depend on communications evidence |
| **Domestic violence survivors** | Communications may be monitored by abusers; seeking help through communications | High -- safety at stake |
| **International communicators** | Cross-border communications subject to Section 702 and EO 12333 | High -- lower legal protections for international communications |

| Stakeholder | Committee | Position |
|-------------|-----------|----------|

| **Senate Judiciary Committee** | ECPA reform, FISA | Mixed; some members advocate reform (Wyden, Lee) |

### International Stakeholders

| Stakeholder | Interest | Relevance |
|-------------|----------|-----------|
| **Five Eyes intelligence alliance** (US, UK, Canada, Australia, New Zealand) | Shared intelligence collection; mutual surveillance | Five Eyes share communications intelligence; agreements affect US privacy |
| **European Union** | GDPR compliance; transatlantic data transfers | EU-US Data Privacy Framework; Schrems litigation |
| **European Court of Justice** | EU fundamental rights | *Schrems I* and *Schrems II* invalidated US data transfer frameworks due to inadequate privacy protections |
| **UN High Commissioner for Human Rights** | International human rights | Reports criticizing mass surveillance as human rights violation |

## Power Analysis

| Power Dimension | Who Holds Power | Who Lacks Power |
|-----------------|----------------|-----------------|
| **Information** | Intelligence agencies (know scope of programs); tech companies (know data they hold) | Public (cannot know what is collected about them); Congress (limited access to operational details) |
| **Legal** | Government (state secrets doctrine; classification; standing barriers) | Individuals (cannot prove surveillance to establish standing; *Clapper v. Amnesty International*, 2013) |
| **Political** | Intelligence agencies and defense contractors (bipartisan support, revolving door) | Privacy advocates (asymmetric political risk) |
| **Technical** | NSA (offensive capabilities); tech companies (infrastructure control) | Individuals (dependent on companies for privacy tools) |
| **Economic** | Tech companies and telecom carriers (multi-billion dollar industries) | Advocacy organizations (fraction of industry resources) |

## Coalition Dynamics

### Privacy Reform Coalition

| Members | Shared Interest | Strength |
|---------|----------------|----------|
| Civil liberties organizations (ACLU, EFF) + tech companies (Apple, Google) | Oppose government overreach in communications access | Strong when interests align; unstable when companies face competing pressures |
| Left-libertarian alliance (Wyden, Lee, Paul) | Bipartisan opposition to warrantless surveillance | Narrow but effective in some floor votes; Section 702 warrant amendment came close |
| Journalist organizations + whistleblower advocates | Source protection; free press | Focused but limited political weight |

### Surveillance Preservation Coalition

| Members | Shared Interest | Strength |
|---------|----------------|----------|
| Intelligence agencies + defense contractors + intelligence committee leadership | Maintain and expand surveillance authority | Very strong; bipartisan; institutional advantages |
| Law enforcement + prosecutors | Access to communications evidence | Strong; "law and order" framing resonates politically |
| Child safety advocates (some) + law enforcement | Scanning encrypted communications for CSAM | Growing; emotional appeal; creates wedge in privacy coalition |
