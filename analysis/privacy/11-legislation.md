# Privacy: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Privacy protection in America requires legislative action at multiple levels: constitutional amendment to update Fourth Amendment doctrine for the digital age, comprehensive federal privacy legislation to establish baseline rights, targeted reforms for government surveillance and data brokers, and model state legislation to fill gaps. This document provides draft text for priority reforms identified in the solutions and roadmap analyses.

## Constitutional Amendments

### Digital Privacy Amendment

**Purpose**: Update the Fourth Amendment to explicitly protect digital information, personal data, and communications from unreasonable collection by government and private actors.

**Draft Text**:

```text
Section 1. The right of the people to be secure in their persons,
papers, communications, and personal information against
unreasonable collection, retention, use, and disclosure shall
not be violated.

Section 2. No government entity shall collect or access personal
information without a warrant issued upon probable cause,
particularly describing the information to be collected and the
person to whom it pertains, except in cases of imminent threat
to life.

Section 3. The purchase, receipt, or use by government of personal
information collected by private entities shall be subject to
the same warrant requirements as direct government collection.

Section 4. No private entity shall collect, retain, use, or
disclose personal information without the informed consent of
the individual, except as reasonably necessary to—
(a) provide a service specifically requested by the individual;
(b) protect the security of systems or data;
(c) prevent fraud or other illegal activity;
(d) comply with a legal obligation; or
(e) conduct research in the public interest with appropriate
    safeguards, as defined by Congress.

Section 5. Every person shall have the right to access, correct,
and delete personal information held about them, and to know
who has accessed such information.

Section 6. Congress and the States shall have the power to enforce
this article by appropriate legislation, provided that State laws
offering greater protection shall not be preempted.

Section 7. This article shall take effect two years after
ratification.
```

**Explanation**:

- Section 1 establishes the core right, extending beyond "papers" to communications and personal information
- Section 2 codifies warrant requirements, closing the third-party doctrine loophole
- Section 3 addresses the government data purchase problem explicitly
- Section 4 creates constitutional protection against private surveillance with limited lawful bases (service provision, security, fraud prevention, legal compliance, public interest research)
- Section 5 establishes affirmative data rights
- Section 6 enables federal and state enforcement while preserving stronger state protections
- Section 7 provides transition time for compliance

**Potential Challenges**:

- Ratification requires 2/3 of both houses of Congress and 3/4 of states
- Industry opposition would be intense
- National security establishment would resist Section 702 implications
- Questions about retroactive application to existing data holdings

**Refinements**:

- Alternative: Pursue through judicial reinterpretation and statutory reform
- Consider phased implementation for different sectors
- Grandfather existing lawful contracts with defined sunset

## Federal Legislation

### American Data Privacy and Protection Act (Enhanced)

**Purpose**: Establish comprehensive federal privacy rights for all Americans, with strong enforcement and no preemption of stronger state laws.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "American Data Privacy and
Protection Act of [Year]".

SEC. 2. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:

(1) COVERED ENTITY.—The term "covered entity" means any person
    or entity that—
    (A) collects, processes, or transfers covered data; and
    (B) is subject to the Federal Trade Commission Act or is
        a common carrier subject to the Communications Act of
        1934.

(2) COVERED DATA.—The term "covered data" means information that
    identifies or is linked or reasonably linkable to an
    individual or a device that identifies or is linked or
    reasonably linkable to an individual. The term does not
    include—
    (A) de-identified data;
    (B) employee data used solely for employment purposes;
    (C) publicly available information.

(3) SENSITIVE DATA.—The term "sensitive data" means—
    (A) government-issued identifiers;
    (B) health information;
    (C) financial information;
    (D) biometric information;
    (E) genetic information;
    (F) precise geolocation information;
    (G) private communications content;
    (H) login credentials;
    (I) information revealing race, ethnicity, religion,
        sexual orientation, or gender identity;
    (J) information concerning a known child under 18;
    (K) information revealing union membership or political
        affiliation.

(4) AFFIRMATIVE EXPRESS CONSENT.—The term "affirmative express
    consent" means an affirmative act by an individual that—
    (A) is made in response to a clear, conspicuous, and
        specific request;
    (B) clearly communicates the individual's authorization;
    (C) is obtained prior to the collection or processing;
    (D) is not obtained through dark patterns, deception, or
        manipulation.

(5) ALGORITHMIC SYSTEM.—The term "algorithmic system" means a
    computational process, including one derived from machine
    learning, statistics, or other data processing techniques,
    that uses covered data to make or facilitate decisions,
    predictions, recommendations, or classifications.

SEC. 3. DUTY OF LOYALTY.

(a) IN GENERAL.—A covered entity shall not collect, process, or
    transfer covered data in a manner that—
    (1) is not reasonably necessary to provide a product or
        service specifically requested by the individual; or
    (2) causes or is reasonably likely to cause substantial
        harm to an individual.

(b) DATA MINIMIZATION.—A covered entity shall—
    (1) limit data collection to what is reasonably necessary;
    (2) limit data retention to what is reasonably necessary;
    (3) delete or de-identify data when no longer necessary.

SEC. 4. CONSUMER RIGHTS.

(a) RIGHT OF ACCESS.—An individual has the right to obtain from
    a covered entity—
    (1) confirmation of whether the entity holds covered data
        about the individual;
    (2) a copy of such data in a portable format;
    (3) a list of third parties to whom data was transferred.

(b) RIGHT OF CORRECTION.—An individual has the right to require
    a covered entity to correct inaccurate covered data.

(c) RIGHT OF DELETION.—An individual has the right to require
    a covered entity to delete covered data, subject to limited
    exceptions for legal obligations and fraud prevention.

(d) RIGHT TO OPT OUT.—An individual has the right to opt out of—
    (1) targeted advertising;
    (2) sale or transfer of covered data;
    (3) profiling in furtherance of decisions that produce
        legal or similarly significant effects.

(e) RIGHT NOT TO BE SUBJECT TO RETALIATION.—A covered entity
    shall not retaliate against an individual for exercising
    rights under this section.

(f) RESPONSE REQUIREMENTS.—A covered entity shall respond to
    verified requests within 45 days, extendable by 45 days
    for complex requests with notice to the individual.

SEC. 5. SENSITIVE DATA PROTECTIONS.

(a) OPT-IN CONSENT.—A covered entity shall not collect, process,
    or transfer sensitive data without the affirmative express
    consent of the individual.

(b) PURPOSE LIMITATION.—Sensitive data may only be used for the
    specific purpose for which consent was obtained.

(c) ENHANCED SECURITY.—Sensitive data shall be subject to
    heightened security requirements as prescribed by the
    Commission.

SEC. 6. TRANSPARENCY.

(a) PRIVACY POLICY.—Each covered entity shall publish a clear,
    conspicuous privacy policy that describes—
    (1) categories of data collected;
    (2) purposes for collection and use;
    (3) categories of third parties receiving data;
    (4) data retention practices;
    (5) individual rights and how to exercise them;
    (6) contact information for privacy inquiries.

(b) JUST-IN-TIME NOTICES.—Before collecting sensitive data, a
    covered entity shall provide a clear, specific notice at the
    point of collection.

(c) DATA BROKER DISCLOSURE.—A covered entity that is a data
    broker shall prominently disclose this fact and provide a
    simple mechanism for individuals to opt out of all data sales.

SEC. 7. PROHIBITION ON DISCRIMINATION.

(a) NONDISCRIMINATION.—A covered entity shall not discriminate
    against an individual for exercising rights under this Act
    by—
    (1) denying goods or services;
    (2) charging different prices;
    (3) providing a different level of quality; or
    (4) retaliating in any other manner.

(b) PERMISSIBLE DIFFERENTIAL.—A covered entity may offer
    loyalty programs or discounts for data sharing if the
    financial incentive is reasonably related to the value of
    the data.

SEC. 8. DATA SECURITY.

(a) SECURITY PRACTICES.—A covered entity shall establish,
    implement, and maintain reasonable data security practices
    appropriate to—
    (1) the volume and sensitivity of covered data;
    (2) the size and complexity of the entity;
    (3) the cost of available security measures.

(b) VULNERABILITY ASSESSMENTS.—Large data holders shall conduct
    annual security assessments and address identified
    vulnerabilities.

(c) LARGE DATA HOLDER DEFINED.—For purposes of this Act, the
    term "large data holder" means a covered entity that—
    (1) has annual gross revenues exceeding $250,000,000;
    (2) collects or processes covered data of more than
        5,000,000 individuals or devices; or
    (3) collects or processes sensitive data of more than
        100,000 individuals.

(d) BREACH NOTIFICATION.—A covered entity that experiences a
    breach affecting 500 or more individuals shall—
    (1) notify affected individuals within 72 hours;
    (2) notify the Commission within 72 hours;
    (3) notify State attorneys general within 72 hours.

SEC. 9. ALGORITHMIC ACCOUNTABILITY.

(a) IMPACT ASSESSMENTS.—A large data holder using covered data
    in an algorithmic system that may produce legal or similarly
    significant effects shall conduct impact assessments
    evaluating—
    (1) design and inputs of the system;
    (2) potential discriminatory impacts;
    (3) mitigation measures.

(b) DISCLOSURE.—Upon request, a covered entity shall disclose
    to an individual whether an algorithmic system was used
    in a decision affecting them and provide a meaningful
    explanation of the system's logic.

SEC. 10. CHILDREN'S PRIVACY.

(a) AGE EXTENSION.—Notwithstanding the Children's Online Privacy
    Protection Act, this section applies to individuals under 18.

(b) PROHIBITION.—A covered entity shall not—
    (1) collect covered data from a known child except as
        strictly necessary to provide the service;
    (2) use covered data from a known child for targeted
        advertising;
    (3) transfer covered data from a known child without
        verifiable parental consent.

(c) DESIGN REQUIREMENTS.—Online services likely to be accessed
    by children shall implement privacy-protective default
    settings.

SEC. 11. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) FTC ENFORCEMENT.—The Commission shall enforce this Act as
    an unfair or deceptive act or practice under the Federal
    Trade Commission Act. Notwithstanding section 5(a)(2) of
    the Federal Trade Commission Act (15 U.S.C. 45(a)(2)), the
    Commission shall have jurisdiction to enforce this Act
    against common carriers subject to the Communications Act
    of 1934.

(b) RULEMAKING.—The Commission shall promulgate regulations
    implementing this Act within 18 months of enactment.

(c) STATE ATTORNEY GENERAL ENFORCEMENT.—A State attorney general
    may bring a civil action on behalf of State residents for
    violations of this Act.

(d) PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.—Any individual aggrieved by a
    violation of this Act may bring a civil action in Federal
    or State court.
    (1) STATUTORY DAMAGES.—An individual may recover the greater
        of actual damages or—
        (A) $100 to $750 per violation involving non-sensitive
            data;
        (B) $1,000 to $5,000 per violation involving sensitive
            data.
    (2) INJUNCTIVE RELIEF.—A court may grant injunctive relief.
    (3) ATTORNEY'S FEES.—A prevailing plaintiff shall recover
        reasonable attorney's fees and costs.

(e) PENALTIES.—
    (1) CIVIL PENALTIES.—A covered entity that violates this
        Act is liable for a civil penalty of up to $50,000 per
        violation, or up to 4% of global annual revenue for
        large data holders.
    (2) KNOWING VIOLATIONS.—A covered entity that knowingly
        violates this Act is liable for treble damages.

SEC. 12. PREEMPTION.

(a) FEDERAL FLOOR.—This Act establishes minimum standards and
    shall not be construed to preempt any State law that provides
    greater protection to individuals.

(b) PRESERVATION OF STATE AUTHORITY.—State attorneys general
    retain authority to enforce State privacy laws.

(c) NO LIMITATION ON COMMON LAW.—This Act does not limit common
    law privacy rights.

SEC. 13. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect 18 months after enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- Broad coverage of personal data for FTC-regulated entities and common carriers (banks, insurers, and nonprofits remain under sector-specific regimes)
- Strong consent requirements, especially for sensitive data
- Full suite of consumer rights (access, correction, deletion, portability)
- Private right of action with statutory damages (essential for enforcement)
- No preemption of stronger state laws (critical for privacy advocates)
- Algorithmic accountability requirements for automated decisions
- Extended children's privacy to age 18

**Potential Challenges**:

- Industry opposition to private right of action
- Preemption debate will be contentious
- Definition of "reasonably necessary" may require regulatory clarification
- Constitutional questions about regulating speech-related data

**Refinements**:

- Consider safe harbor for small businesses with tiered requirements
- May need specific provisions for journalism and academic research
- Enforcement funding will require appropriations

### Government Data Purchase Prohibition Act

**Purpose**: Close the constitutional loophole allowing government agencies to purchase commercially available data to circumvent Fourth Amendment warrant requirements.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Government Data Purchase
Prohibition Act of [Year]".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that—
(1) Government agencies increasingly purchase commercially
    available personal data, including location data, browsing
    history, and communications metadata;
(2) Such purchases allow agencies to obtain information that
    may warrant constitutional protection or would otherwise
    require legal process to compel;
(3) The Supreme Court in Carpenter v. United States (2018)
    recognized that location data reveals intimate details of
    personal life and is protected by the Fourth Amendment;
(4) Purchasing such data through commercial channels cannot
    be permitted to circumvent constitutional protections;
(5) Clear statutory prohibition is necessary to preserve
    Fourth Amendment rights.

SEC. 3. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:

(1) COVERED DATA.—The term "covered data" means any information
    that—
    (A) is linked or reasonably linkable to a specific individual
        or device; and
    (B) includes any of the following categories:
        (i) precise location information;
        (ii) web browsing or search history;
        (iii) communications content or metadata;
        (iv) biometric information;
        (v) financial transaction records;
        (vi) health information;
        (vii) any other category of information designated by
              the Attorney General through notice-and-comment
              rulemaking as warranting protection because it
              reveals patterns of behavior, associations, or
              activities comparable in sensitivity to the
              categories listed above.

(2) FEDERAL GOVERNMENT ENTITY.—The term "Federal government
    entity" means—
    (A) any agency of the Federal Government; and
    (B) any contractor or grantee acting on behalf of a Federal
        agency.

(3) STATE OR LOCAL GOVERNMENT AGENCY.—The term "State or local
    government agency" means any State, county, municipal, or
    other local government agency. The prohibitions in Section 4
    and enforcement provisions in Section 7 apply directly to
    Federal government entities; application to State and local
    agencies is through grant conditions in Section 8 or through
    State adoption of equivalent restrictions.

(4) PURCHASE.—The term "purchase" includes acquisition by
    payment, barter, subscription, or any other form of
    consideration.

(5) PRECISE LOCATION INFORMATION.—The term "precise location
    information" means information derived from a device or
    technology that reveals the past or present physical location
    of an individual or device with sufficient precision to
    identify street-level location or distinguish among commercial
    establishments.

(6) BIOMETRIC INFORMATION.—The term "biometric information"
    means data generated from the measurement or technological
    processing of an individual's physiological, biological, or
    behavioral characteristics that is used or intended to be
    used to identify a specific individual, including fingerprints,
    voiceprints, iris or retina scans, face geometry, and gait.

(7) HEALTH INFORMATION.—The term "health information" means any
    information relating to the past, present, or future physical
    or mental health condition of an individual, provision of
    health care, or payment for health care.

SEC. 4. PROHIBITION.

(a) IN GENERAL.—A Federal government entity may not purchase or
    otherwise acquire covered data from a data broker or other
    commercial source.

(b) WARRANT EXCEPTION.—Subsection (a) does not apply if the
    Federal government entity—
    (1) obtains a warrant issued by a court upon probable cause;
    (2) the warrant particularly describes the data to be
        acquired and the individual or individuals to whom it
        pertains; and
    (3) the warrant is served upon the data broker or commercial
        source.

(c) EXIGENT CIRCUMSTANCES.—Subsection (a) does not apply if—
    (1) there is an imminent threat to the life of an individual;
    (2) a warrant cannot be obtained in time to address the
        threat; and
    (3) within 48 hours, the Federal government entity applies
        for a warrant and reports the acquisition to the
        appropriate Inspector General.

SEC. 5. PROHIBITION ON INDIRECT ACQUISITION.

(a) THIRD-PARTY CIRCUMVENTION.—A Federal government entity may
    not request, encourage, or otherwise cause a private party to
    acquire covered data that the Federal government entity is
    prohibited from acquiring directly.

(b) SHARING AGREEMENTS.—A Federal government entity may not
    receive covered data from another Federal government entity
    that acquired such data in violation of this Act.

SEC. 6. TRANSPARENCY AND REPORTING.

(a) ANNUAL REPORT.—Each Federal agency shall submit an annual
    report to Congress detailing—
    (1) any warrants obtained under Section 4(b);
    (2) any acquisitions under the exigent circumstances
        exception in Section 4(c);
    (3) the types of data acquired and purposes.

(b) PUBLIC SUMMARY.—The Attorney General shall publish an annual
    public summary of all acquisitions reported under subsection
    (a), with appropriate redactions for ongoing investigations.

SEC. 7. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) EXCLUSIONARY RULE.—Covered data acquired in violation of
    this Act—
    (1) is not admissible as evidence in any Federal or State
        proceeding;
    (2) may not be used to obtain a warrant or other legal
        process.

(b) CIVIL LIABILITY.—An individual whose covered data was
    acquired in violation of this Act may bring a civil action
    for—
    (1) actual damages;
    (2) statutory damages of $10,000 per violation;
    (3) reasonable attorney's fees and costs.

(c) ADMINISTRATIVE ACCOUNTABILITY.—A Federal employee who
    knowingly violates this Act is subject to—
    (1) suspension without pay;
    (2) termination; and
    (3) a civil penalty of up to $10,000 per violation.

(d) CRIMINAL PENALTY.—Any Federal employee who willfully violates
    this Act with intent to cause harm or for personal gain shall
    be guilty of a misdemeanor and subject to imprisonment for
    not more than 1 year, a fine under title 18, or both.

SEC. 8. STATE AND LOCAL COMPLIANCE.

(a) GRANT CONDITIONS.—Beginning 2 years after enactment, no
    Federal agency may provide grant funding to a State or local
    agency that acquires covered data in a manner that would
    violate this Act if undertaken by a Federal agency.

(b) MODEL LEGISLATION.—The Attorney General shall publish model
    legislation for States to adopt equivalent restrictions on
    State and local government acquisition of covered data.

SEC. 9. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect 90 days after enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- Directly addresses the constitutional circumvention problem
- Warrant requirement applies to government purchases of intimate personal data
- Narrow exceptions for genuine emergencies with reporting requirements
- Exclusionary rule provides meaningful remedy
- Civil action enables individual enforcement

**Potential Challenges**:

- Intelligence community opposition
- Arguments about national security necessity
- Defining "covered data" broadly enough to prevent circumvention
- State and local enforcement coordination

**Refinements**:

- Consider carve-out for consensual intelligence sharing with allied nations
- May need provisions addressing inherited data from pre-enactment purchases
- Coordination with existing surveillance statutes needed

### Surveillance Transparency and Reform Act

**Purpose**: Reform Section 702 and related surveillance authorities to require warrants for US person queries, enhance transparency, and strengthen oversight.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Surveillance Transparency and
Reform Act of [Year]".

SEC. 2. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:

(1) UNITED STATES PERSON.—The term "United States person" has
    the meaning given in section 101 of the Foreign Intelligence
    Surveillance Act of 1978 (50 U.S.C. 1801), and includes any
    citizen or lawful permanent resident of the United States.

(2) COURT.—The term "court" means the Foreign Intelligence
    Surveillance Court established under section 103 of the
    Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act of 1978.

SEC. 3. WARRANT REQUIREMENT FOR US PERSON QUERIES.

(a) PROHIBITION.—Notwithstanding any other provision of law, no
    officer or employee of the United States may conduct a query
    of information acquired under Section 702 of the Foreign
    Intelligence Surveillance Act using a United States person
    identifier without an order from the Court.

(b) COURT ORDER REQUIREMENT.—An order under this section shall
    be based upon—
    (1) probable cause to believe the query will return foreign
        intelligence information or evidence of a crime; and
    (2) a particularized description of the US person identifier
        and the purpose of the query.

(c) EXCEPTION.—Subsection (a) does not apply to—
    (1) queries reasonably believed to return information
        necessary to prevent an imminent threat to life; or
    (2) queries conducted with the voluntary consent of the
        US person.

(d) REPORTING.—Each agency conducting queries under this section
    shall report quarterly to the congressional intelligence
    committees and the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court—
    (1) the number of court orders sought and obtained;
    (2) the number of queries under the exception in subsection
        (c); and
    (3) the basis for each exception query.

SEC. 4. MINIMIZATION REQUIREMENTS.

(a) PURGE REQUIREMENT.—Information acquired under Section 702
    that is determined to pertain to a US person and is not
    foreign intelligence information or evidence of a crime
    shall be purged within 30 days.

(b) RETENTION LIMITS.—Information acquired under Section 702
    shall not be retained for more than 3 years, except as
    specifically authorized by the Foreign Intelligence
    Surveillance Court.

(c) DISSEMINATION CONTROLS.—Information concerning US persons
    shall not be disseminated outside the intelligence community
    unless—
    (1) necessary to prevent an imminent threat to life;
    (2) constitutes evidence of a crime; or
    (3) authorized by a court order.

SEC. 5. FOREIGN INTELLIGENCE SURVEILLANCE COURT REFORM.

(a) AMICUS CURIAE.—In any proceeding before the Foreign
    Intelligence Surveillance Court involving a novel or
    significant interpretation of law, the court shall appoint
    an amicus curiae to represent privacy and civil liberties
    interests.

(b) APPEALS.—The amicus curiae may appeal any decision of the
    Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court to the Foreign
    Intelligence Surveillance Court of Review.

(c) DECLASSIFICATION.—The Foreign Intelligence Surveillance
    Court shall, to the maximum extent practicable, issue
    unclassified summaries of significant opinions.

(d) ANNUAL REVIEW.—The Privacy and Civil Liberties Oversight
    Board shall conduct an annual review of Foreign Intelligence
    Surveillance Court proceedings and submit a report to
    Congress, with classified annex as necessary.

SEC. 6. TRANSPARENCY REQUIREMENTS.

(a) STATISTICAL TRANSPARENCY REPORT.—The Director of National
    Intelligence shall publish an annual report including—
    (1) the number of targets under Section 702;
    (2) an estimate of US person communications incidentally
        collected;
    (3) the number of US person identifiers used in queries;
    (4) the number of court orders obtained under Section 3;
    (5) the number of criminal prosecutions using Section 702
        information.

(b) COMPANY REPORTING.—Companies receiving directives under
    Section 702 may publish aggregate statistics on—
    (1) the number of accounts affected;
    (2) the number of directives received.

SEC. 7. SUNSET.

The authorities under Section 702 of the Foreign Intelligence
Surveillance Act shall expire 4 years after the enactment of
this Act unless reauthorized by Congress.

SEC. 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect 180 days after enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- Closes the "backdoor search" loophole with warrant requirement
- Strengthens minimization to purge unneeded US person data
- FISA Court reform adds adversarial process
- Enhanced transparency enables public accountability
- Shorter sunset ensures more frequent Congressional review

**Potential Challenges**:

- Intelligence community claims of operational impairment
- FISA Court capacity for increased applications
- Classified information management
- Coordination with executive authority

**Refinements**:

- May need emergency exception procedures
- Consider expedited warrant process for time-sensitive matters
- Coordination with EO 12333 reform needed

### Data Broker Accountability Act

**Purpose**: Require registration, transparency, and consumer rights for data brokers.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Data Broker Accountability Act
of [Year]".

SEC. 2. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:

(1) AFFIRMATIVE EXPRESS CONSENT.—The term "affirmative express
    consent" means an affirmative act by an individual that
    clearly communicates the individual's authorization for a
    specific use of personal data, made in response to a clear
    and conspicuous request that is not obtained through dark
    patterns or deception.

(2) COMMISSION.—The term "Commission" means the Federal Trade
    Commission.

(3) DATA BROKER.—The term "data broker" means an entity that—
    (A) collects, purchases, or otherwise obtains personal data
        concerning individuals with whom the entity does not
        have a direct relationship; and
    (B) sells, licenses, or otherwise transfers such data.

(4) GOVERNMENT ENTITY.—The term "government entity" means any
    Federal, State, or local government agency, or any contractor
    acting on behalf of such agency.

(5) HEALTH INFORMATION.—The term "health information" means any
    information relating to the past, present, or future physical
    or mental health condition of an individual, provision of
    health care, or payment for health care.

(6) MINOR.—The term "minor" means an individual under the age
    of 18.

(7) PERSONAL DATA.—The term "personal data" means information
    that identifies or is linked or reasonably linkable to an
    individual or a device associated with an individual.

(8) PRECISE LOCATION DATA.—The term "precise location data" means
    information derived from a device or technology that reveals
    the past or present physical location of an individual or
    device with sufficient precision to identify street-level
    location or distinguish among commercial establishments.

(9) REGISTRY.—The term "Registry" means the Data Broker
    Registry established under Section 3.

SEC. 3. DATA BROKER REGISTRY.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—The Federal Trade Commission shall establish
    and maintain a public Data Broker Registry.

(b) REGISTRATION REQUIREMENT.—Each data broker shall register
    with the Registry within 90 days of commencing operations
    and annually thereafter.

(c) REGISTRATION INFORMATION.—Each registration shall include—
    (1) the data broker's name, address, and website;
    (2) categories of personal data collected;
    (3) sources of personal data;
    (4) categories of purchasers;
    (5) purposes for which data is sold;
    (6) whether the broker collects data about minors;
    (7) mechanisms for consumer opt-out.

(d) PUBLIC ACCESS.—The Registry shall be publicly accessible
    and searchable online.

(e) REGISTRATION FEE.—The Commission may establish reasonable
    registration fees to fund Registry operations.

SEC. 4. CONSUMER RIGHTS.

(a) ACCESS.—Upon verified request, a data broker shall provide
    an individual with—
    (1) confirmation of whether data is held;
    (2) a copy of data held in portable format;
    (3) the sources of such data;
    (4) the categories of purchasers.

(b) CORRECTION.—Upon verified request, a data broker shall
    correct inaccurate data within 30 days.

(c) DELETION.—Upon verified request, a data broker shall delete
    all personal data concerning an individual within 30 days.

(d) UNIVERSAL OPT-OUT.—
    (1) The Commission shall establish a universal opt-out
        mechanism allowing individuals to opt out of all
        registered data brokers through a single request.
    (2) Data brokers shall honor universal opt-out requests
        within 15 days.
    (3) Opt-out status shall remain in effect indefinitely.

(e) NO FEE.—A data broker may not charge a fee for access,
    correction, deletion, or opt-out requests.

SEC. 5. PROHIBITED PRACTICES.

(a) SALE TO GOVERNMENT.—A data broker shall not sell personal
    data to any government entity except pursuant to a warrant
    issued upon probable cause, consistent with the Government
    Data Purchase Prohibition Act.

(b) SENSITIVE DATA.—A data broker shall not sell or transfer—
    (1) data concerning minors;
    (2) health information (as defined in Section 2);
    (3) precise location data (as defined in Section 2);
    (4) data revealing race, ethnicity, religion, or sexual
        orientation;
    without affirmative express consent.

(c) ACCURACY.—A data broker shall implement reasonable procedures
    to ensure the accuracy of personal data and shall correct
    known inaccuracies.

SEC. 6. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) FTC ENFORCEMENT.—The Commission shall enforce this Act under
    the Federal Trade Commission Act.

(b) STATE ENFORCEMENT.—State attorneys general may bring civil
    actions for violations affecting State residents.

(c) PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.—Individuals may bring civil actions
    for violations, with statutory damages of $500 to $2,500
    per violation.

(d) PENALTIES.—
    (1) Failure to register: $10,000 per day.
    (2) Other violations: up to $50,000 per violation.
    (3) Knowing violations: treble damages.

SEC. 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect 180 days after enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- Creates transparency through mandatory registration
- Consumer rights to access, correct, and delete data
- Universal opt-out simplifies consumer protection
- Prohibits sale of sensitive data without consent
- Restricts sales to government without court order

**Potential Challenges**:

- Defining "data broker" to capture all relevant entities
- Verification procedures for consumer requests
- Cross-border enforcement
- First Amendment claims about information commerce

**Refinements**:

- May need exemptions for credit bureaus governed by FCRA
- Consider coordination with state data broker registries
- Fraud prevention use cases may need accommodation

### Model Biometric Information Privacy Act

**Purpose**: Provide strong biometric privacy protection modeled on Illinois BIPA.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Biometric Information
Privacy Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.

(1) "Biometric identifier" means a retina or iris scan,
    fingerprint, voiceprint, or scan of hand or face geometry.

(2) "Biometric information" means any information based on an
    individual's biometric identifier used to identify an
    individual, regardless of how captured or converted.

(3) "Private entity" means any individual, partnership,
    corporation, or other organization. The term does not
    include a State or local government agency.

SECTION 3. RETENTION; COLLECTION; DISCLOSURE.

(a) WRITTEN POLICY.—A private entity in possession of biometric
    identifiers or biometric information must develop a written
    policy, made available to the public, establishing—
    (1) a retention schedule; and
    (2) guidelines for permanently destroying biometric
        identifiers and information when the initial purpose
        has been satisfied or within 3 years of the individual's
        last interaction, whichever occurs first.

(b) CONSENT REQUIRED.—No private entity may collect, capture,
    purchase, or otherwise obtain biometric identifiers or
    biometric information unless it first—
    (1) informs the subject in writing that biometric
        identifiers or information is being collected or stored;
    (2) informs the subject of the specific purpose and length
        of term for which the biometric identifier or information
        is being collected, stored, and used; and
    (3) receives a written release from the subject.

(c) DISCLOSURE PROHIBITION.—No private entity may sell, lease,
    trade, or otherwise profit from a person's biometric
    identifier or information.

(d) DISCLOSURE LIMITATION.—No private entity may disclose,
    redisclose, or otherwise disseminate biometric identifiers
    or information unless—
    (1) the subject consents;
    (2) disclosure is required by law; or
    (3) disclosure is required by a valid warrant or subpoena.

(e) SECURITY.—A private entity must store, transmit, and protect
    biometric identifiers and information using reasonable
    security measures.

SECTION 4. PRIVATE RIGHT OF ACTION.

(a) RIGHT OF ACTION.—Any person aggrieved by a violation may
    bring a civil action for—
    (1) actual damages or liquidated damages of $1,000 for each
        negligent violation and $5,000 for each intentional or
        reckless violation;
    (2) reasonable attorney's fees and costs; and
    (3) injunctive relief.

(b) STATUTE OF LIMITATIONS.—Actions must be commenced within
    5 years of discovery of the violation.

SECTION 5. EXEMPTIONS.

This Act does not apply to—
(1) financial institutions subject to Gramm-Leach-Bliley;
(2) covered entities under HIPAA;
(3) contractors or subcontractors working on behalf of exempt
    entities, to the extent of such work.

SECTION 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect 180 days after enactment.
```

### Model Data Broker Registration Act

**Purpose**: State-level data broker transparency and opt-out rights modeled on California's law.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Data Broker Registration
Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.

(1) "Consumer" means a natural person who is a resident of
    this State.

(2) "Data broker" means a business that knowingly collects
    and sells to third parties the personal information of a
    consumer with whom the business does not have a direct
    relationship.

(3) "Personal information" means information that identifies,
    relates to, describes, is capable of being associated with,
    or could reasonably be linked to a particular consumer or
    household.

SECTION 3. REGISTRATION.

(a) Each data broker shall register annually with the
    [Attorney General/Secretary of State].

(b) Registration shall include—
    (1) the name and primary address of the data broker;
    (2) a website address;
    (3) an email address for consumer inquiries;
    (4) a description of the categories of data collected;
    (5) a description of opt-out procedures;
    (6) whether the data broker implements purchaser credentialing;
    (7) documentation of data security practices.

(c) The registration fee shall be [$400] annually.

SECTION 4. CONSUMER RIGHTS.

(a) ACCESS.—Upon verified request, a data broker shall disclose
    all personal information held about a consumer.

(b) DELETION.—Upon verified request, a data broker shall delete
    all personal information about a consumer within 45 days.

(c) OPT-OUT.—Data brokers shall provide a simple, free mechanism
    for consumers to opt out of data sales.

SECTION 5. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) The [Attorney General] may bring an action for violations.

(b) Civil penalties: $100 per day of violation, up to a maximum
    of $10,000 per year.

SECTION 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect January 1 following enactment.
```

## Regulatory Framework

### FTC Rulemaking Authority

**Existing Authority**: Section 5 of the Federal Trade Commission Act prohibits unfair or deceptive acts or practices. Rulemaking under Section 5 requires the Magnuson-Moss procedures (lengthy, contested).

**Proposed Regulations**:

| Regulation | Purpose | Authority |
|------------|---------|-----------|
| Commercial surveillance rule | Define unfair data practices | Magnuson-Moss (or new statutory authority) |
| Dark patterns prohibition | Prevent manipulative design | Magnuson-Moss (or new statutory authority) |
| Data broker registration | Industry transparency | Requires new statutory authority |
| Algorithmic accountability | Require impact assessments | Requires new statutory authority |

### CFPB Open Banking Rule

**Existing Authority**: Section 1033 of Dodd-Frank requires consumer access to financial data.

**Proposed Implementation**:

- Consumer control over financial data sharing
- Standardized APIs for data portability
- Liability allocation for unauthorized access
- Revocation rights for data sharing permissions

### HHS HIPAA Expansion

**Existing Authority**: HIPAA regulatory authority over covered entities.

**Proposed Guidance**:

- Clarify that reproductive health data receives heightened protection
- Extend HIPAA principles to health apps through voluntary frameworks
- Strengthen enforcement against unauthorized disclosures

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

| Issue | Analysis |
|-------|----------|
| First Amendment | Data is speech, but commercial regulation permissible for privacy |
| Fourth Amendment | Constitutional floor; legislation can exceed |
| Commerce Clause | Broad federal authority over interstate data commerce |
| State sovereignty | Anti-commandeering limits on state enforcement mandates |

### Preemption Questions

| Scenario | Analysis |
|----------|----------|
| Federal floor (preferred) | States may exceed federal minimums |
| Federal ceiling (opposed) | Would weaken privacy protection |
| Sector-specific | May be appropriate for specific industries |

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| "Reasonably necessary" | Broad interpretation could permit extensive collection | High |
| De-identification | Re-identification techniques may undermine | Medium |
| Consent manipulation | Dark patterns may circumvent consent requirements | Medium |
| Employee data exclusion | Workplace surveillance unaddressed | Medium |
| Research exemption | Could be exploited for commercial purposes | Low |

### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Enforcement resources | FTC cannot handle volume | Appropriations |
| Cross-border data flows | Jurisdiction limits reach | International scope |
| Existing data holdings | Legacy data not covered | Transition design |
| Algorithmic opacity | Hard to audit AI systems | Technical complexity |

### Rectification Procedures

1. **Regulatory clarity**: FTC should issue detailed guidance on "reasonably necessary" standard
2. **De-identification standards**: Adopt technical standards with regular updating
3. **Dark pattern enforcement**: Active FTC enforcement with published examples
4. **Workplace privacy**: Separate legislation addressing employment context
5. **Research safeguards**: Require IRB-equivalent review for commercial "research"
6. **Aggregation prevention**: Apply thresholds to beneficial owners, not just entities
7. **Funding**: Dedicated funding through registration fees
8. **International coordination**: Mutual recognition agreements with GDPR authorities
9. **Transition period**: Require deletion of pre-existing data on request
10. **Audit requirements**: Mandate third-party algorithmic audits

### General Implementation Concerns

- Political opposition from industry may weaken final legislation
- Enforcement agency capacity must be built alongside new requirements
- Consumer awareness campaigns needed to enable right exercise
- Technical standards development required for interoperability
