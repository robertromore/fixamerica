# Government Surveillance: Stakeholders

## Overview

Government surveillance involves a complex web of institutional actors, affected populations, and advocacy organizations. The power imbalance between the surveillance establishment and those who would reform it is among the most extreme in American politics: the intelligence community commands a $100 billion budget, classification authority, and bipartisan deference, while reform advocates must operate with limited resources, restricted access to information, and the constant political risk of being labeled "soft on security."

## Primary Stakeholders

### Those Harmed by Surveillance

| Stakeholder | How They Are Affected | Scale of Impact | Awareness Level |
|-------------|----------------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **General public** | Mass surveillance of communications, location, financial activity | 330+ million Americans | Moderate (post-Snowden); declining |
| **Muslim American communities** | Disproportionate targeting by FBI informant programs, mosque surveillance, travel restrictions | 3.5+ million Muslim Americans (Pew, 2023) | High within community |
| **Black and Brown communities** | Predictive policing, facial recognition bias, historical COINTELPRO legacy | Disproportionate impact documented (Georgetown, 2019) | Moderate-high |
| **Immigrant communities** | ICE/CBP surveillance using commercial data, social media monitoring, biometric collection | 11+ million undocumented; millions more in mixed-status families | High within community |
| **Political activists and protesters** | Social media monitoring, infiltration, surveillance of protest movements | Unknown scope; documented at BLM, pipeline, Occupy protests | Moderate among activists |
| **Journalists** | Source surveillance through metadata collection; Espionage Act prosecutions of sources | All journalists covering national security | High among press freedom advocates |
| **Whistleblowers** | Prosecution rather than protection for exposing illegal surveillance | Small number but extreme individual impact | High within IC |
| **Foreign nationals** | Broad surveillance under Section 702 and EO 12333 with fewer protections | Billions worldwide | Variable by country |
| **Defense attorneys** | Parallel construction conceals surveillance sources; undermines right to confront evidence | Criminal defendants whose cases rely on surveillance-derived evidence | Moderate among criminal defense bar |
| **Political dissidents abroad** | US surveillance shared with authoritarian allies | Unknown scope | Variable |

### Those Who Benefit from Surveillance

| Stakeholder | How They Benefit | Scale of Benefit |
|-------------|-----------------|------------------|
| **Intelligence agencies (NSA, CIA, FBI, DIA, etc.)** | Maximum collection authority; budget justification | $100 billion budget (FY 2024, ODNI) |
| **Defense and intelligence contractors** | Surveillance technology and services contracts | Tens of billions annually |
| **Data brokers** | Government is a major customer | $200+ million in documented federal contracts |
| **Law enforcement (federal)** | Access to intelligence for criminal investigations | FBI, DEA, ATF, Secret Service |
| **Law enforcement (state/local)** | Fusion centers; federal information sharing; technology access | Thousands of agencies |
| **Political leaders** | Surveillance as exercise of executive power | Presidents of both parties have expanded surveillance |
| **Foreign intelligence allies** | Five Eyes and other intelligence-sharing arrangements | UK, Australia, Canada, New Zealand, and 30+ partner nations |
| **Telecom/tech companies (partially)** | Legal immunity for cooperation; compensation; avoid adversarial relationship with government | AT&T, Verizon, major tech companies |

## Secondary Stakeholders

| Stakeholder | Interest | Position |
|-------------|----------|----------|
| **Federal judiciary** | Interpreting constitutional surveillance limits | Mixed; FISC deferential; Article III courts increasingly protective (*Carpenter*) |
| **Academic researchers** | Studying surveillance effects, developing privacy-enhancing technology | Generally pro-reform; provide expert testimony |
| **Tech industry** | Both cooperating with and resisting government demands | Complicated; post-Snowden pushback but ongoing compliance |
| **Foreign governments (allies)** | Intelligence sharing benefits; their citizens also surveilled | Support existing arrangements; some push for reform (EU) |
| **International organizations** | UN privacy rapporteur, EU institutions | Generally critical of US surveillance scope |
| **State legislatures** | Can regulate state/local surveillance; cannot affect federal programs | Growing interest in local surveillance oversight |
| **General public in other democracies** | Subject to US surveillance with fewer protections | Growing awareness; GDPR/privacy movement partly driven by Snowden |

## Decision Makers

| Decision Maker | Authority | Current Position | Influence Level |
|----------------|-----------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **President** | Commander-in-chief; EO 12333; appoints IC leadership | Both parties have expanded surveillance | Decisive |
| **Congress (Intelligence Committees)** | Oversight; reauthorization of surveillance authorities | Historically deferential; bipartisan reform caucus exists | Decisive but often passive |
| **FISC** | Approves FISA orders and Section 702 certifications | 99.97% approval rate; limited adversarial proceedings | Theoretically decisive; practically deferential |
| **Supreme Court** | Fourth Amendment interpretation | *Carpenter* (2018) expanded digital protections; incremental approach | Decisive for constitutional framework |
| **Director of National Intelligence** | Leads IC coordination; sets collection priorities | Institutional incentive to preserve authorities | High |
| **NSA Director** | Leads signals intelligence operations | Institutional incentive to maximize collection | High |
| **FBI Director** | Leads domestic counterintelligence and CT | FBI is primary user of Section 702 for domestic queries | High |
| **Attorney General** | Approves FISA applications; sets DOJ surveillance policy | Gatekeeper but rarely blocks IC requests | High |
| **PCLOB** | Reviews CT programs; advises on civil liberties impact | Advisory only; experienced vacancy and capacity issues | Moderate (when functional) |

## Influence Mechanisms

### How the Intelligence Community Maintains Surveillance Authority

| Mechanism | Description | Effectiveness |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| **Classification** | Classify evidence of abuse; restrict congressional debate | Very high -- prevents informed public debate |
| **Classified briefings** | Brief members in classified settings where they cannot discuss details publicly | High -- creates asymmetric information |
| **Fear appeals** | Invoke specific (often classified) threats to justify broad authorities | High -- "what if there's an attack?" |
| **Revolving door** | Former officials advocate in media, think tanks, consulting | High -- shapes "expert" discourse |
| **Reauthorization pressure** | Create must-pass deadline dynamics that favor status quo | High -- time pressure prevents deliberation |
| **Parallel construction** | Conceal surveillance sources in criminal cases | Moderate -- prevents judicial scrutiny |
| **Regulatory capture** | FISC judges, PCLOB members develop institutional sympathy | Moderate -- oversight becomes deferential |
| **Contractor lobbying** | Defense industry lobbies for surveillance contracts | Moderate -- campaign contributions, revolving door |

### How Reform Advocates Counter

| Mechanism | Description | Effectiveness |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| **Litigation** | Challenge surveillance programs in court | Limited -- standing and state secrets barriers |
| **FOIA requests** | Force disclosure of surveillance practices | Moderate -- delays and redactions limit impact |
| **Congressional advocacy** | Lobby reform-minded members; provide testimony | Moderate -- depends on political moment |
| **Public education** | Raise awareness of surveillance scope | Moderate -- attention fades between revelations |
| **Whistleblower support** | Defend and amplify whistleblower disclosures | High impact when it occurs; but rare and costly |
| **Technical resistance** | Develop encryption and privacy tools | Growing -- Signal, Tor, encrypted email |
| **International pressure** | EU adequacy decisions, UN privacy reports | Moderate -- some impact on executive policy |
| **Bipartisan coalition** | Left-right privacy alliance | Potentially high but fragile |

## Power Dynamics Summary

| Factor | Reform Advocates | Surveillance Establishment |
|--------|------------------|---------------------------|
| **Financial resources** | Limited (nonprofit budgets) | $100 billion IC budget; contractor lobbying |
| **Access to information** | Blocked by classification | Controls classification decisions |
| **Public opinion** | Supportive but diffuse | "Security" framing resonates during crises |
| **Congressional allies** | Bipartisan but minority | Bipartisan support from leadership on both sides |
| **Judicial tools** | Limited by standing, state secrets | FISC as de facto ally; 99.97% approval |
| **Institutional power** | None -- outside government | Vast executive branch apparatus |
| **Media access** | Depends on leaks and FOIA | Classified briefings; background quotes; op-eds by former officials |
