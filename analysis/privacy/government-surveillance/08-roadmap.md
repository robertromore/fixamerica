# Government Surveillance: Roadmap

## Implementation Strategy

Surveillance reform must navigate the most powerful opposition in American politics: the intelligence community, backed by a $100 billion budget, classification authority, and bipartisan deference. The strategy sequences reforms from most achievable to most transformative, leveraging bipartisan coalitions, the 2026 Section 702 reauthorization deadline, and growing public awareness of government data purchases.

## Phase 1: Foundation and Quick Wins (Years 1-2)

### Objective

Establish bipartisan precedents for surveillance constraints; close the data purchase loophole; build grassroots oversight infrastructure.

### Action Table

| Action | Lead Actor | Timeline | Dependencies | Priority |
|--------|-----------|----------|--------------|----------|
| Pass Government Data Purchase Warrant Requirement | Congress (bipartisan) | Year 1 | Wyden-Paul coalition; House Judiciary support | Critical |
| Enact IC Whistleblower Protection Enhancement Act | Congress | Year 1-2 | Bipartisan sponsors | Critical |
| Support local surveillance oversight ordinances (CCOPS model) | City councils, ACLU chapters | Ongoing | Local organizing | High |
| Expand PCLOB capacity and ensure quorum | President, Senate | Year 1 | Presidential nominations; Senate confirmation | High |
| Fund state/local surveillance transparency requirements | Congress (appropriations) | Year 1-2 | Grant program design | Medium |
| Launch public education campaign on government surveillance scope | NGO coalition | Year 1 | Foundation and donor support | Medium |
| Begin litigation challenging warrantless data purchases | ACLU, EFF, others | Year 1 | Standing; appropriate plaintiff | Medium |

## Phase 2: Section 702 Reauthorization (Years 2-3)

### Objective

Use the April 2026 Section 702 reauthorization deadline to achieve warrant requirements for US person queries and FISC reform.

### Action Table

| Action | Lead Actor | Timeline | Dependencies | Priority |
|--------|-----------|----------|--------------|----------|
| Pass US person query warrant requirement (Section 702 amendment) | Congress | Year 2-3 (before April 2026 deadline) | Bipartisan coalition; House and Senate floor votes | Critical |
| Enact FISC reform (adversarial proceedings, published opinions) | Congress | Year 2-3 (Section 702 vehicle) | Same legislative vehicle | Critical |
| Reform congressional oversight structures | Congressional leadership | Year 2-3 | Political will within committees | High |
| Restrict expanded "electronic communications service provider" definition (RISAA 2024 provision) | Congress | Year 2-3 | Section 702 vehicle | High |
| Mandate statistical transparency for all FISA activities | Congress | Year 2-3 | Section 702 vehicle | Medium |
| Extend Section 702 for 5 years (if reformed) or refuse reauthorization (if not) | Congress | Before April 2026 | Reform package agreement | Critical |

### Section 702 Reauthorization Strategy

The April 2026 expiration of Section 702 is the single most important surveillance reform opportunity:

**Leverage points**:

- Intelligence community needs reauthorization to continue collection
- Reform can be conditioned on reauthorization -- no reform, no reauthorization
- Bipartisan reform coalition exists (Wyden-Paul-Lee from left-libertarian side; House Judiciary bipartisan caucus)
- 2024 RISAA narrowly rejected warrant requirement; political dynamics may shift

**Strategic approach**:

1. Build bipartisan coalition for warrant amendment *before* deadline pressure builds
2. Refuse short-term extensions that allow intelligence community to run out the clock
3. Prepare public messaging: "The government should not be able to search your communications without a warrant"
4. Coordinate with civil liberties organizations on grassroots pressure
5. Counter classified briefings with declassified evidence of abuse

| Metric | Baseline (End of Phase 1) | Target (End of Year 3) | Data Source |
|--------|--------------------------|----------------------|-------------|

### Key Risks in Phase 2

| Risk | Probability | Impact | Mitigation |
|------|------------|--------|------------|

## Phase 3: Structural Reform (Years 3-6)

### Objective

Achieve comprehensive surveillance reform including facial recognition regulation, EO 12333 statutory framework, and institutional accountability.

### Action Table

| Action | Lead Actor | Timeline | Dependencies | Priority |
|--------|-----------|----------|--------------|----------|
| Enact federal facial recognition moratorium | Congress | Year 3-4 | Growing city-level movement; documented bias | High |
| Develop and pass comprehensive facial recognition regulation | Congress, federal agency | Year 4-6 | Moratorium provides time; technical standards developed | High |
| Bring EO 12333 collection within statutory framework | Congress | Year 4-6 | Requires sustained political momentum; complex legislation | High |
| Reform Espionage Act to include public interest defense | Congress | Year 4-6 | Whistleblower protection as foundation; political will | Medium |
| Mandate algorithmic accountability for surveillance AI | Congress, agency rulemaking | Year 5-6 | Technical standards development | Medium |
| Achieve international surveillance reform agreements | State Department, allied governments | Year 4-6 | Domestic reform demonstrates credibility | Medium |

### Success Metrics for Phase 3

| Metric | Baseline (End of Phase 2) | Target (End of Year 6) | Data Source |
|--------|--------------------------|----------------------|-------------|
| Federal facial recognition regulation | None | Comprehensive regulation enacted and operational | Congressional record |
| EO 12333 statutory framework | Executive self-regulation | Statutory judicial and congressional oversight | Congressional record |
| Espionage Act public interest defense | None | Available to defendants in surveillance disclosure cases | Congressional record |
| Surveillance AI regulation | None | Federal standards for government use of AI in surveillance | Agency rulemaking |
| International reform coordination | Limited (EU adequacy focus) | Bilateral surveillance reform agreements with allies | State Department |

## Resource Requirements

### Federal Investment

| Resource | Estimated Annual Cost | Justification |
|----------|---------------------|---------------|
| Enhanced congressional oversight staff | $50-100 million | Double intelligence committee staff; independent technical experts |
| FISC special advocate program | $20-30 million | Mandatory adversarial proceedings |
| PCLOB enhanced capacity | $20-30 million | Full staffing and expanded review capacity |
| IC whistleblower ombudsman | $10-20 million | Independent office outside IC chain of command |
| Local surveillance oversight grants | $50 million | State and local surveillance transparency |
| **Total** | **$150-230 million** | |

### Context

$150-230 million for surveillance oversight represents 0.15-0.23% of the $100 billion intelligence community budget. Effective oversight is a fraction of the cost of the programs being overseen.
