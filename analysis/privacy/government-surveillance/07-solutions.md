# Government Surveillance: Solutions

## Overview

Meaningful surveillance reform requires addressing not just specific programs but the structural incentives, institutional dynamics, and legal doctrines that enable surveillance expansion. The solutions presented here draw on historical reform efforts (Church Committee, USA FREEDOM Act), international models, constitutional principles, and the recommendations of expert bodies including the President's Review Group (2013) and the Privacy and Civil Liberties Oversight Board. The goal is a surveillance system that is effective against genuine threats, constitutional in its methods, transparent in its operations, and accountable to the public.

## Solution 1: Warrant Requirement for US Person Queries (Section 702 Reform)

**Description**: Require the FBI and other agencies to obtain a warrant based on probable cause before querying Section 702 databases using US person identifiers (names, phone numbers, email addresses, device identifiers). This closes the "backdoor search" loophole that enables warrantless surveillance of Americans.

**Evidence Base**:

- FBI conducted 119,000+ US person queries of Section 702 data in 2022 (ODNI, 2023)
- PCLOB recommended heightened protections for US person queries (2014)
- President's Review Group recommended warrant requirement for US person content queries (2013)
- *Carpenter v. United States* (2018) recognized Fourth Amendment protection for commercial data revealing intimate life details
- Multiple bipartisan bills (e.g., Government Surveillance Reform Act) have proposed warrant requirements

**Implementation**:

- Amend 50 U.S.C. ss 1881a to require FISC-issued warrant based on probable cause before any query of Section 702 databases using US person identifiers
- Exception for imminent threat to life, with 72-hour retroactive judicial approval requirement
- Exception for queries where the US person is the target's contact and the purpose is to protect the US person (victim exception)
- All warrant applications and outcomes reported to congressional intelligence committees
- Annual public statistical reporting on warrant applications, approvals, and rejections

**Estimated Cost**: Minimal additional cost (warrant process already exists within FISA framework)

**Timeline**: Next Section 702 reauthorization (April 2026) is critical window

**Challenges**: FBI/intelligence community opposition; "impracticality" argument; classified threat briefings designed to sway votes

**Impact Metrics**: Number of US person query warrants; reduction in warrantless queries; congressional oversight reporting

## Solution 2: Government Data Purchase Warrant Requirement

**Description**: Prohibit government agencies from purchasing personal data from data brokers without a warrant. This closes the constitutional loophole identified by the Georgetown "American Dragnet" report and acknowledged by the ODNI's own Senior Advisory Group.

**Evidence Base**:

- Georgetown Law "American Dragnet" (2022) documented systematic government data purchases
- ODNI Senior Advisory Group (2023) acknowledged purchased data "can be as revealing as content"
- *Carpenter v. United States* (2018) recognized that commercial data can reveal "privacies of life"
- Fourth Amendment Is Not For Sale Act introduced by Sen. Wyden with bipartisan support

**Implementation**:

- See [Data Brokers: Legislation](../data-brokers/11-legislation.md) for draft bill text
- Apply to all federal agencies, including intelligence agencies
- Extend to state/local agencies receiving federal funds
- Narrow exceptions for publicly available government records and genuine emergencies
- Suppression remedy for data obtained in violation

**Estimated Cost**: Minimal; reduces agency spending on commercial data

**Timeline**: Could pass independently of Section 702 reauthorization; bipartisan support exists

**Challenges**: Intelligence community opposition; law enforcement agencies (especially ICE, CBP) will resist; argument that purchased data is not "surveillance"

**Impact Metrics**: Reduction in government data purchases; warrant applications for commercial data; transparency reports

## Solution 3: FISC Reform

**Description**: Transform the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court from a rubber stamp into a genuine check on government surveillance by introducing adversarial proceedings, public opinions, and accountability for government misrepresentations.

**Evidence Base**:

- FISC has approved 99.97% of government applications (1979-2023)
- FISC's own compliance opinions have documented repeated government violations with minimal consequences
- USA FREEDOM Act (2015) created FISC amicus but its use has been limited
- President's Review Group (2013) recommended public interest advocate and published opinions

**Implementation**:

- **Mandatory adversarial proceedings**: Require appointment of a special advocate to argue against the government's position in every significant FISC proceeding
- **Published opinions**: Require publication of all significant FISC opinions within 180 days, with classified details redacted but legal reasoning disclosed
- **Consequences for misrepresentation**: Government attorneys who make material misrepresentations to the FISC face sanctions, including referral for disciplinary proceedings
- **Diverse appointment**: FISC judges selected by a judicial commission rather than solely by the Chief Justice; ensure geographic and ideological diversity
- **Compliance enforcement**: FISC empowered to impose sanctions, suppress evidence, and revoke surveillance authority for compliance violations
- **Annual transparency**: Detailed public reporting on application volume, approval rates, modifications, denials, and compliance findings

**Estimated Cost**: Modest -- special advocate program, additional court staff ($20-30 million annually)

**Timeline**: Could be enacted as standalone legislation or as part of Section 702 reauthorization

**Challenges**: Intelligence community will argue adversarial proceedings slow the process; Chief Justice appointment power politically difficult to change; classification concerns about published opinions

**Impact Metrics**: FISC approval rate (should decrease if scrutiny increases); published opinions; adversarial proceedings held; sanctions imposed

## Solution 4: Comprehensive Congressional Oversight Reform

**Description**: Restructure congressional intelligence oversight to be more effective, informed, and independent of the intelligence community.

**Evidence Base**:

- Multiple investigations have found congressional oversight inadequate (Church Committee, 9/11 Commission, Snowden disclosures)
- Intelligence committees have historically been deferential to IC leadership
- Members cannot discuss classified information with constituents or colleagues
- Reauthorization deadlines create pressure favoring surveillance status quo

**Implementation**:

- **Staff capacity**: Double intelligence committee staff; hire independent technical experts
- **Access guarantees**: Statutory right of every member (not just committee members) to review any intelligence program
- **Classified floor debate**: Establish procedures for classified congressional debate on surveillance authorities
- **Independent IG coordination**: Require IGs to brief oversight committees directly on compliance findings
- **Reauthorization reform**: Prohibit short-term extensions that create must-pass deadline pressure; require 5-year reauthorization cycles with mandatory hearings
- **Public reporting**: Quarterly unclassified reports to the public on surveillance program scope and compliance
- **Whistleblower channel**: Create independent channel for IC employees to report abuse to Congress without going through IC chain of command

**Estimated Cost**: $50-100 million annually for enhanced staffing and infrastructure

**Timeline**: Can be enacted at any time; most provisions do not require IC cooperation to implement

**Challenges**: Committee leadership may resist empowering rank-and-file members; intelligence community will resist expanded access; partisanship undermines oversight function

**Impact Metrics**: Committee hearing frequency; public reports issued; whistleblower complaints received; oversight-driven reforms enacted

## Solution 5: Strengthened Whistleblower Protections for Intelligence Community

**Description**: Create meaningful legal protections for intelligence community employees and contractors who report illegal surveillance to appropriate oversight bodies. End the practice of prosecuting whistleblowers under the Espionage Act for disclosing information about illegal government activity.

**Evidence Base**:

- Thomas Drake (NSA), Edward Snowden (NSA), Reality Winner (NSA), Daniel Hale (NGA) all faced criminal prosecution for disclosing information about surveillance
- Existing IC whistleblower protections (Intelligence Community Whistleblower Protection Act of 1998) are inadequate -- no access to courts, no damages, no protection for contractors
- Inspector General channels are controlled by the agencies being reported on
- Chilling effect: IC employees know that reporting abuse through "proper channels" leads to retaliation

**Implementation**:

- **Expand protected disclosures**: Protect disclosures of illegality, waste, or abuse to congressional intelligence committees, IGs, PCLOB, and designated counsel
- **Extend to contractors**: Full whistleblower protections for contractors, not just direct employees
- **Judicial remedy**: Provide access to federal court for whistleblower retaliation claims (currently barred for IC employees)
- **Espionage Act reform**: Amend the Espionage Act to include a public interest defense for disclosures about illegal government surveillance
- **Anti-retaliation**: Prohibit security clearance revocation, reassignment, termination, or investigation as retaliation for protected disclosures
- **Independent oversight**: Create an independent whistleblower ombudsman outside the IC chain of command

**Estimated Cost**: Minimal ($10-20 million for ombudsman office)

**Timeline**: Standalone legislation; could pass at any time

**Challenges**: Intelligence community will argue that protecting whistleblowers encourages unauthorized disclosures; Espionage Act reform faces strong opposition from DOJ; classification concerns

**Impact Metrics**: Number of protected whistleblower disclosures; retaliation claims filed; Espionage Act prosecutions for public interest disclosures

## Solution 6: Local Surveillance Oversight Ordinances

**Description**: Enact community oversight ordinances at the city and county level requiring public disclosure, approval, and review of surveillance technology acquisition and use by local law enforcement.

**Evidence Base**:

- Community Control Over Police Surveillance (CCOPS) model ordinances adopted in 20+ cities (ACLU)
- San Francisco banned government facial recognition (2019)
- Portland banned both government and private facial recognition (2020)
- Oakland's Privacy Advisory Commission model demonstrates effective local oversight

**Implementation**:

- **Pre-acquisition approval**: Require city council approval before law enforcement acquires any surveillance technology (Stingray, ALPR, facial recognition, social media monitoring tools, drones)
- **Impact assessments**: Require public privacy impact assessments before deployment
- **Use policies**: Mandate written policies governing use, retention, sharing, and deletion of surveillance data
- **Annual reporting**: Require annual public reporting on surveillance technology use, costs, and outcomes
- **Community review**: Establish civilian oversight board to review surveillance practices
- **Prohibition on specific technologies**: Ban facial recognition, predictive policing, and social media monitoring as appropriate for local context

**Estimated Cost**: Modest ($100K-$500K per city for oversight infrastructure)

**Timeline**: Can be enacted at any time; growing movement across cities

**Challenges**: Police union opposition; NDA restrictions on some federal technology (Stingray); preemption by state legislatures; resource constraints in smaller cities

**Impact Metrics**: Number of cities with surveillance oversight ordinances; technology acquisitions reviewed and denied; public awareness of local surveillance

## Solution 7: Facial Recognition Moratorium

**Description**: Impose a federal moratorium on government use of facial recognition technology until comprehensive regulation is enacted, with exceptions only for unlocking personal devices and searching specific, consented photo databases.

**Evidence Base**:

- GAO (2021) found 20+ federal agencies using facial recognition with inadequate oversight
- NIST (2019) documented significant racial and gender bias in facial recognition algorithms
- Clearview AI has scraped billions of photos from the internet without consent
- Multiple cities have banned government facial recognition (San Francisco, Portland, Boston, Minneapolis)
- FBI's FACE Services conducts 150,000+ searches per year without comprehensive regulation

**Implementation**:

- Federal moratorium on facial recognition by government agencies for identification or surveillance purposes
- Exceptions: (1) unlocking personal devices, (2) searches of consented mugshot databases for specific suspects
- State and local governments encouraged to adopt similar moratoriums
- During moratorium: Congress to develop comprehensive facial recognition regulation addressing accuracy standards, bias testing, consent, judicial authorization, and accountability
- Moratorium to last until federal regulation is enacted and operational

**Estimated Cost**: Negligible (agencies forgo technology they should not be using without regulation)

**Timeline**: Could be enacted immediately; regulation developed during moratorium (2-3 years)

**Challenges**: FBI and law enforcement opposition; argument that facial recognition solves crimes (selective evidence); DHS border security arguments

**Impact Metrics**: Federal agencies ceasing facial recognition use; bias testing results; comprehensive regulation development timeline

## Solution 8: EO 12333 Reform

**Description**: Bring foreign intelligence collection under Executive Order 12333 within a statutory framework with meaningful judicial and congressional oversight, rather than allowing the executive branch to govern itself.

**Evidence Base**:

- EO 12333 governs the majority of foreign intelligence collection but operates under executive self-regulation
- Americans' data collected incidentally under EO 12333 receives fewer protections than under FISA
- The intelligence community can potentially route collection through EO 12333 to avoid FISA requirements
- Church Committee envisioned FISA covering all foreign intelligence surveillance; EO 12333 created a parallel track outside statutory authority

**Implementation**:

- **Statutory framework**: Enact legislation bringing all foreign intelligence collection that could incidentally collect Americans' data under judicial oversight
- **Minimization requirements**: Statutory minimization procedures for Americans' data collected under EO 12333
- **Congressional notification**: Require notification to intelligence committees of all EO 12333 programs that incidentally collect Americans' data
- **PCLOB review**: Mandate PCLOB review of all EO 12333 programs within 2 years
- **Transparency**: Annual statistical reporting on EO 12333 collection incidentally involving Americans

**Estimated Cost**: Modest (oversight infrastructure)

**Timeline**: Long-term goal; likely requires sustained political momentum

**Challenges**: Intelligence community will argue EO 12333 reform impedes foreign intelligence; classification barriers; limited public understanding of EO 12333

**Impact Metrics**: Statutory framework enacted; judicial oversight of EO 12333 programs; minimization procedures applied; transparency reports published

## International Models

### Comparison of Surveillance Oversight Systems

| Country | Oversight Body | Key Features | Effectiveness |
|---------|---------------|--------------|---------------|
| **Germany** | G10 Commission; Parliamentary Control Panel | Independent commission reviews surveillance; strong parliamentary oversight | Strengthened post-Snowden; BND reform (2017) |
| **United Kingdom** | Investigatory Powers Tribunal; Judicial Commissioners | Judicial authorization for warrants; annual reports; right of complaint | 2016 Investigatory Powers Act created stronger oversight |
| **Canada** | National Security and Intelligence Review Agency (NSIRA) | Independent review body with broad access to classified information | Created 2019; designed to avoid compartmentalization |
| **Australia** | IGIS (Inspector-General of Intelligence and Security) | Independent IG with full access to agency operations | Active oversight; some transparency limitations |
| **European Court of Human Rights** | N/A (supranational court) | Can rule on surveillance by member states under Article 8 (privacy) | Multiple rulings constraining mass surveillance |

### Key Lessons from International Models

- **Independent review bodies with full classified access** are more effective than self-regulation
- **Judicial authorization** for surveillance warrants (not just review after the fact) is the international norm
- **Regular public reporting** on surveillance activity is feasible without compromising security
- **Adversarial proceedings** in surveillance courts improve oversight quality
- **Whistleblower protections** are essential for exposing abuse

## Sequencing Strategy

### Phase 1 (Year 1-2): Bipartisan Quick Wins

1. **Government data purchase warrant requirement** -- broadest bipartisan support
2. **Whistleblower protection enhancement** -- bipartisan appeal
3. **Local surveillance oversight ordinances** -- grassroots action

### Phase 2 (Year 2-3): Section 702 Reauthorization Window

4. **US person query warrant requirement** -- 2026 reauthorization opportunity
5. **FISC reform** -- adversarial proceedings, published opinions
6. **Congressional oversight reform** -- enhanced capacity and access

### Phase 3 (Year 3-6): Structural Reform

7. **Facial recognition moratorium and regulation** -- growing momentum
8. **EO 12333 statutory framework** -- most ambitious; requires sustained effort
