# Government Surveillance: Actions

## Overview

This document outlines concrete steps that individuals, communities, organizations, and professionals can take to advance government surveillance reform. Actions range from personal digital security measures to systemic advocacy aimed at restructuring surveillance authorities.

## Individual Actions

### Protect Your Digital Privacy

| Action | How | Impact |
|--------|-----|--------|
| **Use end-to-end encrypted messaging** | Switch to Signal for messaging and calls; use encrypted email (ProtonMail, Tutanota) | Prevents mass content collection; raises the cost of surveillance |
| **Use a VPN** | Subscribe to a reputable, no-log VPN service (Mullvad, ProtonVPN) | Reduces ISP and upstream collection of browsing data |
| **Use privacy-focused browsers** | Firefox with privacy extensions, Tor Browser for sensitive browsing | Reduces tracking and data available for government purchase |
| **Minimize location tracking** | Disable location services when not needed; review app location permissions | Reduces location data available through commercial purchases |
| **Review and restrict app permissions** | Audit smartphone permissions; remove unnecessary apps | Limits data collection that feeds government data brokers |
| **Use strong authentication** | Enable two-factor authentication; use hardware security keys | Protects against unauthorized access to accounts |
| **Limit social media exposure** | Review privacy settings; limit public posts; use pseudonyms where appropriate | Reduces social media monitoring exposure |
| **Opt out of data broker databases** | Submit opt-out requests to major data brokers (instructions at privacyrights.org) | Reduces commercially available data government agencies purchase |
| **Encrypt your devices** | Enable full-disk encryption on computers and phones | Protects data if devices are seized |
| **Use privacy-respecting search engines** | DuckDuckGo, Startpage, or Brave Search instead of Google | Reduces search data available for government access |

### Understand Your Rights

| Action | How | Impact |
|--------|-----|--------|
| **Learn the Fourth Amendment** | Study the text and major interpretations, including *Carpenter v. United States* | Foundation for all surveillance reform advocacy |
| **Know your rights during encounters with law enforcement** | ACLU "Know Your Rights" guides; National Lawyers Guild resources | Protects against unlawful search and surveillance |
| **Understand FISA and Section 702** | Read EFF, Brennan Center, and CDT explainers on Section 702 | Informed advocacy requires understanding current surveillance law |
| **Monitor the Section 702 reauthorization debate** | Follow Lawfare, Just Security, EFF, and Brennan Center coverage | 2026 reauthorization is a critical reform opportunity |
| **Know the difference between metadata and content** | Understand that metadata collection can be as revealing as content | Counters the "it's just metadata" argument |

### When You Suspect Surveillance

| Step | Action | Resource |
|------|--------|----------|
| 1 | **Document your concerns** | Keep detailed records of suspicious activity, unusual device behavior, or evidence of monitoring |
| 2 | **Consult a lawyer** | Contact a civil liberties attorney; ACLU affiliate intake; EFF legal team; National Lawyers Guild |
| 3 | **File FOIA requests** | Request records from FBI, DHS, and other agencies about surveillance of you or your organization (MuckRock can assist) |
| 4 | **Contact press freedom organizations** (if you are a journalist) | Reporters Committee for Freedom of the Press legal defense hotline: 1-800-336-4243 |
| 5 | **Secure your communications** | Immediately adopt encrypted communications; consult EFF Surveillance Self-Defense guide |
| 6 | **Report to civil liberties organizations** | ACLU, EFF, and Brennan Center track surveillance patterns and may pursue systemic challenges |

## Community Actions

### Education and Awareness

| Action | How | Target Audience |
|--------|-----|-----------------|
| **Host community forums on government surveillance** | Partner with ACLU chapters, EFF, libraries, and community organizations to hold public events | General public |
| **Organize "Surveillance Self-Defense" workshops** | Train community members in digital security basics using EFF materials | Activists, journalists, targeted communities |
| **Screen surveillance documentaries** | Host community viewings of *Citizenfour*, *United States of Secrets*, or *The Program* with discussion | General public |
| **Create community guides on surveillance rights** | Develop and distribute accessible materials in multiple languages | Immigrant communities, communities of color |
| **Support digital security training for targeted communities** | Fund and organize trainings for Muslim communities, immigrant organizations, activist groups | Communities most affected by surveillance |

### Local Advocacy

| Action | How | Target |
|--------|-----|--------|
| **Advocate for Community Control Over Police Surveillance (CCOPS) ordinances** | Push your city council to adopt an ACLU-model surveillance oversight ordinance | City council, mayor |
| **Demand facial recognition bans or moratoriums** | Organize community support for banning government facial recognition (San Francisco, Portland models) | City council, county commissioners |
| **Monitor local law enforcement surveillance technology** | File public records requests about Stingray devices, ALPR systems, social media monitoring tools, drone surveillance | Local police departments |
| **Oppose fusion center expansion** | Attend public meetings; demand transparency about fusion center activities and privacy policies | City/county officials; state legislators |
| **Support local journalist coverage of surveillance** | Tip local reporters to surveillance technology acquisitions; support local investigative journalism | Local media |
| **Demand surveillance impact assessments** | Push for public privacy impact assessments before any new surveillance technology deployment | City council, police oversight boards |

### Coalition Building

| Action | Partners | Goal |
|--------|----------|------|
| **Form local surveillance reform coalitions** | Civil liberties groups, faith communities, immigrant organizations, racial justice groups, libertarian groups | Unified local advocacy |
| **Build left-right privacy alliances** | ACLU + Cato/FreedomWorks model at local level; connect with conservative privacy advocates | Bipartisan reform pressure |
| **Connect affected communities** | Muslim American organizations, Black community groups, immigrant rights groups, journalist associations, protest movements | Center affected voices |
| **Engage faith communities** | Religious leaders and congregations concerned about government overreach and protection of vulnerable | Broaden coalition base |
| **Partner with academic institutions** | Law school clinics, computer science departments, journalism programs | Research and legal support |

## Political and Advocacy Actions

### Federal Level

| Action | How | Target |
|--------|-----|--------|
| **Contact Congress about Section 702 reauthorization** | Call, write, and email demanding warrant requirements for US person queries before the April 2026 deadline | Senators and representatives |
| **Demand a warrant requirement for government data purchases** | Support the Fourth Amendment Is Not For Sale Act; contact your representatives | Senate and House Judiciary Committees |
| **Support FISC reform** | Advocate for adversarial proceedings, published opinions, and consequences for government misrepresentation | Congressional leadership; Judiciary Committees |
| **Demand IC whistleblower protections** | Support enhanced protections for intelligence community whistleblowers; oppose Espionage Act prosecution of those who reveal illegal surveillance | All members of Congress |
| **Support facial recognition moratorium** | Back the Facial Recognition and Biometric Technology Moratorium Act | Senate and House sponsors |
| **Submit public comments on surveillance-related rulemaking** | Comment on ODNI, DOJ, DHS, and other agency rules and policies related to surveillance | Federal agencies |
| **Testify at congressional hearings** | Share personal experiences with surveillance or expertise in surveillance law | Congressional committees |
| **Support civil liberties organizations** | Donate to and volunteer with ACLU, EFF, Brennan Center, CDT, Demand Progress, Restore the Fourth | Advocacy organizations |

### State Level

| Action | How | Target |
|--------|-----|--------|
| **Support state electronic surveillance laws** | Advocate for state laws requiring warrants for electronic surveillance beyond federal minimums | State legislators |
| **Push for state data broker restrictions** | Support state laws restricting government purchases of commercial data | State legislature |
| **Advocate for state facial recognition regulation** | Push for state moratoriums or comprehensive regulation of government facial recognition use | State legislature |
| **Demand state audit of surveillance spending** | Request state auditor review of law enforcement surveillance technology expenditures | State auditor; legislature |
| **Monitor state intelligence fusion centers** | Demand state-level transparency and oversight of fusion center activities | Governor; state legislature |

### Voter Actions

| Action | When | Impact |
|--------|------|--------|
| **Evaluate candidates on surveillance reform positions** | Every election cycle | Electoral pressure for reform |
| **Ask candidates about Section 702, data purchases, and facial recognition** | Town halls, debates, candidate forums | Raises issue salience |
| **Support candidates who champion surveillance reform** | Primaries and general elections at all levels | Elect reform champions |
| **Hold elected officials accountable for surveillance votes** | Track voting records on FISA reauthorization, warrant requirements, oversight reform | Accountability |

## Professional Actions

### For Technologists

| Action | How | Impact |
|--------|-----|--------|
| **Build privacy-preserving tools** | Contribute to Signal, Tor, encrypted email, and other privacy infrastructure | Expand public access to secure communications |
| **Refuse to build surveillance systems** | Exercise professional ethics when asked to build mass surveillance tools | Industry norm-setting; personal integrity |
| **Advocate within tech companies** | Push for transparency reports, resistance to government overreach, and encryption by default | Corporate policy change |
| **Publish research on surveillance capabilities** | Document and share technical analysis of surveillance tools and their limitations | Inform public and policy debates |
| **Support encryption and security** | Oppose backdoor mandates; implement strong encryption in products and services | Protect communications from mass surveillance |
| **Audit government surveillance technology** | Provide technical expertise to oversight bodies, civil liberties organizations, and journalists | Enable informed oversight |

### For Lawyers

| Action | How | Impact |
|--------|-----|--------|
| **Bring surveillance challenges in court** | Challenge warrantless surveillance, data purchases, and facial recognition under Fourth Amendment | Legal precedent |
| **File FOIA litigation** | Sue agencies that fail to comply with FOIA requests about surveillance | Force disclosure |
| **Provide pro bono representation** | Represent individuals and organizations targeted by surveillance | Access to justice |
| **Develop legal theories for data purchase challenges** | Extend *Carpenter* reasoning to government commercial data purchases | Build the legal framework |
| **Serve as FISC amicus** | Apply to serve as FISC-appointed amicus curiae to advocate for civil liberties in surveillance proceedings | Adversarial input in FISC |
| **Draft model legislation** | Develop bill text for surveillance reform at federal, state, and local levels | Legislative infrastructure |

### For Journalists

| Action | How | Impact |
|--------|-----|--------|
| **Investigate government surveillance programs** | File FOIA requests; cultivate intelligence community sources; analyze procurement records | Public accountability |
| **Practice secure journalism** | Use encrypted communications, secure drops (SecureDrop), and operational security | Protect sources; model best practices |
| **Report on local surveillance** | Investigate local law enforcement surveillance technology and practices | Local accountability |
| **Track government data purchases** | Monitor USASpending.gov and agency procurement records for data broker contracts | Expose warrantless surveillance through commercial data |
| **Cover the Section 702 reauthorization** | Provide sustained coverage of the 2026 reauthorization debate | Informed public engagement |

### For Academics

| Action | How | Impact |
|--------|-----|--------|
| **Research surveillance effectiveness** | Study whether mass surveillance actually prevents terrorism compared to targeted methods | Evidence base for reform |
| **Analyze disparate surveillance impact** | Document how surveillance disproportionately affects communities of color, Muslims, and immigrants | Civil rights dimension |
| **Evaluate oversight mechanisms** | Study FISC, congressional oversight, and PCLOB effectiveness | Reform recommendations |
| **Develop surveillance measurement tools** | Create methods to quantify surveillance scope and impact | Enable accountability |
| **Testify before Congress and state legislatures** | Share research findings with policymakers | Translate research into policy |
| **Train the next generation** | Teach courses on surveillance law, digital privacy, and civil liberties | Build future capacity |
