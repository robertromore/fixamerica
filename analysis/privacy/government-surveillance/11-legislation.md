# Government Surveillance: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Meaningful government surveillance reform requires legislative action at the federal and state levels to establish warrant requirements for surveillance of Americans, strengthen judicial and congressional oversight, restrict government purchases of commercial data, protect whistleblowers, and regulate emerging surveillance technologies. This document provides draft legislative text for priority reforms, organized around the solutions and roadmap identified in preceding documents.

## Constitutional Amendments

### Amendment: Digital Privacy Protection

**Purpose**: Extend Fourth Amendment protections to explicitly cover digital data, closing the third-party doctrine loophole and establishing technology-neutral privacy rights.

```text
Section 1. The right of the people to be secure in their
persons, houses, papers, effects, and digital data against
unreasonable searches and seizures shall not be violated.
No warrant shall issue but upon probable cause, supported
by oath or affirmation, and particularly describing the
place or data to be searched, and the persons or things
to be seized.

Section 2. The collection, purchase, or acquisition by the
Government of personal data, including but not limited to
communications content, communications metadata, location
data, financial records, browsing history, and biometric
data, shall constitute a search within the meaning of this
Amendment and shall require a warrant issued upon probable
cause, except in circumstances of imminent threat to life,
in which case judicial approval shall be obtained within
72 hours.

Section 3. No person shall lose the protections of this
Amendment by virtue of having shared data with a third
party, including commercial service providers, data brokers,
or telecommunications carriers.

Section 4. Congress shall have the power to enforce this
article by appropriate legislation.

Section 5. This article shall take effect two years after
the date of ratification.
```

**Explanation**: This amendment would constitutionalize the principle that digital data receives Fourth Amendment protection regardless of whether it has been shared with third parties. It directly overrules the third-party doctrine of *United States v. Miller* (1976) and *Smith v. Maryland* (1979), which the Supreme Court partially limited in *Carpenter v. United States* (2018) but did not fully overturn. Section 2 closes the government data purchase loophole by defining acquisition of personal data as a search. Section 3 ensures that using commercial services does not waive constitutional rights.

**Challenges**: Constitutional amendments require two-thirds of both chambers and ratification by three-fourths of states. This is an aspirational goal that establishes the principle for statutory reforms below.

## Federal Legislation

### The Surveillance Reform and Fourth Amendment Restoration Act

**Purpose**: Require warrants for government queries of Americans' communications in Section 702 databases, close the data purchase loophole, reform the FISC, and strengthen oversight.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Surveillance Reform and Fourth
Amendment Restoration Act of [Year]".

SEC. 2. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:

(1) COVERED DATA.—The term "covered data" means any
    information relating to an identified or identifiable
    individual, including—
    (A) communications content and metadata;
    (B) location data;
    (C) financial transaction records;
    (D) browsing and search history;
    (E) biometric data;
    (F) device identifiers and advertising identifiers;
    (G) social media activity; and
    (H) any other data that, alone or in combination,
        can identify or track an individual.

(2) DATA BROKER.—The term "data broker" means a commercial
    entity that collects, aggregates, or sells covered data
    pertaining to individuals with whom it does not have a
    direct relationship.

(3) GOVERNMENT AGENCY.—The term "government agency" means
    any federal department, agency, bureau, office, or
    instrumentality, including the intelligence community
    as defined in 50 U.S.C. ss 3003.

(4) US PERSON.—The term "US person" has the meaning given
    in 50 U.S.C. ss 1801(i).

(5) US PERSON QUERY.—The term "US person query" means any
    query of data collected under Section 702 of the FISA
    Amendments Act, Executive Order 12333, or any other
    foreign intelligence authority using a US person
    identifier, including a name, phone number, email
    address, device identifier, or other identifier
    associated with a known US person.

TITLE I—SECTION 702 REFORM

SEC. 101. WARRANT REQUIREMENT FOR US PERSON QUERIES.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Section 702 of the Foreign Intelligence
    Surveillance Act of 1978 (50 U.S.C. ss 1881a) is
    amended to add the following subsection:

    "(l) WARRANT REQUIREMENT FOR US PERSON QUERIES.—

    (1) No officer or employee of the United States may
        conduct a query of information acquired under this
        section using a US person identifier without first
        obtaining an order from the Foreign Intelligence
        Surveillance Court based on a showing of probable
        cause that the US person is—
        (A) an agent of a foreign power; or
        (B) in contact with an agent of a foreign power
            and the information sought is relevant to an
            authorized investigation of that foreign power.

    (2) EMERGENCY EXCEPTION.—In cases involving imminent
        threat to human life, a US person query may be
        conducted without prior judicial approval if—
        (A) the Attorney General or a Deputy Attorney General
            personally authorizes the query;
        (B) a warrant application is submitted to the Court
            within 72 hours; and
        (C) if the Court denies the application, any
            information obtained is suppressed and may
            not be used in any proceeding.

    (3) VICTIM EXCEPTION.—A US person query may be conducted
        without a warrant if the sole purpose is to identify
        or protect a victim of a crime and—
        (A) the query is limited to identifying potential
            victims; and
        (B) any information obtained is not used for law
            enforcement or intelligence purposes unrelated
            to victim identification.

    (4) CONSENT EXCEPTION.—A US person query may be conducted
        without a warrant if the US person has provided
        informed, voluntary consent in writing.

    (5) SUPPRESSION REMEDY.—Any information obtained in
        violation of this subsection—
        (A) shall be suppressed and may not be used as
            evidence in any criminal, civil, administrative,
            or immigration proceeding; and
        (B) may not be used as the basis for further
            investigation, surveillance, or any government
            action.

    (6) REPORTING.—The Director of National Intelligence
        shall report to the congressional intelligence
        committees and the public on a quarterly basis—
        (A) the number of US person query warrant
            applications submitted, approved, denied,
            and withdrawn;
        (B) the number of emergency queries conducted;
        (C) the number of queries suppressed due to
            violations; and
        (D) aggregate statistics on the purpose and
            outcome of US person queries."

SEC. 102. RESTRICTION ON EXPANDED PROVIDER DEFINITION.

(a) The definition of "electronic communications service
    provider" in Section 702, as amended by the Reforming
    Intelligence and Securing America Act (RISAA) of 2024,
    is amended to exclude—
    (1) any entity that is not a traditional provider of
        electronic communications services or remote
        computing services;
    (2) any entity that provides incidental access to
        communications equipment or facilities;
    (3) hotels, libraries, coffee shops, and other entities
        that provide wifi or internet access as ancillary
        to their primary business.

TITLE II—GOVERNMENT DATA PURCHASE RESTRICTIONS

SEC. 201. WARRANT REQUIREMENT FOR GOVERNMENT DATA
PURCHASES.

(a) IN GENERAL.—No government agency may purchase, acquire,
    receive, or otherwise obtain covered data from a data
    broker or any commercial entity without—
    (1) a warrant issued by a court of competent jurisdiction
        based on probable cause; or
    (2) the informed, voluntary consent of the individual
        whose data is sought.

(b) SCOPE.—This section applies to all government agencies,
    including—
    (1) intelligence community agencies;
    (2) federal law enforcement agencies;
    (3) military agencies;
    (4) state and local agencies receiving federal funds
        or participating in federal information-sharing
        programs.

(c) EXCEPTIONS.—This section does not apply to—
    (1) publicly available government records (court
        filings, property records, business registrations);
    (2) data necessary for the routine administrative
        functions of an agency unrelated to surveillance
        or investigation (e.g., market research, aggregate
        statistical analysis with no individual identifiers);
    (3) data acquired with the informed, voluntary consent
        of the data subject.

(d) EMERGENCY EXCEPTION.—In cases involving imminent threat
    to human life, a government agency may purchase or
    acquire covered data without prior judicial approval if—
    (1) a senior official personally authorizes the
        acquisition;
    (2) a warrant application is submitted within 48 hours;
    (3) if the warrant is denied, all data obtained is
        deleted and may not be used.

(e) SUPPRESSION REMEDY.—Data obtained in violation of this
    section—
    (1) shall be suppressed in any proceeding;
    (2) may not be used as the basis for further
        investigation, surveillance, or government action;
    (3) must be deleted within 30 days of a finding of
        violation.

(f) REPORTING.—Each government agency that purchases covered
    data shall report annually to Congress and the public—
    (1) the number of warrant applications for data
        purchases, and their disposition;
    (2) the total expenditure on commercial data;
    (3) the categories of data purchased;
    (4) the number of emergency acquisitions.

SEC. 202. PROHIBITION ON CIRCUMVENTION.

(a) No government agency may direct, request, or incentivize
    a private entity to collect data that the government
    would be prohibited from collecting directly without
    a warrant.

(b) No government agency may use intermediaries, contractors,
    or foreign partners to acquire data that this Act
    prohibits the agency from acquiring directly.

TITLE III—FISC REFORM

SEC. 301. MANDATORY ADVERSARIAL PROCEEDINGS.

(a) The Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court shall appoint
    a special advocate to argue against the government's
    position in—
    (1) every application for a FISA traditional order;
    (2) every annual certification under Section 702;
    (3) every novel or significant application, as
        determined by the presiding judge; and
    (4) every proceeding addressing compliance violations.

(b) QUALIFICATIONS.—Special advocates shall—
    (1) hold appropriate security clearances;
    (2) have demonstrated expertise in privacy, civil
        liberties, and constitutional law;
    (3) be selected from a panel maintained by the
        Administrative Office of the United States Courts;
    (4) serve no more than 4 years to prevent institutional
        capture.

(c) ACCESS.—Special advocates shall have access to all
    materials provided to the Court by the government and
    may request additional materials.

SEC. 302. PUBLISHED OPINIONS.

(a) All opinions, orders, and rulings of the Foreign
    Intelligence Surveillance Court and the Foreign
    Intelligence Surveillance Court of Review shall be
    published within 180 days of issuance.

(b) REDACTION.—Published opinions may redact specific
    intelligence sources and methods, identities of
    targets, and operational details, but—
    (1) legal reasoning and interpretations of surveillance
        authorities shall not be redacted; and
    (2) the Court shall appoint the special advocate to
        review proposed redactions and challenge overbroad
        classification.

SEC. 303. FISC APPOINTMENT REFORM.

(a) FISC judges shall be selected by a judicial commission
    consisting of—
    (1) the Chief Justice of the United States;
    (2) two judges designated by the Judicial Conference;
    (3) two judges designated by the chief judges of the
        circuit courts, by rotation.

(b) The commission shall ensure geographic, ideological,
    and demographic diversity in FISC appointments.

(c) No more than 6 of the 11 FISC judges may be appointed
    by a President of the same political party.

SEC. 304. COMPLIANCE ENFORCEMENT.

(a) The FISC may impose sanctions on government attorneys
    who make material misrepresentations to the Court,
    including—
    (1) referral for professional disciplinary proceedings;
    (2) monetary penalties;
    (3) exclusion from practice before the Court.

(b) The FISC may order suppression of evidence obtained
    through surveillance conducted in violation of Court
    orders or applicable law.

(c) The FISC may revoke or modify surveillance authority
    when compliance violations are persistent or willful.

TITLE IV—CONGRESSIONAL OVERSIGHT REFORM

SEC. 401. ENHANCED OVERSIGHT CAPACITY.

(a) STAFFING.—The authorized staff of the Senate Select
    Committee on Intelligence and the House Permanent
    Select Committee on Intelligence shall each be
    increased by not less than 50 percent.

(b) INDEPENDENT TECHNICAL EXPERTISE.—Each committee shall
    employ not fewer than 10 staff with technical expertise
    in signals intelligence, data analytics, and
    surveillance technology.

(c) ACCESS.—Every member of the Senate and House of
    Representatives shall have access, upon request, to
    any intelligence program or activity, subject to
    appropriate security procedures.

SEC. 402. TRANSPARENCY REQUIREMENTS.

(a) The Director of National Intelligence shall submit to
    Congress and the public a quarterly report including—
    (1) the number and type of surveillance orders issued
        under each authority;
    (2) the number of US persons whose data was collected,
        queried, or accessed under each authority;
    (3) compliance violations and their resolution;
    (4) aggregate statistics on government data purchases;
    (5) the number and disposition of whistleblower
        complaints.

(b) The report required under subsection (a) shall be
    unclassified to the maximum extent consistent with
    the protection of intelligence sources and methods.

SEC. 403. REAUTHORIZATION REFORM.

(a) Section 702 and any successor authority shall be subject
    to reauthorization every 5 years.

(b) No short-term extension of Section 702 or any successor
    authority shall be enacted for a period of less than
    one year.

(c) The congressional intelligence committees shall hold
    not fewer than 4 public hearings during each
    reauthorization cycle, including testimony from—
    (1) civil liberties organizations;
    (2) the Privacy and Civil Liberties Oversight Board;
    (3) technical experts;
    (4) affected communities.

TITLE V—WHISTLEBLOWER PROTECTIONS

SEC. 501. ENHANCED IC WHISTLEBLOWER PROTECTIONS.

(a) PROTECTED DISCLOSURES.—A disclosure by an employee or
    contractor of the intelligence community shall be
    protected if the employee or contractor reasonably
    believes the information evidences—
    (1) a violation of any law, rule, or regulation;
    (2) gross mismanagement, waste of funds, or abuse of
        authority;
    (3) a substantial and specific danger to public
        health or safety; or
    (4) a violation of the Constitution, including the
        Fourth Amendment.

(b) PROTECTED CHANNELS.—Protected disclosures may be made
    to—
    (1) an Inspector General;
    (2) the congressional intelligence committees;
    (3) the Privacy and Civil Liberties Oversight Board;
    (4) the independent whistleblower ombudsman
        established under this Act; or
    (5) legal counsel with appropriate clearances.

(c) PROTECTIONS.—No employee or contractor who makes a
    protected disclosure shall be subject to—
    (1) termination, demotion, or reassignment;
    (2) security clearance revocation or suspension;
    (3) investigation or surveillance in retaliation;
    (4) any other adverse personnel action.

(d) JUDICIAL REMEDY.—An employee or contractor who
    experiences retaliation for a protected disclosure
    may bring a civil action in any district court of
    the United States for—
    (1) reinstatement;
    (2) back pay and compensatory damages;
    (3) reasonable attorney's fees; and
    (4) any other appropriate relief.

(e) CONTRACTOR COVERAGE.—All protections in this section
    apply equally to contractors and subcontractors of
    the intelligence community.

SEC. 502. INDEPENDENT WHISTLEBLOWER OMBUDSMAN.

(a) There is established an Independent Intelligence
    Community Whistleblower Ombudsman, appointed by the
    Privacy and Civil Liberties Oversight Board for a
    term of 7 years.

(b) The Ombudsman shall—
    (1) receive and investigate whistleblower complaints
        independently of the intelligence community chain
        of command;
    (2) refer complaints to appropriate oversight bodies;
    (3) monitor retaliation against whistleblowers;
    (4) report annually to Congress and the public on
        whistleblower activity and retaliation.

SEC. 503. ESPIONAGE ACT REFORM.

(a) Section 793 of title 18, United States Code, is amended
    to provide that it shall be a defense to prosecution
    that the disclosure—
    (1) revealed evidence of illegal government
        surveillance or other unconstitutional activity;
    (2) was made to a journalist, congressional
        committee, Inspector General, or other appropriate
        oversight body; and
    (3) did not disclose specific intelligence sources,
        agents, or ongoing covert operations whose
        disclosure would create a clear and present danger
        to national security.

TITLE VI—FACIAL RECOGNITION MORATORIUM

SEC. 601. FEDERAL MORATORIUM.

(a) No federal agency may use facial recognition technology
    for identification or surveillance purposes for a
    period of 3 years following the date of enactment.

(b) EXCEPTIONS.—This section does not apply to—
    (1) unlocking a personal device owned by the user;
    (2) verifying the identity of a person who has
        voluntarily presented themselves for identification
        (e.g., airport check-in with consent).

(c) During the moratorium period, Congress shall develop
    comprehensive facial recognition regulation addressing—
    (1) accuracy and bias standards;
    (2) warrant requirements for law enforcement use;
    (3) consent requirements;
    (4) data retention limits;
    (5) accountability for misidentification.

SEC. 602. STATE AND LOCAL INCENTIVES.

(a) Federal law enforcement grants shall be conditioned
    on compliance with the moratorium established in
    Section 601 or adoption of equivalent state or local
    moratoriums.

TITLE VII—EO 12333 STATUTORY FRAMEWORK

SEC. 701. STATUTORY OVERSIGHT OF EXECUTIVE ORDER 12333
COLLECTION.

(a) Any foreign intelligence collection program conducted
    under Executive Order 12333 or any successor
    presidential directive that incidentally collects
    US person data shall be—
    (1) reported to the congressional intelligence
        committees within 30 days of initiation;
    (2) subject to minimization procedures approved by
        the FISC; and
    (3) reviewed by the Privacy and Civil Liberties
        Oversight Board within 2 years.

(b) US person data collected under EO 12333 shall not be
    queried using US person identifiers without a warrant
    issued by the FISC under the same standards applicable
    to Section 702 queries under Title I of this Act.

(c) The Director of National Intelligence shall include
    statistics on EO 12333 collection involving US person
    data in the quarterly transparency reports required
    under Section 402.

TITLE VIII—ENFORCEMENT AND EFFECTIVE DATE

SEC. 801. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) SUPPRESSION.—Evidence obtained in violation of this Act
    shall be suppressed in any criminal, civil,
    administrative, or immigration proceeding.

(b) CIVIL LIABILITY.—Any person whose rights under this Act
    are violated may bring a civil action for—
    (1) actual damages or statutory damages of $10,000
        per violation;
    (2) injunctive relief;
    (3) reasonable attorney's fees and costs.

(c) GOVERNMENT LIABILITY.—The United States waives sovereign
    immunity for claims arising under this Act.

(d) INSPECTOR GENERAL INVESTIGATION.—Any Inspector General
    with jurisdiction over a government agency that
    violates this Act shall investigate and report findings
    to Congress within 180 days.

SEC. 802. EFFECTIVE DATE.

(a) This Act shall take effect 180 days after the date of
    enactment, except—
    (1) Title I (Section 702 reform) shall take effect
        upon the next reauthorization of Section 702;
    (2) Title VI (facial recognition moratorium) shall
        take effect 90 days after enactment;
    (3) Title VII (EO 12333 framework) shall take effect
        one year after enactment.

SEC. 803. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

There are authorized to be appropriated—
(a) $30,000,000 annually for the FISC special advocate
    program;
(b) $20,000,000 annually for the Independent Whistleblower
    Ombudsman;
(c) $50,000,000 annually for enhanced congressional
    oversight staffing;
(d) $30,000,000 annually for enhanced PCLOB capacity;
(e) such sums as may be necessary for other provisions
    of this Act.
```

**Explanation**:

- Title I closes the "backdoor search" loophole by requiring warrants for US person queries of Section 702 data, with narrow exceptions for emergencies, victim identification, and consent
- Title II prohibits government data purchases without warrants, closing the commercial data end-run around the Fourth Amendment
- Title III transforms the FISC from a rubber stamp into a genuine court with adversarial proceedings, published opinions, and enforcement powers
- Title IV strengthens congressional oversight with enhanced staffing, access, and transparency requirements
- Title V provides meaningful whistleblower protections, including access to courts and an Espionage Act public interest defense
- Title VI imposes a moratorium on government facial recognition pending regulation
- Title VII brings EO 12333 collection within a statutory framework for the first time
- Title VIII provides enforcement through suppression, civil liability, and sovereign immunity waiver

**Potential Challenges**:

- Intelligence community will mount intense opposition, including classified threat briefings to members of Congress
- FBI will argue warrant requirements for Section 702 queries are impractical
- FISC reform may face resistance from the judiciary (Chief Justice appointment power)
- Espionage Act reform faces strong DOJ opposition
- Facial recognition moratorium faces law enforcement opposition
- EO 12333 reform is the most ambitious provision and may face constitutional objections about presidential foreign affairs authority

**Refinements**:

- Consider phased implementation of warrant requirements with compliance reporting
- Develop detailed standards for FISC special advocate qualifications and procedures
- Coordinate with tech company transparency report frameworks
- Address classified information handling in civil actions under Section 801(b)
- Develop specific minimization procedures for EO 12333 data

## State Model Legislation

### Model State Government Surveillance Oversight Act

**Purpose**: Establish state-level requirements for transparency, community approval, and oversight of surveillance technology acquisition and use by state and local law enforcement.

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Government
Surveillance Oversight Act".

SECTION 2. DEFINITIONS.

(a) "Surveillance technology" means any electronic device,
    system, or software used or designed to collect, retain,
    process, or share audio, visual, location, biometric,
    thermal, or similar information specifically associated
    with, or capable of being associated with, any
    individual or group, including but not limited to:
    (1) cell-site simulators (Stingray/IMSI catchers);
    (2) automated license plate readers (ALPRs);
    (3) facial recognition systems;
    (4) social media monitoring tools;
    (5) predictive policing or algorithmic surveillance
        systems;
    (6) drones equipped with surveillance capabilities;
    (7) gunshot detection systems;
    (8) gait analysis or other biometric identification
        systems;
    (9) geofence or reverse keyword data requests; and
    (10) any device or system that intercepts electronic
         communications.

(b) "Law enforcement agency" means any state, county,
    municipal, or other local agency that employs peace
    officers or is authorized to enforce criminal laws.

SECTION 3. PRE-ACQUISITION APPROVAL.

(a) No law enforcement agency shall acquire, borrow, or
    accept as a gift any surveillance technology without
    prior approval by the governing body of the
    jurisdiction (city council, county commission, or
    equivalent).

(b) Before approval, the law enforcement agency shall
    submit to the governing body—
    (1) a description of the technology and its
        capabilities;
    (2) the purpose and intended use;
    (3) a privacy impact assessment;
    (4) a proposed use policy governing operation,
        data retention, sharing, and deletion;
    (5) the cost, including ongoing maintenance and
        data storage;
    (6) an analysis of the technology's impact on
        civil rights and civil liberties; and
    (7) any nondisclosure agreements or restrictions
        imposed by the vendor or federal government.

(c) The governing body shall hold at least one public
    hearing before voting on acquisition.

SECTION 4. USE POLICIES.

(a) Each law enforcement agency deploying surveillance
    technology shall adopt a written use policy that—
    (1) limits use to specific authorized purposes;
    (2) establishes data retention limits;
    (3) restricts data sharing with other agencies;
    (4) prohibits use for monitoring constitutionally
        protected activity;
    (5) requires individual warrants for targeted
        surveillance of identified individuals; and
    (6) provides for regular auditing of compliance.

SECTION 5. ANNUAL REPORTING.

(a) Each law enforcement agency operating surveillance
    technology shall submit an annual public report
    including—
    (1) the type and quantity of surveillance technology
        in use;
    (2) the frequency and purpose of use;
    (3) the number of individuals whose data was
        collected;
    (4) data retention and deletion statistics;
    (5) data sharing with other agencies;
    (6) costs; and
    (7) any policy violations and their resolution.

SECTION 6. PROHIBITED TECHNOLOGIES.

(a) No law enforcement agency in this state shall use
    facial recognition technology for real-time
    surveillance or identification in public spaces.

(b) No law enforcement agency shall use predictive
    policing algorithms that assign risk scores to
    individual persons.

SECTION 7. COMMERCIAL DATA PURCHASES.

(a) No state or local government agency may purchase
    covered data from a data broker without a warrant
    issued by a court of competent jurisdiction.

(b) "Covered data" has the same meaning as defined in
    Section 2 of the federal Surveillance Reform and
    Fourth Amendment Restoration Act, or if such Act
    has not been enacted, means any information relating
    to an identified or identifiable individual including
    location data, communications metadata, browsing
    history, financial records, and biometric data.

SECTION 8. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) Data collected in violation of this Act is
    inadmissible in any state court proceeding.

(b) Any individual whose rights under this Act are
    violated may bring a civil action for actual
    damages, statutory damages of $5,000 per violation,
    injunctive relief, and reasonable attorney's fees.

(c) The state Attorney General may bring enforcement
    actions against non-compliant agencies.

SECTION 9. NONDISCLOSURE AGREEMENTS.

(a) No law enforcement agency may enter into a
    nondisclosure agreement with any vendor or federal
    agency that would prevent the agency from disclosing
    to a court, the governing body, or the public the
    existence, capabilities, or use of surveillance
    technology.

(b) Any such agreement entered into prior to the
    effective date of this Act is void and unenforceable.

SECTION 10. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect on [date 12 months after enactment].
```

## Regulatory Framework

### ODNI Directive on Government Data Purchases

| Element | Proposed Directive |
|---------|-------------------|
| **Scope** | All intelligence community agencies purchasing commercial data |
| **Warrant requirement** | Warrant or FISC order required before purchase of covered data |
| **Inventory** | Complete inventory of existing commercial data contracts within 180 days |
| **Existing data** | Audit and deletion of data previously purchased without warrants within 2 years |
| **Reporting** | Quarterly reports to PCLOB and congressional intelligence committees |
| **Compliance** | ODNI compliance officer with authority to suspend contracts |

### DOJ Guidelines on Section 702 Query Procedures

| Element | Proposed Guideline |
|---------|-------------------|
| **Warrant requirement** | All US person queries require FISC warrant (implementing Title I) |
| **Documentation** | Every query documented with justification, legal basis, and results |
| **Training** | Annual training for all personnel with query access |
| **Auditing** | Random audit of 10% of queries monthly by DOJ NSD |
| **Violations** | Personnel who conduct unauthorized queries subject to suspension, clearance review, and potential criminal referral |
| **Reporting** | Monthly internal reports; quarterly public reports |

### DHS Policy on Surveillance Technology

| Element | Proposed Policy |
|---------|----------------|
| **Privacy impact assessments** | Required before deployment of any surveillance technology |
| **Facial recognition** | Moratorium on use pending federal regulation |
| **Social media monitoring** | Limited to specific, documented investigations; no bulk monitoring of public speech |
| **Data retention** | Maximum 180-day retention for non-target data; 5-year maximum for target data |
| **Sharing** | Data sharing with state/local agencies requires documented legal basis |

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

| Issue | Analysis |
|-------|----------|
| **Fourth Amendment** | Warrant requirements for US person queries and data purchases implement *Carpenter* principles; strong constitutional basis |
| **Article II** | Executive may claim inherent foreign affairs authority over EO 12333 collection; statutory framework under Title VII must be designed to survive challenge |
| **First Amendment** | Surveillance of political activity, religious practice, and press communications raises First Amendment concerns; warrant requirements provide necessary protection |
| **Due Process** | FISC reform with adversarial proceedings strengthens due process; suppression remedy ensures enforcement |
| **Separation of Powers** | Congressional oversight reforms must balance legislative prerogative with executive classification authority |
| **Supremacy Clause** | State model legislation must avoid conflict with federal surveillance authorities; floor-not-ceiling approach |

### Preemption

| Dynamic | Analysis |
|---------|----------|
| **Federal-state relationship** | Federal legislation should set minimum protections; state laws providing greater protection should not be preempted |
| **FISA preemption** | FISA has been interpreted to occupy the field of foreign intelligence surveillance; state legislation should focus on state/local surveillance and data purchases |
| **State sovereignty** | State model legislation governs state and local agencies, which FISA does not directly address |
| **Nondisclosure agreements** | Federal NDAs (e.g., Stingray) currently prevent state and local transparency; model legislation voids these agreements |

### Enforcement Mechanisms

| Mechanism | Strengths | Weaknesses |
|-----------|-----------|------------|
| **Suppression remedy** | Directly impacts government incentive to comply | Requires individual cases; does not prevent collection |
| **Civil liability** | Individual enforcement; damages deter violations | Standing barriers; sovereign immunity complications; litigation cost |
| **Inspector General investigation** | Institutional capacity; classified access | IGs report to agency heads; independence concerns |
| **Congressional oversight** | Constitutional authority; public accountability | Classification constraints; partisan dynamics |
| **FISC enforcement** | Judicial authority; classified access | Ex parte proceedings (addressed by adversarial reform); limited public visibility |
| **Criminal penalties** | Strongest deterrent | Politically difficult to prosecute government officials; DOJ conflict of interest |

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| **Foreign partner collection** | Intelligence community routes collection through Five Eyes partners to avoid US legal restrictions | High |
| **Definitional evasion for data purchases** | Agencies characterize data acquisition as "publicly available information" or "open-source intelligence" rather than "data purchase" | High |
| **Emergency exception abuse** | Agencies invoke emergency provisions routinely to circumvent warrant requirements; retroactive approval becomes formality | High |
| **Contractor intermediaries** | Government uses private contractors to acquire data or conduct surveillance that would be prohibited if done directly | Medium |
| **Aggregate data exception** | Agencies claim purchased data is "aggregate" or "anonymized" when it can be readily re-identified | Medium |
| **Classification of violations** | Agencies classify evidence of their own violations, preventing oversight and public accountability | Medium |
| **FISC special advocate limitations** | Special advocates appointed from too small a pool, develop institutional sympathy, or lack resources to effectively challenge government | Medium |
| **Parallel construction** | Agencies use surveillance-derived information to initiate investigations but conceal the surveillance source, avoiding judicial review | Medium |
| **State agency data sharing** | Federal agencies share data with state agencies to circumvent federal restrictions, or vice versa | Low-Medium |

### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| **Technology outpaces legislation** | New surveillance methods (AI analysis, connected vehicle data, satellite imagery) may not fit existing legal categories | Legislation cannot anticipate all future technologies |
| **Classification barrier to enforcement** | Courts cannot evaluate suppression motions when evidence is classified; standing remains difficult to establish | Structural tension between secrecy and judicial review |
| **International collection** | US surveillance of foreign persons abroad remains largely unrestricted; affects US persons who communicate internationally | Legislation focused on US person protections; foreign persons have fewer rights |
| **Resource asymmetry** | Intelligence community's $100 billion budget dwarfs oversight resources | Structural imbalance between surveillance and oversight |
| **Political will decay** | Post-crisis political energy fades; surveillance establishment outlasts reform movements | Ratchet effect; institutional permanence |
| **Private sector pipeline** | Even with data purchase restrictions, commercial surveillance creates data that exists and could be compelled through legal process | Comprehensive solution requires commercial privacy legislation |

### Rectification Procedures

1. **Anti-circumvention through foreign partners**: Prohibit the use of information obtained by foreign intelligence partners if the collection would have violated US law had it been conducted by a US agency; require annual reporting on intelligence sharing with foreign partners that involves US person data

2. **Broad functional definitions**: Define "data purchase" and "data acquisition" based on the functional effect (government obtaining personal data from commercial sources), not the transactional form; include barter, exchange, free access, and indirect acquisition through intermediaries

3. **Emergency exception safeguards**: Require after-action review by PCLOB of all emergency exceptions; impose declining caps on emergency exceptions (if exceeding 50 per quarter, agency must justify to congressional committees); automatic suppression if retroactive warrant is denied

4. **Contractor oversight**: Extend all restrictions and protections to contractors, subcontractors, and any entity acting on behalf of or at the direction of a government agency; contractor violations subject to contract termination and debarment

5. **Re-identification prohibition**: Treat any data that can be re-identified to a specific individual as covered data regardless of how it is labeled; impose penalties for re-identification of purportedly anonymized data

6. **Declassification of violations**: Require automatic declassification of FISC compliance findings, IG reports on surveillance violations, and any evidence of unconstitutional surveillance within 2 years, with narrow exceptions for ongoing operations

7. **Parallel construction prohibition**: Require government to disclose the initial source of any investigation in which surveillance-derived information played a role; suppression remedy for parallel construction

8. **Technology-neutral drafting**: Define surveillance authorities and restrictions by function and effect rather than specific technology; include catch-all provisions covering "any technology that has the functional effect of collecting, monitoring, or tracking personal data"

### General Implementation Concerns

- **Transition period**: Intelligence community will need time to implement warrant processes; phased implementation with hard deadlines prevents indefinite delay
- **FISC capacity**: Mandatory adversarial proceedings will increase FISC workload; adequate appropriations for special advocate program essential
- **Whistleblower culture change**: Enhanced protections are meaningless without cultural change within IC; ombudsman and congressional oversight must actively demonstrate protection works
- **State-federal coordination**: Federal and state surveillance reforms must be complementary; federal floor should not undermine state efforts to go further
- **Contractor compliance**: Private contractors handling government surveillance data must be subject to same restrictions; contract provisions and debarment authority needed
- **Public education**: Reforms require public understanding and support; transparency provisions and public reporting are essential for sustained accountability
