# Government Surveillance: History

## Overview

American government surveillance has a long and troubled history, marked by recurring cycles of expansion during perceived crises, exposure of abuse, reform, and gradual re-expansion. Each cycle has produced surveillance capabilities more powerful than the last, while reforms have consistently proven inadequate to prevent future abuse. Understanding this pattern is essential for designing reforms that break the cycle.

---

## Era 1: Early Surveillance and the Cold War (1919-1967)

### The Palmer Raids and Early Domestic Surveillance

Government surveillance of American citizens began in earnest during the First Red Scare. Attorney General A. Mitchell Palmer authorized raids and mass surveillance of suspected communists, anarchists, and labor organizers in 1919-1920. J. Edgar Hoover, who organized the raids as a young Justice Department official, would go on to build the FBI into a domestic surveillance powerhouse.

### The FBI Under Hoover (1924-1972)

J. Edgar Hoover directed the FBI for 48 years, building an extensive domestic surveillance apparatus:

- **1930s-1940s**: FBI compiled dossiers on suspected communists, fascists, and political dissidents
- **1940s**: FBI monitored Japanese Americans during internment; surveillance of civil rights organizations began
- **1947**: Truman administration created the CIA and NSA, establishing the foreign intelligence infrastructure that exists today
- **1950s**: McCarthyism fueled FBI surveillance of suspected communists in government, academia, media, and entertainment
- **1956-1971**: **COINTELPRO** -- FBI's Counterintelligence Program conducted covert operations against civil rights leaders, Black activists, anti-war protesters, and political dissidents

### COINTELPRO: The Definitive Surveillance Abuse

| Target | Operations | Documentation |
|--------|-----------|---------------|
| Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. | Wiretapping, blackmail, letter urging suicide | Senate Church Committee findings (1975) |
| Black Panther Party | Infiltration, disinformation, instigation of violence | FBI files; Church Committee |
| Anti-Vietnam War movement | Infiltration, disruption, targeting of leaders | Church Committee |
| Women's liberation movement | Monitoring, infiltration | FBI files released under FOIA |
| Socialist Workers Party | Decades of surveillance and infiltration | FBI files; *Alliance to End Repression v. City of Chicago* |
| Native American organizations | Surveillance, infiltration (AIM) | FBI files; Senate investigations |

### Key Developments

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 1919-1920 | Palmer Raids | First mass domestic surveillance program |
| 1924 | Hoover appointed FBI director | Beginning of 48-year domestic surveillance empire |
| 1947 | National Security Act | Created CIA, NSC; foundation of intelligence community |
| 1952 | NSA established by Truman directive | Signals intelligence agency; existence classified for years |
| 1956 | COINTELPRO launched | Systematic FBI campaign against domestic dissent |
| 1967 | NSA begins Project MINARET | Warrantless surveillance of Americans' international communications |

## Era 2: Exposure, Reform, and the Church Committee (1967-1978)

### The Revelations

A series of exposures in the early 1970s revealed the scope of government surveillance abuse:

- **1971**: Activists burglarized FBI office in Media, PA, stealing COINTELPRO files that were leaked to journalists
- **1971**: Pentagon Papers revealed systematic government deception (and surveillance of Daniel Ellsberg)
- **1972**: Watergate break-in and subsequent investigation exposed Nixon administration surveillance of political opponents
- **1974**: Seymour Hersh reported in the *New York Times* on CIA domestic surveillance programs (Operation CHAOS, Project MINARET)

### The Church Committee (1975-1976)

The Senate Select Committee to Study Governmental Operations with Respect to Intelligence Activities, chaired by Senator Frank Church, conducted the most comprehensive investigation of intelligence community abuses in American history:

**Key findings**:

- FBI's COINTELPRO was a systematic campaign of political repression
- NSA conducted warrantless surveillance of Americans' international communications
- CIA operated domestic surveillance programs violating its charter
- Intelligence agencies operated with virtually no oversight
- The "National Security" justification was routinely used to cover illegal activity

**Church Committee legacy**:

- Led directly to the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act (FISA, 1978)
- Created the Senate Select Committee on Intelligence (SSCI, 1976)
- Created the House Permanent Select Committee on Intelligence (HPSCI, 1977)
- Led to Executive Order 12333 (1981) governing intelligence activities
- Established the principle that intelligence agencies require external oversight

### The Post-Church Reforms

| Year | Reform | Purpose | Effectiveness |
|------|--------|---------|---------------|
| 1976 | SSCI established | Congressional oversight of intelligence | Mixed -- oversight often deferential |
| 1977 | HPSCI established | House counterpart to SSCI | Mixed -- increasingly partisan |
| 1978 | FISA enacted | Judicial oversight of foreign intelligence surveillance | Created FISC; but court became rubber stamp |
| 1978 | FISA Court (FISC) created | Judicial review of surveillance applications | 99.97% approval rate |
| 1980 | FISA pen register provisions | Metadata collection oversight | Lower standard than full warrants |
| 1981 | Executive Order 12333 | Comprehensive intelligence community governance | Self-regulation with limited accountability |

## Era 3: Pre-9/11 Erosion and Post-9/11 Expansion (1978-2008)

### Gradual Erosion (1978-2001)

The Church Committee reforms held relatively well through the 1980s and 1990s, but several developments began to erode privacy protections:

| Year | Development | Impact |
|------|-------------|--------|
| 1986 | Electronic Communications Privacy Act (ECPA) | Addressed new technologies but with weaker protections than Fourth Amendment would require for physical surveillance |
| 1994 | CALEA | Required telecommunications carriers to build wiretapping capabilities into their systems |
| 1996 | FISA pen register authority expanded | Broadened metadata collection |
| 2000 | Carnivore/DCS1000 | FBI internet surveillance system deployed at ISPs |

### The Post-9/11 Transformation

The September 11, 2001 attacks fundamentally transformed American surveillance law and practice:

| Year | Action | Impact |
|------|--------|--------|
| 2001 | USA PATRIOT Act | Massively expanded surveillance authorities: "roving" wiretaps, business records (Section 215), information sharing, reduced standards |
| 2001 | President's Surveillance Program (PSP) / "Stellar Wind" | Bush administration authorized warrantless wiretapping of Americans' international communications, bypassing FISA |
| 2002 | Total Information Awareness (TIA) | DARPA proposal to mine commercial data for terrorist patterns; defunded after backlash but concepts continued |
| 2003 | Department of Homeland Security created | New federal department integrating domestic security and intelligence |
| 2004 | Intelligence Reform and Terrorism Prevention Act | Created Director of National Intelligence (DNI); reorganized intelligence community |
| 2005 | *New York Times* reveals warrantless wiretapping program | Bush administration's secret surveillance of Americans exposed |
| 2006 | *USA Today* reveals NSA bulk telephone metadata program | NSA collecting call records of millions of Americans from phone companies |
| 2007 | Protect America Act | Temporary FISA amendment authorizing warrantless foreign intelligence surveillance |
| 2008 | FISA Amendments Act (Section 702 enacted) | Legalized and expanded warrantless collection targeting non-US persons abroad; provided retroactive immunity to telecom companies that participated in PSP |

### The Warrantless Wiretapping Scandal

In December 2005, the *New York Times* revealed that President Bush had authorized the NSA to intercept international communications of Americans without FISA court approval:

- **Program**: President's Surveillance Program (PSP), also known as "Stellar Wind"
- **Authorization**: Presidential directive, bypassing FISA
- **Scope**: Thousands of Americans' international calls and emails intercepted
- **Legal basis**: Administration claimed inherent executive authority and AUMF authorization
- **Outcome**: Rather than prosecuting the illegal surveillance, Congress retroactively legalized similar authorities through the FISA Amendments Act (2008) and immunized participating telecom companies

## Era 4: The Snowden Era and Its Aftermath (2013-2020)

### The Snowden Revelations (2013)

Edward Snowden, an NSA contractor, disclosed thousands of classified documents to journalists Glenn Greenwald, Laura Poitras, and Barton Gellman, revealing the full scope of post-9/11 surveillance:

| Program | Description | Impact |
|---------|-------------|--------|
| **PRISM** | Collection of stored communications from nine major tech companies | Revealed scope of Section 702 collection |
| **Upstream** | Interception of communications on internet backbone cables | Revealed bulk internet surveillance |
| **Bulk telephony metadata** | NSA collecting all Americans' phone records under Section 215 | Led directly to USA FREEDOM Act |
| **XKEYSCORE** | NSA tool for searching vast databases of collected communications | Demonstrated analyst access to collected data |
| **MUSCULAR** | NSA/GCHQ tapping Google and Yahoo internal data links | Collection outside legal frameworks |
| **BOUNDLESS INFORMANT** | NSA tool counting metadata records collected worldwide | 97 billion metadata records in one 30-day period (March 2013) |
| **STELLARWIND** | Full scope of Bush-era warrantless surveillance | Confirmed prior reporting |

### Post-Snowden Reforms

| Year | Reform | Impact |
|------|--------|--------|
| 2014 | Presidential Policy Directive 28 (PPD-28) | Extended some privacy protections to non-US persons |
| 2014 | PCLOB Section 702 report | Detailed analysis with reform recommendations |
| 2015 | **USA FREEDOM Act** | Ended bulk telephony metadata collection; created FISC amicus; required declassification of significant FISC opinions |
| 2015 | Judicial Redress Act | Extended Privacy Act rights to citizens of allied nations |
| 2018 | FISA Amendments Reauthorization Act | Section 702 reauthorized for 6 years; modest FBI query reforms |

### Snowden's Legacy

The Snowden disclosures had profound but mixed effects:

- **Public awareness**: Mass surveillance became a mainstream political issue for the first time since the 1970s
- **Legislative reform**: USA FREEDOM Act ended one program but left Section 702 and EO 12333 intact
- **Corporate response**: Tech companies strengthened encryption and challenged government access
- **International impact**: EU-US data transfer frameworks disrupted (Safe Harbor invalidated, Privacy Shield challenged)
- **Whistleblower treatment**: Snowden charged under Espionage Act; fled to Russia; remains in exile
- **Durability**: Many revealed programs continued in modified form

## Era 5: Commercial Data, AI, and Modern Surveillance (2020-Present)

### The Data Purchase Era

Beginning in 2020, a series of investigations revealed that government agencies had found a new way to circumvent surveillance law: purchasing commercial data.

| Year | Revelation | Source |
|------|-----------|--------|
| 2020 | CBP/ICE purchasing location data from Venntel | Sen. Wyden; *WSJ* |
| 2020 | IRS purchasing location data | *WSJ*; IRS IG |
| 2021 | DIA acknowledging commercial data purchases | *NYT* |
| 2022 | Georgetown "American Dragnet" comprehensive investigation | FOIA-based research |
| 2023 | ODNI declassified report on commercially available information | ODNI Senior Advisory Group |
| 2024 | NSA documented purchasing advertising data | *WSJ* |
| 2024 | EO 14117 (restricted bulk transfers to adversaries, not domestic purchases) | White House |

### Section 702 Reauthorization (2024)

Section 702 was reauthorized in April 2024 after months of contentious debate:

- **Warren-Wyden amendment requiring warrants for US person queries**: Failed narrowly
- **Expanded definition of "electronic communications service provider"**: Passed (raised concerns about compelling more entities to assist with surveillance)
- **New compliance requirements for FBI**: Imposed additional procedures for US person queries
- **Two-year extension**: Shorter than previous reauthorizations; next expiration April 2026

### Emerging Surveillance Technologies

| Technology | Government Use | Oversight |
|-----------|---------------|-----------|
| **Artificial intelligence** | Automated analysis of surveillance data; pattern recognition | Minimal; no specific AI surveillance regulations |
| **Facial recognition** | FBI, ICE, CBP, state/local law enforcement | GAO reports; some local bans; no federal regulation |
| **Social media monitoring** | DHS visa vetting; FBI investigations; local police | DHS privacy impact assessments; limited restrictions |
| **Connected vehicle data** | CBP, ICE accessing vehicle telematics | No specific regulations |
| **Drone surveillance** | CBP border surveillance; growing local police use | FAA regulations; limited privacy rules |
| **Generative AI for analysis** | Intelligence community exploring AI tools for data analysis | Under development; oversight frameworks nascent |

## Key Turning Points

| Date | Event | Why It Mattered |
|------|-------|-----------------|
| 1956 | COINTELPRO launched | Systematic domestic political surveillance by FBI |
| 1975-76 | Church Committee investigations | Exposed surveillance abuses; led to FISA and oversight reforms |
| 1978 | FISA enacted | First statutory framework for foreign intelligence surveillance |
| 2001 | PATRIOT Act; warrantless wiretapping begins | Dramatic post-9/11 surveillance expansion |
| 2005 | Warrantless wiretapping exposed | Public learned of illegal NSA program; but Congress legalized it |
| 2008 | FISA Amendments Act (Section 702) | Legalized warrantless foreign-targeted collection with incidental US collection |
| 2013 | Snowden disclosures | Full scope of mass surveillance revealed; sparked global debate |
| 2015 | USA FREEDOM Act | Ended bulk metadata collection; but left most surveillance intact |
| 2018 | *Carpenter v. United States* | Supreme Court recognized Fourth Amendment protection for digital data |
| 2020-present | Government data purchase revelations | Exposed new constitutional end-run via commercial data |
| 2024 | Section 702 reauthorized (RISAA) | Surveillance authorities extended; warrant requirement narrowly defeated |

## Lessons from History

1. **Surveillance expands during crises and rarely contracts afterward**: Post-WWI, Cold War, post-9/11 surveillance expansions have never been fully reversed
2. **Abuse is the norm, not the exception**: Every major surveillance authority has been misused -- COINTELPRO, warrantless wiretapping, bulk metadata, Section 702 query violations
3. **Reforms consistently prove inadequate**: FISA, the Church Committee reforms, the USA FREEDOM Act -- each was intended to prevent future abuse, and each has been circumvented
4. **The intelligence community resists oversight**: Classification, compartmentalization, and institutional resistance repeatedly frustrate congressional and judicial oversight
5. **Technology always outpaces law**: From telegraph wiretapping to internet surveillance to data purchases, legal frameworks have never kept pace with surveillance capabilities
6. **Whistleblowers are essential but punished**: From Daniel Ellsberg to Edward Snowden, those who expose illegal surveillance face prosecution rather than protection
7. **Legalization of illegal programs is a recurring pattern**: When illegal surveillance is exposed, the political response is often to legalize it rather than prosecute those responsible (PSP to FISA Amendments Act)
8. **Commercial data has become the new frontier**: Government agencies have discovered that purchasing data from brokers avoids the legal constraints that apply to direct collection
