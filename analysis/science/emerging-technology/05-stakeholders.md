# Emerging Technology Research: Stakeholders

## Overview

Emerging technology research governance involves a complex ecosystem of federal agencies, private companies, academic institutions, international partners, and civil society organizations. Understanding their interests, power, and relationships is essential to designing effective reform.

## Federal Government

### Executive Branch Agencies

| Agency | Role | Budget (Approx.) | Key Interest |
|--------|------|-------------------|--------------|
| DARPA | High-risk defense research | $4.1B/year | Mission autonomy, breakthrough capabilities |
| NSF | Fundamental research funding | $9.9B/year | Broad science support, academic partnerships |
| NIH | Biomedical research | $47.3B/year | Health breakthroughs, institutional preeminence |
| DOE | Energy and national security research | $8.8B (science) | National lab system, computing leadership |
| NIST | Standards and measurement | $1.6B/year | Technical standards, metrology |
| ARPA-H | Health research breakthroughs | $2.5B/year | Institutional establishment, impact demonstration |
| Commerce/CHIPS | Semiconductor manufacturing | $52.7B (total) | Industrial policy implementation |
| OSTP | Science policy coordination | ~$6M/year | Cross-agency coordination, presidential advice |

**Power**: High--controls funding, sets priorities, issues regulations
**Interest**: Maintaining and expanding budgets, protecting institutional autonomy, demonstrating relevance

### Congress

**Key committees:**

- Senate Commerce, Science, and Transportation
- Senate Armed Services (defense research)
- Senate Appropriations Subcommittee on Commerce, Justice, Science
- House Science, Space, and Technology
- House Armed Services
- House Appropriations Subcommittee on Commerce, Justice, Science

**Power**: Authorizes and appropriates funding, conducts oversight, shapes policy through legislation
**Interest**: Directing research investment to constituents, demonstrating national security seriousness, responding to public concerns about technology

### Intelligence Community

- NSA involvement in quantum computing and post-quantum cryptography
- Intelligence Advanced Research Projects Activity (IARPA)
- Classified research programs with limited public accountability
- Interest in maintaining information advantage while managing disclosure

### Large Technology Companies

| Company | Key Research Areas | Estimated R&D Spend |
|---------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Google/Alphabet | AI, quantum computing, biotech | $45B+ (2024) |
| Microsoft | AI, quantum computing, cloud | $30B+ (2024) |

| Apple | AI, semiconductors, health tech | $30B+ (2024) |

| Intel | Semiconductors, AI accelerators | $16B+ (2024) |

**Power**: Control compute infrastructure, attract top talent, shape standards through market dominance
**Interest**: Minimizing regulation, protecting IP, influencing government procurement, maintaining talent access

### AI-Focused Companies

- **OpenAI** - Leading LLM developer; complex nonprofit-to-profit transition
- **Anthropic** - AI safety-focused lab; advocates for responsible development
- **xAI** - Elon Musk's AI venture
- **Cohere, Mistral, Inflection** - Competing foundation model developers

**Power**: Shape AI capabilities and safety norms through development choices
**Interest**: Regulatory environment that favors their approach, compute access, talent

### Semiconductor Companies

- **NVIDIA** - Dominant in AI accelerator chips; $52B revenue in FY2025
- **TSMC** - World's largest contract chipmaker; building US fabs under CHIPS Act
- **Samsung** - Memory and logic chips; US manufacturing expansion
- **Qualcomm, AMD, Broadcom** - Specialized chip design

**Power**: Control critical supply chains, major CHIPS Act recipients
**Interest**: CHIPS Act funding, export control influence, workforce development

### Biotech and Pharma

- **Illumina** - Dominant in gene sequencing
- **Moderna, BioNTech** - mRNA technology platforms
- **Ginkgo Bioworks** - Synthetic biology platform
- **Major pharma** (Pfizer, J&J, Merck) - Drug development leveraging emerging tech

**Power**: Commercialize biotech discoveries, control key platforms
**Interest**: Patent protection, regulatory predictability, federal research partnerships

### Defense Industry

- **Lockheed Martin, Raytheon, Northrop Grumman, Boeing** - Traditional defense primes with growing tech divisions
- **Anduril, Palantir, Shield AI** - New entrants bringing Silicon Valley approaches to defense
- **Venture capital** - Defense-focused funds (Lux Capital, a16z defense)

**Power**: Major government contractors, influence defense R&D priorities
**Interest**: Defense research contracts, technology transition programs, export policy

## Academic and Research Institutions

### Research Universities

**Top recipients of federal research funding:**

- Johns Hopkins, University of Michigan, University of Washington, UC San Diego, Stanford
- MIT, Caltech, Carnegie Mellon - Especially in AI and quantum
- Historically Black Colleges and Universities (HBCUs) and Minority-Serving Institutions (MSIs) - Significantly underfunded

**Power**: Produce researchers, generate discoveries, influence policy through expertise
**Interest**: Research funding levels, overhead rates, talent pipelines, academic freedom, technology transfer policies

### Professional Societies

- American Association for the Advancement of Science (AAAS)
- Association of American Universities (AAU)
- IEEE, ACM (computing and engineering)
- American Society for Microbiology
- National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine

**Power**: Shape norms, convene expert consensus, advise policymakers
**Interest**: Research funding, scientific integrity, governance frameworks that preserve scientific autonomy

### National Laboratories

The 17 DOE national laboratories and other federally funded research and development centers (FFRDCs):

- **Unique assets**: Exascale computers, synchrotron light sources, user facilities
- **Governance**: Managed by universities or private entities under contract
- **Challenges**: Bureaucratic technology transfer, aging infrastructure, recruitment competition

**Power**: Possess unique research infrastructure and expertise
**Interest**: Sustained funding, infrastructure modernization, streamlined technology transfer

## Civil Society and Advocacy

### AI Safety and Governance Organizations

- **Center for AI Safety (CAIS)** - Catastrophic risk research
- **Partnership on AI** - Multi-stakeholder AI governance
- **AI Now Institute** - Social impact of AI
- **Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF)** - Digital rights and surveillance concerns
- **Center for Security and Emerging Technology (CSET)** at Georgetown

**Power**: Shape public discourse, provide policy expertise, influence legislation
**Interest**: Responsible AI development, transparency, public accountability

### Biosecurity Organizations

- **Johns Hopkins Center for Health Security** - Pandemic preparedness
- **Nuclear Threat Initiative (NTI) Biosecurity** - Biological risk reduction
- **Bipartisan Commission on Biodefense** - Policy recommendations

**Power**: Expert analysis, congressional testimony, media influence
**Interest**: Strengthened biosafety governance, dual-use oversight, pandemic preparedness

### Science Policy Organizations

- **Federation of American Scientists (FAS)** - Science-informed policy
- **Information Technology and Innovation Foundation (ITIF)** - Innovation economics
- **R Street Institute** - Free-market science policy
- **Brookings Institution** - Technology governance research

**Power**: Policy analysis, media influence, bipartisan credibility (varying)
**Interest**: Evidence-based policy, specific policy positions aligned with organizational mission

## International Stakeholders

### Strategic Competitors

- **China** - Largest rival in AI, quantum, biotech, semiconductors; centrally coordinated research strategy
- **Russia** - Advanced in some military technology domains; cyber capabilities

### Allies and Partners

- **European Union** - Regulatory leadership (AI Act, GDPR), Horizon Europe research
- **United Kingdom** - ARIA (UK ARPA equivalent), AI Safety Institute
- **Japan** - Semiconductor cooperation, quantum research
- **South Korea** - Samsung and SK Hynix semiconductor investment
- **Australia** - AUKUS Pillar II technology cooperation
- **Taiwan** - TSMC and semiconductor ecosystem; geopolitical vulnerability
- **Israel** - Defense technology innovation, AI research
- **Canada** - AI research excellence (Bengio, Hinton), quantum computing (D-Wave)

### International Organizations

- OECD (AI principles, science policy)
- United Nations (bioweapons convention, emerging technology discussions)
- World Health Organization (biosafety standards)
- International Standards Organization (technology standards)

## Affected Populations

### Workers in Disrupted Industries

- Manufacturing workers affected by automation
- Knowledge workers facing AI-driven displacement
- Gig economy workers subject to algorithmic management
- Workers in communities chosen for or bypassed by tech hub investment

**Power**: Low individually; potentially high through organized labor
**Interest**: Job preservation, transition support, benefit-sharing from automation

### Communities Near Research Facilities

- Biosafety concerns from proximity to BSL-3 and BSL-4 laboratories
- Environmental concerns from semiconductor manufacturing (water, chemicals)
- Economic impacts (positive and negative) of research facility siting

**Power**: Local political influence, NIMBY organizing capacity
**Interest**: Safety, environmental protection, economic opportunity

### Future Generations

- Bear consequences of AI safety failures, biosafety accidents, climate-related research decisions
- Inherit whatever governance frameworks are established now
- Cannot advocate for themselves in current political processes

**Power**: None directly
**Interest**: Long-term safety, environmental sustainability, equitable technology access
