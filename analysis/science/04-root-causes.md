# Science Policy: Root Causes

## Overview

The problems facing American science -- funding instability, workforce challenges, declining public trust, and competitive pressure from abroad -- are not random failures but the predictable outcomes of structural, economic, political, and cultural forces. Understanding these root causes is essential for designing effective reforms rather than merely treating symptoms.

---

## Structural Causes

### The Appropriations System Is Incompatible with Research

The federal budget process operates on an annual cycle, while scientific research inherently requires multi-year commitments. This mismatch creates cascading problems:

- **Continuing resolutions**: When Congress fails to pass appropriations bills on time (which has happened in 8 of the last 10 fiscal years), agencies cannot start new grants or increase funding for existing ones
- **No-cost extensions and carryover restrictions**: Rules designed for general government spending create perverse incentives for researchers to spend money quickly rather than efficiently
- **Sequestration vulnerability**: Research funding is classified as non-defense discretionary spending, the most vulnerable budget category
- **Annual uncertainty**: Labs cannot make hiring commitments, equipment purchases, or long-term experimental plans when next year's funding is unknown

### The Grant System's Design Flaws

The competitive grant system, while meritocratic in principle, has become dysfunctional as the ratio of applicants to awards has worsened:

- **Success rates have fallen**: NIH R01 success rates dropped from ~30% in the 1990s to ~21% today, meaning four out of five proposals are rejected
- **Time spent writing grants**: Principal investigators spend 40-50% of their time writing and reviewing grant applications rather than conducting research
- **Conservatism bias**: With low success rates, reviewers gravitate toward safe, incremental proposals rather than risky, innovative ones
- **Short funding cycles**: Three-to-five-year grants are too short for ambitious research programs but too long to be responsive to emerging opportunities
- **Indirect cost overhead**: Universities charge 50-65% overhead on federal grants, meaning that for every dollar of research, institutions take 50-65 cents for facilities and administration

### Geographic Concentration

Federal research funding is heavily concentrated in a small number of states and institutions:

| Concentration Metric | Value |
|---------------------|-------|
| Top 10 states' share of federal R&D | ~65% |
| Top 20 universities' share of federal R&D | ~30% |
| States receiving less than $500M in federal R&D | 20+ |
| EPSCoR-eligible states | 28 |

This concentration reflects a self-reinforcing cycle: established institutions have the infrastructure and expertise to win grants, which enables them to build more infrastructure and hire more talent, which makes them more competitive for future grants. This leaves large swaths of the country without research capacity, limiting both scientific output and public engagement with science.

## Economic Causes

### The Shift to Private R&D

The private sector now funds approximately 75% of U.S. R&D, up from 50% in the 1970s. While total investment has grown, this shift has consequences:

- **Development dominance**: Industry spending is overwhelmingly focused on development (D), not basic or applied research (R). Only ~6% of business R&D goes to basic research
- **Short time horizons**: Corporate R&D is oriented toward products with near-term market potential, not fundamental discoveries
- **Concentration in a few sectors**: Tech companies (Alphabet, Amazon, Apple, Meta, Microsoft) dominate business R&D; manufacturing R&D has declined
- **Reduced corporate basic research**: The closure or downsizing of industrial research labs (Bell Labs, Xerox PARC, IBM Research) has left basic research almost entirely to universities and government

### Perverse Incentives in Academic Research

The economic structure of academic research creates incentives that often conflict with scientific quality:

| Incentive | Intended Purpose | Perverse Effect |
|-----------|-----------------|----------------|
| Publication counts for promotion | Encourage productivity | Quantity over quality; salami-slicing of results |
| Journal impact factors | Measure influence | Pressure to publish in prestige journals; bias against replication |
| Grant funding as metric | Ensure research productivity | Professors become grant-writing machines rather than mentors or scientists |
| Indirect cost recovery | Cover institutional costs | Universities expand research volume to increase overhead revenue |
| Postdoc labor | Training for independence | Cheap labor pool with minimal career advancement |

### The Postdoc Exploitation Problem

The academic labor market relies on a large pool of postdoctoral researchers who are highly trained but poorly compensated:

- NRSA minimum stipend: $56,484 (for PhDs who typically spent 5-7 years in graduate school)
- Average postdoc duration: 4-6 years (up from 1-2 years in the 1970s)
- Industry starting salaries for PhDs: $100,000-$200,000+
- Percentage of postdocs who obtain tenure-track positions: ~15-20%

This creates a "pyramid scheme" dynamic where the research enterprise depends on producing far more PhDs than the academic system can absorb, promising careers that most will never achieve.

## Political Causes

### Politicization of Science

Science has become increasingly entangled with partisan politics:

- **Climate science**: The most prominent example, where acceptance of scientific evidence has become a proxy for political identity
- **COVID-19 pandemic**: Public health measures, vaccine acceptance, and trust in scientific institutions became deeply partisan
- **Stem cell research, evolution, and other hot-button issues**: Scientific questions are reframed as cultural or moral disputes
- **"War on science" narratives**: Both sides accuse the other of distorting science for political purposes

The partisan trust gap in scientists has widened from 8 percentage points (2016) to 37 percentage points (2024), making it increasingly difficult to build bipartisan support for science funding.

### Congressional Dysfunction and Science Capacity

Congress has limited capacity to engage with scientific issues:

- **Office of Technology Assessment (OTA) abolished (1995)**: Congress's own science advisory body was defunded as a cost-cutting measure, leaving legislators without independent technical expertise
- **Understaffed committees**: Congressional science committees have far fewer staff than in the 1980s
- **Earmarks and politics**: Science funding decisions are often driven by parochial interests rather than scientific merit
- **Short-term thinking**: Election cycles incentivize visible, near-term results rather than long-term research investment
- **Authorizations vs. appropriations gap**: Congress routinely authorizes far more science funding than it appropriates (e.g., the CHIPS and Science Act)

### Agency Turf Wars

The fragmented structure of federal research agencies creates coordination challenges:

- More than 20 federal agencies fund R&D, each with its own mission, culture, and congressional oversight committees
- Overlapping jurisdictions lead to duplication in some areas and gaps in others
- Agencies compete for budget share rather than collaborating on cross-cutting challenges
- No single entity coordinates the overall federal research portfolio

## Cultural Causes

### The Publish-or-Perish Culture

Academic science operates under a culture that prioritizes publication volume and journal prestige above all else:

- **Hiring and promotion decisions** are heavily weighted by publication records and grant funding
- **Negative results go unpublished**, creating systematic bias in the scientific literature
- **Replication studies are unrewarded**, contributing to the reproducibility crisis
- **Hyper-specialization** is rewarded over interdisciplinary work
- **Mentorship and teaching** are undervalued relative to research output

### Anti-Intellectualism and Science Skepticism

American culture has long contained strains of skepticism toward expertise and intellectual authority:

- Distrust of elites and "establishment" figures, including scientists
- Conflation of scientific uncertainty with unreliability
- Social media amplification of misinformation and pseudoscience
- Declining science literacy (only 28% of Americans qualify as scientifically literate)
- Identity-protective cognition: people reject scientific evidence that conflicts with their group identity

### Institutional Conservatism

Scientific institutions themselves resist change:

- **Peer review** tends to favor established paradigms and incremental work
- **Tenure systems** protect individual researchers but can insulate institutions from reform
- **Professional societies** often serve as gatekeepers that maintain disciplinary boundaries
- **University administrators** prioritize revenue generation (grants, overhead) over research quality
- **Federal agencies** develop institutional cultures resistant to new approaches

## Interconnections

These root causes form a reinforcing system:

1. **Funding instability** forces researchers to spend more time writing grants, reducing time for actual research and mentoring
2. **Low success rates** create conservatism bias in peer review, discouraging innovative work
3. **Publish-or-perish culture** incentivizes quantity over quality, contributing to the reproducibility crisis
4. **The reproducibility crisis** erodes public trust in science
5. **Declining public trust** makes it harder to build political support for science funding
6. **Political dysfunction** perpetuates funding instability, closing the loop

Similarly:

1. **Geographic concentration** of research funding leaves most Americans disconnected from the scientific enterprise
2. **Disconnection** breeds unfamiliarity and distrust
3. **Distrust** makes it harder to build political coalitions for science funding
4. **Reduced funding** reinforces concentration at established institutions
5. **Concentration** perpetuates geographic inequality

Breaking these feedback loops requires interventions at multiple points simultaneously -- no single reform will suffice.
