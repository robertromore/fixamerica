# Science Policy: Overview

## Executive Summary

The United States built its global preeminence on a foundation of scientific research and technological innovation. Since World War II, federal investment in basic and applied research has fueled breakthroughs from the transistor to the COVID-19 vaccines, generating trillions of dollars in economic activity and improving the lives of billions worldwide. Yet this foundation is showing cracks: federal R&D spending as a share of GDP has declined from its 1960s peak, the research workforce faces structural challenges, China has reached parity in total R&D expenditure, and public trust in science has become a partisan flashpoint.

The federal government invests approximately $200 billion annually in research and development through agencies including the National Institutes of Health (NIH), the National Science Foundation (NSF), the Department of Energy (DOE), NASA, and the Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency (DARPA). This investment supports approximately 300,000 researchers at more than 2,500 universities and institutions, operates 17 national laboratories, and funds the scientific enterprise that underpins American competitiveness, national security, and public health.

The challenges facing American science policy are interconnected: funding instability caused by continuing resolutions and sequestration disrupts long-term research planning; a hypercompetitive grant system wastes researcher time and discourages risk-taking; the publish-or-perish culture undermines reproducibility and scientific integrity; the STEM workforce pipeline leaks talent at every stage, particularly among underrepresented groups; and the politicization of science erodes the public trust necessary for evidence-based policymaking.

This analysis examines the American scientific enterprise across twenty critical dimensions, proposing reforms to stabilize funding, strengthen integrity, open the research ecosystem, diversify the workforce, and restore the social compact between science and society.

## Core Tensions

### Discovery vs. Application

The scientific community and policymakers continually debate the balance between curiosity-driven basic research and mission-oriented applied research. History shows that many transformative technologies emerged from basic research without predetermined applications, yet political pressure favors demonstrable near-term impact.

### Openness vs. Security

International scientific collaboration accelerates discovery, but the rise of strategic competition with China has prompted concerns about intellectual property theft and foreign influence. Overly aggressive security measures risk chilling legitimate collaboration and driving away international talent.

### Meritocracy vs. Equity

The scientific enterprise aspires to evaluate ideas on their merits, yet systemic barriers based on race, gender, socioeconomic status, and institutional prestige persistently shape who enters and succeeds in science. Genuine meritocracy requires active efforts to ensure equitable access.

### Public Good vs. Private Profit

Federally funded research generates knowledge that benefits society, but the commercialization pathway through patents and licensing can restrict access. Balancing incentives for innovation with public access to publicly funded discoveries remains contentious.

### Autonomy vs. Accountability

Scientists value research autonomy, but public funding creates obligations for transparency, responsible use of resources, and responsiveness to societal needs. The tension between scientific self-governance and democratic accountability shapes institutional design.

## Vision of Success

A successful American science policy would feature:

- **Stable, growing investment**: Federal R&D spending returns to at least 1% of GDP, with predictable multi-year funding that enables long-term research planning
- **A thriving workforce**: The STEM workforce reflects the diversity of America, with viable career paths, fair compensation, and supportive institutional cultures
- **Research integrity**: Scientific findings are trustworthy, reproducible, and free from political interference, with robust protections for whistleblowers and dissenting researchers
- **Open science**: Federally funded research, data, and publications are freely accessible to the public, accelerating discovery and enabling innovation
- **Effective translation**: Discoveries move efficiently from lab to market and from evidence to policy, benefiting the public that funded the research
- **Global leadership**: The U.S. maintains its position as a global science leader through strategic investment, international collaboration, and an open environment that attracts the world's best talent
- **Public trust**: Citizens understand and trust the scientific process, enabling evidence-based policymaking on critical issues from public health to climate change
