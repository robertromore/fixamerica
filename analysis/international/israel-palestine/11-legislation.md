# Israel-Palestine: US Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

US legislative engagement with the Israeli-Palestinian conflict is distinctive in American foreign policy. Few bilateral relationships are as deeply embedded in statute, and few foreign policy issues generate as much congressional activity. This document examines the existing legal framework, identifies proposed reforms, and analyzes legislative concepts that could align US policy with stated objectives of peace, security, and human rights.

> **Note:** This analysis focuses on US legislation—what Congress has enacted, what has been proposed, and what could be enacted to make US policy more constructive. It does not draft international agreements or Israeli/Palestinian domestic legislation. The focus is on what the US government can do within its own legal authority.

## Current Legal Framework

### The US-Israel Security Relationship: Statutory Foundation

The US-Israel security relationship rests on decades of legislation that has embedded military cooperation, diplomatic support, and economic ties into law.

**Key Statutes**

| Law | Year | Key Provisions |
|-----|------|---------------|
| Foreign Assistance Act (FAA) | 1961 | Authorizes foreign military and economic assistance; contains human rights conditions (Section 502B) |
| Arms Export Control Act (AECA) | 1976 | Governs foreign military sales; requires end-use monitoring and restricts use to defensive purposes |
| Leahy Law (FAA Sec. 620M; DoD appropriations) | 1997 | Prohibits assistance to foreign security force units credibly accused of gross human rights violations |
| United States-Israel Enhanced Security Cooperation Act | 2012 | Codifies commitment to Israel's Qualitative Military Edge (QME); expands cooperation |
| United States-Israel Strategic Partnership Act | 2014 | Designates Israel a "major strategic partner"; expands defense, energy, and homeland security cooperation |
| US-Israel Memorandum of Understanding (MOU) | 2016 | $38 billion over 10 years (FY2019-2028); $3.3B military + $500M missile defense annually; legally a political commitment, not a treaty |

**Qualitative Military Edge (QME)**

Codified in Section 36(h) of the Arms Export Control Act, the US is required to ensure that any arms sale to any country in the Middle East does not diminish Israel's qualitative military edge—defined as "the ability to counter and defeat any credible conventional military threat from any individual state or possible coalition of states." This requirement means every arms sale in the region is assessed for its impact on Israeli military superiority.

**Tax-Exempt Donations**

Under US tax law (Section 501(c)(3) of the Internal Revenue Code), donations to qualifying organizations that fund activities in Israel—including some that support settlement construction—are tax-deductible. This means US taxpayers indirectly subsidize settlement expansion, even when official US policy opposes it.

### Aid Conditionality: Existing Requirements

The US legal framework already contains human rights conditions on foreign military assistance that are relevant to the Israeli-Palestinian context. These conditions are rarely enforced with respect to Israel.

**Section 502B of the Foreign Assistance Act**

Prohibits security assistance to any country that engages in "a consistent pattern of gross violations of internationally recognized human rights." The State Department has never applied this finding to Israel, despite documentation of violations by Human Rights Watch, Amnesty International, B'Tselem, and UN bodies.

**Leahy Law**

Prohibits assistance to specific foreign security force units where there is "credible information" that the unit has committed "a gross violation of human rights." Applies to both State Department (FAA Sec. 620M) and Department of Defense (annual appropriations) assistance. Implementation requires the State Department to vet recipient units. Critics argue that vetting of Israeli units has been inadequate, with the State Department rarely finding sufficient evidence to trigger the prohibition despite extensive documentation by human rights organizations.

**National Security Memorandum-20 (NSM-20)**

Issued by the Biden administration in February 2024, this memorandum required recipient countries of US weapons to provide written assurances that they would use the weapons in compliance with international humanitarian law and would not restrict delivery of US humanitarian assistance. Israel provided assurances; critics argued they were contradicted by documented actions in Gaza. The memorandum lacked enforcement mechanisms.

**Section 620I of the Foreign Assistance Act**

Prohibits US foreign assistance to any country that "prohibits or otherwise restricts, directly or indirectly, the transport or delivery of United States humanitarian assistance." This provision is directly relevant to restrictions on aid delivery to Gaza but has not been formally invoked against Israel.

### Anti-BDS Legislation

A significant body of state and federal legislation targets the Boycott, Divestment, and Sanctions (BDS) movement.

**State Anti-BDS Laws**

As of 2025, approximately 38 states have enacted anti-BDS measures, including:

- **Anti-boycott executive orders and statutes** requiring state contractors to certify they do not boycott Israel
- **Divestment prohibitions** preventing state pension funds from divesting from companies doing business with Israel
- **Anti-discrimination provisions** classifying boycotts of Israel as discriminatory

**Federal Anti-BDS Proposals**

Several federal bills have been introduced but not enacted:

- **Israel Anti-Boycott Act**: Would impose penalties on US persons who participate in boycotts of Israel called for by international organizations
- **Combating BDS Act**: Would protect states' right to enact anti-BDS laws from legal challenge

**Constitutional Issues**

Anti-BDS laws face First Amendment challenges. Federal courts have reached mixed conclusions:

- Some courts have upheld anti-BDS laws as regulation of commercial conduct, not speech
- Others have struck them down as compelled speech violating the First Amendment
- The Supreme Court has not yet ruled on the constitutionality of anti-BDS statutes

### Palestinian-Related Legislation

**Taylor Force Act (2018)**

Prohibits certain US economic aid to the Palestinian Authority until the PA ceases payments to families of individuals imprisoned for or killed while committing acts of terrorism against Israeli or American citizens ("pay-for-slay" payments). The PA has not complied; relevant aid has been withheld.

**Anti-Terrorism Clarification Act (AACA, 2018)**

Allows US courts to exercise jurisdiction over the PA and PLO in terrorism-related civil suits if they accept certain forms of US assistance. This effectively forced the PA to choose between accepting US aid and exposing itself to potentially bankrupting lawsuits. The PA stopped accepting relevant aid.

**UNRWA Funding**

UNRWA funding is subject to annual appropriations and has been repeatedly contested. The Trump administration (2017-2021) cut all UNRWA funding. The Biden administration restored partial funding. Congress has imposed various conditions on UNRWA funding related to allegations of employee involvement in terrorism and curriculum content.

## Recent Congressional Activity

### Post-October 7 Legislative Response

The October 7, 2023 Hamas attack and subsequent Israeli military operations in Gaza generated significant congressional activity, revealing the contours of current political dynamics around Israel-Palestine legislation.

**Supplemental Appropriations**

Congress approved multiple supplemental aid packages for Israel following October 7:

- $14.3 billion in the April 2024 national security supplemental (Pub. L. 118-50)
- Additional emergency funding for Iron Dome and David's Sling missile defense replenishment
- Total supplemental military aid since October 2023 estimated at $17-26 billion (figures vary based on what is counted)

These supplementals passed with large bipartisan majorities, demonstrating the continued strength of pro-Israel consensus in Congress even during controversial military operations.

**Resolutions and Statements**

- Multiple resolutions affirming Israel's "right to defend itself" passed with near-unanimous support
- Attempts to condition aid on humanitarian access or civilian protection standards were defeated
- A small but growing minority of members (primarily progressive Democrats) voted against unconditional aid packages or spoke publicly about conditioning aid

**UNRWA Funding Restrictions**

Following Israeli allegations that UNRWA employees participated in the October 7 attack, Congress imposed additional restrictions on UNRWA funding. Several bills were introduced to permanently defund UNRWA, though none has been enacted as standalone legislation. The debate highlighted the tension between humanitarian needs and accountability concerns.

**Arms Sales Oversight**

Several members introduced resolutions of disapproval under the Arms Export Control Act to block specific arms sales to Israel, including:

- Joint Resolutions of Disapproval targeting tank ammunition and other offensive weapons
- These resolutions received more votes than similar measures in previous conflicts, indicating shifting political dynamics, but none passed
- The process highlighted that Congress has legal authority to block individual arms sales but rarely exercises it

### Voting Patterns and Trends

Several trends in congressional voting on Israel-Palestine are worth noting:

- **Bipartisan consensus on aid** remains strong but is narrowing, particularly in the Democratic Party
- **Generational shift**: Younger members of Congress are more willing to question unconditional support
- **Progressive caucus**: Has become increasingly vocal but remains a minority within the Democratic caucus
- **Republican unity**: Near-unanimous Republican support for unconditional Israel aid continues; the few dissenters tend to be libertarian-leaning members with broader anti-foreign-aid positions
- **Lobbying influence**: AIPAC and affiliated organizations spent record amounts in the 2024 election cycle, including supporting primary challengers against incumbents critical of Israel

## Proposed Reforms

### Aid Conditionality Reforms

The most substantive proposed reforms center on conditioning US military aid on compliance with existing law and policy.

**Concept 1: Leahy Law Enforcement Enhancement**

**Purpose:** Ensure meaningful implementation of existing legal requirements rather than pro forma compliance.

**Key Elements:**

- Require the State Department to conduct and publish annual assessments of Israeli military units' compliance with human rights standards
- Establish an independent review mechanism for credible allegations of gross human rights violations
- Require suspension of assistance to specific units upon credible finding, with restoration upon demonstrated remediation
- Mandate congressional reporting on Leahy Law implementation, including a list of units investigated and findings

**Concept 2: Human Rights Compliance Framework**

**Purpose:** Establish a comprehensive framework linking US military assistance to compliance with international humanitarian law.

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Foreign Military Assistance
Accountability Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds the following:
(a) The United States provides substantial military assistance
to foreign governments for the purposes of legitimate
self-defense and regional stability.
(b) Existing law requires that such assistance not be used in
a manner inconsistent with internationally recognized
human rights.
(c) Implementation of these requirements has been inconsistent,
undermining both the credibility of US human rights policy
and the effectiveness of US assistance.

SEC. 3. CERTIFICATION REQUIREMENT.

(a) IN GENERAL.—Not later than 90 days after any determination
by the Secretary of State that a recipient of United States
military assistance under section 503 of the Foreign Assistance
Act of 1961 may have used such assistance in a manner
inconsistent with international humanitarian law, the Secretary
shall—
    (1) conduct an investigation into the alleged violation;
    (2) submit to the appropriate congressional committees
    a report containing the findings of such investigation;
    and
    (3) if the investigation finds credible evidence of a
    violation, suspend delivery of the category of
    assistance implicated until the recipient demonstrates
    remediation to the satisfaction of the Secretary.

(b) WAIVER.—The President may waive the suspension requirement
under subsection (a)(3) upon certification to Congress that—
    (1) the waiver is in the vital national security interest
    of the United States; and
    (2) the recipient has taken concrete, verifiable steps
    to prevent recurrence of the violation.

(c) ANNUAL REPORTING.—The Secretary of State shall submit
to the appropriate congressional committees an annual report
detailing—
    (1) all investigations conducted under subsection (a);
    (2) findings of each investigation;
    (3) any suspensions imposed and their status;
    (4) any waivers exercised and their justification; and
    (5) an assessment of the recipient's overall compliance
    with international humanitarian law.

SEC. 4. INDEPENDENT REVIEW.

(a) The Secretary shall establish an Independent Review Board
composed of individuals with expertise in international
humanitarian law, military affairs, and human rights to
review compliance assessments under this Act.

(b) The Board shall have access to relevant classified and
unclassified information.

(c) The Board shall publish an annual unclassified summary
of its findings.

SEC. 5. DEFINITIONS.

For purposes of this Act—
(a) "International humanitarian law" means the Geneva
Conventions of 1949 and their Additional Protocols,
and customary international humanitarian law as
recognized by the International Committee of the Red
Cross.
(b) "Appropriate congressional committees" means the
Committee on Foreign Relations and the Committee on
Appropriations of the Senate and the Committee on
Foreign Affairs and the Committee on Appropriations
of the House of Representatives.
```

**Explanation:** This legislation would create a structured process for investigating and responding to potential violations of international humanitarian law by recipients of US military assistance. It applies to all recipients, not just Israel, which provides political cover and reinforces the principle of consistent application. The waiver provision acknowledges that national security may sometimes require continued assistance, but demands transparency and remediation rather than silence.

**Potential Challenges:**

- Strong opposition from pro-Israel lobby (AIPAC and allied organizations)
- Executive branch resistance to congressionally mandated constraints on foreign policy flexibility
- Definitional disputes over what constitutes a "violation" and what constitutes "credible evidence"
- Practical challenges of investigation in active conflict zones

**Refinements:**

- Classified annex for sensitive findings to protect intelligence sources
- Graduated response options (not just full suspension)
- Provision for emergency security assistance that bypasses normal timeline
- Whistleblower protections for government officials who report violations

### Diplomatic Authorization

**Concept 3: Middle East Peace Authorization Act**

**Purpose:** Provide congressional authorization and mandate for sustained US diplomatic engagement toward Israeli-Palestinian resolution.

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Middle East Peace Authorization Act".

SEC. 2. STATEMENT OF POLICY.

It is the policy of the United States—
(a) to support a negotiated resolution to the Israeli-
Palestinian conflict that provides for—
    (1) the security of the State of Israel;
    (2) the self-determination of the Palestinian people
    in a viable, contiguous state;
    (3) a just and agreed resolution of the refugee issue;
    (4) arrangements for Jerusalem that respect its
    significance to all faiths; and
    (5) normalization of relations between Israel and
    Arab and Muslim-majority states;
(b) to appoint and maintain a Special Envoy for Israeli-
Palestinian Negotiations with the rank of Ambassador;
(c) to use all appropriate diplomatic tools, including
multilateral engagement, to advance negotiations; and
(d) to condition future increases in military assistance
on demonstrable progress toward a negotiated resolution.

SEC. 3. SPECIAL ENVOY.

(a) APPOINTMENT.—The President shall appoint, by and with
the advice and consent of the Senate, a Special Envoy for
Israeli-Palestinian Negotiations.

(b) MANDATE.—The Special Envoy shall—
    (1) serve as the principal diplomatic representative
    of the United States in all matters relating to
    Israeli-Palestinian negotiations;
    (2) engage with Israeli, Palestinian, regional, and
    international counterparts;
    (3) coordinate US government policy across agencies;
    (4) report to Congress on a quarterly basis on the
    status of diplomatic efforts; and
    (5) propose policy adjustments to advance negotiations.

(c) TERM.—The Special Envoy shall serve for a term of
4 years, which may be renewed. The term shall not be
coterminous with any presidential term to provide
continuity across administrations.

SEC. 4. DIPLOMATIC SUPPORT.

(a) The Secretary of State shall provide the Special Envoy
with adequate staff, budget, and resources.

(b) There are authorized to be appropriated $25,000,000
annually for the operations of the Special Envoy's office.

SEC. 5. REPORTING.

(a) The Special Envoy shall submit to the appropriate
congressional committees—
    (1) quarterly reports on the status of negotiations
    and diplomatic engagement;
    (2) annual assessments of conditions on the ground,
    including settlement activity, humanitarian conditions,
    and security situation; and
    (3) recommendations for policy adjustments.
```

**Explanation:** This legislation would institutionalize US diplomatic engagement beyond any single administration's commitment. The Senate-confirmed appointment and fixed term provide continuity and accountability. Quarterly reporting ensures congressional oversight and public transparency.

**Potential Challenges:**

- Resistance from administrations that prefer executive flexibility in foreign policy
- Congressional micromanagement concerns
- Political difficulty of the "conditions on future increases" provision
- Risk that codifying two-state preference constrains diplomatic flexibility

### Settlement-Related Legislation

**Concept 4: Settlement Transparency Act**

**Purpose:** Require transparency about the use of US-origin equipment and tax-exempt funds in settlement construction and enforcement.

**Key Elements:**

- Require annual reporting on the use of US-origin military equipment in settlement-related operations (demolitions, land clearing, checkpoints serving settlements)
- Require the IRS to report on tax-exempt donations to organizations that fund settlement construction or settler violence
- Prohibit tax-exempt status for organizations whose primary purpose is funding construction in occupied territories as defined by international law
- Require labeling of products originating from settlements to distinguish them from products originating from within Israel's internationally recognized borders

**Constitutional Considerations:** The labeling provision may face First Amendment compelled speech challenges, though similar requirements exist for country-of-origin labeling in other contexts.

## Regulatory Framework

### Executive Branch Authorities

Several regulatory actions could advance policy objectives without new legislation.

**State Department**

- **End-Use Monitoring Enhancement:** The Bureau of Political-Military Affairs could allocate additional resources to end-use monitoring of defense articles provided to Israel, using existing authority under the Arms Export Control Act
- **Leahy Law Vetting Expansion:** The Bureau of Democracy, Human Rights, and Labor could implement more rigorous vetting procedures for Israeli security force units under existing Leahy Law authority
- **Country Reports on Human Rights Practices:** Annual country reports could provide more detailed and candid assessments of human rights conditions in Israel and the occupied territories

**Treasury Department**

- **Tax-Exempt Status Review:** The IRS could review whether organizations whose primary activity is funding construction in occupied territories qualify for 501(c)(3) status under existing definitions of "charitable purpose"
- **Sanctions Authority:** The Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC) has authority to sanction individuals involved in serious human rights abuses; this authority has been used against some extremist settlers and could be expanded

**Commerce Department**

- **Country-of-Origin Labeling:** Existing customs regulations could be clarified to require accurate country-of-origin labeling for products from settlements, distinguishing them from products made within Israel's internationally recognized borders (as the EU has done)

### International Legal Cooperation

- **ICC Engagement:** The executive could shift from active opposition to ICC jurisdiction to neutral engagement, without requiring legislative action
- **ICJ Advisory Opinions:** The State Department could issue updated legal guidance acknowledging ICJ findings on the occupation, adjusting US legal positions accordingly
- **UN Security Council:** The President could instruct the US delegation to abstain on (rather than veto) Security Council resolutions reflecting international consensus on settlements and occupation

## Legal Considerations

### Executive vs. Legislative Authority in Foreign Policy

The Constitution divides foreign affairs authority between the executive and legislative branches. Understanding this division is essential for evaluating what legislation can accomplish.

**Executive Authority:**

- Commander in chief (Article II, Section 2)
- Treaty power (with Senate consent)
- Appointment of ambassadors (with Senate consent)
- Recognition of foreign governments
- Conduct of diplomacy

**Legislative Authority:**

- Power of the purse (Article I, Section 8)—Congress controls appropriations, including foreign assistance
- Declare war and authorize use of military force
- Regulate foreign commerce
- Confirm appointments
- Oversight and investigation

**Key Tensions:**

- Congress can condition aid but cannot dictate diplomatic strategy
- The President can suspend arms deliveries but cannot appropriate funds Congress has not provided
- Executive agreements (like the MOU with Israel) are political commitments, not treaties requiring Senate ratification
- The War Powers Resolution applies to deployment of US forces but not to provision of military equipment

**Implication:** Legislative reforms can shape the framework within which executive decisions are made—particularly through the power of the purse—but cannot replace executive leadership in diplomacy. Effective reform requires both branches working in alignment.

### International Legal Obligations

US legislation intersects with several international legal frameworks:

- **Geneva Conventions (1949):** The US is a party; prohibits transfer of civilian population into occupied territory (Article 49, Fourth Convention) and requires humane treatment of civilians
- **Arms Trade Treaty:** The US signed but has not ratified; prohibits arms transfers where there is knowledge they would be used to commit war crimes
- **UN Charter:** Prohibits acquisition of territory by force (Article 2(4))
- **Customary International Law:** The ICJ has affirmed that the occupation and settlements violate international law; while ICJ advisory opinions are not directly binding on the US, they reflect the international legal consensus

### Constitutional Questions

**First Amendment and BDS:**

- Anti-BDS laws raise free speech concerns; commercial boycotts have received varying levels of First Amendment protection
- Political boycotts are generally protected speech; commercial boycott activity occupies contested ground
- The Supreme Court has not directly addressed whether anti-BDS statutes survive First Amendment scrutiny

**Due Process and Anti-Terrorism Laws:**

- The Taylor Force Act and AACA raise due process questions about conditioning aid on actions of third parties
- Holding the PA responsible for payments to individual families while cutting development aid affects civilian populations

**Treaty Clause:**

- The MOU with Israel is an executive agreement, not a treaty, so it does not require Senate ratification and can be modified by subsequent presidents
- This means the $3.8 billion annual commitment is a political promise, not a legal obligation, though breaking it would have significant diplomatic consequences

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Existing Framework

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Leahy Law implementation gap | State Department has broad discretion in determining what constitutes "credible information" of violations, effectively allowing non-enforcement | Critical |
| QME override | The QME requirement prevents arms sales to Arab states that might create leverage with Israel, limiting US diplomatic flexibility in the region | High |
| Tax-exempt settlement funding | US tax-deductible donations fund settlement construction even when official policy opposes settlements | High |
| Executive agreement structure | The MOU is not a treaty, so it lacks Senate oversight, but it functions as a near-binding commitment that constrains policy flexibility | Medium |
| Waiver provisions | Most aid conditions include national security waivers that allow the executive to bypass restrictions, rendering conditions largely symbolic | Critical |
| Vetting resource constraints | Insufficient personnel and investigative capacity for meaningful Leahy Law vetting | Medium |
| End-use monitoring gaps | Arms Export Control Act requires end-use monitoring, but resources are insufficient for comprehensive tracking of US-origin equipment | High |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Inconsistent enforcement | Human rights conditions exist but are not applied to favored allies, undermining credibility | Political pressure; institutional reluctance to create diplomatic friction |
| Congressional deference | Congress rarely exercises oversight of arms sales to Israel, despite having legal authority to do so | Lobbying pressure; fear of political consequences; pro-Israel consensus |
| Information asymmetry | Congress and the public lack timely, detailed information about how US aid is used | Classification overuse; executive reluctance to report embarrassing information |
| State-level proliferation of anti-BDS laws | Creates chilling effect on constitutionally protected expression | Coordinated lobbying campaigns; model legislation distributed to state legislators |
| Aid as entitlement | Military aid is treated as an unchangeable commitment rather than a policy tool | Decades of practice; MOU framework; domestic political dynamics |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Strengthen Leahy Law implementation** by establishing clear evidentiary standards, requiring timely investigation of credible allegations, and providing adequate resources for vetting
2. **Require annual certification** of compliance with human rights conditions before aid disbursement, with meaningful consequences for adverse findings
3. **Close the tax-exempt loophole** by clarifying that organizations primarily funding construction in occupied territory do not qualify for 501(c)(3) status
4. **Enhance congressional oversight** by requiring detailed reporting on end-use of military equipment and establishing a dedicated congressional monitoring capacity
5. **Address First Amendment concerns** in anti-BDS legislation by narrowly tailoring provisions to avoid restricting protected political expression
6. **Make waiver authority meaningful** by requiring detailed justification, time-limited duration, and congressional notification with review period

### Proposed Legislation

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| National security waiver abuse | Any legislation with a national security waiver will face pressure to invoke the waiver routinely, rendering the condition meaningless | High |
| Definitional ambiguity | Terms like "credible evidence," "international humanitarian law compliance," and "demonstrable progress" are subject to interpretation | Medium |
| Reporting without consequences | Reporting requirements alone do not change behavior if reports are filed and ignored | Medium |
| Special Envoy co-option | A politically appointed envoy may prioritize domestic political considerations over diplomatic effectiveness | Medium |
| Sunset provisions | Legislation without sunset review may become outdated; legislation with sunset provisions may simply expire | Low |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Political feasibility | Most proposed reforms face strong political opposition and may not pass | Lobbying; electoral dynamics; bipartisan pro-Israel consensus |
| Executive resistance | Even enacted legislation may face executive non-implementation or minimal compliance | Executive prerogative in foreign policy; political costs of enforcement |
| Unilateral US approach | US legislation alone cannot resolve the conflict; it can only shape US behavior | Inherent limitation of domestic law in international affairs |
| Potential backlash | Conditioning aid could alienate Israel without producing behavioral change if conditions are perceived as hostile rather than constructive | Risk of counterproductive application |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Design waivers narrowly** with specific criteria, time limits, congressional notification, and mandatory reporting on waiver justification
2. **Include clear definitions** with reference to established international legal standards (e.g., Geneva Conventions, ICRC customary international humanitarian law study)
3. **Pair reporting with triggers** so that specific findings automatically activate consequences (e.g., aid suspension), reducing discretionary non-enforcement
4. **Insulate the Special Envoy** from political pressure through fixed terms, bipartisan confirmation, and professional (not political) staffing
5. **Build bipartisan coalition** by framing reforms as strengthening the US-Israel relationship by making it more sustainable, not undermining it

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|------------------|
| Lobbying pressure on enforcement | Transparency requirements for lobbying contacts with enforcement officials; whistleblower protections for government employees who report political interference |
| Classification abuse | Require unclassified summaries of compliance reports; prohibit classification solely to avoid embarrassment |
| Aid fungibility | Recognize that even earmarked military aid frees Israeli budget resources for other purposes (including settlement infrastructure); account for indirect effects |
| Transition risk | Phase implementation to avoid shock; provide transition periods for policy changes; maintain security cooperation during adjustment |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

- All proposed legislation should include five-year review requirements with mandatory congressional hearings
- Compliance standards should be updated periodically to reflect evolving conditions and international legal developments
- The Special Envoy mandate should be reviewed after each presidential term
- Reporting requirements should include assessments of whether the legislation is achieving its stated purposes
