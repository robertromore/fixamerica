# What American Citizens Can Do

## Purpose

This document provides actionable steps for American citizens who want to engage constructively with the Israeli-Palestinian issue. It is designed to be useful regardless of where the reader falls on the political spectrum, focusing on informed engagement, civic participation, and support for peace and human rights.

> **Note:** This is among the most polarizing issues in American public life. Engaging constructively requires intellectual humility, willingness to hear uncomfortable perspectives, and commitment to principles over partisanship. The actions below reflect these values.

## Individual Actions

### Educate Yourself

The most important first step is developing an informed understanding of the conflict, its history, and the perspectives of those involved.

**Read Widely and Critically**

- Read sources from multiple perspectives, not just those that confirm your existing views
- Seek out Israeli, Palestinian, and independent voices
- Distinguish between news reporting, opinion, and advocacy
- Be skeptical of social media content that reduces a complex conflict to simple narratives
- Understand the difference between criticism of Israeli government policy and antisemitism; between support for Palestinian rights and support for violence

**Recommended Starting Points**

- This analysis series (start with [00-overview.md](00-overview.md) and [01-historical-context.md](01-historical-context.md))
- International Crisis Group reports on Israel-Palestine
- Carnegie Endowment for International Peace Middle East program
- Books that present multiple perspectives (see [07-resources.md](07-resources.md))

**Understand Key Concepts**

- The difference between the 1948 and 1967 borders
- What "occupation" means in international law
- The settlement enterprise and its implications
- The refugee issue and right of return
- The distinction between Hamas, the PA, and Palestinian civil society
- The difference between Zionism as a spectrum of political ideas and the policies of any particular Israeli government
- Why Jerusalem is contested and what "status quo" means for holy sites

### Stay Informed

**Quality News Sources**

No single source is perfectly balanced. Use multiple sources.

*Israeli perspectives:*

- *Haaretz* (English edition) - Center-left Israeli daily with investigative depth
- *Times of Israel* - Broad-spectrum Israeli English-language news
- *+972 Magazine* - Israeli-Palestinian joint publication, critical of occupation
- *Israel Hayom* - Right-leaning perspective reflecting government views

*Palestinian perspectives:*

- *Mondoweiss* - Palestinian-focused analysis and reporting
- *Al Jazeera English* - Qatari-funded but with extensive regional coverage
- *Palestine Chronicle* - Palestinian news and commentary
- *Middle East Eye* - UK-based, covers Palestinian perspectives

*International/analytical:*

- *The Economist* - Balanced analytical coverage
- *BBC* - International perspective
- *Reuters and AP* - Wire service factual reporting
- *Foreign Affairs* - In-depth policy analysis

**Track Key Indicators**

- Congressional votes on aid, arms sales, and resolutions
- Settlement construction data (Peace Now settlement tracker)
- Humanitarian conditions in Gaza (UN OCHA reports)
- Diplomatic developments (State Department briefings)
- ICC and ICJ proceedings

### Examine Your Own Biases

**Questions to Ask Yourself**

- Do I hold both sides to the same moral and legal standards?
- Am I willing to criticize the side I generally sympathize with when it commits wrongs?
- Do I distinguish between a government and its people?
- Am I attributing the worst motives to one side and the best to the other?
- Can I acknowledge legitimate concerns on the other side without feeling I am betraying my own position?
- Am I consuming information from sources that challenge my views, or only those that confirm them?

**Common Cognitive Traps**

| Trap | Description | Remedy |
|------|-------------|--------|
| Selective empathy | Feeling compassion for one side's suffering while minimizing the other's | Deliberately seek out personal stories from both sides |
| Historical cherry-picking | Citing only events that support your narrative | Study the full timeline, including inconvenient facts |
| False equivalence | Treating all actions as equivalent regardless of scale or context | Acknowledge power asymmetry while condemning all violence |
| Guilt by association | Attributing extremists' views to entire populations | Recognize diversity within Israeli and Palestinian societies |
| Sunk cost attachment | Defending your existing position because you have held it long | Be willing to update your views based on new information |

## Community Actions

### Interfaith and Intercommunity Dialogue

**Why This Matters**

The Israeli-Palestinian conflict creates real tensions within American communities, particularly among Jewish, Arab, and Muslim Americans. Constructive dialogue can build understanding and reduce the domestic toxicity of the issue.

**How to Engage**

- **Join or organize interfaith dialogue groups** that include Jewish, Muslim, and Christian participants discussing the conflict respectfully
- **Attend community events** hosted by organizations from perspectives different from your own
- **Listen first** - In dialogue, prioritize understanding over persuading
- **Acknowledge pain** - The conflict causes real grief for Americans with connections to the region; recognize this
- **Focus on shared values** - Peace, security, human rights, dignity, and self-determination are values shared across communities
- **Avoid scoring points** - Dialogue is not debate; the goal is understanding, not winning

**Interfaith Organizations and Programs**

- Interfaith America (formerly IFYC) - Promotes interfaith cooperation
- Seeds of Peace - Youth leadership programs crossing conflict lines
- Abrahamic Initiative - Jewish-Muslim-Christian dialogue programs
- Local interfaith councils and dialogue circles

### Campus Engagement

The Israeli-Palestinian issue has become intensely divisive on college campuses. Constructive engagement means:

- **Protect free speech and assembly** - Peaceful protest is a fundamental right; support it even for views you disagree with
- **Distinguish criticism from hatred** - Criticizing Israeli policy is legitimate; antisemitic harassment is not. Supporting Palestinian rights is legitimate; intimidating Jewish students is not. These distinctions matter.
- **Demand nuance from institutions** - Push universities to create spaces for informed discussion rather than choosing sides
- **Support academic programs** - Middle Eastern studies, peace and conflict studies, and international law courses provide essential context
- **Engage with student organizations** on all sides respectfully; avoid demonizing those who disagree

### Local Advocacy

- **Engage with local government** on relevant issues (municipal resolutions, public investment policies, community relations)
- **Support local organizations** that bring together diverse communities around shared interests
- **Counter hate** - Report antisemitic, anti-Arab, or anti-Muslim incidents; support affected community members; participate in anti-hate coalitions
- **Organize community education events** featuring speakers from multiple perspectives

## Political Actions

### Contact Your Representatives

Your elected officials vote on military aid, arms sales, diplomatic appointments, and resolutions that directly affect US policy toward the conflict.

**Key Issues to Raise**

- **Military aid conditions**: Should US aid be conditioned on compliance with human rights standards?
- **Arms sales oversight**: Should Congress exercise greater scrutiny over weapons transfers?
- **Diplomatic engagement**: Should the US more actively mediate?
- **Humanitarian assistance**: Should the US fund reconstruction and UNRWA?
- **Palestinian statehood**: Should the US recognize Palestine?
- **ICC cooperation**: Should the US cooperate with international legal proceedings?
- **Settlement policy**: Should the US take a stronger stance against settlement expansion?

**How to Contact**

- **Phone calls** are more effective than emails for most offices
- **In-person meetings** at district offices are most impactful
- **Town halls** are opportunities for public questions
- **Written letters** (physical mail) carry more weight than form emails

**Tips for Effective Communication**

- Be specific about what you are asking for (a vote, a statement, a policy position)
- Explain why the issue matters to you personally
- Be respectful and factual
- Reference specific legislation or policy proposals
- Follow up; one contact is rarely sufficient

### Template Letter to Members of Congress

Below is a framework that can be adapted to your own views. It is deliberately nonpartisan and focused on US interests.

---

*Dear [Representative/Senator] [Name],*

*As your constituent in [city/state], I am writing about US policy toward the Israeli-Palestinian conflict.*

*I believe it is in America's national interest to pursue a sustainable resolution to this conflict. The current approach of [describe current policy concern] has not produced progress toward peace and [describe specific cost or concern: undermines US credibility / costs taxpayer resources / increases security risks / contributes to domestic polarization].*

*Specifically, I urge you to:*

*[Choose specific asks that reflect your priorities:]*

- *Support [specific legislation or resolution]*
- *Oppose [specific legislation or resolution]*
- *Hold hearings on [specific issue]*
- *Condition military aid on [specific requirement]*
- *Support diplomatic engagement toward [specific goal]*

*I am available to discuss this further at your convenience. I will be following your position on this issue and will take it into account in future elections.*

*Sincerely,*
*[Your name, address, phone number]*

---

### Questions for Political Candidates

When candidates seek your vote, ask informed questions.

**Questions That Reveal Substance**

- What specific steps would you take to advance Israeli-Palestinian peace?
- Do you support conditioning US military aid on compliance with US law (including the Leahy Law and the Foreign Assistance Act)?
- What is your position on Israeli settlement expansion in the occupied West Bank?
- Do you support US recognition of Palestinian statehood? Under what conditions?
- How would you address the humanitarian crisis in Gaza?
- What role should international law and institutions (ICJ, ICC) play in resolving the conflict?
- How do you balance supporting Israeli security with supporting Palestinian human rights?

**Red Flags in Candidate Responses**

- Answering only about one side without acknowledging the other
- Refusing to engage with the question entirely
- Treating the issue as purely a domestic political calculation
- Demonizing all Palestinians or all Israelis
- Claiming the issue is "too complicated" to have a position on

### Voting and Electoral Engagement

- Research candidates' positions and voting records on Israel-Palestine issues
- Attend candidate forums and ask questions
- Support candidates who demonstrate understanding of the complexity and commitment to constructive engagement
- Engage with both primary and general elections, as positions on this issue are often shaped in primaries
- Consider this issue alongside other priorities; single-issue voting can be counterproductive

## Key Talking Points

When discussing the issue with others, these evidence-based points can ground conversation in facts.

### On US Interests

- The US provides approximately $3.8 billion in annual military aid to Israel, plus billions in supplemental packages. Americans have a legitimate interest in how these funds are used and what outcomes they produce.
- Regional instability caused by the unresolved conflict affects US security, energy markets, and diplomatic effectiveness worldwide.
- The conflict is used as a recruitment tool by extremist groups targeting Americans.
- Resolution would free diplomatic resources for other US priorities, including competition with China and managing the energy transition.

### On the Conflict Itself

- Both Israelis and Palestinians have legitimate claims to self-determination and security. These claims are not mutually exclusive.
- The power asymmetry is significant: Israel is a nuclear-armed state with one of the world's most advanced militaries; Palestinians have no state, no army, and limited governance capacity. This affects what it is reasonable to expect from each side.
- Over 700,000 Israeli settlers live in the West Bank and East Jerusalem, making any territorial solution increasingly difficult to implement.
- Gaza's population of over 2 million has experienced catastrophic destruction since October 2023, with tens of thousands killed and the majority of housing and infrastructure destroyed.
- The Palestinian Authority governs limited areas of the West Bank with diminishing legitimacy. Hamas governed Gaza until October 2023; its governmental capacity has been largely destroyed.
- International legal consensus (ICJ, ICC, UN General Assembly) increasingly characterizes the occupation and settlement enterprise as illegal.

### On Solutions

- No solution is easy; every proposed framework requires painful compromises by both sides.
- The two-state solution remains the international consensus but faces severe obstacles from settlement expansion and political opposition.
- The status quo is not stable; it trends toward a one-state reality that will force difficult choices about democracy, demographics, and rights.
- Any sustainable solution requires the consent of both peoples; externally imposed solutions do not endure.

## Common Misconceptions

| Misconception | Reality |
|---------------|---------|
| "The conflict is ancient and unsolvable" | The modern conflict dates to the late 19th/early 20th century. It has specific historical causes and can theoretically be resolved, though doing so is extremely difficult. |
| "One side is entirely right and the other entirely wrong" | Both peoples have legitimate claims and both have committed serious wrongs. Moral clarity on specific issues does not require total vindication of one side. |
| "The US is a neutral mediator" | The US has been Israel's primary military, diplomatic, and economic supporter for decades. This does not make mediation impossible, but it shapes what the US can credibly offer. |
| "Nothing Americans do matters" | US policy is the single most important external factor in the conflict. Congressional votes on aid, arms sales, and diplomatic positions directly affect outcomes. American citizens influence that policy. |
| "Supporting Palestinian rights means opposing Israel's existence" | The vast majority of Palestinian rights advocates support a solution that includes Israeli security and self-determination. Framing any criticism as existential threat shuts down legitimate debate. |
| "Supporting Israel means supporting everything the Israeli government does" | Many Israelis themselves strongly criticize government policies on settlements, Gaza, and democratic norms. Supporting Israel's security and right to exist does not require supporting every policy. |
| "Peace just requires the right deal" | The obstacle is not lack of good proposals; dozens exist. The obstacle is political conditions that make acceptance impossible. Changing those conditions is the real challenge. |
| "BDS will solve the problem" | The BDS movement is a legitimate form of nonviolent advocacy, but economic pressure alone has never resolved a conflict of this nature. Political engagement remains essential. |
| "Military force will solve the problem" | Decades of military operations have not produced security for Israel or freedom for Palestinians. Military tools can address immediate threats but cannot substitute for political resolution. |

## A Note on Emotional Engagement

This conflict evokes strong emotions, especially for Americans with personal connections to the region—Jewish Americans, Arab Americans, Muslim Americans, and others with family, friends, or community ties to Israel and Palestine.

Emotional engagement is not a weakness; it reflects the human stakes of the conflict. But effective advocacy requires channeling emotion into constructive action rather than letting it produce only outrage, despair, or hostility toward those who see the situation differently.

**Practices for constructive engagement:**

- Take breaks from the news when it becomes overwhelming
- Distinguish between your emotional response and your policy analysis
- Remember that people who disagree with you are usually motivated by genuine concern, not malice
- Seek out personal stories and experiences from people on both sides
- Focus on what you can influence (US policy) rather than what you cannot (decisions by Israeli or Palestinian leaders)
- Build relationships across disagreement rather than retreating into ideological silos
