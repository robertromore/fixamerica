# Roadmap: US Engagement Toward Israeli-Palestinian Resolution

## Purpose

This document outlines a phased strategy for US engagement toward a sustainable resolution of the Israeli-Palestinian conflict. It is written from a US policy perspective, focusing on what the United States can realistically do given domestic political constraints, strategic interests, and the current state of the conflict.

> **Note:** This roadmap assumes that the US has a genuine interest in resolution (see [00-overview.md](00-overview.md) for why). It does not assume any particular solution framework; rather, it proposes a process that could lead to an agreed outcome. The roadmap is aspirational but grounded in political realities as of early 2026. Actual implementation would require sustained political will that has been absent from US policy for decades.

## Guiding Principles

Any credible US engagement strategy must be built on principles that distinguish it from previous failed efforts.

### Lessons from Past Failures

| Failed Approach | Why It Failed | Lesson |
|-----------------|---------------|--------|
| Oslo process (1993-2000) | No enforcement; settlement expansion continued; interim became permanent | Agreements without enforcement mechanisms are meaningless |
| Camp David (2000) | Insufficient preparation; all-or-nothing summit | Process must build incrementally; avoid premature final status talks |
| Road Map (2003) | Phase 1 never completed; no consequences for noncompliance | Phased approaches require verification and consequences at each stage |
| Kerry Initiative (2013-14) | No leverage applied; no consequences for failure | Mediation without willingness to apply pressure is theater |
| Trump Plan (2020) | One-sided; excluded Palestinians from design | Plans designed without Palestinian input will be rejected |
| Biden approach (2023-25) | "Bear hug" without conditions; private pressure without public follow-through | Support without conditions produces no behavioral change |

### Principles for Effective Engagement

1. **Consistency over favoritism** - Apply the same standards to both parties; end the practice of shielding either side from consequences of violations
2. **Leverage requires willingness to use it** - US military aid, diplomatic protection, and economic engagement are tools, not entitlements; they must be connected to behavioral expectations
3. **Process before outcome** - Focus on creating conditions for negotiation rather than imposing a predetermined solution
4. **Inclusivity** - Any process must ultimately include all major Palestinian and Israeli constituencies, not just those convenient for the US
5. **Humanitarian baseline** - Regardless of political progress, maintain minimum humanitarian standards as a non-negotiable floor
6. **Regional integration** - Embed bilateral Israeli-Palestinian engagement within a broader regional framework that creates incentives for both sides
7. **Domestic transparency** - American citizens deserve honest assessment of what US policy is accomplishing and what it is costing

## Phase 1: Foundation Building (Years 1-2)

### Objective

Establish the preconditions for meaningful engagement by restoring US credibility, stabilizing humanitarian conditions, and creating institutional frameworks for sustained diplomacy.

### 1.1 Immediate Diplomatic Steps

**Restore US Credibility as Mediator**

The US cannot mediate effectively when perceived as Israel's unconditional advocate. Restoring credibility requires:

- **Articulate clear principles**: Issue a presidential policy statement affirming that US engagement is guided by international law, mutual self-determination, and security for both peoples; that settlements in occupied territory are illegal under international law; and that the final status of borders, Jerusalem, refugees, and security will be determined through negotiation, not unilateral action
- **Appoint a full-time envoy**: Designate a Senate-confirmed Special Envoy for Israeli-Palestinian Affairs with direct access to the President, a dedicated staff, and a mandate extending beyond any single administration's term
- **Reopen diplomatic channels**: Restore the US consulate in Jerusalem serving Palestinians; reestablish formal engagement with Palestinian leadership; end the practice of excluding Palestinian representation from regional discussions
- **Re-engage multilateral institutions**: Resume funding to UNRWA with appropriate oversight; engage constructively with the ICC and ICJ rather than reflexively opposing jurisdiction; support UN Security Council resolutions establishing clear expectations for both parties

**Begin Regional Consultation**

- Conduct consultations with Egypt, Jordan, Saudi Arabia, UAE, and Qatar on a framework for comprehensive engagement
- Explore the relationship between Israeli-Palestinian progress and broader normalization (particularly Saudi)
- Engage European allies on coordinated approach
- Establish a Contact Group of key stakeholders to maintain diplomatic momentum

### 1.2 Humanitarian Measures

**Establish Humanitarian Baseline**

Regardless of political progress, the US should insist on minimum humanitarian standards as conditions for continued assistance.

- **Gaza reconstruction initiation**: Establish an international reconstruction mechanism with transparent oversight, ensuring aid reaches civilians while preventing diversion to armed groups; the US should contribute its proportional share and mobilize international donors
- **Civilian protection standards**: Condition future arms transfers on demonstrable compliance with international humanitarian law, including independent verification mechanisms
- **Freedom of movement**: Press for measurable improvements in Palestinian freedom of movement in the West Bank, including reduction of checkpoints and permit requirements for economic activity
- **Prisoner and detainee rights**: Insist on due process for administrative detainees, access by international monitors, and end to detention of minors under military law
- **Healthcare access**: Ensure medical evacuation from Gaza, restore healthcare infrastructure, and guarantee Palestinian access to East Jerusalem hospitals

**Accountability and Transparency**

- Establish an independent monitoring mechanism to track humanitarian conditions and report publicly to Congress
- Require the State Department to certify compliance with the Leahy Law before each arms transfer, with meaningful investigation rather than pro forma approval
- Support international investigations into potential violations of international humanitarian law by all parties, including the October 7 attack and subsequent military operations

### 1.3 Confidence-Building Measures

**Israeli Confidence Building**

Measures that demonstrate US commitment to Israeli security while shifting the framework:

- Reaffirm the US commitment to Israel's security and right to defend itself against genuine threats
- Expand joint missile defense cooperation (Iron Dome, David's Sling, Arrow) to demonstrate that security can be enhanced without territorial expansion
- Facilitate intelligence sharing focused on Iranian threats
- Support Israeli-Arab normalization efforts as part of a broader framework that includes Palestinian progress

**Palestinian Confidence Building**

Measures that demonstrate US seriousness about Palestinian rights:

- Provide economic development support for Areas A and B focused on private sector growth and infrastructure
- Support PA institutional reform including judicial independence, anti-corruption measures, and preparation for elections
- Press Israel to transfer withheld Palestinian tax revenues
- Facilitate international support for Palestinian state-building institutions

**Bilateral Measures**

- Support and expand people-to-people programs that bring Israelis and Palestinians together across divides
- Encourage Israeli-Palestinian economic cooperation where it benefits both communities equitably
- Promote joint environmental projects (water management, pollution, climate adaptation) that create shared interests
- Support media exchanges that humanize the other side for both populations

### 1.4 Domestic Political Groundwork

**Congressional Engagement**

- Brief congressional leadership from both parties on the engagement strategy and its connection to US national interests
- Propose bipartisan legislation establishing a framework for conditional engagement (see [11-legislation.md](11-legislation.md))
- Hold hearings examining the costs of the status quo and the benefits of resolution
- Build a coalition of members willing to support conditional aid as a tool, not a punishment

**Public Communication**

- Frame the engagement in terms of US national interest: reducing terrorism risk, stabilizing energy markets, strengthening alliances, freeing diplomatic resources
- Avoid framing that appears to "take sides" in the conflict
- Emphasize that supporting both Israeli security and Palestinian rights is not contradictory
- Be honest about costs, risks, and the likelihood of progress

### Phase 1 Success Metrics

| Metric | Target | Measurement |
|--------|--------|-------------|
| Humanitarian access | Unimpeded humanitarian aid delivery to Gaza | UN reporting; independent monitors |
| Diplomatic infrastructure | Envoy appointed; consulate reopened; Contact Group established | Institutional markers |
| Settlement activity | No new settlement construction; no outpost legalization | Peace Now monitoring; satellite imagery |
| PA reform | Electoral preparations underway; anti-corruption measures enacted | International monitoring |
| Congressional support | Bipartisan letter supporting engagement framework | Congressional action |
| Regional buy-in | Contact Group meetings with participation from key Arab states | Diplomatic participation |

### Phase 1 Risk Mitigation

| Risk | Likelihood | Mitigation |
|------|------------|------------|
| Israeli government rejects engagement | High | Frame as enhancing Israeli security; build relationship with Israeli security establishment; maintain aid but tie future increases to cooperation |
| Palestinian Authority collapses | Medium | Develop contingency planning for alternative governance; support institutional resilience; accelerate reform |
| Spoiler violence derails process | High | Maintain separation between security response and political process; condemn violence from all sides; do not allow attacks to serve as pretexts for ending engagement |
| Domestic political backlash | High | Build bipartisan coalition; frame in national interest terms; engage pro-Israel and Arab American communities |
| Regional conflict escalation | Medium | Maintain deterrence posture; avoid linkage that allows regional actors to veto progress |

## Phase 2: Framework Negotiations (Years 3-5)

### Objective

Move from confidence building to substantive negotiations on a framework for resolution, embedded in a regional context that creates incentives for both parties.

### Prerequisites for Phase 2

Phase 2 should not begin until Phase 1 has produced:

- Demonstrable improvement in humanitarian conditions
- Credible Palestinian governance reform including electoral legitimacy
- At minimum, a freeze on settlement expansion
- Regional Contact Group consensus on engagement principles
- Congressional authorization for the engagement framework

If these prerequisites are not met, Phase 1 should be extended and pressure intensified. Moving to negotiations without adequate preparation would repeat past failures.

### 2.1 Negotiation Framework

**Format**

- Direct Israeli-Palestinian negotiations with active US mediation
- Regional framework providing context and incentives (normalization, security guarantees, economic integration)
- International backing through Contact Group and UN Security Council endorsement
- Clear timeline with benchmarks, not open-ended process
- Parallel tracks for security, territory, refugees, Jerusalem, and economic arrangements
- Technical committees on each track with expert support

**Scope**

The negotiations should address all final status issues in an integrated framework:

| Track | Key Issues | Complexity |
|-------|------------|------------|
| Territory and borders | 1967 lines as reference; land swaps; settlement disposition | High |
| Security | Demilitarization; international presence; border control; airspace | High |
| Jerusalem | Sovereignty; holy sites; municipal governance; access | Very high |
| Refugees | Acknowledgment; right of return formula; compensation; resettlement | Very high |
| Recognition | Mutual recognition; end of conflict; end of claims | High |
| Economic arrangements | Trade; customs; labor; resources; water | Medium |
| Regional integration | Normalization; security architecture; economic cooperation | Medium |

**US Role**

- Active mediator with its own proposals when parties are stuck
- Guarantor of security arrangements
- Provider of economic incentives (reconstruction, development, investment)
- Enforcer of agreements through aid conditionality and diplomatic support
- Honest broker willing to publicly identify which party is blocking progress

### 2.2 Institution Building

**Palestinian State-Building**

Support Palestinian institutional development so that any agreement can be implemented:

- Professional security forces under civilian control, trained and equipped for law enforcement (not internal repression)
- Judicial system with independence and capacity
- Economic institutions capable of managing a state economy
- Education system preparing for coexistence
- Environmental and resource management capacity (especially water)
- Electoral commission with demonstrated capability and independence

**Israeli Preparation**

Support Israeli society and institutions in preparing for agreement:

- Support Israeli civil society organizations working on coexistence and public education
- Facilitate economic planning for transition (settler relocation, security restructuring)
- Encourage Israeli political leaders to educate their public about the costs of the status quo and benefits of agreement
- Work with the Israeli security establishment on security transition planning

**Joint Institutions**

- Establish joint Israeli-Palestinian mechanisms for conflict resolution, economic cooperation, and environmental management
- Create a joint security coordination center for the transition period
- Develop shared infrastructure plans (transportation, water, energy)

### 2.3 Regional Integration

**Normalization Package**

Work with Arab states to develop a comprehensive normalization offer contingent on Israeli-Palestinian agreement:

- Full diplomatic relations with Saudi Arabia, other remaining states
- Economic integration and investment
- Security cooperation against shared threats (Iran)
- Cultural and educational exchanges
- Tourism and people-to-people connections

**Security Architecture**

- Develop a regional security framework that addresses Iranian threats, provides collective defense arrangements, and reduces reliance on bilateral military aid
- Explore multilateral security guarantees for both Israel and a Palestinian state
- Address nuclear nonproliferation concerns (Iran, broader region)

### Phase 2 Success Metrics

| Metric | Target | Measurement |
|--------|--------|-------------|
| Negotiations launched | Formal talks with agreed terms of reference | Joint statement |
| Progress on tracks | Agreement in principle on at least 2 of 6 tracks | Negotiation reports |
| Palestinian elections | Free and fair elections for president and legislature | International monitoring |
| Security transition | Joint security coordination operational | Implementation reporting |
| Regional buy-in | Normalization contingent on agreement endorsed by 5+ Arab states | Diplomatic commitments |
| Settlement freeze | Verified halt to all construction beyond existing footprint | Independent monitoring |

### Phase 2 Risk Mitigation

| Risk | Likelihood | Mitigation |
|------|------------|------------|
| Negotiations deadlock | High | Active US mediation with own proposals; time-limited talks with consequences for obstruction |
| Government changes derail process | Medium | Embed commitments in legislation, treaties, and international frameworks that survive elections |
| Violence escalation | High | Maintain separation between security and political tracks; rapid response capability; condemn all violence |
| Regional spoilers (Iran) | High | Maintain deterrence; coordinate with allies; do not allow spoiler veto over process |
| Public opposition | Medium | Transparency; public education; demonstrate benefits of progress |

## Phase 3: Implementation and Stabilization (Years 6-10)

### Objective

Implement an agreed framework, manage the transition, and build sustainable peace through institutional development and reconciliation.

### Prerequisites for Phase 3

Phase 3 begins only upon:

- Signed framework agreement endorsed by both parties and the UN Security Council
- Legislative approval in Israel and ratification by Palestinian institutions
- Regional normalization commitments formalized
- International donor conference commitment for implementation funding
- Security transition plans operational

### 3.1 Agreement Implementation

**Territorial Implementation**

- Phased Israeli withdrawal from agreed areas with security transition
- Settlement evacuation and relocation with compensation (drawing on 2005 Gaza disengagement lessons—both what worked and what did not)
- Border demarcation with international verification
- Land swap implementation with property transfers

**Security Implementation**

- Deployment of international security presence (if agreed)
- Palestinian security force assumption of responsibilities
- Israeli security withdrawal from agreed areas
- Joint security coordination for transition period
- Border security handover with monitoring

**Jerusalem Implementation**

- Implementation of agreed governance arrangements
- Holy sites access and administration per agreement
- Municipal service provision
- International monitoring of access guarantees

**Refugee Resolution**

- Implementation of agreed return/compensation/resettlement formula
- International fund for refugee compensation and integration
- UNRWA transition to successor arrangements
- Host country (Jordan, Lebanon, Syria) adjustments

### 3.2 Stabilization Measures

**Economic Development**

- Major international investment program for Palestinian state development
- Trade facilitation between Israel and Palestine
- Regional economic integration through free trade arrangements
- Infrastructure development: transportation, energy, water, telecommunications
- Private sector development with international partnership

**Reconciliation and Coexistence**

- Truth and acknowledgment processes (not necessarily a single "truth commission" but mechanisms for historical recognition)
- Educational reform in both societies to promote accurate history and coexistence
- Media programs supporting understanding across the divide
- Cultural exchanges, joint projects, and tourism
- Memorialization of victims on both sides

**Institution Strengthening**

- Continued international support for Palestinian governance capacity
- Anti-corruption mechanisms with international oversight
- Judicial reform and rule of law development
- Civil society support in both societies

### 3.3 Long-Term Framework

**Treaty Monitoring**

- International monitoring body with mandate to verify compliance
- Regular review conferences for agreement adjustment
- Dispute resolution mechanism with binding authority
- Periodic reporting to the UN Security Council

**US Ongoing Role**

- Security guarantees as part of agreement framework
- Continued economic assistance transitioning to development partnerships
- Diplomatic support for implementation
- Congressional reporting requirements

### Phase 3 Success Metrics

| Metric | Target | Measurement |
|--------|--------|-------------|
| Territorial implementation | All territorial transfers completed per timeline | International verification |
| Security transition | Palestinian security forces operational; no significant security incidents | Monitoring body |
| Jerusalem arrangements | Agreed governance functioning; access guaranteed | International monitors |
| Refugee resolution | Compensation fund operational; returns/resettlement proceeding | UNHCR/successor |
| Economic development | Palestinian GDP growth; employment targets; infrastructure milestones | World Bank reporting |
| Normalization | Full diplomatic relations between Israel and normalizing states | Diplomatic markers |
| Violence reduction | Significant decline in conflict-related casualties | UN reporting |

## Resource Requirements

### Financial Resources

| Category | Estimated Cost (10 years) | Primary Funders |
|----------|--------------------------|-----------------|
| Gaza reconstruction | $50-80 billion | International donors (US, EU, Gulf states, World Bank) |
| Palestinian state building | $10-20 billion | US, EU, Arab states |
| Settler relocation/compensation | $15-30 billion | Israel (with international support) |
| Refugee compensation | $30-50 billion | International fund (US, EU, Arab states, Israel) |
| Security arrangements | $5-10 billion | US, international partners |
| Economic development | $20-40 billion | International investment; private sector |
| Diplomacy and monitoring | $1-3 billion | US, UN, international partners |

**Total estimated range: $130-230 billion over 10 years**

For context:

- The US spent approximately $2.4 trillion on the Iraq and Afghanistan wars
- Annual US military aid to Israel is $3.8 billion (plus supplementals totaling tens of billions since October 7)
- The cost of continued conflict—in security spending, regional instability, diplomatic costs, and human suffering—is incalculable but substantial

### Diplomatic Resources

- Senate-confirmed Special Envoy with dedicated staff (20-30 professionals)
- Expanded Embassy/Consulate capacity in Israel and Palestine
- Dedicated negotiating teams for each track
- Regional coordination staff
- Congressional liaison team

### Political Capital

This is the most constrained resource. Sustained US engagement in Israeli-Palestinian peacemaking requires:

- Presidential commitment extending across administrations
- Bipartisan congressional support (or at least acquiescence)
- Public understanding of why resolution serves US interests
- Willingness to absorb political costs from lobbying groups on all sides
- Alliance with international partners to share the diplomatic burden

## What Could Accelerate This Roadmap

Certain developments could create opportunities for faster progress:

- **Israeli political shift**: A government willing to accept Palestinian statehood would transform possibilities. This could result from elections, coalition realignment, or the political impact of ICC proceedings
- **Palestinian reunification**: Unified Palestinian leadership with democratic legitimacy could negotiate credibly. This requires elections and a framework for including Hamas in political process
- **Saudi normalization leverage**: If Saudi Arabia firmly conditions normalization on Palestinian statehood, this creates significant Israeli incentive for concessions
- **Regional threat convergence**: Shared Iranian threat may create circumstances where Israeli-Arab security cooperation requires Palestinian issue resolution
- **International legal pressure**: ICC and ICJ proceedings could increase the diplomatic and legal costs of the status quo
- **Generational change**: New leadership on both sides may bring different perspectives and less attachment to failed positions
- **Economic crisis**: A significant economic shock (to Israel from international isolation, or to the region from energy transition) could change cost-benefit calculations

## What Could Derail This Roadmap

- **Major security event**: A large-scale attack by either side could collapse support for engagement
- **US domestic politics**: Administration change, congressional opposition, or single-issue lobbying could prevent sustained policy
- **Regional war**: Conflict with Iran or broader regional conflagration would overwhelm bilateral concerns
- **Palestinian Authority collapse**: Loss of Palestinian institutional capacity would eliminate a negotiating partner
- **Israeli annexation**: Formal annexation of West Bank territory would fundamentally change conditions
- **International attention shift**: A major global crisis could reduce bandwidth for Israeli-Palestinian engagement

## Honest Assessment

This roadmap is ambitious and faces enormous obstacles. No US administration has sustained the level of engagement described here, and domestic political incentives work against it. The current political conditions in Israel and among Palestinians make even Phase 1 difficult.

But the alternative—continued drift toward a one-state reality with permanent disenfranchisement—serves neither US interests nor the interests of the peoples who live there. The question is not whether this roadmap is easy to implement but whether the costs of continued inaction are acceptable.

Previous US policy has oscillated between disengagement and episodic engagement without leverage. This roadmap proposes a different approach: sustained engagement with clear principles, measurable benchmarks, and willingness to apply consequences for noncompliance by either party. Whether the US will summon the political will for this approach remains to be seen.
