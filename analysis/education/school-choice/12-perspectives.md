# School Choice: Political Perspectives Analysis

## Overview

This document analyzes school choice policy -- including charter schools, vouchers, education savings accounts, magnet schools, controlled choice, accountability frameworks, and integration mechanisms -- through nine distinct political perspectives. The core questions are: should public funds flow to private and independently operated schools, what accountability standards should apply to all publicly funded education, how should choice mechanisms be designed to promote or avoid segregation, and what balance should be struck between parental autonomy and the public interest in education? Each perspective receives engagement-consistency ratings and position scores, culminating in compromise proposals that bridge multiple viewpoints.

School choice is distinctive in American education politics because it generates unusual cross-cutting alliances. The movement has drawn support from conservative market advocates, religious families seeking faith-based education, and urban Black and Latino parents frustrated with failing schools -- while opposition has united teacher unions, civil liberties groups concerned about church-state separation, and rural communities worried about the viability of their public schools. The recent wave of universal education savings account programs in seven states, combined with the Supreme Court's *Carson v. Makin* (2022) ruling requiring inclusion of religious schools in public tuition programs, has accelerated the debate. The evidence base on choice outcomes is mixed -- some charter networks show significant gains, the average charter performs similarly to comparable public schools, and voucher programs show neutral to negative test score effects -- making this a domain where ideology often outpaces evidence on all sides.

### Engagement Consistency (1-5)

This metric assesses how reliably a perspective's stated positions align with its underlying principles, and how stable those positions are when concerns are addressed. Higher scores indicate more predictable negotiating partners.

| Level | Label | Indicators |
|-------|-------|------------|
| 5 | Highly Consistent | Stated positions reflect underlying principles; positions stable when concerns addressed |
| 4 | Mostly Consistent | Principles generally align with positions; minor strategic flexibility |
| 3 | Mixed Consistency | Some positions principled, others reflect coalition pressures or unstated concerns |
| 2 | Low Consistency | Positions shift frequently; stated concerns differ from underlying priorities |
| 1 | Unpredictable | Positions appear driven primarily by opposition dynamics rather than principles |

**Coalition-Building Considerations:**

- Alignment gap: When stated concerns differ from underlying priorities, addressing stated concerns may not secure support
- Position stability: Perspectives with shifting positions require ongoing negotiation
- Principle consistency: Perspectives that apply principles uniformly are more predictable partners
- Internal diversity: Some perspectives contain factions with different priorities

## Perspective Analyses

### Conservative Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 3/5 - Mixed Consistency**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Stated motivation is parental empowerment and competition; actual motivations include weakening teacher unions (a Democratic constituency), advancing religious school funding, and serving donors in the education management industry; some conservatives genuinely prioritize parental choice |
| Principle consistency | "Accountability" is championed for public schools but resisted for voucher schools receiving public funds; "competition" is invoked but regulations that would create level playing fields are opposed; "limited government" does not extend to limiting government subsidies to private schools |
| Goalpost stability | Moderate; have shifted from voucher-focused advocacy to ESAs, and from targeted low-income programs to universal eligibility; accountability positions weaken as choice programs expand |
| Zero-sum behavior | Significant; school choice is treated as a political weapon against teacher unions rather than purely as education policy; support for choice programs correlates more strongly with union opposition than with evidence of student outcomes |

**Justification:** Conservatives have a genuine philosophical commitment to parental choice and market mechanisms in education, but this commitment is entangled with political calculations regarding teacher unions, religious school funding, and donor interests. The clearest inconsistency is on accountability: conservatives who demand rigorous testing, transparency, and consequences for traditional public schools often oppose applying the same standards to voucher schools. This gap between stated principles (accountability, taxpayer protection) and actual positions (minimal oversight for choice programs) reduces the consistency score. Additionally, the shift from targeted programs for low-income families to universal ESAs -- which disproportionately subsidize families already in private schools -- reveals that equity concerns are secondary to program expansion.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Republican state legislatures expanding ESAs to universal eligibility while removing accountability requirements (Arizona, Florida, Iowa)
- Heritage Foundation and American Federation for Children advocacy for vouchers with minimal accountability
- Conservative opposition to requiring state testing for voucher students in Indiana and Louisiana after initially supporting it
- Republican platform language on school choice emphasizing parental rights over accountability
- DeVos family and Koch network political spending tied to choice expansion and union weakening

- Charter school expansion with reduced regulatory barriers
- Backpack funding model where 100% of per-pupil funding follows the student
- Parental notification requirements rather than government accountability standards
- A-F school grading systems to empower market-based accountability

#### Coalition Potential

- **Natural allies:** Libertarian (on market mechanisms and deregulation), Religious Right (on parental rights and religious school funding)
- **Potential bridges:** Centrist (on charter quality and information systems), Populist (on parental empowerment framing)
- **Unlikely partners:** Progressive, Democratic Socialist (on accountability standards and integration requirements)

### Liberal Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 4/5 - Mostly Consistent**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Genuine commitment to public education and equity; concern about segregation and accountability gaps is principled and evidence-based; some tension between union political alliance and education reform impulses |
| Principle consistency | Equality, accountability, and social responsibility principles consistently applied; support for public education aligns with broader public goods framework; civil rights concerns about segregation are principled |
| Goalpost stability | Largely stable; evolved from cautious openness to charters (1990s-2000s) toward more skeptical position as evidence accumulated; positions have become more clearly defined rather than shifting opportunistically |
| Zero-sum behavior | Low; willing to support well-designed charter schools and controlled choice; do not oppose all forms of choice, but insist on accountability and equity safeguards |

**Justification:** Liberals maintain a principled position that public funds require public accountability, and that education policy must advance equity and integration. Their support for charter schools with strong accountability, magnet schools, and controlled choice -- combined with opposition to unaccountable voucher programs -- reflects consistent application of their values. The slight reduction from a perfect score reflects the political alliance with teacher unions, which occasionally constrains liberal positions on charter schools and choice mechanisms that could benefit students. Some liberal organizations have become reflexively anti-choice rather than pro-accountability, reducing the nuance of their engagement.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Democratic platform support for charter school accountability and opposition to unaccountable vouchers
- Center for American Progress publications distinguishing between well-regulated and unregulated choice
- NAACP moratorium resolution on charter school expansion (2016) reflected civil rights concerns about segregation
- Liberal support for community schools as alternative investment strategy
- State-level liberal advocacy for voucher accountability in Indiana, Louisiana, and D.C.

### Progressive Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 5/5 - Highly Consistent**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | School choice opposition rooted in structural analysis of privatization, segregation, and the commodification of public goods; stated and actual motivations are aligned |
| Principle consistency | Economic justice, structural reform, and democratic control principles applied uniformly; opposition to choice as privatization is consistent with opposition to privatization in other public sectors |
| Goalpost stability | Highly stable; consistent advocacy for fully funded public schools, desegregation, and community schools; positions have not shifted with political winds |
| Zero-sum behavior | None; positions are based on structural analysis rather than opposition dynamics |

**Justification:** Progressives have the most structurally coherent analysis of school choice. Their argument -- that choice mechanisms represent the privatization of a public good, that market competition in education increases inequality, and that the choice movement has been driven by wealthy donors and corporate interests rather than organic parent demand -- is directly supported by the root causes analysis in the project files. The identification of philanthropic influence ($2 billion+), for-profit interest group dynamics, and the deliberate weakening of public schools through funding diversion aligns precisely with progressive structural critique. Progressives also have the most consistent position on accountability: they support the same rigorous standards for all schools and do not selectively apply accountability to advance a political agenda.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Network for Public Education advocacy against voucher expansion and for public school investment
- Rethinking Schools publications on choice as privatization and segregation mechanism
- Economic Policy Institute analysis of charter school labor practices and outcomes
- Progressive support for community schools as alternative model (full-service, democratically governed)
- Consistency between opposition to education privatization and opposition to privatization of other public services

### Libertarian Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 4/5 - Mostly Consistent**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Genuine philosophical commitment to individual liberty and free markets in education; opposition to government monopoly on schooling is principled and consistent with broader libertarian framework |
| Principle consistency | Free market and individual choice principles applied consistently; support for vouchers and ESAs with minimal regulation follows logically from libertarian philosophy; however, acceptance of public funding for private education creates tension with pure anti-government-spending principles |
| Goalpost stability | Stable; have consistently advocated for maximum parental choice with minimal government oversight since Friedman's 1955 voucher proposal |
| Zero-sum behavior | Low; positions are philosophically driven rather than politically strategic |

**Justification:** Libertarians have the most philosophically coherent pro-choice position. Their argument that education is a service best delivered through market competition, and that parents rather than government should determine where children are educated, follows directly from core libertarian principles. The slight reduction from a perfect score reflects the tension between accepting public funding (vouchers, ESAs) and the libertarian ideal of separating education from government entirely. Pure libertarian philosophy would advocate for eliminating public education funding, not redirecting it to private schools -- but pragmatic libertarians have embraced school choice as a step toward educational freedom. This pragmatism is reasonable but introduces a minor inconsistency.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Cato Institute education publications consistently advocate for educational freedom with minimal regulation
- Milton Friedman Foundation (now EdChoice) long-term advocacy for vouchers and choice
- Reason Foundation school choice research and advocacy

- Consistent opposition to government accountability for private schools receiving vouchers

#### Alternative Proposals

- Full and equitable public school funding through progressive taxation, eliminating conditions that create demand for choice
- Community schools with comprehensive services, democratic governance, and strong teacher unions
- Phase-out of voucher and ESA programs; redirect funding to public schools
- Charter school reintegration into public districts with elected board governance and teacher union representation
- Federal desegregation enforcement under Title VI with funding consequences
- Teacher investment: higher pay, smaller class sizes, professional autonomy, and collective bargaining rights
- Public pre-K through college education eliminating privatization across the education spectrum

#### Coalition Potential

- **Natural allies:** Progressive (on public goods and anti-privatization), Liberal (on accountability and integration)
- **Potential bridges:** Populist (on anti-billionaire framing and community school investment), Centrist (on evidence-based community school strategy)
- **Unlikely partners:** Conservative, Libertarian, Religious Right (on fundamental disagreement about privatization, market mechanisms, and public funding for private/religious schools)

## Compromise Proposals

### Compromise 1: School Quality Transparency and Information Act

**Bridges:** Conservative + Liberal + Progressive + Libertarian + Constitutionalist + Populist + Centrist + Religious Right + Democratic Socialist

**Core Agreement:** All families deserve access to comparable, reliable information about all schools -- public, charter, and private -- to make informed educational decisions. Transparency serves both the market accountability model (where parents are consumers who need information) and the democratic accountability model (where taxpayers deserve to know how public funds are used).

**Policy Description:**

State-level legislation establishes a comprehensive school information system covering all schools that receive public funds through any mechanism (enrollment, voucher, ESA, tax credit). Required information includes: standardized academic outcomes (state assessment results or approved nationally norm-referenced alternatives, reported with comparable methodology), enrollment demographics by race, income, English learner and disability status, teacher credentials and experience, financial information including total revenue by source and total expenditures by category, suspension and expulsion rates, and graduation rates where applicable. Information is published on a state education department website in a common format with plain-language explanations, available in multiple languages, and searchable by geography and school type. Schools may voluntarily provide additional information including mission statements, specialized programs, and parent satisfaction surveys. Navigator programs funded at $50 million per year nationally assist families in underserved communities with interpreting information and navigating school options. Religious schools may present their mission and values alongside required data without government editorial control.

**Concern Mapping:**

| Perspective | How This Addresses Their Concerns |
|-------------|-----------------------------------|
| Conservative | Information empowers parental choice; market accountability works better with informed consumers; minimal regulatory burden |
| Liberal | Comparable information across sectors enables genuine accountability; navigator programs ensure equitable access to information |
| Progressive | Transparency exposes disparities in how choice programs serve different populations; demographic data reveals segregation patterns |
| Libertarian | Information provision is less intrusive than regulatory compliance mandates; enhances market functioning |
| Constitutionalist | State-level implementation respects federalism; information provision is less constitutionally problematic than regulatory mandates |
| Populist | Exposes both failing public schools and scam private operators; gives working families tools to evaluate options |
| Centrist | Evidence-based approach to enabling informed choice; builds foundation for quality improvement across all sectors |
| Religious Right | Religious schools present their distinctive mission alongside data; no government control of curriculum or hiring |
| Democratic Socialist | Transparency reveals the realities of choice programs including segregation, funding diversion, and for-profit extraction |

**Concessions:**

| Perspective | What They Give Up |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Conservative | Accept mandatory reporting requirements for private schools receiving public funds |
| Liberal | Accept that transparency alone does not ensure accountability; enforcement provisions are separate |
| Progressive | Accept that transparency framework may legitimize choice programs they would prefer to eliminate |
| Libertarian | Accept government-mandated disclosure rather than purely voluntary information sharing |
| Constitutionalist | Accept state-level reporting mandates for private schools in choice programs |
| Populist | Accept that information systems require bureaucratic infrastructure |
| Centrist | Accept that some schools may game reporting without stronger enforcement mechanisms |
| Religious Right | Accept mandatory disclosure of financial and outcome data for publicly funded religious schools |
| Democratic Socialist | Accept information-based approach rather than structural change to choice programs |

**Viability Assessment:**

- **Coalition Size:** 9/9 perspectives
- **High Consistency Ratio:** 5/9 coalition members rated 4+ consistency
- **Durability:** Very high; transparency is the single area of broadest agreement in school choice policy; the combination of information provision (market framing) and public disclosure (democratic accountability framing) creates mutual buy-in; constituency demand for school quality information is universal
- **Implementation Path:** State legislation; multiple states already have partial school information systems that could be expanded; federal incentive grants could encourage adoption; low political controversy relative to other choice reforms

### Compromise 2: Charter School Quality and Authorization Standards Act

**Bridges:** Liberal + Progressive + Conservative + Centrist + Populist + Constitutionalist + Democratic Socialist

**Core Agreement:** Charter school quality depends on authorizer quality, and strong authorization standards serve everyone's interest -- parents deserve quality schools, taxpayers deserve accountability for public funds, and the charter sector's credibility depends on closing failing schools and preventing fraud.

**Policy Description:**

State-level legislation establishes minimum standards for all charter school authorizers: organizational capacity to evaluate applications and oversee schools, evidence-based approval criteria using performance contracts with measurable academic, financial, and operational goals, annual school reviews including site visits, consistent renewal and revocation standards, independence from schools authorized (no conflicts of interest), and published annual reports on authorized school performance. Schools ranking in the bottom 15% statewide for three consecutive years, or committing financial malfeasance, are subject to non-renewal or revocation. Authorizers failing to meet standards within two years lose authorization authority. For-profit management contracts are limited to no more than 50% of a school's budget. Charter schools participate in the state's unified enrollment system where one exists. A federal companion bill reforms the Charter Schools Program to prioritize grants to states with strong authorizer standards, require grantees to demonstrate that authorized schools serve student populations comparable to nearby public schools, and publish annual reports on charter school performance compared to comparable public schools.

**Concern Mapping:**

| Perspective | How This Addresses Their Concerns |
|-------------|-----------------------------------|
| Liberal | Strong accountability for publicly funded schools; closing failing charters protects students; for-profit limitations address exploitation concerns |
| Progressive | Closure of failing charters and for-profit limitations improve the charter landscape while it exists; demographic reporting reveals segregation |
| Conservative | Quality authorization improves the charter sector's credibility and outcomes; strong charters make the case for choice; bad charters undermine it |
| Centrist | NACSA best practices have the strongest evidence base for improving charter quality; evidence-driven approach |
| Populist | Stops scam operators from exploiting families and taxpayers; protects communities from bad schools |
| Constitutionalist | State-level implementation respects federalism; federal provisions limited to the Charter Schools Program under existing Spending Clause authority |
| Democratic Socialist | Quality standards and closure authority reduce the worst abuses of the charter system; for-profit limitations address extraction concerns |

**Concessions:**

| Perspective | What They Give Up |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Liberal | Accept that quality authorization strengthens rather than restricts the charter sector |
| Progressive | Accept that improving charter authorization legitimizes the charter model they would prefer to end |
| Conservative | Accept for-profit limitations and demographic reporting requirements; accept that some charter applications will be denied |
| Centrist | Accept bottom-15% closure threshold that may be more aggressive than evidence-only analysis would suggest |
| Populist | Accept that quality standards require bureaucratic infrastructure |
| Constitutionalist | Accept federal Charter Schools Program conditions that influence state authorization practices |
| Democratic Socialist | Accept charter quality improvement rather than charter elimination |

**Viability Assessment:**

- **Coalition Size:** 7/9 perspectives
- **High Consistency Ratio:** 5/7 coalition members rated 4+ consistency
- **Durability:** High; charter authorization quality has broad support because all stakeholders benefit from better charter outcomes; NACSA standards are already widely recognized; evidence from states with strong authorizers (Massachusetts, Colorado, Indiana post-reform) demonstrates effectiveness; Libertarian and Religious Right exclusion from coalition is stable but their opposition is moderate (scores of 3-4) rather than fundamental
- **Implementation Path:** State legislation with federal incentives through Charter Schools Program; NACSA provides ready-made standards and technical assistance; multiple states have already adopted elements; bipartisan sponsors available in most state legislatures

### Compromise 3: Community Schools and Public School Investment Act

**Bridges:** Liberal + Progressive + Populist + Centrist + Democratic Socialist + Religious Right (partial)

**Core Agreement:** Investing in public schools with comprehensive community services addresses root causes of school failure and reduces demand for choice alternatives by making neighborhood schools excellent. Community schools benefit children and families regardless of political perspective.

**Policy Description:**

Federal legislation authorizes $2 billion annually for community school transformation grants, available to high-poverty schools that commit to the four pillars: integrated student supports (health clinics, mental health services, nutrition programs), expanded learning time and opportunities (before/after school, summer programs), family and community engagement (parent education, community partnerships, democratic governance councils), and collaborative leadership and practices (strong professional development, teacher retention strategies). States receive block grants with flexibility to target highest-need communities. Community schools must partner with community organizations including faith-based organizations for wraparound services. Evaluation provisions require rigorous outcome measurement including academic achievement, attendance, family engagement, and community health metrics. A complementary school facility improvement program provides $3 billion for building repair and modernization in high-poverty districts. Separate state-level legislation establishes weighted student funding formulas with additional weights for low-income students (0.3), English learners (0.3), and students with disabilities (0.5-2.0 depending on need), with hold-harmless provisions protecting districts from enrollment loss due to choice programs during a five-year transition period.

**Concern Mapping:**

| Perspective | How This Addresses Their Concerns |
|-------------|-----------------------------------|
| Liberal | Massive investment in public schools addresses root causes; equity-focused weighted funding helps highest-need schools; comprehensive services improve outcomes |
| Progressive | Community schools represent structural reform; democratic governance councils empower communities; addresses conditions that create demand for choice |
| Populist | Invests in the neighborhood schools that working families depend on; keeps communities together; visible improvement in schools their kids attend |
| Centrist | Community schools have strong evidence base (RAND, Learning Policy Institute); weighted funding is best-practice school finance; evaluation provisions ensure accountability |
| Democratic Socialist | Public investment in public institutions; democratic governance; teacher investment; community control; addresses underfunding that is root cause of school failure |
| Religious Right (partial) | Faith-based organizations explicitly eligible as community school partners; family engagement provisions respect parental authority; moral and spiritual supports can be included through faith partnerships |

**Concessions:**

| Perspective | What They Give Up |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Liberal | Accept that community schools do not directly address accountability gaps in existing choice programs |
| Progressive | Accept incremental investment strategy rather than immediate full-equity funding; accept partnership with faith-based organizations |
| Populist | Accept significant new government spending on public education |
| Centrist | Accept large spending levels that may face appropriations challenges; accept hold-harmless provisions that may protect some inefficient districts |
| Democratic Socialist | Accept incremental investment within existing system rather than transformative restructuring; accept faith-based partnerships |
| Religious Right | Accept investment in public schools rather than private school choice; accept that community schools may include health and social services they have reservations about |

**Viability Assessment:**

- **Coalition Size:** 6/9 perspectives (with Religious Right support partial and conditional)
- **High Consistency Ratio:** 4/6 coalition members rated 4+ consistency
- **Durability:** Moderate to high; community schools have a strong and growing evidence base; the combination of federal investment with state flexibility and faith-based inclusion broadens appeal; the coalition faces pressure from the Conservative-Libertarian-Religious Right bloc that views community schools as competing with choice programs; durability depends on demonstrating measurable results that reduce public demand for alternative schools
- **Implementation Path:** Federal legislation with bipartisan rural and urban support; community school legislation has been introduced in multiple Congresses with modest bipartisan co-sponsorship; state-level weighted funding reform has bipartisan precedents; the key political challenge is framing community school investment as complementary to rather than competing with existing choice programs

## Strategic Implications

### Most Viable Coalition

The most effective coalition for school choice reform operates on three tiers. The broadest coalition (8-9 perspectives) can advance *transparency and information systems* -- reforms that serve both market and democratic accountability frameworks. The mid-range coalition (6-7 perspectives) can advance *charter authorization quality standards* and *community school investment*, which command support from the center-left plus moderate conservatives and populists. The narrowest but most impactful coalition (4-5 perspectives) is needed for *universal accountability standards for voucher schools* and *controlled choice for integration*, which require a center-left majority willing to confront the Conservative-Libertarian-Religious Right bloc. The strategic sequence should move from broad-coalition reforms that build trust and institutional capacity toward narrower-coalition reforms that address the most significant accountability and segregation gaps.

### Key Obstacles

- **Accountability asymmetry:** The political coalition that supports choice expansion (Conservative, Libertarian, Religious Right) opposes accountability for choice programs, while the coalition that supports accountability (Liberal, Progressive, Democratic Socialist) opposes choice expansion. This creates a structural stalemate where choice programs expand without adequate oversight.
- **Religious liberty as shield:** The *Carson v. Makin* decision and Religious Right advocacy position religious liberty as a barrier to accountability for publicly funded religious schools. Civil rights and special education requirements that apply universally to public schools may not survive constitutional challenge when applied to religious schools.
- **Philanthropic asymmetry:** Pro-choice philanthropic spending ($1 billion+) vastly outmatches pro-public-education advocacy, creating an uneven political playing field that makes accountability legislation difficult to advance.
- **Evidence selectivity:** All perspectives selectively cite research that supports their positions, making evidence-based consensus difficult despite a substantial research base.
- **Rural dynamics:** School choice works differently in rural communities where there is no critical mass for multiple school options; rural legislators in both parties are often skeptical of choice programs that could threaten the viability of the community school.

### Low Consistency Partners

Populist (2/5) and, to a lesser extent, Conservative (3/5) and Religious Right (3/5) perspectives are the least predictable on school choice. Populist positions are highly framing-dependent: the same accountability reform framed as "stopping scam artists from exploiting families" gets support while framed as "government regulation of parental choice" gets opposition. The strategic approach should frame accountability and transparency measures using anti-fraud, anti-corruption, and taxpayer protection language that resonates with populist anti-elite instincts. Conservative engagement is most reliable on charter quality (where they share interest in defending the sector's credibility) and least reliable on voucher accountability (where political alliances with the Religious Right and libertarian donors create cross-pressure). Religious Right engagement is driven primarily by religious school funding and autonomy concerns; reforms that do not threaten religious school independence gain their support or at least neutrality, while those that apply secular accountability to religious schools face fundamental opposition.

### Recommended Approach

1. **Lead with transparency:** The School Quality Transparency and Information Act (Compromise 1) commands 9/9 perspective support and establishes the institutional infrastructure for accountability. Information systems make all subsequent reforms easier to build and harder to oppose because they create public evidence of both successes and failures across all school types.

2. **Strengthen charter authorization simultaneously:** The Charter School Quality and Authorization Standards Act (Compromise 2) commands 7/9 perspective support and has the strongest evidence base linking authorization quality to student outcomes. This is achievable at the state level and creates a culture of accountability within the choice sector that makes broader accountability legislation less threatening.

3. **Invest in public school alternatives:** The Community Schools and Public School Investment Act (Compromise 3) addresses root causes of demand for choice by making neighborhood public schools excellent. This reframes the debate from "choice versus no choice" to "investment in quality across all sectors" and reduces the political salience of the choice debate by giving families good options within the public system.

4. **Build the case for universal accountability:** As transparency systems reveal performance disparities and charter authorization demonstrates that accountability improves quality, the case for extending accountability standards to voucher schools strengthens. The School Choice Accountability Act remains the most important legislative reform but requires a center-left coalition that can overcome Religious Right and Conservative opposition.

5. **Use evidence strategically:** The research and evaluation provisions in all three compromise proposals generate evidence that can shift the debate over time. Rigorous studies of community schools, charter authorization, and voucher accountability create facts on the ground that are harder to dismiss than ideological arguments.

6. **Separate the charter and voucher debates:** Charter schools operate under public authorization with some degree of accountability; voucher and ESA programs often operate with virtually none. The strategic case for distinguishing between these sectors is strong: charter accountability has 7/9 perspective support while voucher accountability has only 4-5/9. Building quality standards for charters first creates precedent and public expectation for accountability that can later extend to vouchers.

7. **Frame integration as quality:** Controlled choice and integration measures are among the most contested reforms, but the evidence that diverse schools benefit all students (including White and affluent students) provides a quality framing that transcends the equity framing. Communities that adopt controlled choice should be supported as demonstrations of a model that improves outcomes for everyone, not just as equity interventions.
