# School Choice: Stakeholders

## Overview

School choice policy involves a complex web of stakeholders with varying interests, power, and resources. Understanding who benefits, who bears costs, and who has influence is essential to navigating policy debates.

## Primary Stakeholders

### Families and Students

**The Choosers**

Families are the central actors in school choice, though their experiences and interests vary widely.

| Family Type | Choice Interests | Choice Challenges |
|-------------|------------------|-------------------|
| Low-income urban | Escape failing schools | Transportation, information access |
| Middle-class suburban | Specialized programs | Limited options; satisfied with TPS |
| Affluent | Already have choice | May benefit from universal programs |
| Religious | Faith-based education | Availability, accreditation |
| Special needs | Appropriate services | Quality concerns; reduced rights |
| English learner | Language services | Limited information in home language |
| Rural | Any alternative | Geographic barriers |

**Parent Motivations**

| Motivation | % of Choosers (Survey Data) |
|------------|----------------------------|
| Academic quality | 55% |
| Safety/discipline | 25% |
| Special programs | 20% |
| Religious instruction | 20% |
| School culture/values | 35% |
| Location/convenience | 30% |
| Dissatisfaction with assigned school | 45% |

**Student Impacts**

| Impact | Evidence |
|--------|----------|
| Academic achievement | Varies by school; charters mixed, vouchers neutral to negative |
| Satisfaction | Higher for choice students and parents |
| Social-emotional | Mixed; depends on school quality |
| Peer effects | Stratification may harm those left behind |
| Long-term outcomes | Limited data; some positive graduation effects |

### Traditional Public School Districts

**The Affected Institutions**

School districts bear significant consequences from choice expansion.

| Stakeholder Group | Interest | Impact from Choice |
|-------------------|----------|-------------------|
| Superintendents | System viability, performance | Budget pressure, enrollment volatility |
| School boards | Democratic governance | Reduced scope, political challenges |
| Central office staff | Employment, influence | Downsizing, restructuring |
| Principals | School leadership | Competition for students, uncertainty |

**District Financial Impacts**

| Enrollment Loss | Revenue Impact | Response Required |
|----------------|----------------|-------------------|
| 5% | -6-7% revenue | Minor adjustments |
| 10% | -12-15% revenue | Staff cuts, program reduction |
| 20% | -25-30% revenue | School closures, major restructuring |
| 30%+ | -35%+ revenue | System viability threatened |

**Stranded Costs**

| Cost Category | Why It's Stranded |
|---------------|-------------------|
| Facilities | Buildings don't shrink with enrollment |
| Administration | Some overhead required regardless of size |
| Transportation | Fixed routes, buses |
| Debt service | Bond payments are fixed |
| Special programs | Minimum viable size for AP, arts, etc. |

### Teachers and School Staff

**The Workforce**

Teachers are affected differently depending on where they work.

| Teacher Group | Interests | Choice Impact |
|---------------|-----------|---------------|
| TPS teachers | Job security, working conditions | Layoffs if enrollment drops |
| TPS union leaders | Member protection, influence | Threatened by choice expansion |
| Charter teachers | Employment, working conditions | More jobs; often non-union, lower pay |
| Private school teachers | Religious mission, employment | Vouchers increase funding |

**Working Condition Comparison**

| Condition | Traditional Public | Charter | Private (Voucher) |
|-----------|-------------------|---------|-------------------|
| Salary | Higher average | 10-15% lower | 20-30% lower |
| Benefits | Strong | Variable | Often limited |
| Job security | Tenure protections | At-will | At-will |
| Union representation | Common | Rare | Very rare |
| Class size | Regulated | Unregulated | Variable |
| Credentials required | Full certification | Often reduced | Often none |
| Work hours | Defined | Often longer | Variable |

### Charter School Operators

**The Providers**

Charter operators range from small nonprofits to large national networks.

| Operator Type | Characteristics | Examples |
|---------------|-----------------|----------|
| Standalone nonprofit | Single school, community-based | Most charters by count |
| Nonprofit CMO | Network of schools, replicable model | KIPP, Success Academy, Uncommon |
| For-profit EMO | Corporate management, profit motive | K12 Inc., Imagine Schools |
| Hybrid | Nonprofit with for-profit vendors | Various arrangements |

**Operator Interests**

| Interest | Manifestation |
|----------|---------------|
| Revenue growth | Advocate for higher per-pupil funding |
| Regulatory freedom | Resist accountability mandates |
| Facilities access | Push for building funding, co-location |
| Expansion opportunity | Support lifting charter caps |
| Political protection | Lobby against unfavorable legislation |

**Major Charter Networks**

| Network | Schools | Students | Approach |
|---------|---------|----------|----------|
| KIPP | 280 | 120,000 | No-excuses, extended day |
| Success Academy | 50 | 20,000 | High expectations, test focus |
| Uncommon Schools | 55 | 20,000 | Data-driven, college prep |
| Achievement First | 40 | 15,000 | College prep, character |
| IDEA Public Schools | 130 | 70,000 | Texas-based, rapid growth |
| Aspire Public Schools | 40 | 16,000 | California-focused |

### Private School Operators

**The Voucher Beneficiaries**

Private schools receiving voucher funds include diverse institutions.

| School Type | Characteristics | Voucher Participation |
|-------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Catholic schools | Largest private sector; declining enrollment | High; vouchers are lifeline |
| Protestant Christian | Growing sector; evangelical focus | High; religious motivation |
| Non-sectarian private | Elite academies; diverse missions | Variable; some avoid |
| Jewish, Islamic, other | Religious minority schools | Moderate |
| Special needs private | Specialized services | Growing |
| New "voucher schools" | Created to capture voucher funding | Concerning quality |

**Private School Interests**

| Interest | Concern |
|----------|---------|
| Revenue | Voucher funding supplements tuition |
| Autonomy | Resist accountability requirements |
| Admission control | Maintain ability to select students |
| Religious freedom | Protect faith-based practices |
| Accreditation | Avoid mandated standards |

## Stakeholder Conflicts

### Key Tensions

| Conflict | Parties | Issues |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Funding | Districts vs. Choice operators | Per-pupil allocation |
| Accountability | Reformers vs. Private schools | Testing requirements |
| Facilities | Charters vs. Districts | Building access |
| Enrollment | Choice schools vs. Districts | Student recruitment |
| Governance | Charter boards vs. Elected boards | Democratic control |
| Labor | Unions vs. Charter management | Working conditions |
| Civil rights | Advocates vs. Voucher schools | Discrimination |

### Unrepresented Interests

| Interest | Why Unrepresented |
|----------|-------------------|
| Students left in TPS | Can't vote, no organization |
| Future taxpayers | No current voice |
| Communities of concentrated poverty | Limited political power |
| Special education students in voucher schools | Reduced legal protections |
| Non-English-speaking families | Information barriers |
