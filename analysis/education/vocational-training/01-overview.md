# Vocational Training: Overview

## Executive Summary

The United States has systematically underinvested in vocational and technical education for decades, creating a skills gap that harms workers, employers, and the broader economy. While other developed nations like Germany, Switzerland, and Austria have maintained robust apprenticeship systems that provide pathways to middle-class careers, America has pushed nearly all young people toward four-year college degrees regardless of aptitude, interest, or labor market demand.

The result is a mismatch of historic proportions: millions of workers lack the skills employers need, while millions more are overqualified and underemployed with college degrees that provided neither practical skills nor economic return. Meanwhile, 30 million jobs that require more than a high school diploma but less than a four-year degree go unfilled or are filled by workers without proper training.

Reversing this trend requires challenging deep-seated cultural assumptions that equate dignity and success with college attendance, rebuilding institutional capacity in high schools and community colleges, creating meaningful partnerships between education and industry, and establishing credential systems that provide genuine labor market value.

## Scope

### What This Topic Covers

- **Career and Technical Education (CTE)**: Programs in high schools and community colleges teaching occupational skills
- **Apprenticeships**: Earn-while-you-learn programs combining on-the-job training with classroom instruction
- **Workforce development**: Adult training and retraining programs for displaced workers
- **Industry-recognized credentials**: Certifications, licenses, and competency-based qualifications
- **Employer partnerships**: Sector strategies and industry-led training initiatives
- **Community college workforce programs**: Two-year degrees and certificates designed for employment
- **Middle-skill occupations**: Jobs requiring postsecondary training but not bachelor's degrees
- **International comparisons**: German, Swiss, and other apprenticeship models
- **Federal policy**: Perkins Act, WIOA, and other federal workforce legislation

## The Core Problem

### Skills Mismatch in Numbers

| Metric | Value | Implication |
|--------|-------|-------------|
| Middle-skill job openings | ~30 million | Significant unmet demand |
| Workers with appropriate training | ~20 million | Gap of ~10 million workers |
| College graduates underemployed | ~41% | Oversupply of bachelor's degrees |
| Apprentices in U.S. (2023) | ~600,000 | Compare to 1.5M in Germany (1/4 population) |
| High school CTE concentrators | ~4 million | Declining share of students |
| Community college workforce completions | ~800,000/year | Most don't complete programs |

### The College-for-All Problem

The conventional wisdom since the 1980s has been that every young person should pursue a four-year college degree:

**Assumptions vs. Reality**

| Assumption | Reality |
|------------|---------|
| College is the only path to middle class | Only 35% of jobs require bachelor's degree |
| All students benefit from college | Only ~60% of enrollees complete within 6 years |
| College degrees guarantee good jobs | 41% of recent grads are underemployed |
| Non-college paths lead to poverty | Skilled trades can earn $50,000-$100,000+ |
| Academic skills trump practical skills | Employers cite skills gaps as major concern |

### Who Is Harmed

**Students**

- Channeled toward college regardless of fit
- Accumulate debt for degrees with poor returns
- Lack exposure to career alternatives
- Enter labor market unprepared for available jobs

**Workers**

- Displaced workers lack retraining options
- Credentials don't transfer across employers
- Training access depends on employer willingness
- Career changers face high barriers

**Employers**

- Cannot find workers with needed skills
- Must train from scratch or poach from competitors
- Lack influence over educational curricula
- Face aging workforce in skilled trades

**Economy**

- Productivity losses from unfilled positions
- Wage inflation in skilled occupations
- Reduced competitiveness versus countries with better training
- Infrastructure projects delayed by workforce shortages

### Occupational Structure

| Skill Level | Education Typically Required | % of Jobs | Examples |
|-------------|------------------------------|-----------|----------|
| High-skill | Bachelor's or higher | ~35% | Engineers, physicians, lawyers |
| Middle-skill | Some college, certificate, associate's | ~35% | Nurses, electricians, technicians |
| Entry-level | High school or less | ~30% | Retail, food service, laborers |

The U.S. education system produces too many graduates for high-skill jobs and too few for middle-skill positions.

## International Comparison

### Apprenticeship Penetration

| Country | Apprentices | % of Youth | Duration | Completion |
|---------|-------------|------------|----------|------------|
| Germany | 1.5 million | ~50% | 2-3.5 years | ~90% |
| Switzerland | 230,000 | ~70% | 3-4 years | ~95% |
| Austria | 120,000 | ~40% | 2-4 years | ~85% |
| United Kingdom | 850,000 | ~25% | 1-4 years | ~60% |
| **United States** | **600,000** | **~5%** | **1-4 years** | **~50%** |

### Key Differences

| Feature | German Model | U.S. Approach |
|---------|--------------|---------------|
| Employer role | Central, legally mandated | Peripheral, voluntary |
| Start age | 15-16 | Mostly adults |
| Integration with education | Parallel tracks from age 10 | Add-on to college-prep |
| National standards | Unified across sectors | Fragmented, inconsistent |
| Social prestige | Respected pathway | Stigmatized |
| Government support | Substantial subsidies | Minimal funding |
| Union involvement | Required by law | Rare |

## Why This Matters

### For Economic Mobility

Vocational training provides one of the clearest pathways from poverty to middle-class stability. Workers who complete apprenticeships earn median wages above $60,000 with minimal debt, often outearning college graduates in their first decade of work.

### For Regional Economic Development

Many communities, particularly in rural and post-industrial areas, cannot attract employers due to workforce skill deficits. Robust vocational training can make these areas competitive again.

### For Dignity of Work

The stigmatization of non-college pathways implicitly devalues the millions of Americans who build, repair, and maintain the physical world. Elevating vocational training affirms the dignity of skilled manual labor.

### For Fiscal Sustainability

Public investment in education currently flows disproportionately to four-year institutions serving students who may not complete degrees. Reallocating toward higher-ROI vocational programs would benefit both students and taxpayers.

### For Employers and Competitiveness

American companies compete against firms in countries with superior workforce training systems. The skills gap handicaps U.S. manufacturing, construction, and healthcare sectors.
