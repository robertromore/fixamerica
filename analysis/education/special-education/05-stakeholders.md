# Special Education: Stakeholders

## Primary Stakeholders

### Students with Disabilities

**Population**: 7.5 million students ages 3-21 served under IDEA Part B; 400,000 infants/toddlers under Part C.

**Interests**

- Access to quality education alongside non-disabled peers
- Individualized services that meet their needs
- Respectful treatment and high expectations
- Preparation for adult life, employment, and independence
- Voice in decisions about their education

**Power and Influence**

- Limited direct power in system designed for adults
- Growing self-advocacy movement, especially among autistic individuals
- Youth transition councils emerging in some states
- Legal rights through IDEA procedural safeguards
- Influence primarily through parent advocacy

**Key Concerns**

- Being included rather than segregated
- Having IEPs that are meaningful, not paperwork exercises
- Access to grade-level curriculum with appropriate supports
- Teachers who understand their needs and believe in them
- Transition services that lead to real outcomes

### Parents and Families

**Population**: Parents and guardians of 7.5 million students with disabilities; extended family members involved in care and support.

**Interests**

- Quality education and services for their children
- Genuine partnership with schools in IEP development
- Clear information about rights and options
- Affordable access to evaluation and advocacy support
- Smooth transitions between grade levels and to adult services

**Power and Influence**

- Legal rights as IEP team members
- Due process and complaint mechanisms
- Political voice through advocacy organizations
- Economic power to secure private evaluations, attorneys
- Collective action through parent groups

**Key Concerns**

- Schools not providing services written in IEP
- Feeling excluded from meaningful participation in IEP meetings
- Lack of information about rights and options
- Cost of independent evaluations and legal representation
- Inadequate transition planning for adult life

### Variation Among Parents

**Parents with Resources**

- Can afford private evaluations, advocates, attorneys
- Know how to navigate system and assert rights
- Can pursue due process and litigation
- Can place children in private schools and seek reimbursement
- More likely to secure appropriate services

**Parents Without Resources**

- Depend on school-provided evaluations
- May not know rights or how to enforce them
- Cannot afford private alternatives or legal help
- Accept whatever schools offer
- Less likely to receive appropriate services

**Parents of Color**

- May face additional barriers to navigation
- Cultural factors may affect approach to disability
- Language barriers for immigrant families
- History of distrust with schools due to discriminatory practices
- Under-representation in parent advocacy organizations

## Education Professionals

### Special Education Teachers

**Population**: Approximately 470,000 special education teachers nationwide.

**Interests**

- Manageable caseloads that allow individualized attention
- Competitive compensation for specialized work
- Administrative support and reasonable paperwork
- Professional development and collaboration opportunities
- Resources to meet student needs

**Power and Influence**

- Union representation in most states
- Professional expertise in IEP development
- Control over day-to-day instruction
- Can influence placement recommendations
- Voice in policy through professional organizations

**Key Concerns**

- Caseloads too high to provide quality services
- Paperwork consuming instructional time
- Lack of resources and administrative support
- Feeling blamed when students do not make progress
- Isolation from colleagues

### General Education Teachers

**Population**: 3.2 million general education teachers, majority of whom have students with disabilities in their classrooms.

**Interests**

- Support for including students with disabilities
- Training in differentiation and accommodation
- Manageable class sizes and adequate aides
- Collaboration time with special education staff
- Protection from liability

**Power and Influence**

- Required IEP team members (since 1997)
- Union representation
- Gatekeeping over general education access
- Can influence placement recommendations
- Implementation of inclusion ultimately in their hands

**Key Concerns**

- Inadequate training for inclusion
- No support or resources for differentiation
- Large class sizes make individualization difficult
- Special education students seen as "not my responsibility"
- Fear of being blamed for student failures

### Paraprofessionals

**Population**: 500,000+ paraprofessionals (aides) working in special education.

**Interests**

- Living wage and benefits
- Training for their responsibilities
- Clear role definition and supervision
- Respect and professional recognition
- Career advancement opportunities

**Power and Influence**

- Limited power as hourly workers
- Some union representation
- Direct contact with students gives informal influence
- Often know students better than teachers who see them less

**Key Concerns**

- Low pay (often $12-15/hour)
- Minimal training for complex responsibilities
- Expected to provide instruction without teacher credentials
- High turnover due to low pay and lack of support
- May be assigned to students without regard for match

### Related Service Providers

**Population**: Speech-language pathologists, occupational therapists, physical therapists, school psychologists, counselors, social workers, and others providing specialized services.

**Interests**

- Professional autonomy and standards
- Reasonable caseloads
- Collaboration with educational staff
- Compensation competitive with medical settings
- Resources to provide quality services

**Power and Influence**

- Professional licensure and expertise
- IEP team membership for relevant decisions
- Control over specialized evaluation and intervention
- Can recommend services and placement

**Key Concerns**

- School caseloads often higher than recommended
- Pressure to minimize service recommendations
- Compensation lower than medical/private practice
- Limited time for collaboration with teachers
- Being pulled between compliance and quality

### School Administrators

**Principals**

- Balance building-level priorities
- Implement inclusion or maintain segregation
- Allocate building resources
- Create culture of inclusion or resistance
- Supervise special and general education staff

**Special Education Directors**

- Manage district special education programs
- Allocate district resources
- Navigate compliance requirements
- Develop local policies and procedures
- Respond to due process and complaints

**Superintendents and School Boards**

- Set district priorities
- Allocate budgets across programs
- Make policy decisions
- Respond to community pressure
- Accountable for outcomes

**Key Concerns**

- Compliance costs and litigation risk
- Pressure to reduce special education spending
- Staffing shortages
- Balancing special and general education needs
- Managing parent expectations

## Government Stakeholders

### U.S. Department of Education

**Office of Special Education Programs (OSEP)**

- Administers IDEA and distributes funds
- Monitors state compliance
- Issues guidance and regulations
- Provides technical assistance
- Makes state determinations

**Office for Civil Rights (OCR)**

- Enforces disability civil rights in education
- Investigates complaints of discrimination
- Monitors compliance with Section 504, ADA, IDEA civil rights provisions
- Issues guidance on civil rights obligations

**Key Concerns**

- Balancing enforcement with state/local autonomy
- Limited capacity for monitoring
- Political pressure on enforcement approach
- Coordinating with other federal agencies

### State Education Agencies

**Population**: 50 state education agencies plus D.C. and territories, each with special education division.

**Interests**

- Meeting federal requirements to receive IDEA funds
- Minimizing state liability
- Effective use of state resources
- Student outcomes and school improvement

**Power and Influence**

- Administer state share of special education funding
- Monitor district compliance
- Issue state regulations and guidance
- Adjudicate state complaints
- Determine state performance indicators

**Key Concerns**

- Federal requirements with inadequate federal funding
- District capacity and compliance
- Staffing and resources for monitoring
- Balancing compliance with improvement focus
- Political pressure from various stakeholders

### Local School Boards

**Population**: ~13,000 local school boards governing public school districts.

**Interests**

- Educational quality for all students
- Fiscal responsibility to taxpayers
- Compliance with legal requirements
- Community satisfaction
- Staff support

**Power and Influence**

- Set local policy within state/federal constraints
- Approve budgets including special education
- Hire superintendent and set direction
- Respond to community concerns
- Can prioritize or deprioritize special education

## Advocacy Organizations

### National Organizations

**Council for Exceptional Children (CEC)**

- Largest professional association for special educators
- Sets professional standards
- Advocates for policy and funding
- Provides professional development
- Publishes research and practice guidance

**National Center for Learning Disabilities (NCLD)**

- Advocacy for students with learning disabilities
- Parent resources and advocacy
- Policy advocacy at federal and state levels
- Research and data analysis

**The Arc**

- Advocacy for people with intellectual and developmental disabilities
- Founded by parents in 1950
- State and local chapters nationwide
- Policy advocacy and direct services
- Self-advocacy support

**Autism Society of America**

- Advocacy for autistic individuals and families
- Public awareness and education
- Policy advocacy
- Support services and resources

**National Down Syndrome Society**

- Advocacy for individuals with Down syndrome
- Policy advocacy for inclusion
- Parent resources
- Research support

**Autistic Self-Advocacy Network (ASAN)**

- Led by autistic individuals
- "Nothing about us without us" philosophy
- Policy advocacy for civil rights
- Opposition to some traditional autism interventions
- Promotion of neurodiversity perspective

### Legal Advocacy Organizations

**National Disability Rights Network (NDRN)**

- Network of Protection and Advocacy (P&A) agencies in each state
- Federally mandated legal advocacy
- Individual case representation
- Systemic advocacy and litigation
- Monitoring of institutions

**Disability Rights Education and Defense Fund (DREDF)**

- National disability civil rights law and policy center
- Litigation and advocacy
- Technical assistance
- Policy analysis

**Council of Parent Attorneys and Advocates (COPAA)**

- Network of attorneys and advocates representing families
- Training and support for advocates
- Policy advocacy for parent rights
- Resources for families

**Bazelon Center for Mental Health Law**

- Advocacy for people with mental disabilities
- Litigation and policy advocacy
- Includes education rights work

### State and Local Organizations

- State-level parent training and information centers (PTIs)
- Community parent resource centers (CPRCs)
- State chapters of national organizations
- Local parent support groups
- Independent advocates and attorneys

## Research and Academic Community

### University Research Centers

**IRIS Center (Vanderbilt University)**

- Free resources on evidence-based practices
- Training modules for educators
- Materials on IDEA implementation

**National Center on Educational Outcomes (NCEO)**

- Research on assessment and accountability for students with disabilities
- State assessment policy analysis
- Graduation rate research

**SWIFT Center**

- Research on inclusive education transformation
- Technical assistance for systems change
- Multi-tiered systems of support

**University Centers for Excellence in Developmental Disabilities (UCEDDs)**

- Network of 67 centers nationwide
- Research, training, and service
- Technical assistance to states and communities

### Think Tanks and Policy Organizations

**National Conference of State Legislatures (NCSL)**

- Nonpartisan support for state legislatures
- Special education policy analysis
- Model legislation and best practices

**Education Commission of the States (ECS)**

- Research on education policy
- 50-state policy comparisons
- Technical assistance to states

**Center for American Progress**

- Progressive policy research
- Special education funding and equity analysis
- Policy recommendations

**Thomas B. Fordham Institute**

- Conservative/reform-oriented research
- Special education accountability analysis
- School choice for students with disabilities

## Business and Community

### Employers

- Future employers of students with disabilities
- Interest in workforce preparation
- May partner with schools on transition
- Concerns about hiring and accommodation costs

### Healthcare Providers

- Pediatricians who identify developmental concerns
- Specialists who diagnose conditions
- Private therapists who provide services
- Medical perspective on disability

### Disability Service Providers

- Vocational rehabilitation agencies
- Adult service providers
- Residential facilities
- Day programs
- Transition to adult services

## Power Analysis

### Most Powerful Stakeholders

| Stakeholder | Source of Power |
|-------------|-----------------|
| Congress | Controls federal IDEA funding |
| State legislatures | Control state funding and policy |
| School administrators | Control implementation |
| Affluent parents | Resources to navigate system |
| Teachers' unions | Collective bargaining, political influence |

### Least Powerful Stakeholders

| Stakeholder | Barriers to Power |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Students with disabilities | Youth, disability, dependence on system |
| Low-income parents | Lack resources, knowledge, time |
| Paraprofessionals | Low pay, limited voice |
| Parents of color | Historical exclusion, distrust |

### Key Dynamics

- Funding decisions made by those who do not experience consequences
- Those with most to gain (students) have least voice
- Those with resources secure services; those without do not
- Compliance focus benefits administrators over students
- Advocacy fragmented by disability category
