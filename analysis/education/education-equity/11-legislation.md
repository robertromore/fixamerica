# Education Equity: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

This document provides model legislation for advancing education equity at federal and state levels. Given the constraints of *Parents Involved in Community Schools v. Seattle* (2007) and *Milliken v. Bradley* (1974), the legal strategies focus on permissible approaches: socioeconomic integration, funding equity, discipline reform, and strengthening civil rights enforcement.

## Federal Legislation

### Educational Equity and Integration Act (Model)

**Purpose**: Incentivize voluntary school integration and strengthen equity requirements in federal education programs.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Educational Equity and Integration Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that—
(1) school segregation by race and socioeconomic status has been
    increasing since the late 1980s;
(2) students in racially and socioeconomically isolated schools
    have less access to experienced teachers, rigorous coursework,
    and adequate facilities;
(3) integrated schools benefit all students academically and
    socially;
(4) voluntary integration programs have demonstrated success in
    many communities;
(5) federal investment can expand access to integrated educational
    opportunities; and
(6) closing the comparability loophole in Title I would redirect
    billions in resources to high-poverty schools.

SEC. 3. VOLUNTARY INTEGRATION INCENTIVE GRANTS.

(a) PROGRAM AUTHORIZED.—The Secretary of Education shall award
    grants to local educational agencies and consortia of agencies
    to develop and implement voluntary integration programs.

(b) AUTHORIZED ACTIVITIES.—Grant funds may be used for—
    (1) development and operation of magnet schools;
    (2) controlled choice student assignment systems;
    (3) interdistrict transfer programs;
    (4) regional magnet schools serving multiple districts;
    (5) transportation to support integration;
    (6) professional development on integrated instruction;
    (7) community engagement and outreach; and
    (8) evaluation and continuous improvement.

(c) APPLICATION REQUIREMENTS.—An applicant shall submit—
    (1) analysis of current racial and socioeconomic isolation
        in schools;
    (2) description of proposed integration strategy;
    (3) evidence that the strategy is likely to reduce isolation;
    (4) plan for community engagement;
    (5) plan for sustainability beyond grant period; and
    (6) evaluation plan with measurable goals.

(d) PRIORITY.—The Secretary shall give priority to applications
    that—
    (1) demonstrate greatest potential to reduce isolation;
    (2) serve students in persistently segregated schools;
    (3) include interdistrict cooperation;
    (4) demonstrate strong community support; and
    (5) include plans for sustainability.

(e) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.—There are authorized to be
    appropriated $2,000,000,000 for each of fiscal years 2025
    through 2030.

SEC. 4. TITLE I COMPARABILITY REFORM.

(a) AMENDMENT.—Section 1118(c) of the Elementary and Secondary
    Education Act (20 U.S.C. 6321(c)) is amended—
    (1) by striking paragraph (2) and inserting the following:
    "(2) COMPARABILITY REQUIREMENT.—
    "(A) IN GENERAL.—A local educational agency may receive
        funds under this part only if State and local funds
        used in schools served under this part, when combined
        with funds from sources other than this part, are at
        least equal to State and local funds used in schools
        not served under this part.
    "(B) ACTUAL EXPENDITURES.—For purposes of this paragraph,
        expenditures shall include actual personnel expenditures
        based on salaries of individual staff members, not
        average salary calculations.
    "(C) REPORTING.—Each local educational agency shall
        annually report actual per-pupil expenditures by school,
        including all personnel costs."

(b) EFFECTIVE DATE.—The amendments made by this section shall
    take effect with respect to appropriations for fiscal year
    2027 and succeeding fiscal years.

SEC. 5. CIVIL RIGHTS DATA AND ENFORCEMENT.

(a) ENHANCED DATA COLLECTION.—The Department of Education
    Civil Rights Data Collection shall include—
    (1) per-pupil expenditures by school, including actual
        personnel costs;
    (2) teacher experience and credentials by school;
    (3) advanced course enrollment by student subgroup;
    (4) discipline incidents and consequences by student subgroup;
    (5) gifted program enrollment by student subgroup;
    (6) school-level segregation indices; and
    (7) chronic absenteeism by student subgroup.

(b) ENFORCEMENT RESOURCES.—There are authorized to be appropriated
    $200,000,000 for each fiscal year for the Office for Civil
    Rights to—
    (1) investigate civil rights complaints;
    (2) conduct proactive compliance reviews;
    (3) provide technical assistance to districts; and
    (4) enforce remedial agreements.

SEC. 6. DISCIPLINE EQUITY.

(a) DATA TRANSPARENCY.—Each local educational agency receiving
    federal funds shall annually report—
    (1) out-of-school suspension rates by school and student
        subgroup;
    (2) expulsion rates by school and student subgroup;
    (3) referrals to law enforcement by school and student
        subgroup;
    (4) school-based arrests by school and student subgroup; and
    (5) use of exclusionary discipline for subjective offenses.

(b) INTERVENTION THRESHOLD.—When a local educational agency shows
    significant disparities in discipline rates by race, disability
    status, or other protected characteristic, the Office for
    Civil Rights shall—
    (1) require a discipline equity review;
    (2) provide technical assistance on alternatives to
        exclusionary discipline; and
    (3) monitor implementation of discipline reforms.

SEC. 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

Except as otherwise provided, this Act takes effect on the date
of enactment.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 3**: Creates substantial funding for voluntary integration
- **Section 4**: Closes the comparability loophole, redirecting billions to high-poverty schools
- **Section 5**: Improves data collection and enforcement capacity
- **Section 6**: Creates accountability for discipline disparities

### Teacher Equity Act (Model)

**Purpose**: Address inequitable distribution of experienced and qualified teachers.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Teacher Equity Act".

SEC. 2. TEACHER EQUITY PLANS.

(a) STATE PLANS.—As a condition of receiving funds under Part A
    of Title II of the Elementary and Secondary Education Act,
    each State shall submit a plan to ensure equitable access to
    effective teachers that includes—
    (1) analysis of teacher quality gaps between high-poverty
        and low-poverty schools, and high-minority and low-
        minority schools;
    (2) strategies for closing those gaps;
    (3) measurable progress indicators; and
    (4) timeline for achieving equitable distribution.

(b) REQUIRED STRATEGIES.—State plans shall include—
    (1) financial incentives for teaching in high-need schools;
    (2) working condition improvements in high-need schools;
    (3) residency programs preparing teachers for high-need
        schools;
    (4) support for early-career teachers; and
    (5) limits on forced transfers from high-need schools.

(c) REPORTING.—Each State shall annually report—
    (1) teacher experience distribution by school poverty level;
    (2) teacher credential status by school poverty level;
    (3) teacher vacancy rates by school; and
    (4) teacher turnover rates by school.

SEC. 3. TEACHER INCENTIVE FUND.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.—There are authorized to be appropriated
    $500,000,000 for each fiscal year to provide incentives
    for effective teachers to serve in high-need schools.

(b) ALLOWABLE USES.—Funds may be used for—
    (1) salary supplements of at least $10,000 annually;
    (2) loan forgiveness;
    (3) housing assistance;
    (4) professional development opportunities; and
    (5) reduced class sizes in participating schools.

SEC. 4. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect on the date of enactment.
```

## State Model Legislation

### School Integration Incentive Act (Model)

**Purpose**: Create state incentives for voluntary integration.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] School Integration
Incentive Act".

SECTION 2. FINDINGS.

The Legislature finds that—
(a) Diverse schools benefit all students;
(b) School segregation has been increasing in [State];
(c) Voluntary programs can promote integration without
    mandatory assignment; and
(d) State incentives can encourage district participation.

SECTION 3. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:
(a) "Integration goal" means a school composition within 15
    percentage points of the district or regional average by
    race and socioeconomic status.
(b) "Socioeconomically diverse school" means a school where
    neither low-income nor non-low-income students exceed
    70% of enrollment.

SECTION 4. INTEGRATION BONUS.

(a) A school district shall receive additional state aid of
    $500 per pupil for each student attending a school that
    meets integration goals.

(b) A regional magnet school serving students from multiple
    districts shall receive additional state aid of $1,000
    per pupil.

SECTION 5. INTERDISTRICT TRANSFER SUPPORT.

(a) The State shall fund transportation costs for voluntary
    interdistrict transfers that promote integration.

(b) Sending and receiving districts shall both receive per-
    pupil funding for transfer students.

SECTION 6. CONTROLLED CHOICE TECHNICAL ASSISTANCE.

(a) The Department of Education shall provide technical
    assistance to districts implementing controlled choice
    systems.

(b) The Department shall develop model controlled choice
    policies for district adoption.

SECTION 7. APPROPRIATION.

There is appropriated $[amount] for fiscal year [year] to
implement this Act.

SECTION 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect July 1, [year].
```

### School Funding Equity Act (Model)

**Purpose**: Reform state school funding for equity.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] School Funding Equity Act".

SECTION 2. WEIGHTED STUDENT FUNDING.

(a) BASE ALLOCATION.—Each student shall generate a base
    allocation of $[amount], adjusted annually for inflation.

(b) WEIGHTS.—Additional allocations shall be provided for—
    (1) Students eligible for free or reduced-price meals:
        25% additional;
    (2) English language learners: 30% additional;
    (3) Students with disabilities (tiered by need):
        (A) Tier 1: 50% additional;
        (B) Tier 2: 100% additional;
        (C) Tier 3: 200% additional;
    (4) Students in foster care: 25% additional;
    (5) Students experiencing homelessness: 25% additional.

(c) CONCENTRATION FACTOR.—Districts with more than 50%
    high-need students shall receive an additional 10%
    increase to all weights.

SECTION 3. LOCAL WEALTH EQUALIZATION.

(a) The State shall provide sufficient aid such that all
    districts can meet adequacy standards with a local
    tax rate no higher than the statewide average.

(b) No district shall receive less state aid than in the
    prior year (hold-harmless), except that hold-harmless
    protections shall be phased out over 5 years.

SECTION 4. WITHIN-DISTRICT EQUITY.

(a) Each district receiving state funds shall ensure that
    per-pupil spending at each school, including actual
    personnel costs, is within 5% of the district average
    after adjusting for student need.

(b) Districts shall annually publish per-pupil spending
    by school.

SECTION 5. ADEQUACY STUDY.

(a) The [State Education Agency] shall conduct a study every
    5 years to determine the cost of providing an adequate
    education.

(b) The base allocation shall be adjusted based on the
    adequacy study.

SECTION 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect July 1, [year], with full implementation
by July 1, [year + 3].
```

### Discipline Equity Act (Model)

**Purpose**: Reduce exclusionary discipline and disparities.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Discipline Equity Act".

SECTION 2. LIMITATIONS ON EXCLUSIONARY DISCIPLINE.

(a) OUT-OF-SCHOOL SUSPENSION.—No student in grades K-3 may
    be suspended out of school except for conduct that poses
    a serious and immediate safety threat.

(b) EXPULSION.—Expulsion shall be used only as a last resort
    after other interventions have been attempted.

(c) SUBJECTIVE OFFENSES.—Exclusionary discipline may not be
    used for subjective offenses such as defiance, disrespect,
    or dress code violations.

SECTION 3. REQUIRED ALTERNATIVES.

(a) Each school district shall implement restorative practices
    as an alternative to exclusionary discipline.

(b) Before any suspension exceeding 3 days, a school shall
    document attempts at alternative interventions.

SECTION 4. DATA COLLECTION AND REPORTING.

(a) Each district shall collect and publicly report discipline
    data disaggregated by race, disability status, English
    learner status, gender, and school.

(b) The Department of Education shall identify districts with
    significant disparities and require corrective action plans.

SECTION 5. SCHOOL RESOURCE OFFICERS.

(a) School resource officers may not be involved in routine
    student discipline matters.

(b) Any district employing SROs shall have a memorandum of
    understanding defining the SRO role.

(c) Districts shall report all school-based arrests and law
    enforcement referrals.

SECTION 6. TRAINING.

(a) All school staff shall receive annual training on—
    (1) Restorative practices;
    (2) Trauma-informed approaches;
    (3) Implicit bias; and
    (4) De-escalation techniques.

SECTION 7. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect on [date].
```

### Gifted Program Equity Act (Model)

**Purpose**: Ensure equitable access to gifted programs.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Gifted Program Equity Act".

SECTION 2. UNIVERSAL SCREENING.

(a) Each school district shall universally screen all students
    for gifted program eligibility at least once in grades K-2
    and once in grades 3-5.

(b) Screening shall not require parent nomination or teacher
    referral as a prerequisite.

SECTION 3. MULTIPLE CRITERIA.

(a) Gifted identification shall use multiple measures,
    including—
    (1) Nonverbal ability assessments;
    (2) Achievement measures;
    (3) Student work samples; and
    (4) Creativity assessments.

(b) No single measure shall be determinative.

SECTION 4. LOCAL NORMS.

(a) Districts shall use building-level norms in identification,
    ensuring students are compared to peers in similar contexts.

(b) The top 10% of students at each school shall be considered
    for gifted services based on local norms.

SECTION 5. REPORTING.

(a) Districts shall report gifted program enrollment by race,
    socioeconomic status, English learner status, and disability
    status.

(b) The Department shall identify districts with significant
    underrepresentation and require improvement plans.

SECTION 6. EFFECTIVE DATE.

This Act takes effect with the [year] school year.
```

## Regulatory Framework

### Department of Education Regulations

**Title I Comparability Regulations**

| Current Rule | Proposed Reform |
|--------------|-----------------|
| Exclude personnel from comparability | Include actual salaries |
| District-wide comparison | School-level comparison |
| Paper compliance | Actual expenditure equity |
| Limited enforcement | Active monitoring |

**Civil Rights Data Collection Expansion**

| New Element | Purpose |
|-------------|---------|
| Actual per-pupil spending | Document within-district inequity |
| Teacher equity metrics | Track distribution |
| Course access by school | Document opportunity gaps |
| Discipline detail | Enable disparity analysis |

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

**Federal Power**

| Basis | Application |
|-------|-------------|
| Spending Clause | Conditions on federal funds |
| Commerce Clause | Limited education application |
| 14th Amendment enforcement | Congressional authority |

**State Power**

| Basis | Application |
|-------|-------------|
| Education clauses | Most states require education |
| Equal protection (state) | Some states have stronger protections |
| General police power | Broad state authority over education |

### Avoiding *Parents Involved* Constraints

| Permissible | Prohibited |
|-------------|------------|
| Socioeconomic factors | Race of individual students |
| School composition goals (general) | Racial quotas |
| Site selection considering diversity | Individual race-based assignment |
| Magnet themes attracting diversity | Race as assignment factor |

### State Constitutional Claims

Many state constitutions provide stronger bases for equity claims:

| State | Provision | Interpretation |
|-------|-----------|----------------|
| Connecticut | Education clause + equal protection | Required integration (*Sheff*) |
| New Jersey | Thorough and efficient | Parity funding required |
| Texas | Efficient system | Finance reform required |

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Educational Equity and Integration Act

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Definition of "integration" | Districts could claim integration with minimal diversity | High |
| Sustainability requirements | Programs may end when grants expire | High |
| Voluntary participation | Districts can decline without consequence | Medium |
| Data gaming | Districts could manipulate reported demographics | Medium |
| Priority criteria subjectivity | Political influence on grant awards | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| No mandatory integration | Relies on voluntary participation | Constitutional constraints |
| Limited funding | $2B insufficient for national transformation | Political feasibility |
| Interdistrict obstacles | Milliken still blocks mandatory cross-district | Legal precedent |
| Sustainability concerns | Grant-funded programs often discontinued | Funding structure |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Define measurable integration standards**: Specify demographic targets and measurement methods
2. **Sustainability requirements**: Require matching local commitment and maintenance of effort
3. **Consequences for non-participation**: Consider differential treatment in other federal programs
4. **Independent data verification**: Third-party audits of reported demographics
5. **Competitive scoring transparency**: Publish scoring criteria and decisions

### Title I Comparability Reform

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Salary reporting manipulation | Districts could misclassify expenditures | High |
| Compliance window | Multi-year phase-in allows delay | Medium |
| Comparison methodology | Districts could game comparison calculations | Medium |
| Enforcement discretion | Administration may not enforce vigorously | High |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Only affects Title I recipients | Non-Title I schools not covered | Statutory scope |
| Within-district only | Doesn't address between-district inequity | Local control |
| Political resistance | Wealthy districts oppose | Interest group politics |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Standardized reporting format**: Uniform categories prevent manipulation
2. **Accelerated implementation**: Reduce phase-in period
3. **Clear comparison methodology**: Specify calculation in regulation
4. **Mandatory enforcement**: Remove discretion; automatic consequences
5. **Public transparency**: Real-time public access to spending data

### School Funding Equity Act (State Model)

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Hold-harmless provisions | Wealthy districts protected even during phase-out | High |
| Weight manipulation | Categories could be defined to benefit certain districts | Medium |
| Local supplement | Wealthy districts can still raise additional local funds | High |
| Adequacy study capture | Studies could be designed to minimize costs | Medium |
| Within-district exception | 5% variance allows significant gaps | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Local supplement not addressed | Wealthy districts maintain advantage | Local control ideology |
| Hold-harmless delays equity | Transition takes years | Political necessity |
| Adequacy vs. equity | Adequacy floor may perpetuate gaps | Different standards |
| Implementation complexity | Weighted funding hard to administer | Technical challenge |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Cap local supplements**: Limit additional local revenue above state formula
2. **Accelerate hold-harmless phase-out**: 3 years instead of 5
3. **Independent adequacy studies**: Remove political control of methodology
4. **Tighten within-district variance**: Reduce to 3% or less
5. **Equity targets**: Add explicit gap-closing goals beyond adequacy

### Discipline Equity Act (State Model)

#### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| "Safety threat" exception | Subjective interpretation could be abused | High |
| Documentation requirements | Paper compliance without real change | Medium |
| SRO scope creep | Memoranda could be broadly written | Medium |
| Training without accountability | Training completed but not applied | Medium |
| Data reporting delays | Annual reporting delays intervention | Medium |

#### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| No private right of action | Parents cannot sue for violations | Enforcement limitation |
| Disparity threshold undefined | "Significant" is subjective | Drafting ambiguity |
| Resource requirements | Restorative practices need funding | Unfunded mandate |
| Culture change slow | Legal change doesn't ensure practice change | Implementation reality |

#### Rectification Procedures

1. **Define safety exceptions narrowly**: List specific covered conduct
2. **Outcome monitoring**: Track actual discipline rates, not just documentation
3. **SRO limitations**: Enumerate prohibited activities
4. **Training effectiveness assessment**: Measure impact, not just completion
5. **Real-time data systems**: Quarterly reporting with automatic alerts
6. **Funding appropriation**: Dedicated funding for implementation

### General Implementation Concerns

#### Systemic Issues

| Issue | Proposed Solution |
|-------|-------------------|
| Political sustainability | Constitutional amendments where possible |
| Enforcement variation | Automatic triggers, reduced discretion |
| Data manipulation | Independent verification, penalties |
| Implementation capacity | Technical assistance, phased rollout |
| Backlash potential | Coalition building, incremental approach |

#### Sunset and Review Provisions

All provisions should include:

- **5-year comprehensive review** assessing effectiveness
- **Automatic adjustments** based on outcome data
- **Stakeholder input processes** for revision
- **Legislative reporting requirements** ensuring accountability
- **Independent evaluation funding** to assess impact
