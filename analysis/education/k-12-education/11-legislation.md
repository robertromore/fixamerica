# K-12 Public Education: Legislation and Legal Framework

## Overview

Reforming K-12 education requires legislative action at federal and state levels, along with regulatory implementation and potential constitutional amendments. This document provides model legislation, analysis of existing law, and identification of legal loopholes that undermine reform.

## Constitutional Considerations

### Federal Constitution

**Current Status**

- Constitution does not mention education
- *San Antonio v. Rodriguez* (1973): No federal constitutional right to education
- 10th Amendment reserves education to states
- Federal role limited to:
    - Spending Clause (conditions on funding)
    - Equal protection (discrimination cases)
    - 14th Amendment due process

**Implications for Reform**

- Federal legislation must work through funding conditions
- Cannot mandate most reforms directly
- Civil rights enforcement is strongest federal lever
- Constitutional amendment would be necessary for federal guarantee

### State Constitutions

**Every State Has Education Clause**

- Most require "thorough and efficient" or similar
- Basis for adequacy litigation
- Varying levels of court enforcement
- Some explicitly include funding requirements

**Examples of Strong Clauses**

- California: "a general diffusion of knowledge and intelligence being essential to the preservation of the rights and liberties of the people"
- New Jersey: "thorough and efficient system of free public schools"
- Texas: "efficient system" with "suitable" funding

## Federal Legislation

### Title I Equity Enhancement Act (Model)

**Purpose**: Increase federal investment in high-poverty schools and improve targeting of funds.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Title I Equity Enhancement Act".

SEC. 2. FINDINGS.

Congress finds that:
(a) Title I of the Elementary and Secondary Education Act was
    enacted in 1965 to provide additional resources to schools
    serving low-income students.
(b) Federal Title I funding has not kept pace with inflation
    or with the concentration of poverty in many schools.
(c) Current allocation formulas spread funds too thinly and
    fail to adequately target the highest-need schools.
(d) Adequate and equitable funding is essential to providing
    all students the opportunity to succeed.

SEC. 3. AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS.

(a) IN GENERAL.—There are authorized to be appropriated to
    carry out Title I, Part A of the Elementary and Secondary
    Education Act—
    (1) $40,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2025;
    (2) $50,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2026;
    (3) $60,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2027 and each
        subsequent fiscal year.

(b) ADJUSTMENT.—The amounts authorized in subsection (a)
    shall be adjusted annually for inflation as measured by the
    Consumer Price Index for All Urban Consumers.

SEC. 4. IMPROVED TARGETING.

(a) CONCENTRATION GRANTS.—Section 1124A of the Elementary
    and Secondary Education Act is amended to increase the
    concentration grant allocation to 50 percent of all Title I
    funds.

(b) POVERTY THRESHOLD.—For purposes of Title I, Part A—
    (1) a school shall be considered high-poverty if not less
        than 40 percent of enrolled students are from
        low-income families; and
    (2) a school shall be considered extremely high-poverty
        if not less than 75 percent of enrolled students are
        from low-income families.

(c) WEIGHTED ALLOCATIONS.—
    (1) Schools meeting the extremely high-poverty threshold
        shall receive an additional weight of 1.5 in
        calculating Title I allocations.
    (2) Schools meeting the high-poverty threshold but not
        the extremely high-poverty threshold shall receive
        an additional weight of 1.25.

SEC. 5. MAINTENANCE OF EQUITY.

(a) IN GENERAL.—As a condition of receiving funds under this
    Act, a State shall demonstrate that—
    (1) per-pupil State and local funding for schools serving
        the highest quartile of low-income students is not
        less than per-pupil funding for schools serving the
        lowest quartile of low-income students; or
    (2) the State is making annual progress toward meeting
        the standard in paragraph (1).

(b) WAIVER.—The Secretary may waive the requirement in
    subsection (a) for a State that demonstrates it is
    implementing a comprehensive school finance reform plan
    that will achieve equity within 5 years.

SEC. 6. ALLOWABLE USES.

Funds under this Act may be used for:
(a) High-dosage tutoring programs meeting evidence standards;
(b) Reduced class sizes, particularly in grades K-3;
(c) Full-service community school programs;
(d) Extended learning time initiatives;
(e) Recruitment and retention of effective teachers in
    high-poverty schools;
(f) Mental health and counseling services;
(g) Family engagement programs;
(h) Other evidence-based interventions.

SEC. 7. EVALUATION.

(a) The Secretary shall conduct rigorous evaluations of the
    effectiveness of programs funded under this Act.
(b) Such evaluations shall be submitted to Congress annually.
(c) Findings shall inform future allocation decisions.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 3**: Triples current Title I funding from approximately $18 billion
- **Section 4**: Better targets funds to highest-poverty schools
- **Section 5**: Requires states to address within-state inequities
- **Section 6**: Prioritizes evidence-based interventions

**Challenges**:

- Requires significant new federal spending
- States may resist maintenance of equity requirement
- Implementation and enforcement capacity needed

### Teacher Investment Act (Model)

**Purpose**: Address teacher shortages and improve teacher quality through federal investment in preparation, compensation, and retention.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Teacher Investment Act".

SEC. 2. TEACHER RESIDENCY EXPANSION.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.—There are authorized to be appropriated
    $500,000,000 annually for grants to establish, expand, or
    strengthen teacher residency programs.

(b) ELIGIBILITY.—To be eligible for a grant, a residency
    program shall—
    (1) partner with one or more high-need local educational
        agencies;
    (2) provide at least one full academic year of clinical
        experience in a high-need school;
    (3) provide intensive mentorship by an effective mentor
        teacher;
    (4) provide a living stipend to residents during training;
    (5) require a commitment to teach in high-need schools
        for not less than 4 years.

(c) PRIORITY.—The Secretary shall give priority to programs
    that—
    (1) recruit candidates from underrepresented backgrounds;
    (2) prepare teachers for shortage subject areas;
    (3) demonstrate strong retention outcomes.

SEC. 3. LOAN FORGIVENESS EXPANSION.

(a) HIGH-NEED SCHOOL LOAN FORGIVENESS.—
    (1) A teacher who has been employed full-time for 5
        consecutive years in a high-need school shall be
        eligible for forgiveness of 100 percent of the
        balance of qualifying federal student loans.
    (2) Partial forgiveness shall be available after 3 years
        of service at 50 percent of the loan balance.

(b) SHORTAGE AREA BONUS.—Teachers in designated shortage
    subject areas (including special education, mathematics,
    science, and bilingual education) shall be eligible for
    forgiveness after 3 years of service.

(c) AUTHORIZATION.—There are authorized to be appropriated
    such sums as necessary to carry out this section.

SEC. 4. HIGH-NEED SCHOOL INCENTIVE GRANTS.

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—The Secretary shall award grants to States
    to provide salary supplements for teachers in high-need
    schools.

(b) AMOUNT.—Grants shall provide not less than $10,000 annually
    per teacher serving in eligible high-need schools.

(c) STATE MATCH.—States shall provide a match of not less than
    50 percent of federal funds.

(d) AUTHORIZATION.—There are authorized to be appropriated
    $2,000,000,000 annually for grants under this section.

SEC. 5. TEACHER DIVERSITY GRANTS.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.—There are authorized to be appropriated
    $200,000,000 annually for grants to increase the diversity
    of the teacher workforce.

(b) ALLOWABLE ACTIVITIES.—
    (1) Grow-your-own programs recruiting community members;
    (2) Partnerships with minority-serving institutions;
    (3) Scholarships for underrepresented candidates;
    (4) Support services for candidates from diverse backgrounds.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 2**: Funds effective teacher preparation model
- **Section 3**: Provides strong incentive to serve in high-need schools
- **Section 4**: Directly addresses compensation gap
- **Section 5**: Addresses lack of teacher diversity

### Community Schools Expansion Act (Model)

**Purpose**: Scale full-service community schools as a strategy for supporting high-poverty communities.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SEC. 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "Community Schools Expansion Act".

SEC. 2. FULL-SERVICE COMMUNITY SCHOOLS.

(a) AUTHORIZATION.—There are authorized to be appropriated
    $3,000,000,000 for fiscal year 2025 and each fiscal year
    thereafter for full-service community schools grants.

(b) DEFINITION.—A full-service community school is a public
    school that—
    (1) participates in a community-based effort to coordinate
        educational, developmental, family, health, and other
        comprehensive services through community-based
        organizations and public and private partnerships;
    (2) provides access to such services during and outside
        of the school day;
    (3) involves collaborative leadership that includes
        families and community partners.

(c) REQUIRED COMPONENTS.—To receive funds, a community school
    shall provide—
    (1) integrated student supports (health, mental health,
        nutrition, counseling);
    (2) expanded learning time and opportunities;
    (3) active family and community engagement;
    (4) collaborative leadership and practice.

SEC. 3. TECHNICAL ASSISTANCE.

(a) The Secretary shall establish regional technical assistance
    centers to support community school implementation.
(b) There are authorized to be appropriated $50,000,000 annually
    for technical assistance under this section.

SEC. 4. EVALUATION.

The Secretary shall conduct rigorous evaluation of community
school implementation and outcomes, with annual reporting to
Congress.
```

## State Model Legislation

### School Finance Adequacy Act (Model)

**Purpose**: Establish a state school funding system that provides adequate and equitable resources to all students.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] School Finance Adequacy Act".

SECTION 2. FINDINGS.

The Legislature finds that:
(a) The [State] Constitution requires an adequate education for
    all students.
(b) The current funding system fails to provide adequate
    resources to many school districts.
(c) Funding disparities based on property wealth violate
    principles of equal opportunity.
(d) Students with greater needs require greater investment.

SECTION 3. ADEQUACY STUDY.

(a) The [State Board of Education/Legislative Fiscal Office]
    shall conduct a comprehensive study to determine the cost
    of providing an adequate education to all students.

(b) The study shall use multiple methodologies including:
    (1) Professional judgment panels;
    (2) Successful schools analysis;
    (3) Evidence-based resource models.

(c) The study shall be updated every 5 years.

SECTION 4. BASE STUDENT ALLOCATION.

(a) The base student allocation shall be set at the level
    determined by the adequacy study to provide an adequate
    education to a student with no additional needs.

(b) For [fiscal year], the base allocation shall be not less
    than [$X] per pupil.

(c) The base allocation shall be adjusted annually for
    inflation.

SECTION 5. WEIGHTED STUDENT FUNDING.

(a) Students with additional needs shall generate additional
    funding through the following weights:

    (1) Low-income students (eligible for free or reduced-price
        lunch): [0.25-0.40]
    (2) English learners: [0.25-0.50]
    (3) Students with disabilities: [cost-based, estimated
        0.50-2.00 depending on category]
    (4) Students in foster care: [0.15-0.25]

(b) Schools with concentrated poverty (75% or more low-income
    students) shall receive an additional per-pupil
    concentration supplement of [0.10-0.25] times the base.

SECTION 6. GEOGRAPHIC ADJUSTMENT.

The base allocation shall be adjusted for regional cost
differences based on [cost of living index/comparable wage
index] not to exceed [X%] above or below the statewide base.

SECTION 7. DISTRICT CONTRIBUTIONS.

(a) Each district shall levy a minimum local tax effort of
    [X mills] on assessed property value.

(b) The State shall provide sufficient funding to ensure that
    all districts, regardless of property wealth, receive the
    full adequate funding level.

(c) Districts may levy additional local taxes above the minimum
    to supplement state funding, provided that total per-pupil
    spending in any district does not exceed [150%] of the
    state average.

SECTION 8. HOLD HARMLESS AND PHASE-IN.

(a) No district shall receive less per-pupil funding in the
    first year of implementation than received in the prior
    year.

(b) The funding formula shall be fully implemented over a
    period of [5] years, with equal annual progress.

SECTION 9. ACCOUNTABILITY FOR USE OF FUNDS.

(a) Districts receiving increased funding shall demonstrate
    that funds are used for effective educational purposes.

(b) The [State Board] shall develop guidelines for allowable
    uses and required reporting.

(c) Districts failing to demonstrate effective use may be
    subject to technical assistance or intervention.

SECTION 10. ENFORCEMENT.

(a) Any citizen may bring an action in [State] court to
    enforce the provisions of this Act.

(b) The court may order the Legislature to appropriate
    sufficient funds to implement this Act.
```

**Explanation**:

- **Section 4-5**: Establishes adequacy floor with weights for need
- **Section 7**: Ensures state responsibility while allowing local supplement
- **Section 7(c)**: Limits ability of wealthy districts to spend excessively more
- **Section 10**: Provides enforcement mechanism

### Teacher Compensation and Pipeline Act (Model)

**Purpose**: Address teacher shortages through competitive compensation and improved preparation.

**Draft Text**:

```text
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.

This Act may be cited as the "[State] Teacher Compensation and
Pipeline Act".

SECTION 2. MINIMUM TEACHER SALARY.

(a) Effective [date], the minimum annual salary for a
    full-time teacher with a bachelor's degree and standard
    certification shall be not less than [$50,000-60,000,
    depending on state cost of living].

(b) This minimum shall be adjusted annually by the greater of:
    (1) The Consumer Price Index; or
    (2) The percentage increase in state median household income.

SECTION 3. SALARY SCHEDULE REQUIREMENTS.

(a) Each district shall maintain a salary schedule that
    provides for increases based on:
    (1) Years of experience, for at least [15] years; and
    (2) Advanced degrees and national board certification.

(b) The maximum salary on any district schedule shall be not
    less than [175%] of the minimum salary.

SECTION 4. HIGH-NEED SCHOOL SUPPLEMENTS.

(a) Teachers serving in designated high-need schools shall
    receive an annual supplement of not less than [$5,000].

(b) High-need schools are those with:
    (1) 75% or more students from low-income families; or
    (2) Historical difficulty in filling vacancies; or
    (3) Designation as a shortage area by the [State Board].

SECTION 5. TEACHER RESIDENCY PROGRAMS.

(a) The [State Board] shall establish teacher residency
    programs in partnership with institutions of higher
    education and high-need school districts.

(b) Residents shall receive:
    (1) A living stipend during the residency year;
    (2) Intensive mentorship;
    (3) Coursework integrated with clinical practice.

(c) Residents shall commit to teach in [State] high-need
    schools for [4] years following completion.

(d) There is appropriated [$X million] annually for this
    section.

SECTION 6. GROW YOUR OWN PROGRAMS.

(a) The [State Board] shall support programs to recruit
    community members, paraprofessionals, and others into
    teaching careers.

(b) Eligible programs shall:
    (1) Target members of the local community;
    (2) Provide financial support for preparation;
    (3) Require commitment to return to serve in local schools.

SECTION 7. WORKING CONDITIONS.

(a) Each district shall conduct annual working conditions
    surveys of teachers and staff.

(b) Results shall be publicly reported and used in school
    improvement planning.

(c) The [State Board] shall develop standards for acceptable
    working conditions including class sizes, planning time,
    and support staff.
```

## Regulatory Framework

### Federal Regulations Needed

**Title I Implementation**

| Area | Regulatory Need |
|------|-----------------|
| Evidence requirements | Define "strong," "moderate," "promising" evidence |
| Maintenance of equity | Specify measurement methodology |
| Allowable uses | Clarify eligible interventions |
| Reporting | Standardize data collection |

**Civil Rights Enforcement**

| Area | Regulatory Need |
|------|-----------------|
| Resource equity | Define metrics for comparison |
| Discipline disparities | Set intervention thresholds |
| Access to courses | Specify required offerings |
| School facilities | Establish minimum standards |

### State Regulations Needed

| Area | Regulatory Need |
|------|-----------------|
| Funding formula implementation | Calculate weights, distribute funds |
| Adequacy definition | Translate study findings to standards |
| Teacher certification | Set preparation requirements |
| School accountability | Define multiple measures |

## Loopholes, Shortcomings, and Rectification

### Potential Loopholes

| Loophole | Description | Severity |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| "Local enrichment" exceptions | Wealthy districts can exceed equity caps | High |
| Supplement not supplant manipulation | Districts shift funds to avoid requirements | High |
| Poverty measurement gaming | Use of school-level vs. actual poverty | Medium |
| Charter school exemptions | Charters may not be subject to same rules | Medium |
| Hold-harmless extensions | Transition provisions become permanent | Medium |
| Phase-in delays | Implementation timelines repeatedly extended | High |
| Adequacy study manipulation | Methodology choices can drive results | Medium |
| Working conditions survey gaming | Low response rates, biased questions | Low |

### Shortcomings

| Issue | Impact | Root Cause |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Enforcement mechanisms weak | Violations go unaddressed | Political resistance to sanctions |
| Funding dependent on appropriations | Authorized levels not actually funded | Budget constraints, competing priorities |
| Local control preserved | Inequities continue at district level | Constitutional and political limits |
| Teacher distribution not addressed | Incentives may be insufficient | Union contracts, working conditions |
| Integration not included | Segregation continues | Legal constraints, political opposition |
| Out-of-school factors ignored | Schools can't compensate alone | Scope limitations |

### Rectification Procedures

1. **Close "local enrichment" loophole**: Cap total per-pupil spending at percentage of state average; require recapture above cap

2. **Strengthen supplement not supplant**: Track actual resource allocation, not budgetary categories; require demonstration of additionality

3. **Improve poverty measurement**: Use individual-level data (direct certification); update annually

4. **Extend requirements to charters**: Apply funding formula and accountability to all public schools; require comparable reporting

5. **Sunset hold-harmless provisions**: Built-in expiration; require affirmative reauthorization

6. **Mandate implementation timelines**: Judicial enforcement if legislature fails to appropriate; citizen suit provisions

7. **Independent adequacy studies**: Conducted by nonpartisan entity; methodology specified in statute

8. **Working conditions enforcement**: Minimum response rates; third-party administration; consequences for violations

### General Implementation Concerns

| Concern | Mitigation |
|---------|-----------|
| Political will for funding | Build coalitions; litigation backup |
| Capacity to implement | Technical assistance; phased rollout |
| Gaming and compliance focus | Focus on outcomes, not just inputs |
| Administrator turnover | Institutionalize in regulation and contracts |
| Changing political environment | Constitutional protections where possible |

## Legal Considerations

### Constitutional Issues

**Federal**

- Must work through spending power; cannot mandate directly
- Equal protection claims difficult after *Rodriguez*
- Due process may support procedural protections

**State**

- Education clauses provide basis for adequacy claims
- Equal protection stronger in some state constitutions
- Separation of powers limits court remedies

### Preemption

| Federal Law | State Impact |
|-------------|--------------|
| ESSA | Sets minimum requirements; states can exceed |
| IDEA | Mandates services; states supplement |
| Civil Rights Acts | Floor for nondiscrimination |

### Enforcement Mechanisms

| Mechanism | Strength | Weakness |
|-----------|----------|----------|
| Federal funding conditions | Significant leverage | Can be waived; political |
| State constitutional litigation | Can require action | Courts can't appropriate |
| Citizen suit provisions | Access to courts | Resource-intensive |
| Administrative enforcement | Expert capacity | Varies by administration |
