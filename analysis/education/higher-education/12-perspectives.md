# Higher Education: Political Perspectives Analysis

## Overview

This document analyzes higher education reform -- specifically affordability, student debt, institutional accountability, access and equity, workforce alignment, and faculty labor conditions -- through nine distinct political perspectives. The core questions are: who should pay for higher education, how should institutions be held accountable for student outcomes, what role should the federal government play in regulating and financing post-secondary education, and how can the system be restructured to deliver on its promise of economic mobility? Each perspective receives engagement-consistency ratings and position scores, culminating in compromise proposals that bridge multiple viewpoints.

Higher education is distinctive within the broader education policy debate because it generates unusually complex cross-ideological tensions. Conservatives who champion market solutions must confront the fact that market mechanisms have failed spectacularly in higher education, producing a $1.75 trillion debt crisis with poor outcomes for many students. Liberals who advocate for public investment must contend with evidence that federal aid has enabled tuition inflation. Libertarians who oppose government involvement must acknowledge that credential inflation -- driven by employer behavior, not government mandates -- makes higher education effectively compulsory. And progressives who seek free public college must address whether subsidizing institutions without accountability reforms merely subsidizes dysfunction. These tensions make higher education reform simultaneously more necessary and more politically fraught than simpler policy domains.

| Score | Meaning |

| 3-4 | Substantial disagreement |

### Engagement Consistency (1-5)

This metric assesses how reliably a perspective's stated positions align with its underlying principles, and how stable those positions are when concerns are addressed. Higher scores indicate more predictable negotiating partners.

| Level | Label | Indicators |
|-------|-------|------------|
| 5 | Highly Consistent | Stated positions reflect underlying principles; positions stable when concerns addressed |
| 4 | Mostly Consistent | Principles generally align with positions; minor strategic flexibility |
| 3 | Mixed Consistency | Some positions principled, others reflect coalition pressures or unstated concerns |
| 2 | Low Consistency | Positions shift frequently; stated concerns differ from underlying priorities |
| 1 | Unpredictable | Positions appear driven primarily by opposition dynamics rather than principles |

**Coalition-Building Considerations:**

- Alignment gap: When stated concerns differ from underlying priorities, addressing stated concerns may not secure support
- Position stability: Perspectives with shifting positions require ongoing negotiation
- Principle consistency: Perspectives that apply principles uniformly are more predictable partners
- Internal diversity: Some perspectives contain factions with different priorities

## Perspective Analyses

### Conservative Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 2/5 - Low Consistency**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Stated concern about cost and accountability is genuine, but opposition to reforms often protects institutional status quo beneficiaries (wealthy alumni, endowed private colleges) while punishing students; "market solutions" rhetoric clashes with support for tax-exempt endowments and nonprofit status |
| Principle consistency | "Individual responsibility" applied to student borrowers but not to institutions that produced poor outcomes; "market solutions" invoked despite higher education's well-documented market failures; "limited government" does not extend to opposing the federal tax subsidies that benefit wealthy institutions |
| Goalpost stability | Low; conservative positions on higher education have shifted dramatically from supporting the GI Bill and Pell Grants to questioning whether college is worthwhile; culture war concerns increasingly drive positions on universities |
| Zero-sum behavior | Significant; opposition to student debt relief appears driven partly by resentment politics ("I paid mine off") rather than principled fiscal analysis; hostility toward universities as cultural institutions increasingly shapes policy positions |

**Justification:** Conservatives face a distinctive inconsistency on higher education. They simultaneously argue that college is overpriced (correct), that market forces should discipline institutions (reasonable in theory), and that the federal government should not intervene to fix the problem (which leaves the market failures in place). The culture war dimension -- viewing universities as liberal indoctrination centers -- increasingly drives conservative higher education positions in ways that disconnect from principled fiscal or market analysis. Conservative support for accountability measures (gainful employment, transparency) is genuine when applied to for-profit colleges but weakens when applied to traditional institutions with conservative alumni bases. The score of 2 reflects the growing gap between principled conservative analysis of higher education dysfunction and the increasingly culture-war-driven policy positions.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Heritage Foundation has published cogent analyses of administrative bloat and cost disease while opposing the federal interventions most likely to address them
- Republican support for for-profit college deregulation under the Trump administration contradicted stated accountability principles
- Conservative governors have cut state university funding while criticizing tuition increases, a directly contradictory combination
- Republican proposals to cap or eliminate the student loan interest deduction reflect fiscal conservatism; proposals to eliminate PSLF reflect resentment politics
- Growing conservative movement to defund universities perceived as politically hostile, divorced from outcome-based analysis

#### Alternative Proposals

- Eliminate federal student loan program and replace with private lending with income-share agreements
- End tax-exempt status for universities with endowments exceeding $1 billion per student unless they reduce tuition
- Require institutions to co-sign student loans (skin in the game)
- Expand apprenticeship and vocational alternatives with employer tax credits
- Deregulate accreditation to allow new entrants and innovative models
- Reduce regulatory compliance burden on institutions
- Performance-based state funding tied to completion and employment outcomes

#### Coalition Potential

- **Natural allies:** Libertarian (on deregulation and market mechanisms), Constitutionalist (on limiting federal role), Populist (on skepticism of universities as elite institutions)
- **Potential bridges:** Centrist (on accountability and transparency), Religious Right (on cultural concerns about universities)
- **Unlikely partners:** Progressive, Democratic Socialist (on free college, debt relief, and faculty labor)

### Liberal Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 4/5 - Mostly Consistent**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Genuine commitment to expanding access, reducing cost, and improving equity in higher education; positions follow directly from equality and social responsibility principles |
| Principle consistency | Government-as-problem-solver principle consistently applied; support for Pell expansion, free community college, and borrower protections all reflect core values; minor tension between supporting institutional autonomy and demanding accountability |
| Goalpost stability | Stable; has consistently advocated for expanded access, increased funding, and borrower protections through multiple administrations; debt relief positions have escalated over time but in a consistent direction |
| Zero-sum behavior | Low; willing to support reforms that benefit students across demographic and geographic lines; some tension between prioritizing higher education spending and other social investments |

**Justification:** Liberals maintain a coherent and largely consistent position on higher education reform. Their analysis that state disinvestment, inadequate federal aid, and market failures have produced the current crisis is well-supported by evidence. The slight reduction from a perfect score reflects two tensions: first, liberal support for institutional autonomy can conflict with the accountability measures needed to address poor outcomes; second, the emphasis on expanding access through existing institutional structures may not adequately address the structural dysfunction within those institutions. Liberals sometimes resist accountability measures that would sanction community colleges and minority-serving institutions that serve the populations liberals most want to help, even when those institutions produce poor outcomes.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Biden administration pursued Pell Grant increases, free community college (ultimately dropped from Build Back Better), and multiple rounds of targeted debt relief
- Democratic platform has consistently included higher education affordability as a priority
- Liberal think tanks (CAP, Brookings) have produced detailed, evidence-based higher education reform proposals
- Liberal governors have expanded state financial aid and free college programs
- Obama administration created and Trump administration eliminated gainful employment rules, which Biden administration reinstated

### Progressive Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 5/5 - Highly Consistent**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Higher education crisis viewed as a direct consequence of commodifying education and treating it as a private good rather than a public one; positions follow directly from structural analysis of economic inequality |
| Principle consistency | Economic justice and structural reform principles applied uniformly; free public college, debt cancellation, and faculty labor protections all flow from the same analysis of education as a public good captured by market logic |
| Goalpost stability | Highly stable; has consistently argued for free public college and debt cancellation; positions have not shifted based on political convenience |
| Zero-sum behavior | None; supports reforms that benefit all students while emphasizing equity for the most disadvantaged |

**Justification:** Progressives have the most structurally coherent analysis of the higher education crisis. Their argument that treating education as a commodity produced predictable market failures -- tuition spiraling, debt accumulation, stratified access, and institutional prioritization of revenue over outcomes -- is directly supported by the evidence in the root causes analysis. The comparison to other developed nations that provide free or low-cost higher education while achieving comparable outcomes validates the progressive claim that the American crisis is a policy choice, not an economic inevitability. The progressive position that free public college, funded by progressive taxation, would be both more efficient and more equitable than the current loan-and-aid system is supported by international evidence and domestic modeling.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Sen. Sanders and Rep. Ocasio-Cortez have introduced College for All legislation in multiple Congresses
- Congressional Progressive Caucus higher education platform addresses root causes identified in the analysis
- Roosevelt Institute and Economic Policy Institute research on higher education as public good
- Progressive advocacy for debt cancellation preceded Biden administration action and has remained consistent
- Progressive labor organizing in higher education (graduate student unions, adjunct campaigns) reflects principled position on faculty conditions

### Libertarian Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 4/5 - Mostly Consistent**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Genuine commitment to free market principles; principled analysis of how government intervention distorts higher education markets is partially compelling and internally consistent |
| Principle consistency | Deregulation and market competition principles applied consistently; opposition to federal student aid as market distortion follows directly from first principles; support for alternative credentials and innovative models is genuine |
| Goalpost stability | Stable; has consistently argued that federal aid causes tuition inflation and that deregulation would reduce costs; positions have not shifted significantly |
| Zero-sum behavior | Low; opposition to government programs is principled rather than partisan; willing to critique institutions across the political spectrum |

**Justification:** Libertarians have a principled and partially compelling analysis of higher education. Their version of the Bennett Hypothesis -- that federal aid inflates tuition by disconnecting price from consumer decision-making -- has empirical support, particularly for for-profit institutions and less selective schools. Their advocacy for alternative credentials, competency-based education, and deregulated accreditation addresses real barriers to innovation. The slight reduction from a perfect score reflects the difficulty of applying pure market solutions to a sector characterized by profound information asymmetries, delayed returns, and signaling functions that make it fundamentally different from consumer goods markets. The libertarian framework struggles to explain why market-based higher education in the United States produces worse outcomes at higher cost than publicly funded systems in other developed nations.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Reason Foundation advocacy for accreditation reform and alternative credentials

### Constitutionalist Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 4/5 - Mostly Consistent**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | Genuine commitment to federalism and enumerated powers; constitutional analysis provides principled framework for higher education positions; education is not among Congress's enumerated powers |
| Principle consistency | Federalism principles consistently applied; states should control education policy; federal student aid accepted under Spending Clause but viewed with suspicion; consistent across higher education issues |
| Goalpost stability | Stable; constitutional text provides consistent reference point; accept existing federal programs (Pell Grants, student loans) as established under Spending Clause while opposing expansion |
| Zero-sum behavior | Low; constitutional analysis is principled rather than partisan; same framework applied regardless of which institutions or students benefit |

**Justification:** Constitutionalists bring a coherent federalism analysis to higher education. Their observation that education is not among Congress's enumerated powers and that the federal role has expanded through Spending Clause authority is historically accurate. Their preference for state-level experimentation is validated by the diversity of state approaches to higher education funding and governance. The slight reduction from a perfect score reflects the pragmatic tension between strict enumerated-powers analysis (which would question the constitutionality of the entire federal student aid system) and acceptance of established programs. Most constitutionalists do not propose eliminating Pell Grants or federal student loans, suggesting accommodation of political reality.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- *Biden v. Nebraska* (2023) constitutionalist argument that executive student debt cancellation exceeded statutory authority
- Federalist Society education panels emphasizing state authority over education
- Tenth Amendment Center education policy positions
- Constitutional challenges to Department of Education authority
- Constitutionalist support for state-level higher education innovation through Section 1115 waivers

## Compromise Proposals

### Compromise 1: Higher Education Accountability and Transparency Act

**Bridges:** Conservative + Liberal + Progressive + Libertarian + Constitutionalist + Populist + Centrist + Religious Right + Democratic Socialist

**Core Agreement:** Institutions receiving federal student aid should be held accountable for student outcomes, and students and families deserve transparent information to make informed enrollment decisions. The current system allows institutions to capture federal aid without consequences for poor outcomes.

**Policy Description:**

Federal legislation establishes minimum outcome standards for Title IV eligibility: institutions must demonstrate a completion rate of at least 25% (with risk adjustment for student population), a cohort default rate below 25%, and median graduate earnings exceeding the earnings threshold (25% above high school median). Programs must meet gainful employment standards with debt-to-earnings ratios not exceeding 8% of discretionary income or 20% of total income, applied to all career programs regardless of institutional sector. Institutions falling below standards face a three-year escalating sanction regime: warning and improvement plan, heightened monitoring, and loss of Title IV eligibility for new enrollees. Risk-sharing provisions require institutions to pay 5% of defaulted loan amounts for completers and 10% for non-completers, with risk adjustment for Pell recipient percentage and part-time student share. Transparency requirements mandate program-level outcome reporting (completion, debt, earnings) disaggregated by race, gender, and income, with data made publicly available on an enhanced College Scorecard. All institutions must use a standardized financial aid offer letter clearly distinguishing grants from loans and showing net price, expected debt, and comparison to graduate earnings. Accreditation reform requires outcome standards in accreditor criteria, public accreditation reports, and annual monitoring of institutions with declining outcomes. Religious institutions may be evaluated using mission-appropriate metrics with a religious liberty advisory panel review.

- **Implementation Path:** Federal legislation with broad bipartisan sponsorship; builds on existing gainful employment rules and College Scorecard infrastructure; components have appeared in both Republican and Democratic HEA reauthorization proposals; phased implementation over 2-3 years allows institutional adjustment

### Compromise 2: Pell-Plus Affordability and Completion Act

**Bridges:** Liberal + Progressive + Centrist + Populist + Democratic Socialist + Conservative (partial)

**Core Agreement:** The Pell Grant has lost most of its purchasing power since 1980 (from covering 79% to 29% of public university costs), and restoring and expanding need-based aid is the most effective way to improve access for low-income students without the political controversy of free college.

**Policy Description:**

Federal legislation increases the maximum Pell Grant to $14,790 (doubling the current level), phased in over three years at $2,465 per year. The maximum is permanently indexed to the Consumer Price Index to prevent future erosion. Lifetime eligibility is extended from 12 to 14 semesters, and year-round Pell is available without prior full-time enrollment requirement. Eligibility is restored for incarcerated individuals. FAFSA is simplified to 20 questions for families below $60,000 AGI, with automatic zero EFC for families below 150% of poverty. IRS data sharing pre-populates income information. A new $500 million Completion Grant program provides per-student completion bonuses to institutions that graduate Pell recipients at rates equal to or above their overall graduation rate, with enhanced bonuses for community colleges and minority-serving institutions. A $200 million emergency aid fund provides short-term grants to students facing financial crises that would otherwise force withdrawal. A federal-state compact requires participating states to maintain per-student funding for public institutions at the three-year average preceding participation, with a 2:1 penalty for violations. States that maintain effort receive enhanced Pell matching that increases institutional per-student resources.

**Concern Mapping:**

| Perspective | How This Addresses Their Concerns |
|-------------|-----------------------------------|
| Liberal | Doubled Pell restores purchasing power; restored eligibility for incarcerated students; FAFSA simplification removes barriers; completion grants address equity gaps |
| Progressive | Significant expansion of need-based aid; incarcerated student eligibility restored; completion grants incentivize serving disadvantaged students well |
| Centrist | Evidence-based approach; Pell expansion is the most studied and supported affordability reform; completion grants create institutional incentives aligned with student success; phased implementation is fiscally responsible |
| Populist | Helps working families afford college; FAFSA simplification reduces bureaucratic burden; completion bonuses reward institutions that deliver for regular people |
| Democratic Socialist | Major expansion of public investment in education access; helps the most disadvantaged students; completion grants move toward outcome-based public funding |
| Conservative (partial) | No new entitlement program; builds on existing, established program; accountability through completion grants; maintenance of effort prevents state free-riding; phased implementation limits fiscal shock |

**Concessions:**

| Perspective | What They Give Up |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Liberal | Accept phased implementation rather than immediate doubling; accept that bill does not include free college or debt relief |
| Progressive | Accept Pell expansion within existing system rather than free public college; accept that the bill does not address structural commodification |
| Centrist | Accept $30 billion annual cost of doubled Pell; accept that some additional spending may not be offset |
| Populist | Accept that expanded Pell primarily benefits college-goers; accept incarcerated student eligibility |
| Democratic Socialist | Accept need-based aid expansion rather than universal free college; accept incremental approach |
| Conservative | Accept significant new federal spending ($30+ billion annually); accept restored eligibility for incarcerated students |

**Viability Assessment:**

- **Coalition Size:** 6/9 perspectives (5 strong, 1 partial)
- **High Consistency Ratio:** 4/6 coalition members rated 4+ consistency
- **Durability:** High; Pell Grant expansion has historically enjoyed bipartisan support; indexing prevents future erosion without requiring new legislative action; completion grants create institutional constituencies for the program; phased implementation reduces fiscal shock; the combination of expanded aid with completion incentives addresses both access and accountability concerns
- **Implementation Path:** Federal legislation; Pell increases have been included in bipartisan appropriations; FAFSA simplification has already been partially enacted; completion grants build on existing Title IV infrastructure; state maintenance of effort has precedent in stimulus legislation

### Compromise 3: Student Borrower Bill of Rights

**Bridges:** Liberal + Progressive + Centrist + Constitutionalist + Democratic Socialist + Populist (partial)

**Core Agreement:** Regardless of disagreements about free college or debt cancellation, current student loan borrowers deserve basic consumer protections, a simpler repayment system, and a safety valve when repayment becomes impossible. Student loans should not be treated worse than every other form of consumer debt.

**Policy Description:**

Federal legislation consolidates all income-driven repayment plans into a single plan: 5% of discretionary income (income above 225% of poverty) for undergraduate loans, 10% for graduate loans, with forgiveness after 20 years (10 years for balances under $12,000). Unpaid interest is not capitalized and balances cannot grow beyond the original principal. Borrowers 90 days delinquent are automatically enrolled in IDR using IRS data, with annual automatic recertification. Student loan bankruptcy discharge is restored for loans in repayment for at least 10 years, removing the "undue hardship" standard that has made discharge effectively impossible. Loan servicers face enforceable standards: accurate information about repayment options, 30-day response deadlines, written notice of adverse actions, and seven-year record retention. Borrowers have a private right of action against servicers with statutory damages of $1,000 per violation and attorney's fees for prevailing plaintiffs. Public Service Loan Forgiveness is made permanent as an entitlement with streamlined application, 90-day processing deadlines, and expanded qualifying employment including tribal organizations. Borrower defense to repayment is strengthened with 180-day adjudication deadlines, group discharge authority, and automatic discharge for students at institutions subject to major enforcement actions. Collection protections prohibit Social Security offset, limit wage garnishment to 10% of disposable income, and require 60-day advance notice for tax refund offset.

**Concern Mapping:**

| Perspective | How This Addresses Their Concerns |
|-------------|-----------------------------------|
| Liberal | Comprehensive borrower protections; simplified IDR prevents default; PSLF made permanent and reliable; servicer accountability with enforcement teeth |
| Progressive | Strong consumer protections for current borrowers; bankruptcy restoration is a step toward justice; collection protections prevent the most harmful outcomes; harm reduction while working toward systemic change |
| Centrist | Simplified IDR reduces administrative complexity and default; bankruptcy after 10 years is a reasonable safety valve; servicer accountability has bipartisan appeal; evidence-based protections |
| Constitutionalist | Bankruptcy restoration is within congressional Commerce Clause and bankruptcy authority; IDR modification is within Spending Clause authority; legislative action rather than executive overreach |
| Democratic Socialist | Borrower protections and collection limits reduce immediate harm; bankruptcy restoration provides safety valve; harm reduction for current borrowers while pursuing free college for future students |
| Populist (partial) | Bankruptcy restoration is fair -- why treat student debt differently? Servicer accountability punishes corporate bad actors; collection protections prevent abuse of working people |

**Concessions:**

| Perspective | What They Give Up |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Liberal | Accept that the bill does not include broad debt cancellation; accept 10-year waiting period for bankruptcy |
| Progressive | Accept borrower protections within existing system rather than debt cancellation or free college; accept that some borrowers will still struggle |
| Centrist | Accept that simplified IDR with automatic enrollment is a significant fiscal commitment; accept private right of action against servicers |
| Constitutionalist | Accept expanded federal regulation of loan servicing industry; accept PSLF as permanent entitlement |
| Democratic Socialist | Accept that the bill works within the existing debt system rather than abolishing it |
| Populist | Accept that PSLF expansion primarily benefits college-educated public employees; accept that the bill helps borrowers rather than punishing universities |

**Viability Assessment:**

- **Coalition Size:** 6/9 perspectives (5 strong, 1 partial)
- **High Consistency Ratio:** 4/6 coalition members rated 4+ consistency
- **Durability:** Moderate to high; borrower protections and IDR simplification have incremental bipartisan support; bankruptcy restoration faces opposition from lending industry but has growing cross-ideological appeal; PSLF reform is popular with the public; servicer accountability resonates in a consumer protection framework; the coalition is strong on the center-left but lacks full conservative or libertarian buy-in
- **Implementation Path:** Federal legislation; IDR simplification builds on existing SAVE Plan structure (pending litigation resolution); bankruptcy restoration has been introduced in bipartisan bills; servicer accountability builds on existing CFPB framework; PSLF reform is administrative in nature and can be partially implemented without legislation

## Strategic Implications

### Most Viable Coalition

The most effective coalition for higher education reform operates on three tracks. The broadest coalition (9/9 perspectives) supports institutional accountability and transparency -- requiring outcome measurement, risk-sharing, and honest information for students. This consensus should be the foundation of any legislative package. The moderate coalition (6/9 perspectives) can advance Pell Grant expansion and borrower protections -- reforms with strong evidence bases, established precedent, and genuine cross-ideological appeal when framed correctly. The narrowest coalition (4-5 perspectives, center-left) is needed for free college and broad debt cancellation -- reforms that address root causes most directly but face structural opposition from right-of-center perspectives. The strategic implication is to lead with accountability, build momentum with Pell expansion and borrower protections, and pursue free college as the ultimate destination.

### Key Obstacles

- **Culture war contamination:** Higher education has become a front in the culture war, with conservative hostility toward universities as institutions contaminating policy analysis; positions driven by cultural resentment rather than fiscal or educational concerns are resistant to evidence and negotiation
- **Bennett Hypothesis uncertainty:** The degree to which expanded aid causes tuition inflation remains debated; this uncertainty gives ammunition to those opposing any federal spending increase and complicates the case for Pell expansion without accompanying price controls
- **State disinvestment cycle:** Federal affordability investments risk enabling further state disinvestment, as has occurred historically; maintenance of effort provisions are essential but politically difficult to enforce
- **For-profit industry lobbying:** The for-profit college industry has successfully blocked accountability measures through lobbying and campaign contributions, despite overwhelming evidence of poor outcomes; regulatory capture of the Department of Education remains a risk
- **Student debt politics:** Broad debt cancellation is simultaneously one of the most impactful policies and one of the most politically toxic; the framing as "giveaway to the educated" resonates even when most borrowers are working-class
- **Institutional resistance:** Universities oppose accountability measures that threaten their autonomy and revenue; administrator salary caps and administrative spending limits face opposition from the concentrated interests of university leadership
- **Adjunct labor market:** Faculty labor reform faces the collective action problem that underpaid adjuncts are individually powerless against institutions that depend on their cheap labor; absent external mandate, institutional incentives perpetuate exploitation

### Low Consistency Partners

Conservative (2/5) and Populist (2/5) perspectives are the lowest-consistency partners on higher education reform, both significantly lower than their ratings on other policy domains. This reflects the uniquely culture-war-inflected nature of higher education politics. Conservative positions are increasingly driven by hostility toward universities as cultural institutions rather than principled fiscal analysis, making them unpredictable on even accountability measures that align with their stated principles. Populist positions are highly sensitive to framing -- the same policy supported as "holding elite universities accountable" may be opposed as "government bureaucrats telling colleges what to do." The strategic approach should separate higher education policy from the culture war to the maximum extent possible, framing reforms in terms of cost, outcomes, and working-family affordability rather than institutional values or social engineering. The Religious Right (3/5) is a more predictable partner on specific provisions -- particularly those affecting family affordability and faith-based institutional protections -- but their overall engagement is limited by the tendency to prioritize religious liberty exemptions over systemic reform.

### Recommended Approach

1. **Lead with accountability:** The Higher Education Accountability and Transparency Act (Compromise 1) commands 9/9 perspective support and addresses the concern shared across the political spectrum -- that institutions capture federal aid without delivering adequate outcomes. Enacting accountability creates the foundation for larger investments by establishing that additional public money will produce results
2. **Build the affordability case through Pell expansion:** The Pell-Plus Affordability and Completion Act (Compromise 2) commands strong 6/9 support and builds on the most established, least controversial federal higher education program. Doubling and indexing the Pell Grant is the single most effective near-term policy for improving low-income student access without the political baggage of "free college"
3. **Protect current borrowers immediately:** The Student Borrower Bill of Rights (Compromise 3) addresses the $1.75 trillion existing debt crisis through consumer protections and system simplification rather than broad cancellation, making it achievable without resolving the deeper ideological divide on debt forgiveness
4. **Separate affordability from the culture war:** Higher education reform succeeds when framed as economic policy -- cost reduction, workforce development, institutional efficiency, and family financial security. It fails when it becomes a proxy for debates about campus culture, free speech, or political bias. Every reform proposal should be grounded in dollars, completion rates, and employment outcomes
5. **Use state innovation as evidence:** Tennessee Promise, New Mexico Opportunity Scholarship, CUNY ASAP, and other state-level innovations provide compelling evidence for reforms that can be cited across the ideological spectrum. Supporting and evaluating state experiments builds the evidence base for federal action
6. **Address the Bennett Hypothesis directly:** Pair any new federal spending with anti-inflation mechanisms -- maintenance of effort requirements, tuition stabilization provisions, and institutional spending constraints -- to prevent aid increases from being captured through tuition increases. This neutralizes the strongest substantive argument against expanded aid
7. **Build toward free public college as the long-term goal:** Free community college, then free four-year public college funded by progressive taxation, is the destination that most directly addresses root causes. Getting there requires building the political foundation through accountability, Pell expansion, and borrower protections that demonstrate the value of public investment in higher education
