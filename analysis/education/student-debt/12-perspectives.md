# Student Debt: Political Perspectives Analysis

## Overview

This document analyzes student debt reform -- specifically targeted and broad debt cancellation, income-driven repayment restructuring, bankruptcy restoration, servicer accountability, and prevention of future excessive borrowing -- through nine distinct political perspectives. The core questions are: what obligation does the federal government have to address $1.77 trillion in outstanding student loan debt held by 45 million borrowers, who should bear the cost of a system that failed to deliver on the promise of affordable higher education, and what combination of relief, reform, and prevention can resolve the crisis while discouraging future recurrence? Each perspective receives engagement-consistency ratings and position scores, culminating in compromise proposals that bridge multiple viewpoints.

Student debt is politically distinctive because it sits at the intersection of competing American values -- individual responsibility, equality of opportunity, government accountability, and the role of higher education in economic mobility. The crisis generates sharp disagreement about whether borrowers are victims of systemic failure or individuals who made voluntary choices. It also exposes tensions between those who view broad cancellation as an act of justice and those who view it as a regressive transfer to college graduates at the expense of those who did not attend. The racial dimension -- Black borrowers owe $25,000 more than white borrowers four years after graduation -- adds urgency for some perspectives and complicates the politics for others. These dynamics make student debt one of the most politically charged domestic policy issues, with coalition-building requiring careful navigation of fairness narratives, fiscal concerns, and competing visions of government responsibility.

### Engagement Consistency (1-5)

This metric assesses how reliably a perspective's stated positions align with its underlying principles, and how stable those positions are when concerns are addressed. Higher scores indicate more predictable negotiating partners.

| Level | Label | Indicators |
|-------|-------|------------|
| 5 | Highly Consistent | Stated positions reflect underlying principles; positions stable when concerns addressed |
| 4 | Mostly Consistent | Principles generally align with positions; minor strategic flexibility |
| 3 | Mixed Consistency | Some positions principled, others reflect coalition pressures or unstated concerns |
| 2 | Low Consistency | Positions shift frequently; stated concerns differ from underlying priorities |
| 1 | Unpredictable | Positions appear driven primarily by opposition dynamics rather than principles |

**Coalition-Building Considerations:**

- Alignment gap: When stated concerns differ from underlying priorities, addressing stated concerns may not secure support
- Position stability: Perspectives with shifting positions require ongoing negotiation
- Principle consistency: Perspectives that apply principles uniformly are more predictable partners
- Internal diversity: Some perspectives contain factions with different priorities

## Perspective Analyses

### Conservative Perspective

#### Engagement Consistency Assessment

**Score: 2/5 - Low Consistency**

| Indicator | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Stated vs actual motivations | "Individual responsibility" framing is genuine but inconsistently applied; conservatives do not apply the same framework to corporate bailouts, agricultural subsidies, or PPP loan forgiveness, suggesting the objection to student debt relief is partly partisan |
| Principle consistency | "Fiscal restraint" principle is selectively deployed; tax cuts that increase deficits by trillions draw less concern than student debt cancellation costing hundreds of billions; "market solutions" principle conflicts with support for the non-dischargeable status quo that insulates lenders from market discipline |
| Goalpost stability | Low; objections shift between cost, fairness, moral hazard, legal authority, and regressive distribution depending on the specific proposal, suggesting opposition is to the concept rather than the details |
| Zero-sum behavior | Significant; student debt relief is framed as a transfer from non-college to college Americans, ignoring that the system's beneficiaries include lenders, servicers, and institutions that conservatives rarely target |

**Justification:** Conservatives score low on consistency because their stated principles often conflict with their policy positions on student debt. The "individual responsibility" argument treats the 18-year-old who borrowed for college as a fully informed market actor while ignoring the institutional actors -- for-profit schools, predatory servicers, state legislatures that cut funding -- whose decisions drove the crisis. The "fiscal restraint" objection to cancellation coexists with support for tax policies that cost more than proposed relief programs. Most tellingly, the conservative defense of non-dischargeable student debt is anti-market: eliminating bankruptcy protection removes the market signal that would discipline lenders and institutions. A consistently conservative position would support bankruptcy restoration as a market mechanism and oppose the moral hazard created by lending without underwriting.

**Evidence for assessment:**

- Republican opposition to Biden's $10,000-$20,000 cancellation plan while supporting $800+ billion in PPP loan forgiveness
- Conservative support for the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act ($1.9 trillion cost) while citing fiscal concerns about $400 billion student debt relief
- Heritage Foundation and AEI student debt publications emphasizing individual choice while ignoring institutional failures
- Senate Republican opposition to bankruptcy restoration despite its market-discipline rationale
- Some conservative commentators (Oren Cass, American Compass) have broken from orthodoxy to acknowledge systemic failures

**Justification:** Liberals maintain consistent, principled positions on student debt reform rooted in their analysis that the crisis results from systemic failures -- state disinvestment, predatory lending, servicer misconduct, and inadequate safety nets. **Justification:** Libertarians bring a uniquely coherent critique to the student debt debate. Their opposition to federal lending is also principled: without unlimited federal loans, institutions could not capture tuition increases from students unable to resist. The slight reduction from a perfect score reflects that pure libertarian solutions -- eliminating federal lending entirely -- would dramatically reduce college access for millions of Americans, a consequence that even some libertarians acknowledge requires transition planning.

## Compromise Proposals

### Compromise 1: Student Loan Bankruptcy and Institutional Accountability Act

**Bridges:** Conservative + Liberal + Progressive + Libertarian + Constitutionalist + Populist + Centrist + Religious Right + Democratic Socialist

**Core Agreement:** Borrowers who truly cannot pay should have a legal escape valve, and institutions that produce graduates unable to repay should bear some of the cost. Bankruptcy restoration and institutional risk-sharing are both market-discipline mechanisms that realign incentives without requiring government spending on direct cancellation.

**Policy Description:**

Federal legislation restores standard bankruptcy treatment for student loans after a 7-year waiting period, replacing the Brunner test with a totality-of-circumstances standard as specified in the Student Loan Bankruptcy Fairness Act. The legislation applies to both federal and private student loans. Simultaneously, the legislation establishes institutional risk-sharing: institutions whose borrowers default at rates above a threshold must reimburse a percentage of defaulted loan amounts to the federal government, with the threshold adjusted for the socioeconomic profile of the student body to avoid penalizing schools that serve disadvantaged populations. Institutions with default rates exceeding 30% for three consecutive years lose Title IV eligibility. Gainful employment rules are applied to all programs at all institutions (not just for-profit). The legislation includes a $50 million annual fund for financial literacy at institutions receiving Title IV funds and requires standardized financial aid offers that clearly distinguish grants from loans.

**Concern Mapping:**

| Perspective | How This Addresses Their Concerns |
|-------------|-----------------------------------|
| Conservative | Risk-sharing creates market accountability without government spending; bankruptcy restoration disciplines lenders; gainful employment standards enforce institutional quality; no direct cancellation or new government programs |
| Liberal | Bankruptcy provides escape valve for millions in distress; risk-sharing addresses institutional accountability; gainful employment protects future students; financial aid transparency improves informed choice |
| Progressive | Bankruptcy restoration is essential rights restoration; risk-sharing moves toward holding institutions accountable for commodifying education; gainful employment addresses for-profit predation |
| Libertarian | Bankruptcy restoration is the most important free-market reform -- restores price signals and market discipline to student lending; risk-sharing aligns institutional incentives without regulatory command-and-control |
| Constitutionalist | Bankruptcy power is explicitly enumerated in Article I, Section 8; risk-sharing operates through Spending Clause conditions on federal funds; no constitutional concerns with this approach |
| Populist | Gives ordinary people the same bankruptcy rights as corporations; makes schools that ripped off students pay back; no bailout for elites |
| Centrist | Evidence-based approach combining market mechanisms with accountability; bipartisan coalition is achievable; addresses systemic problems without massive government spending |
| Religious Right | 7-year provision echoes biblical sabbatical year; mercy for those who cannot pay; accountability for institutions that acted dishonestly |
| Democratic Socialist | Bankruptcy restoration is essential rights restoration; institutional accountability is a step toward systemic reform; insufficient alone but a meaningful advance |

**Concessions:**

| Perspective | What They Give Up |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Conservative | Accept that some borrowers will discharge debt through bankruptcy; accept risk-sharing costs imposed on institutions including private colleges |
| Liberal | Accept that bankruptcy requires waiting period and court process rather than administrative cancellation; accept that this alone does not address the full crisis |
| Progressive | Accept 7-year waiting period rather than immediate dischargeability; accept risk-sharing rather than elimination of for-profit sector |
| Libertarian | Accept risk-sharing regulation of institutions; accept that some government oversight is involved in gainful employment rules |
| Constitutionalist | Accept federal conditions on institutional participation in Title IV |
| Populist | Accept that relief requires court process rather than immediate action |
| Centrist | Accept that bankruptcy will impose some costs on the federal student loan program |
| Religious Right | Accept that some borrowers will use bankruptcy strategically; accept federal regulation of educational institutions |
| Democratic Socialist | Accept that this is incremental rather than transformative; accept continuation of the lending system with better safeguards |

**Viability Assessment:**

- **Coalition Size:** 9/9 perspectives
- **High Consistency Ratio:** 5/9 coalition members rated 4+ consistency
- **Durability:** Very high; bankruptcy restoration and institutional accountability are the two reforms with the broadest principled support; the Libertarian-Progressive alliance on bankruptcy is anchored in genuinely shared values (market discipline and individual rights respectively); risk-sharing has bipartisan precedent in policy discussions across multiple Congresses; the combination creates mutual buy-in
- **Implementation Path:** Federal legislation with bipartisan sponsorship; Sen. Durbin has introduced bankruptcy reform repeatedly; institutional accountability has been proposed by members of both parties; combination package increases political appeal by offering something to every perspective; most viable first step in student debt reform

### Compromise 2: Student Loan Repayment Reform and Servicer Accountability Act

**Bridges:** Conservative + Liberal + Progressive + Constitutionalist + Centrist + Religious Right + Democratic Socialist

**Core Agreement:** The student loan repayment system is broken, servicers have systematically failed borrowers, and PSLF must honor the government's promise. Fixing repayment and holding servicers accountable are obligations of basic competent governance regardless of ideological orientation.

**Policy Description:**

Federal legislation codifies the SAVE Plan with modifications: consolidates all IDR plans into a single program with 5% undergraduate and 10% graduate payment rates, discretionary income defined as above 225% of the poverty line, no negative amortization, and forgiveness after 20 years (undergraduate) or 25 years (graduate) with tax-free treatment made permanent. Congressional codification addresses *Biden v. Nebraska* concerns about executive authority. PSLF is permanently fixed with all payment types counting, automatic employment tracking using IRS data, prospective employer certification, and processing within 90 days. The legislation establishes the Servicer Accountability Act's provisions: outcome-based compensation, private right of action with statutory damages, state enforcement authority, CFPB supervision, mandatory licensing, and prohibition on arbitration clauses. A transition period allows existing borrowers in legacy IDR plans to move to SAVE or remain in their current plan. Congressional codification establishes clear statutory authority that is not vulnerable to legal challenges based on executive overreach.

**Concern Mapping:**

| Perspective | How This Addresses Their Concerns |
|-------------|-----------------------------------|
| Conservative | Congressional codification is the proper legislative process; servicer accountability punishes genuine misconduct; PSLF fix honors existing promises without creating new ones; consolidation simplifies a wasteful bureaucratic system |
| Liberal | IDR reform provides affordable payments for millions; PSLF fix delivers promised forgiveness to public servants; servicer accountability addresses documented misconduct; tax-free forgiveness is permanently established |
| Progressive | Favorable payment terms reduce borrower burden; interest subsidy prevents negative amortization; servicer accountability with private right of action gives borrowers real power; permanent tax-free forgiveness is important |
| Constitutionalist | Congressional legislation provides clear constitutional authority under Spending Clause; addresses *Biden v. Nebraska* major questions concerns through explicit statutory authorization; proper legislative process |
| Centrist | Evidence-based reforms addressing documented failures; bipartisan appeal of servicer accountability; congressional process provides legitimacy; SAVE terms are well-designed based on policy research |
| Religious Right | Affordable payments reduce family financial stress; PSLF fix benefits faith-based nonprofit workers; accountability for dishonest servicers aligns with moral values |
| Democratic Socialist | Favorable terms reduce borrower exploitation; servicer accountability addresses corporate misconduct; insufficient alone but meaningful improvement in existing system |

**Concessions:**

| Perspective | What They Give Up |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Conservative | Accept generous IDR terms (5% payments, interest subsidy, 20-year forgiveness) that effectively subsidize repayment; accept expanded PSLF |
| Liberal | Accept that IDR reform is not cancellation; accept that some borrowers will still carry debt for 20-25 years |
| Progressive | Accept working within the existing lending system rather than replacing it with free college; accept that IDR reform is incremental |
| Constitutionalist | Accept expanded federal loan administration programs |
| Centrist | Accept that SAVE terms are generous and may have significant fiscal cost |
| Religious Right | Accept expansion of government financial programs |

- **High Consistency Ratio:** 5/7 coalition members rated 4+ consistency
- **Durability:** High; IDR reform and PSLF fix address specific, documented program failures that have bipartisan recognition; servicer accountability has broad support based on documented misconduct; congressional codification provides legal stability and addresses court challenges; the combination of fixing broken systems (PSLF), improving existing systems (IDR), and punishing bad actors (servicers) creates a package with multiple entry points for different perspectives
- **Implementation Path:** Federal legislation building on existing proposals; IDR codification addresses ongoing litigation over SAVE Plan; PSLF fix builds on successful temporary waiver; servicer accountability provisions have been introduced in prior Congresses; strongest legislative vehicle would be Higher Education Act reauthorization

### Compromise 3: Targeted Debt Cancellation with Prevention Investment

**Bridges:** Liberal + Progressive + Centrist + Populist (partial) + Religious Right (partial) + Democratic Socialist

**Core Agreement:** Borrowers who were defrauded by for-profit schools, who have been paying for decades without relief, and who are carrying debt into retirement have been failed by the system in ways that transcend ideological disagreement. Simultaneously, preventing future borrowers from facing the same crisis requires investment in grants and affordable public higher education.

**Policy Description:**

Federal legislation provides targeted cancellation: full discharge for borrowers defrauded by for-profit schools with pending borrower defense claims exceeding 180 days, full discharge for borrowers in default for 10+ years, full discharge for borrowers over 65, $20,000 cancellation for Pell Grant recipients with AGI below $125,000, and $10,000 for other borrowers below the same income threshold. Automatic implementation using existing data eliminates application barriers. Cancelled amounts are excluded from taxable income permanently. The legislation pairs cancellation with prevention: Pell Grant maximum is doubled to $14,000 and indexed to inflation; a federal-state partnership provides 75% matching funds for states that maintain or increase higher education appropriations; and institutional risk-sharing is established (as in Compromise 1) to prevent future cost-shifting to borrowers. The legislation is paid for through a combination of savings from reduced default costs, recoupment from institutions found to have committed fraud, and a 1% surcharge on endowment investment income for institutions with endowments exceeding $10 billion.

**Concern Mapping:**

| Perspective | How This Addresses Their Concerns |
|-------------|-----------------------------------|
| Liberal | Meaningful cancellation for those most harmed; Pell doubling addresses affordability; state reinvestment addresses root cause; automatic implementation reduces barriers; racial wealth gap partially addressed through Pell targeting |
| Progressive | $20,000 Pell cancellation provides substantial relief to those with greatest need; prevention investment addresses root causes; institutional accountability included; scope is insufficient but direction is right |
| Centrist | Targeted approach has stronger distributional justification than broad cancellation; pairing relief with prevention addresses root causes; pay-for provisions demonstrate fiscal responsibility; evidence supports targeting Pell recipients |
| Populist (partial) | Fraud victims get full relief; older borrowers freed from lifelong debt; universities with massive endowments help pay for it; does not bail out high-earning professionals |
| Religious Right (partial) | Older borrowers and fraud victims receive mercy; prevention measures reduce future family financial stress; endowment surcharge makes wealthy institutions contribute; concerns about scale of government spending remain |
| Democratic Socialist | Any cancellation is better than none; Pell targeting provides most relief to those with least wealth; prevention investment is essential; insufficient but a meaningful step |

**Concessions:**

| Perspective | What They Give Up |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Liberal | Accept that cancellation is targeted rather than broad; accept income limits; accept that many struggling borrowers above the threshold receive no direct relief |
| Progressive | Accept $20,000 cap rather than full cancellation; accept means-testing; accept that this is incremental rather than transformative |
| Centrist | Accept $200-400 billion in cancellation spending despite fiscal concerns; accept that prevention measures add ongoing costs |
| Populist | Accept that some college graduates receive cancellation; accept government spending on higher education investment |
| Religious Right | Accept significant government spending on cancellation and prevention; accept expanded federal role in higher education |
| Democratic Socialist | Accept targeting rather than universality; accept that the lending system continues for future borrowers albeit with better terms |

**Viability Assessment:**

- **Coalition Size:** 6/9 perspectives (Conservative, Libertarian, and Constitutionalist excluded)
- **High Consistency Ratio:** 3/6 coalition members rated 4+ consistency
- **Durability:** Moderate; the coalition requires center-left agreement with partial support from Populist and Religious Right perspectives whose consistency ratings are lower; pairing cancellation with prevention strengthens the package by addressing the "what about future borrowers" objection; endowment surcharge funding mechanism appeals to populist sentiment; however, the coalition could fracture if Populist and Religious Right partners face partisan pressure to oppose what will be characterized as a Democratic policy priority
- **Implementation Path:** Federal legislation requiring center-left coalition with some moderate Republican support; most viable as standalone legislation rather than reconciliation (to enable bipartisan framing); targeted cancellation has stronger legal foundation than broad cancellation (*Biden v. Nebraska* distinguishes between executive and congressional authority); Pell Grant increase has bipartisan support in principle; state reinvestment matching is modeled on existing federal-state partnership programs

## Strategic Implications

### Most Viable Coalition

The most effective strategy for student debt reform operates on three tracks with decreasing coalition breadth. The broadest coalition (8-9 perspectives) can advance bankruptcy restoration and institutional accountability -- reforms with genuinely cross-ideological principled support. The mid-range coalition (7 perspectives) can advance IDR codification, PSLF fix, and servicer accountability -- reforms that fix demonstrably broken systems. The narrowest coalition (center-left, 4-6 perspectives) is needed for debt cancellation of any form. Student debt reform's strategic advantage is that the first two tracks deliver meaningful results -- bankruptcy provides an escape valve, institutional accountability prevents future predation, IDR reform makes payments affordable, PSLF fix honors government promises, and servicer accountability ends documented abuse -- without requiring resolution of the ideological divide over cancellation.

### Key Obstacles

- **Cancellation framing:** Any form of cancellation triggers the "bailout for college graduates" frame that activates populist, conservative, and libertarian opposition; the fairness objection is the most politically potent obstacle and cannot be addressed solely through policy design
- **Legal uncertainty:** *Biden v. Nebraska* established limits on executive cancellation authority; while congressional legislation would address the major questions doctrine, the political threshold for passing legislation is higher than for executive action
- **Lending industry opposition:** Student loan servicers, guaranty agencies, and private lenders have organized political opposition to bankruptcy reform, servicer accountability, and cancellation; their lobbying expenditures and campaign contributions are significant obstacles
- **Fiscal cost:** Even targeted cancellation costs $200-400 billion; broad cancellation exceeds $1 trillion; pay-for mechanisms are politically contentious; deficit concerns constrain centrist and conservative support
- **Prevention requires sustained investment:** Pell Grant doubling ($25 billion annually) and free college ($80-100 billion annually) require ongoing appropriations that face annual political challenge; one-time cancellation without prevention merely resets the clock
- **Higher Education Act reauthorization stalemate:** The HEA has not been comprehensively reauthorized since 2008; reauthorization is the natural legislative vehicle for comprehensive reform but has been stalled by partisan disagreement on a wide range of education issues

### Low Consistency Partners

Conservative (2/5) and Populist (2/5) perspectives are the least consistent partners on student debt reform. Conservative positions are driven more by partisan opposition to a Democratic priority than by consistent application of principles -- as evidenced by the tension between supporting PPP loan forgiveness and opposing student loan cancellation, or defending the anti-market status quo of non-dischargeable debt while claiming to support free markets. Populist positions are driven by framing sensitivity: identical policies receive dramatically different support depending on whether they are presented as "helping families ripped off by the system" or "bailing out college elites." The Religious Right (3/5) is a potentially movable partner if reform is framed in terms of family welfare and biblical jubilee rather than government spending. The strategic approach should focus on building durable coalitions with high-consistency partners (Liberal, Progressive, Libertarian, Constitutionalist, Centrist, Democratic Socialist -- all rated 4+) while designing specific reforms that appeal to low-consistency partners' most stable positions: institutional accountability for populists, bankruptcy restoration for the principles-first minority of conservatives, and family-welfare framing for the Religious Right.

### Recommended Approach

1. **Lead with bankruptcy restoration and institutional accountability:** The Student Loan Bankruptcy and Institutional Accountability Act (Compromise 1) commands 9/9 perspective support and addresses the two most fundamental structural problems -- borrowers have no escape valve, and institutions bear no risk. This builds the broadest possible coalition and establishes momentum for further reform
2. **Codify IDR reform and fix PSLF:** The Student Loan Repayment Reform and Servicer Accountability Act (Compromise 2) addresses documented program failures with 7/9 perspective support. Congressional codification of SAVE resolves ongoing litigation and provides legal stability. PSLF fix honors the government's promise. Servicer accountability ends documented abuse
3. **Build the case for targeted cancellation through results:** As bankruptcy provides relief for those who truly cannot pay, IDR reform prevents default and distress, and institutional accountability reduces future predation, the remaining case for direct cancellation narrows to those harmed by specific systemic failures -- Pell recipients, fraud victims, long-term borrowers, and older borrowers. This targeting is more politically defensible and legally sound
4. **Invest in prevention simultaneously:** Pell Grant expansion and state reinvestment incentives prevent the next generation from facing the same crisis. Without prevention, cancellation merely resets the clock. Prevention has broader political support than cancellation because it addresses root causes rather than consequences
5. **Use the Libertarian-Progressive bankruptcy alliance as a model:** The unusual agreement between Libertarians and Progressives on bankruptcy restoration demonstrates that principled analysis can produce unexpected coalitions. Emphasize that bankruptcy restoration is simultaneously a free-market reform (restoring price signals and market discipline) and a rights restoration (providing basic fairness to borrowers). This "strange bedfellows" framing builds credibility with uncommitted perspectives
6. **Separate student debt reform from partisan identity:** Student debt reform succeeds when framed as fixing broken systems, holding bad actors accountable, and honoring government promises rather than as a partisan priority. Every reform in this package can be defended on conservative principles (market discipline, government accountability, fiscal efficiency) as well as progressive ones (justice, equity, public goods)
7. **Address the fairness objection directly:** The most potent political obstacle is the perception that student debt relief is unfair to those who did not attend college, who paid off their loans, or who chose affordable options. Pairing relief with prevention (so future students do not need relief), pairing cancellation with institutional accountability (so institutions pay their share), and targeting relief to those most clearly failed by the system (fraud victims, long-term borrowers, Pell recipients) all reduce the fairness objection's force
