# Education Governance: Stakeholders

## Overview

Education governance involves a complex web of stakeholders with varying degrees of power, different interests, and often conflicting goals. Understanding who has influence, what they want, and how they exercise power is essential for navigating reform efforts.

## Primary Stakeholders

### School Board Members

**Profile**

| Characteristic | Typical Pattern |
|----------------|-----------------|
| Number nationwide | ~90,000 |
| Demographics | Older, whiter, wealthier than students |
| Professional background | Business, retirees, some educators |
| Compensation | Mostly unpaid or nominal stipend |
| Training | Limited, often voluntary |
| Time commitment | 10-30+ hours/month |
| Term length | 2-4 years |

**Interests and Motivations**

| Type | Primary Interest | Secondary Interests |
|------|------------------|---------------------|
| Parent-activists | Specific grievance | Children's school experience |
| Ideological members | Advance agenda | Political positioning |
| Career politicians | Resume building | Political network |
| Community pillars | Civic service | Maintain status quo |
| Reform advocates | System improvement | Student outcomes |
| Union-aligned | Labor relations | District stability |

**Power and Limitations**

| Power | Limitations |
|-------|-------------|
| Hire/fire superintendent | May lack expertise to evaluate |
| Approve budget | Often constrained by state formulas |
| Set policy | Staff must implement |
| Approve curriculum | May not understand content |
| Negotiate contracts | Legal complexity |

**Internal Dynamics**

Board dysfunction often stems from:

- 5-4 ideological splits
- Personal conflicts
- Micromanagement tendencies
- Lack of role clarity
- Competition for attention
- Individual agendas vs. collective action

### Superintendents

**Profile**

| Characteristic | Data |
|----------------|------|
| Number nationwide | ~13,500 |
| Average tenure | 3-5 years (urban: 2-4 years) |
| Salary range | $80,000-$400,000+ |
| Demographics | ~73% male, ~80% white |
| Education | Typically Ed.D. or Ph.D. |
| Career path | Teacher → Principal → Central office → Superintendent |

**Role and Responsibilities**

| Function | Reality |
|----------|---------|
| CEO of district | But reports to amateur board |
| Strategic leader | But tenure too short for long-term |
| Face of district | First blamed when things go wrong |
| Political navigator | Must manage board, unions, community |
| Educational leader | Often consumed by management |

**Interests**

| Interest | Expression |
|----------|------------|
| Job security | Board management, avoiding controversy |
| Career advancement | Building resume for larger district |
| Professional reputation | Association with improvements |
| Autonomy | Resisting board micromanagement |
| Adequate resources | Advocating for funding |

**Power**

Superintendents have significant practical power:

- Information control (board depends on superintendent for knowledge)
- Agenda setting (shapes what board discusses)
- Staff loyalty (administrators serve at superintendent's pleasure)
- Public communication (voice of the district)
- Implementation control (decides how policies are enacted)

### Teachers and Teachers' Unions

**Teacher Profile**

| Characteristic | Data |
|----------------|------|
| K-12 teachers nationwide | ~3.7 million |
| Union membership | ~50% nationally (varies by state) |
| Major unions | NEA (~3 million), AFT (~1.7 million) |
| Political alignment | Lean Democratic |
| Gender | ~77% female |

**Union Interests**

| Interest | Priority |
|----------|----------|
| Compensation | Salary, benefits, pensions |
| Working conditions | Class size, prep time, safety |
| Job security | Tenure, due process |
| Voice in governance | Seat at the table |
| Professional autonomy | Control over classroom |

**Union Power in Governance**

| Mechanism | Effect |
|-----------|--------|
| Board elections | Endorse, fund, mobilize for candidates |
| Contract negotiations | Shape working conditions |
| Political contributions | Influence state policy |
| Strike threat | Leverage in negotiations |
| Information | Members throughout system |
| Public opinion | Trusted more than administrators |

**Union Influence Varies**

| Factor | High Union Power | Low Union Power |
|--------|------------------|-----------------|
| State law | Collective bargaining required | Right-to-work states |
| Membership | High density | Low density |
| Political context | Democratic leadership | Republican leadership |
| Historical strength | Long union presence | New districts |

### Parents and Families

**Engagement Levels**

| Group | Characteristics | Engagement |
|-------|-----------------|------------|
| Highly engaged | Higher income, more education | Board meetings, PTA, advocacy |
| Moderately engaged | Working class, time-constrained | Back-to-school, some events |
| Minimally engaged | Multiple jobs, transportation barriers | Basic communication |
| Disengaged | Crisis-focused, system distrust | Only when problems arise |

**Parent Interests**

| Interest | Expression |
|----------|------------|
| Child's education | Individual advocacy |
| School quality | School choice, boundary decisions |
| Safety | Discipline policies, facilities |
| Values alignment | Curriculum, policies |
| Voice | Access to teachers, administrators |

**Parent Power**

| Source | Limitation |
|--------|------------|
| Exit (choice, moving) | Requires resources |
| Voice (board meetings, advocacy) | Time-intensive |
| Organizing (PTA, groups) | Fragmented, often captured |
| Political pressure | Individual, episodic |
| Legal action | Expensive, slow |

**Inequity in Parent Voice**

| Group | Typical Voice | Barriers |
|-------|---------------|----------|
| Affluent parents | Strong | Few |
| Working-class parents | Limited | Time, language, transportation |
| Low-income parents | Weak | Multiple jobs, distrust |
| Immigrant families | Very limited | Language, legal status fears |
| Parents of color | Varies | Historical exclusion |

### Students

**The Voiceless Stakeholder**

| Reality | Consequence |
|---------|-------------|
| No vote | No electoral power |
| No organization | No collective voice |
| No resources | No lobbying capacity |
| Developmental stage | Discounted as "not ready" |
| Compulsory attendance | Cannot exit |

**Student Interests**

| Interest | Rarely Represented By |
|----------|----------------------|
| Quality teaching | Teachers advocate for teachers |
| Engaging curriculum | Adults decide |
| Safe environment | Varies |
| Fair treatment | Discipline policies favor adults |
| Future preparation | Long-term, invisible |

**Emerging Student Voice**

| Development | Status |
|-------------|--------|
| Student board members | ~20 states allow, usually non-voting |
| Student organizations | Rare, limited influence |
| Youth activism | Episodic (Parkland, climate) |
| Legal advocacy | Organizations represent students |

## Government Stakeholders

### State Education Agencies

**Profile**

| Characteristic | Description |
|----------------|-------------|
| Staff size | Ranges from hundreds to thousands |
| Budget | Varies widely by state |
| Authority | Significant but often unused |
| Leadership | Appointed chief or elected |
| Political position | Caught between governor and districts |

**Interests**

| Interest | Expression |
|----------|------------|
| Compliance | Monitoring, reporting |
| Stability | Avoid interventions |
| Federal relations | Maintain funding |
| Political survival | Please governor/legislature |
| Professional reputation | State rankings, comparisons |

**Powers**

| Power | Usage |
|-------|-------|
| Standard setting | Universal |
| Testing/accountability | Universal |
| Funding distribution | Universal |
| Teacher licensing | Universal |
| District intervention | Rare, reluctant |
| Charter authorization | Some states |

### State Legislators

**Interests in Education**

| Interest | Expression |
|----------|------------|
| Constituent service | Protecting local districts |
| Ideology | Curriculum, standards, choice |
| Political positioning | Education as issue |
| Fiscal responsibility | Funding levels |
| Union relations | Labor law, collective bargaining |

**Power Over Governance**

| Power | Effect |
|-------|--------|
| Funding formulas | Shape district resources |
| Governance laws | Can restructure boards |
| State takeover authority | Enable or limit intervention |
| Charter law | Alternative governance |
| Election rules | Timing, method, requirements |

### Mayors

**Mayoral Interest in Education**

| Interest | Explanation |
|----------|-------------|
| City competitiveness | Schools affect economic development |
| Accountability | Voters blame mayors for school problems |
| Political ambition | Education success as credential |
| Integration | Schools as part of city services |
| Fiscal | School budgets affect city finances |

**Mayoral Control Models**

| Model | Cities | Degree of Control |
|-------|--------|-------------------|
| Full mayoral control | NYC, Boston | Mayor appoints board, picks superintendent |
| Partial mayoral control | Cleveland | Shared appointment authority |
| Advisory role | Many cities | Mayor nominates, council confirms |
| No formal role | Most cities | Separate elected board |

### Federal Government

**Interests**

| Interest | Expression |
|----------|------------|
| Civil rights | Enforcement |
| National competitiveness | Standards, testing |
| Equity | Title I, IDEA |
| Political priorities | Administration focus |

**Limited Powers**

| Tool | Limitation |
|------|------------|
| Funding conditions | ~8% of K-12 spending |
| Civil rights enforcement | Requires violation |
| Research and information | No direct authority |
| Bully pulpit | Political variability |

## Stakeholder Power Analysis

### Power Distribution Matrix

| Stakeholder | Electoral Power | Financial Power | Information Power | Organizational Power |
|-------------|----------------|-----------------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Students | None | None | Low | Very Low |
| Parents | Low | Low | Low | Low-Moderate |
| Teachers/Unions | High | Moderate | High | Very High |
| Superintendents | Low | Moderate | Very High | High |
| Board Members | Moderate | Moderate | Moderate | Moderate |
| State Officials | High | Very High | High | High |
| Business | Moderate | High | Moderate | Moderate |
| Reformers | Low-Moderate | Moderate | Moderate | Moderate |

### Who Wins and Loses

**Winners in Current System**

| Stakeholder | Why They Win |
|-------------|--------------|
| Wealthy suburban districts | Boundary protection, tax base |
| Teachers' unions | Low-turnout elections |
| Status quo administrators | System inertia |
| Incumbent board members | Low accountability |
| Charter operators | Weak oversight |

**Losers in Current System**

| Stakeholder | Why They Lose |
|-------------|---------------|
| Low-income students | No voice, no exit |
| Students of color | Historical exclusion, inequitable resources |
| Parents without resources | Cannot exit or effectively voice |
| Taxpayers (sometimes) | Inefficient system |
| Communities with failing schools | Trapped in dysfunctional governance |

### Coalition Dynamics

**Reform Coalitions (Typical)**

| Coalition | Members | Agenda |
|-----------|---------|--------|
| Business-reformer | Business groups, reform orgs | Accountability, choice |
| Civil rights-equity | NAACP, Urban League, legal groups | Funding, desegregation |
| Parent choice | Choice advocates, some parents | Charter expansion, vouchers |
| Efficiency | Taxpayer groups, business | Consolidation, cost control |

**Anti-Reform Coalitions (Typical)**

| Coalition | Members | Defense |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| Union-board | Unions, board associations | Status quo, local control |
| Local control | Rural communities, suburban parents | Autonomy, boundaries |
| Anti-choice | Some Democrats, unions | Public school protection |
| Anti-intervention | Local advocates | State takeover opposition |

## Stakeholder Engagement in Reform

### Building Reform Coalitions

**Keys to Success**

| Factor | Requirement |
|--------|-------------|
| Breadth | Multiple stakeholder groups |
| Funding | Resources for sustained campaign |
| Narrative | Compelling story of need and solution |
| Leadership | Trusted figures from multiple sectors |
| Endurance | Multi-year commitment |

**Common Coalition Failures**

| Failure Mode | Example |
|--------------|---------|
| Business-only | Seen as elitist, privatization |
| Parent-only | Insufficient political muscle |
| Ideological | Alienates potential allies |
| Top-down | Lacks community buy-in |
| Single-issue | Fragments support |

### Engaging Marginalized Stakeholders

**Barriers to Engagement**

| Barrier | Affected Groups |
|---------|-----------------|
| Time | Working-class parents |
| Language | Immigrant families |
| Distrust | Communities with poor school experiences |
| Transportation | Rural, low-income |
| Childcare | All parents |
| Information | Those outside networks |

**Effective Engagement Strategies**

| Strategy | Application |
|----------|-------------|
| Meet people where they are | Community centers, churches |
| Provide support | Childcare, translation, transportation |
| Build trust over time | Consistent presence |
| Share power | Community members as leaders |
| Multiple channels | Digital and in-person |
