# Curriculum Standards: Overview

## Executive Summary

America is fighting over what children should learn. Curriculum standards--the framework defining what students should know and be able to do at each grade level--have become one of the most politically contentious areas in education. The Common Core State Standards, adopted by 46 states between 2010 and 2014, triggered a backlash that has shaped education politics for over a decade. Today, states are withdrawing from or renaming Common Core, passing laws restricting how history and race are taught, banning books at unprecedented rates, and fighting over science, health, and social studies content.

These curriculum wars reflect genuine tensions in American society. Parents want influence over what their children learn. Communities have different values about history, science, and morality. Teachers need professional autonomy to adapt instruction. States seek coherence and accountability. Publishers and testing companies profit from complexity. The result is a fragmented, politicized system where curriculum decisions often reflect political power rather than educational evidence.

The stakes are substantial. Curriculum quality matters for learning--students cannot learn content they are not taught, and poorly sequenced or watered-down standards produce weaker outcomes. The United States performs mediocrely on international assessments, with significant gaps between states. Students in states with weak standards may graduate unprepared for college or careers. Meanwhile, curriculum battles consume school board time, drive teacher burnout, and divide communities.

Resolving curriculum conflicts requires transparency, local voice, and evidence. Parents deserve to know what their children are learning. Teachers need high-quality materials and professional discretion. Standards should be coherent and rigorous without prescribing ideology. Achieving this balance is difficult but essential for an education system that serves all students.

### State Standards Variation

Despite Common Core's adoption, significant variation persists:

**Standards Quality Ratings** (Fordham Institute, 2018-2023):

- **A-rated standards**: 12 states
- **B-rated standards**: 18 states
- **C-rated standards**: 14 states
- **D or F-rated standards**: 6 states

States that withdrew from or heavily modified Common Core often saw their standards quality decline.

**NAEP Score Variation by State** (4th Grade Reading, 2022):

- **Highest performing**: Massachusetts (230), New Jersey (226), Connecticut (225)
- **Lowest performing**: New Mexico (203), Alaska (206), Louisiana (209)
- **Gap**: 27-point difference (approximately 2.5 grade levels)

## Why This Matters

### Curriculum Quality Affects Learning

Students cannot learn content they are not taught. Research consistently shows that access to rigorous curriculum is one of the strongest predictors of student achievement. A study by TNTP found that students in classrooms using grade-appropriate materials and assignments performed significantly better than those in classrooms using below-grade materials--a gap equivalent to months of learning.

Yet curriculum quality varies enormously. EdReports, which reviews curriculum alignment to standards, found that only 30% of widely-used ELA curricula and 40% of math curricula met expectations for quality. Many districts use materials that are poorly aligned, lack coherent sequencing, or fail to build knowledge systematically.

### Standards Shape Equity

Curriculum standards should ensure all students have access to rigorous content regardless of their zip code or school. Without common expectations, students in some schools learn advanced content while others receive watered-down versions. This variation correlates with race and socioeconomic status--students in high-poverty schools are more likely to receive instruction using below-grade materials.

### Democratic Stakes

What students learn about history, civics, and science shapes their capacity for democratic citizenship. If students graduate without understanding the Constitution, the Civil War, or climate science, they are poorly prepared to participate in democracy. Curriculum decisions are thus inherently political--but ideally should reflect democratic deliberation rather than partisan capture.

### Teacher Quality and Retention

Teachers need coherent curriculum to teach effectively. Constant curriculum changes, mandated materials, and politicized content battles undermine teacher professionalism and contribute to burnout. When teachers lack autonomy or are forced to use poor materials, their effectiveness and job satisfaction suffer.

## The Current Landscape

### Standards Architecture

Most states use a three-level structure:

1. **State Standards**: Define what students should know/do at each grade level
2. **Curriculum**: Specific scope and sequence, often selected at district level
3. **Instructional Materials**: Textbooks, resources, and programs aligned to curriculum

This structure creates complexity. State standards may be high-quality, but districts may adopt poor curricula, or teachers may lack materials aligned to the curriculum they're supposed to teach.

### The Common Core Legacy

The Common Core State Standards Initiative (2009-2010) was the most ambitious attempt at national curriculum coherence in American history. Developed by the National Governors Association and Council of Chief State School Officers with substantial Gates Foundation funding, Common Core established common math and English Language Arts standards across states.

At peak adoption, 46 states had adopted Common Core. By 2024, approximately 41 states retain standards closely aligned to Common Core, though many have rebranded them to avoid political controversy. Several states (Texas, Virginia, Alaska, Nebraska) never adopted Common Core, and others (Indiana, Oklahoma, South Carolina) formally withdrew.

### The Curriculum Wars

Beyond standards, states are fighting over curriculum content itself:

**History and Social Studies**:

- 18 states have passed laws restricting how race and history are taught
- Debates over "critical race theory" (a graduate-level academic framework rarely taught in K-12)
- Conflicts over 1619 Project, 1776 Commission, and teaching slavery and civil rights

**Science**:

- Evolution and climate change remain contested in some states
- Next Generation Science Standards adopted by 44 states face resistance
- Religious perspectives on origins remain politically potent

**Health Education**:

- Sex education content varies from abstinence-only to comprehensive
- LGBTQ+ inclusive content banned or restricted in some states
- Mental health curriculum emerging as new battleground

**Book Bans**:

- Over 4,000 book challenges in 2022-23 school year
- Organized campaigns targeting LGBTQ+ themes and racial content
- Some states requiring explicit parental consent for library access

## Path Forward

Effective curriculum reform requires:

1. **Transparency**: Parents and communities should know what is being taught
2. **Quality**: Standards and materials should be rigorous and evidence-based
3. **Coherence**: Standards, curriculum, and assessment should align
4. **Voice**: Local communities should shape values-based decisions
5. **Expertise**: Educators and subject-matter experts should guide technical decisions
6. **Protection**: Teachers and librarians need professional autonomy and legal protection
