# Curriculum Standards: Actions

## What Citizens Can Do

### Understanding Your Schools' Curriculum

**Step 1: Learn What's Being Taught**

| Action | How to Do It |
|--------|--------------|
| Find state standards | Search "[state] academic standards" online |
| Request curriculum overview | Contact district curriculum office |
| Review textbooks | Ask teacher or librarian to see materials |
| Attend curriculum night | Schools typically offer annual events |
| Access online portals | Many districts post curriculum information |

**Step 2: Evaluate Quality**

| Question | Where to Find Answer |
|----------|---------------------|
| Is curriculum aligned to standards? | EdReports.org ratings |
| Does curriculum build knowledge systematically? | Curriculum scope and sequence |
| Are materials engaging and rigorous? | Review materials yourself |
| Do teachers have quality training? | Ask about professional development |

**Step 3: Engage Constructively**

| Approach | How to Do It |
|----------|--------------|
| Attend school board meetings | Most are public; speak during public comment |
| Join curriculum committees | Many districts include parent representatives |
| Communicate with teachers | Email or schedule conferences |
| Participate in surveys | Respond to district/school surveys |

### Advocating for Curriculum Quality

**Promoting High-Quality Materials**

| Action | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Learn about curriculum quality | Read EdReports reviews for your district's materials |
| Ask questions at adoption time | Attend adoption committee meetings, ask about quality ratings |
| Advocate for improvement | If materials are low-quality, advocate for replacement |
| Support funding | High-quality materials require adequate funding |

**Supporting Science of Reading**

| Action | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Learn about reading research | Resources at thereadingleague.org |
| Ask about reading curriculum | What approach does your school use? |
| Advocate for evidence-based instruction | If school uses debunked methods, advocate for change |
| Support teacher training | Reading instruction improvement requires PD |

**Building a Coalition**

| Step | Action |
|------|--------|
| 1 | Connect with like-minded parents |
| 2 | Educate yourselves on curriculum issues |
| 3 | Develop shared priorities |
| 4 | Present unified message to board |
| 5 | Sustain engagement over time |

### Defending Against Harmful Restrictions

**If Books Are Challenged in Your Community**

| Step | Action |
|------|--------|
| 1 | Attend school board meetings on the issue |
| 2 | Speak in support of intellectual freedom |
| 3 | Connect with librarians and teachers |
| 4 | Organize other community members |
| 5 | Contact organizations like NCAC, ALA, PEN |
| 6 | Document and publicize restrictions |

**If Curriculum Restrictions Are Proposed**

| Step | Action |
|------|--------|
| 1 | Understand what is actually being proposed |
| 2 | Research the legislation or policy |
| 3 | Testify at hearings or board meetings |
| 4 | Write letters to legislators or board |
| 5 | Organize community opposition |
| 6 | Support affected teachers and librarians |

**Supporting Teachers**

| Action | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Express appreciation | Teachers facing attacks need support |
| Speak at board meetings | Counter negative voices |
| Join teacher advocacy groups | Support union or professional advocacy |
| Write op-eds | Public support matters |

### Running for School Board

School board races are increasingly important for curriculum decisions.

**Preparation**

| Step | Action |
|------|--------|
| 1 | Attend board meetings to understand issues |
| 2 | Review board policies on curriculum |
| 3 | Understand district demographics and politics |
| 4 | Build relationships with educators |
| 5 | Develop clear platform on curriculum |

**Campaign Priorities**

| Issue | Position to Consider |
|-------|---------------------|
| Curriculum quality | Support evidence-based, high-quality materials |
| Transparency | Reasonable transparency, not surveillance |
| Teacher autonomy | Professional discretion within curriculum |
| Library access | Protect intellectual freedom |
| Community input | Meaningful but not politicized processes |

**Resources for Candidates**

- Run for Something: runforsomething.net
- State school board associations
- Local party organizations
- Education advocacy groups

### Engaging in State Policy

**Contacting Legislators**

| Method | When to Use | Tips |
|--------|-------------|------|
| Phone call | Urgent issues, votes | Brief, specific ask |
| Email | Any issue | Personal story helps |
| Letter | Significant issue | More detailed |
| In-person | Lobby days, town halls | Most impactful |
| Social media | Visibility | Tag and be public |

**Testifying at Hearings**

| Preparation | Tips |
|-------------|------|
| Sign up to testify | Often first-come registration |
| Prepare written testimony | Leave copies for committee |
| Practice oral remarks | Stay within time limit |
| Bring personal stories | Legislators remember stories |
| Follow up | Thank supportive legislators |

**Key Messages for Legislators**

On curriculum quality:

- "High-quality curriculum is the most cost-effective education investment."
- "Our district uses materials that don't meet standards. Students deserve better."
- "Teachers need quality materials to be effective."

On academic freedom:

- "Teachers should teach the truth about history and science."
- "Book bans hurt students who need diverse perspectives."
- "Vague laws create chaos in classrooms."

On transparency:

- "Parents deserve to know what's taught, but not to control every lesson."
- "Reasonable transparency builds trust."
- "Surveillance of teachers undermines education."

### Informed Voting

**School Board Elections**

| Research Step | What to Look For |
|---------------|------------------|
| Candidate websites | Positions on curriculum, books |
| Endorsements | Teacher unions, advocacy groups |
| Campaign donors | Outside groups, local donors |
| Public statements | Interviews, social media |
| Forum attendance | How they engage with questions |

**State Elections**

| Office | Curriculum Relevance |
|--------|---------------------|
| Governor | Appoints education officials, signs/vetoes bills |
| State legislature | Passes curriculum laws, sets budgets |
| State board of education | Sets standards, approves materials (if elected) |
| State superintendent | Leads education department (if elected) |

**Questions to Ask Candidates**

1. What role should state government play in curriculum decisions?
2. How would you address curriculum quality in our schools?
3. What is your position on book challenges and removal?
4. How would you protect teacher academic freedom?
5. How should parents be involved in curriculum decisions?

## Strategies for Different Contexts

### In Conservative Communities

| Challenge | Approach |
|-----------|----------|
| Anti-Common Core sentiment | Focus on quality, not labels |
| Book ban pressure | Emphasize parental choice, not censorship |
| Distrust of educators | Build personal relationships |
| Religious concerns | Respect values while defending science |

**Effective Frames**:

- "Parental choice means MY child can read this book too"
- "Quality curriculum prepares students for jobs in our community"
- "Teachers deserve professional respect"
- "Local control means our community decides, not national groups"

### In Progressive Communities

| Challenge | Approach |
|-----------|----------|
| Resistance to standards | Emphasize equity, not standardization |
| Distrust of testing | Focus on curriculum, not accountability |
| Progressive curriculum concerns | Support culturally responsive approaches |
| Overconfidence about quality | Review actual materials, not reputation |

**Effective Frames**:

- "All students deserve rigorous curriculum, not just affluent schools"
- "Quality curriculum is an equity issue"
- "Diverse perspectives belong in our curriculum"
- "Evidence-based reading instruction helps struggling readers"

### In Mixed/Contested Communities

| Challenge | Approach |
|-----------|----------|
| Polarization | Find common ground on quality |
| Competing demands | Focus on students, not politics |
| Hostile board meetings | Model civil engagement |
| Burnout | Pace yourself, build coalition |

**Finding Common Ground**:

- Parents want their children to learn and succeed
- Quality curriculum benefits all students
- Teachers deserve support and respect
- Transparency builds trust
- Extreme positions don't help students
