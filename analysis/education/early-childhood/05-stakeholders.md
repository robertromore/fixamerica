# Early Childhood Education: Stakeholders

## Children

### Population Overview

The primary stakeholders in early childhood education are the nearly 20 million American children from birth through age five:

| Age Group | Population | % of Total U.S. |
|-----------|------------|-----------------|
| Under 1 | 3.6 million | 1.1% |
| Age 1 | 3.7 million | 1.1% |
| Age 2 | 3.8 million | 1.2% |
| Age 3 | 3.9 million | 1.2% |
| Age 4 | 4.0 million | 1.2% |
| Age 5 | 4.0 million | 1.2% |
| **Total under 6** | **23.0 million** | **6.9%** |

### Developmental Stakes

Children's interests in quality early experiences are profound and well-documented:

**Brain Development**:

- 90% of brain growth occurs before age 5
- One million neural connections form per second in early years
- Early experiences physically shape brain architecture
- Toxic stress causes lasting neurological damage
- Critical periods for language, attachment, and self-regulation cannot be recovered

**Long-Term Outcomes Affected**:

- Cognitive development and school readiness
- Social-emotional skills and self-regulation
- Physical health and stress response systems
- Mental health and resilience
- Educational attainment
- Economic mobility and lifetime earnings

### Differential Vulnerability

Some children face heightened vulnerability:

**Children in Poverty**:

- 17.5% of children under 5 live below poverty line
- Experience more toxic stress, less language exposure
- Less likely to access quality programs
- Greater benefit from intervention

**Children with Disabilities**:

- 14% of preschoolers have developmental delays
- Many delays identifiable early but undiagnosed
- Early intervention dramatically improves outcomes
- Access to services is highly variable

**Children of Color**:

- Disproportionately in poverty
- Less access to quality programs
- Subject to harsher discipline in preschool settings
- Culturally responsive care often lacking

**English Language Learners**:

- 22% of children under 5 live in households where English is not primary language
- Dual language development requires appropriate support
- Programs often lack bilingual staff
- Cultural discontinuity between home and care settings

### Representation Gap

Children have no voice in policy debates:

- Cannot vote or advocate for themselves
- Depend entirely on adults to represent their interests
- Immediate needs (food, comfort) more visible than developmental needs
- Long-term interests often sacrificed to short-term budget pressures

## Families

### Working Parents

The 12.5 million children under 5 with all parents in the workforce represent families whose economic survival depends on childcare:

**Family Employment Patterns**:

| Family Structure | % of Children Under 5 |
|-----------------|----------------------|
| Two-parent, both working | 48% |
| Single parent, working | 16% |
| Two-parent, one working | 28% |
| Single parent, not working | 8% |

For working families, childcare is infrastructure enabling employment, not a discretionary expense.

**Financial Burden**:

- Median family income with young children: $67,000
- Average childcare cost: $10,000-16,000/year
- Childcare as % of income: 15-24%
- "Affordable" threshold (HHS): 7%

### Stay-at-Home Parents

Approximately 28% of two-parent families have one parent (overwhelmingly mothers) who stays home:

**Motivations**:

- Preference for parental care (54%)
- Childcare costs exceed potential earnings (32%)
- Unable to find quality care (12%)
- Other factors (2%)

Many "choose" to stay home because of affordability and access problems, not pure preference. When asked, 60% of stay-at-home mothers say they would prefer to work if quality, affordable care were available.

### Grandparents and Extended Family

18% of children under 5 are cared for primarily by grandparents or other relatives:

**Grandparent Caregivers**:

- 7.8 million grandparents live with grandchildren
- 2.4 million are primary caregivers
- Average age of grandparent caregiver: 56
- Many sacrifice employment, retirement, health

Grandparents provide critical flexibility but often lack support, training, or compensation for their caregiving labor.

### Family Needs and Interests

**Common Family Needs**:

- Affordable care that doesn't impoverish the family
- Safe, nurturing environments for children
- Hours matching work schedules (often non-traditional)
- Convenient locations near home or work
- Responsive communication with providers
- Cultural and linguistic responsiveness
- Stability and continuity of care

**Tensions Within Family Interests**:

- Cost vs. quality trade-offs
- Convenience vs. highest quality options
- Work demands vs. child's needs
- Immediate needs vs. long-term development

## Early Childhood Workforce

### The Care Workforce

An estimated 1.2 million workers are directly employed in early childhood care and education:

| Setting | Estimated Workforce |
|---------|---------------------|
| Center-based childcare | 600,000 |
| Family childcare | 150,000 |
| Head Start | 220,000 |
| Public school pre-K | 120,000 |
| Nannies and babysitters | 300,000+ |

### Workforce Demographics

The early childhood workforce is predominantly female, diverse, and economically vulnerable:

**Demographics**:

- 94% female
- 40% people of color
- 19% immigrants
- Median age: 37
- 30% have children under 18

**Economic Status**:

- Median wage: $13.22/hour
- Median annual earnings: $27,490
- 53% on public assistance
- 15% have employer health insurance
- 25% below 200% poverty level

### Workforce Interests

**Primary Needs**:

- Living wages sufficient for economic security
- Health insurance and retirement benefits
- Paid sick leave and family leave
- Professional development and advancement pathways
- Respect and recognition as educators
- Manageable ratios and class sizes
- Safe and supportive work environments

**Barriers to Organizing**:

- Dispersed across small employers
- Low union density (limited to public sector pre-K and some Head Start)
- High turnover prevents sustained organizing
- Many workers are independent contractors (nannies, family childcare)
- Employer resistance

### Emerging Workforce Advocacy

In recent years, early childhood workforce organizing has increased:

**Key Organizations**:

- Child Care Providers United (California, SEIU affiliate)
- Worthy Wages Campaign
- National Head Start Association
- NAEYC (National Association for the Education of Young Children)

California's home-based childcare providers won the right to collectively bargain in 2020, representing a potential model.

## Childcare Providers and Operators

### Provider Types

The early childhood sector includes diverse provider types with different interests:

**For-Profit Chains**:

- Bright Horizons, KinderCare, Learning Care Group
- Serve higher-income families and employer contracts
- Interest in stable subsidy rates and minimal regulation
- Employ approximately 15% of center workforce

**Independent For-Profit Centers**:

- Single-location or small multi-site operators
- Razor-thin margins, high failure rates
- Interest in reducing costs and bureaucratic burden
- Often owner-operated with minimal staffing

**Nonprofit Centers**:

- Community-based organizations, churches, universities
- Mission-driven but financially constrained
- Interest in public funding to expand access
- Often cross-subsidize with other organizational resources

**Family Childcare Providers**:

- Home-based care, typically 6-12 children
- Often serving their own communities
- Extremely limited profit margins
- Interest in recognition, support, and fair subsidy rates

**Head Start Grantees**:

- Nonprofit organizations or local governments
- Dependent on federal Head Start funding
- Interest in funding increases and regulatory flexibility
- Often provide comprehensive services beyond education

### Provider Economics

The economics of childcare provision are precarious:

**Typical Center Budget (100-child center)**:

| Category | % of Budget |
|----------|-------------|
| Staff wages and benefits | 60-70% |
| Rent/facilities | 10-15% |
| Food | 5-8% |
| Supplies and curriculum | 3-5% |
| Administration | 5-8% |
| Margin/reserves | 2-5% |

Profit margins are typically 2-5%, leaving no buffer for unexpected costs. Many nonprofit centers operate at break-even or loss.

### Provider Interests

**Common Provider Interests**:

- Sustainable revenue sufficient to cover costs
- Subsidy rates that match true cost of care
- Timely subsidy payments (delays cause cash flow crises)
- Regulatory streamlining where possible
- Technical assistance for quality improvement
- Workforce recruitment and retention support

**Tensions Among Providers**:

- For-profit vs. nonprofit competition
- Large chains vs. small independents
- Family childcare vs. center-based care
- Market-rate vs. subsidized programs

## States and State Agencies

### State Roles

States are the primary regulators and significant funders of early childhood:

**Regulatory Functions**:

- Licensing standards and enforcement
- Background check administration
- QRIS development and implementation
- Subsidy eligibility determination
- Fraud prevention and oversight

**Funding Functions**:

- Administer CCDBG and other federal funds
- Operate state pre-K programs
- Set subsidy reimbursement rates
- Provide quality improvement grants
- Fund workforce development

### State Variation

States vary enormously in their early childhood investments:

**High-Investment States**:

- Universal pre-K (D.C., Florida, Oklahoma, Vermont)
- Robust subsidy systems with high reimbursement rates
- Strong licensing standards and enforcement
- Comprehensive QRIS tied to funding
- Workforce investment and wage supplements

**Low-Investment States**:

- No state pre-K (Idaho, Montana, Wyoming)
- Limited subsidy funding with long waiting lists
- Minimal licensing standards
- Voluntary or weak QRIS
- No workforce supports

### State Agency Interests

**Common State Interests**:

- Flexibility in using federal funds
- Reduced reporting burden
- Evidence of effectiveness for investments
- Workforce availability for economic development
- Political credit for popular programs

## Federal Government

### Federal Agencies

Multiple federal agencies administer early childhood programs:

**Administration for Children and Families (HHS)**:

- Head Start and Early Head Start
- Child Care and Development Block Grant
- Preschool Development Grants
- Home visiting programs

**Department of Education**:

- Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (Part C early intervention, Part B preschool)
- Title I preschool funding
- Race to the Top-Early Learning Challenge (concluded)

**Internal Revenue Service (Treasury)**:

- Child and Dependent Care Tax Credit
- Employer-provided childcare exclusion

**Department of Agriculture**:

- Child and Adult Care Food Program (meals in childcare)

### Federal Interests

**Cross-Administration Interests**:

- Cost control and efficient use of funds
- Evidence of program effectiveness
- Fraud prevention and accountability
- State compliance with federal requirements

**Political Interests** (vary by administration):

- Expansion of access (Democratic administrations)
- Private sector solutions, reduced spending (Republican administrations)
- Workforce participation support (bipartisan)
- Child development focus vs. work support (ideological divide)

## Employers

### Employer Dependence on Childcare

Employers depend on childcare infrastructure even if they don't directly provide it:

**Workforce Impacts of Childcare Breakdown**:

- Absenteeism when care falls through
- Reduced productivity from distraction and stress
- Turnover when parents cannot manage care
- Reduced labor force participation
- Difficulty recruiting in childcare deserts

**Quantified Employer Costs**:

- $57 billion annual lost productivity from childcare problems
- $4.4 billion in excess turnover costs
- 1.5 million parents involuntarily part-time due to childcare

### Employer Responses

Employers respond to childcare needs in various ways:

**Direct Provision**:

- On-site or near-site childcare centers
- Backup childcare programs
- Typically large employers serving headquarters locations

**Financial Assistance**:

- Dependent Care Flexible Spending Accounts
- Direct childcare subsidies
- Tuition discounts at partner providers

**Schedule Flexibility**:

- Telecommuting options
- Flexible scheduling
- Part-time and job-sharing options

**Advocacy**:

- Business coalitions for public investment
- Chamber of Commerce positions
- Regional economic development initiatives

### Employer Interests

**Primary Business Interests**:

- Workforce availability and reliability
- Competitive benefits packages for recruitment
- Reduced absenteeism and turnover
- Community economic development
- Limited direct costs and liability

**Tensions**:

- Short-term cost minimization vs. long-term workforce development
- Individual employer free-riding on public or other employers' investments
- Low-tax ideology vs. need for public infrastructure

**Focus Areas**:

**Functions**:

## Opposition Stakeholders

Detailed in [Opposition](06-opposition.md), key opponents include:

- **Fiscal conservatives**: Oppose spending increases
- **Family values organizations**: Oppose non-maternal care
- **Market-oriented think tanks**: Oppose government provision
- **Some religious providers**: Oppose regulation and standards

## Stakeholder Power Analysis

| Stakeholder | Resources | Access | Organization | Overall Power |
|-------------|-----------|--------|--------------|---------------|
| Children | None | None | None | Very Low |
| Parents | Votes | Limited | Weak | Low-Medium |
| Workforce | Votes | Limited | Weak | Low |
| Providers | Campaign $$ | Moderate | Moderate | Medium |
| States | Institutional | High | Strong | High |
| Federal Government | Ultimate | Highest | N/A | Highest |
| Employers | Campaign $$ | High | Strong | High |
| Advocates | Expertise | Moderate | Strong | Medium |
| Opposition | Campaign $$ | High | Strong | High |

The fundamental imbalance: those with the greatest stake (children, families, workers) have the least power, while those with power (employers, governments) have less direct stake.
