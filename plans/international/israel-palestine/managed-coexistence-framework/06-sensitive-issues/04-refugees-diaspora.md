**Status:** Final Draft

## Purpose

Refugees and Diaspora defines MCF's approach to Palestinian refugee and diaspora questions through **structured postponement**â€”explicitly deferring resolution while preserving all claims and allowing limited, non-territorial participation.

MCF does not attempt to resolve the Palestinian refugee issue or the right of return. Instead, it separates present-day conflict management from future political resolution, on the premise that unmanaged violence today makes just outcomes tomorrow less likely.

---

## Core Principles

### 1. Explicit Deferral with Non-Preclusion

Nothing in MCF forecloses future refugee claims. Nothing in MCF operationalizes those claims in early or mid phases.

**What This Means:**
- Right of return remains a political question
- Compensation claims preserved
- Restitution options unaffected
- Future negotiation space maintained

### 2. Separation of Tracks

Refugee questions are intentionally separated from in-territory conflict management.

**What This Means:**
- MCF focuses on current violence reduction
- Refugee resolution is a separate political track
- Neither track hostage to the other
- Progress possible on both independently

### 3. Limited Participation Without Territorial Rights

Refugees can participate in MCF in limited ways without that participation conferring territorial rights.

**What This Means:**
- Non-residential program access possible
- Credential portability available
- Institutional participation permitted
- No residency or return rights through MCF

### 4. Avoiding Two Errors

MCF avoids treating refugees as irrelevant (delegitimizing) and treating CMS as backdoor return (destabilizing).

**What This Means:**
- Refugees acknowledged as part of conflict
- Limited participation available
- Clear limits on what participation means
- Balance between inclusion and stability

## Refugee Context

### 1. Population Overview

**UNRWA Registered Refugees (2023):**
- Jordan: ~2.4 million
- West Bank: ~900,000
- Gaza: ~1.5 million
- Syria: ~580,000
- Lebanon: ~490,000
- Total: ~5.9 million

**Additional Diaspora:**
- Non-registered Palestinians worldwide
- Varying connection to 1948/1967 displacement
- Multiple generations

### 2. Legal Status Variations

**Jordan:**
- Most have Jordanian citizenship
- Relatively integrated
- Camp populations remain
- Complex political relationship with Palestinian identity

**Lebanon:**
- No citizenship path
- Severe restrictions on employment, property
- Camp populations isolated
- Precarious legal status

**Syria:**
- Had relatively good treatment pre-war
- War displacement created secondary displacement
- Ongoing crisis situation

**West Bank/Gaza:**
- Refugees within Palestinian territories
- Different status from host-country refugees
- Internal displacement dynamics

### 3. Right of Return

**Palestinian Position:**
- Right of return to original homes/lands
- UN Resolution 194 basis
- Individual choice
- Compensation as alternative or addition

**Israeli Position:**
- No right of return to Israel
- Refugee status should end
- Resettlement in Palestinian state/elsewhere
- Compensation possible

**MCF Position:**
- No position on right of return
- Deferred to political negotiation
- Non-prejudice to all claims
- Separation from conflict management

## CMS Eligibility Framework

### 1. General Principle

Palestinian refugees are **not categorically excluded** from CMS, but eligibility is **functionally constrained and phased**.

### 2. Initial Phase Eligibility

**Phase 0-1:**
- CMS primarily for in-territory participants
- Limited refugee eligibility
- Focus on stable populations
- Careful expansion

**Refugee Eligibility (Limited):**
- Professional credentialing participation
- Arbitration access (for relevant disputes)
- Training programs
- Institutional roles in external SAZs

### 3. What CMS Provides Refugees

**Available:**
- Framework benefit access (non-residential)
- Credential portability
- Arbitration access
- Professional development
- Institutional participation (outside Israel/Palestine)

**Not Available:**
- Residency rights in Israel
- Residency rights in Palestinian territories (through MCF)
- Return mechanism
- Territorial claims

### 4. What CMS Does Not Alter

**Unchanged:**
- Refugee legal status under UNRWA
- Host-country legal status
- International law rights
- Political claims

**Explicit Statement Required:**
> "CMS participation by refugees does not prejudice, satisfy, waive, or adjudicate claims related to return, compensation, or restitution."

## Non-Preclusion Guarantees

### 1. Claim Preservation

**MCF Explicitly Preserves:**
- Right of return claims
- Compensation claims
- Restitution claims
- All legal positions

**Mechanism:**
- Non-prejudice language in all documents
- CMS terms explicitly preserve claims
- No MCF authority over refugee questions
- Deferred to political track

### 2. No Implicit Resolution

**MCF Does Not:**
- Constitute a mechanism of return
- Substitute for return rights
- Provide alternative to return
- Satisfy return claims

**MCF Does Not:**
- Deny return rights
- Foreclose return options
- Create precedent against return
- Prejudice negotiations

### 3. Future Political Process

**Refugee Resolution Requires:**
- Political negotiation between parties
- International support and facilitation
- Comprehensive agreement
- Separate from MCF

**MCF Role:**
- Provides stability for negotiations
- Does not determine outcome
- Supports whatever parties agree
- Preserves all options

## Phased Expansion

### 1. Phase 0-1: Minimal Engagement

**Focus:**
- In-territory conflict management
- Limited refugee participation
- Non-territorial benefits only
- Careful eligibility screening

**Eligible Activities:**
- Professional credentialing
- Training programs
- External SAZ participation
- Arbitration (limited)

### 2. Phase 2: Expanded Access

**If Stability Achieved:**
- Broader credential portability
- More training access
- Economic program participation
- Institutional roles expansion

**Still Limited:**
- No residential rights
- No territorial claims
- No return mechanism

### 3. Phase 3+: Political Track Convergence

**If Political Progress:**
- MCF could support implementation of agreements
- Refugee return management (if agreed)
- Integration support (if agreed)
- Only at parties' explicit request

**MCF Alone Cannot:**
- Create return mechanism
- Determine refugee outcomes
- Override political negotiations

## Host Country Considerations

### 1. Jordan

**Considerations:**
- Most refugees have citizenship
- Political sensitivity around Palestinian identity
- Large refugee population
- Generally stable integration

**MCF Approach:**
- Respect Jordanian sovereignty
- CMS available to eligible individuals
- No interference with Jordanian policy
- Coordination with Jordanian authorities

### 2. Lebanon

**Considerations:**
- Refugees denied basic rights
- Political sensitivity extreme
- Camp populations isolated
- Resettlement rejected

**MCF Approach:**
- Extreme caution
- Limited engagement
- Respect Lebanese policy (even if unjust)
- Non-residential participation only

### 3. Syria

**Considerations:**
- Ongoing conflict
- Secondary displacement
- Uncertain conditions
- Limited access

**MCF Approach:**
- Minimal current engagement
- Documentation and monitoring
- Humanitarian coordination
- Future engagement when possible

### 4. Other Locations

**Considerations:**
- Diaspora worldwide
- Varying connection to conflict
- Different legal statuses

**MCF Approach:**
- CMS eligibility based on individual circumstances
- Non-territorial participation
- Credential portability benefits
- No special status based on location

## Avoiding Destabilization

### 1. No Demographic Manipulation

**MCF Does Not:**
- Encourage refugee movement
- Facilitate demographic changes
- Support population transfers
- Create return pressure

**Balance:**
Individual participation available without creating collective demographic dynamics.

### 2. No Political Weaponization

**MCF Does Not:**
- Use refugees as political leverage
- Create refugee-dependent policies
- Make refugees MCF success metric
- Tie MCF progress to refugee resolution

**Independence:**
MCF conflict management success separate from refugee question resolution.

### 3. No False Hopes

**MCF Does Not:**
- Promise return through MCF
- Suggest CMS leads to return
- Create expectation of territorial rights
- Imply MCF solves refugee issue

**Honesty:**
Clear communication about what MCF can and cannot provide.

## Diaspora Engagement

### 1. Non-Residential Participation

**Available:**
- Professional credentialing
- Economic program participation (remote/external)
- Institutional roles
- Arbitration access

**Focus:**
Engagement that doesn't require physical presence in contested territory.

### 2. External SAZ Participation

**If External SAZ Established:**
- Diaspora can participate in external SAZ
- Training and institutional roles
- Professional development
- Limited-term engagement

**No Permanent Relocation:**
External SAZ is not resettlement mechanism.

### 3. Economic Integration

**Supported:**
- Trade facilitation
- Investment channels
- Business development
- Professional networks

**Limits:**
Economic engagement does not confer territorial rights.

## Institutional Safeguards

### 1. Eligibility Screening

**Screening Ensures:**
- Individual meets conduct standards
- No security concerns
- Understanding of CMS limitations
- Voluntary participation

**Not a Barrier:**
Screening is for MCF integrity, not to exclude refugees categorically.

### 2. Clear Terms

**CMS Terms for Refugees:**
- Explicit non-return statement
- Claim preservation acknowledgment
- Limitation acceptance
- Voluntary participation confirmation

### 3. Monitoring

**Monitor For:**
- Misuse of CMS for territorial claims
- False promises about return
- Political manipulation
- Expectation management failure

## Future Political Constituency

### 1. Long-Term Vision

MCF treats refugees as a **future political constituency**, not an early operational driver.

**What This Means:**
- Refugee voice matters for political resolution
- Political negotiations will address refugees
- MCF doesn't substitute for that voice
- Inclusion is goal, timing is strategic

### 2. Incremental Inclusion

**Inclusion Is:**
- Incremental (phased)
- Optional (voluntary)
- Non-territorial (no residency rights)
- Expandable (as conditions permit)

### 3. Conditions for Expansion

**Expansion When:**
- In-territory stability demonstrated
- Political track active
- Host countries engaged
- Security concerns manageable

**Expansion Not:**
- Automatic
- Guaranteed
- MCF-determined alone
