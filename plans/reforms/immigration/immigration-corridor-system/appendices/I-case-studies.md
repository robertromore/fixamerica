# Appendix I: Case Studies

## Overview

These hypothetical case studies illustrate how different individuals and families would navigate the Immigration Corridor System.

## Case Study 1: Single Adult Worker

### Profile: Carlos

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Age** | 28 |
| **Country of origin** | Guatemala |
| **Education** | High school graduate |
| **Skills** | Construction experience |
| **Family** | None in US |
| **Entry** | Border crossing |

### ICS Journey

**Phase 0: Intake (Week 1-4)**

- Presents at border processing point
- Biometrics collected; background check initiated
- Health screening; vaccinations updated
- Provisional CLP issued
- Placement algorithm matches Carlos to Buffalo hub (construction labor demand, housing availability)

**Tier A: Hub City (Months 1-8)**

- Arrives in Buffalo; placed in shared housing
- Enrolls in evening ESL (10 hours/week)
- Connects with construction union apprenticeship program
- Works daytime; studies evenings
- Achieves CEFR A1 at month 4
- No legal issues; regular check-ins

**Tier B: Corridor City (Months 8-26)**

- Advances to Tier B; transfers to Rochester (more construction demand)
- Continues apprenticeship; promoted to crew lead
- Achieves CEFR A2 at month 12
- Saves for car purchase; gets driver's license
- Completes civic orientation
- Achieves CEFR B1 at month 24

**Tier C: National Mobility (Months 26-38)**

- Advances to Tier C; full US mobility
- Accepts job offer in Phoenix; relocates
- Opens bank account; builds credit
- Begins permanent residence application

**Permanent Residence (Month 38+)**

- PR application approved at month 42
- Now can sponsor future spouse
- Continues working toward citizenship

### Key Takeaways

- Clear timeline with defined benchmarks
- Work authorization from day one
- Skills-based placement matched needs
- Language acquisition over 2+ years achievable
- Geographic progression incentivized advancement

## Case Study 2: Family with Children

### Profile: The Martinez Family

| Member | Age | Notes |
|--------|-----|-------|
| **Maria (mother)** | 35 | Principal applicant |
| **Roberto (husband)** | 38 | Derivative |
| **Sofia (daughter)** | 12 | Derivative |
| **Diego (son)** | 7 | Derivative |

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Country of origin** | Honduras |
| **Education** | Maria: nursing assistant training; Roberto: mechanic |
| **Entry** | Border crossing together |

### ICS Journey

**Phase 0: Intake (Week 1-3)**

- Family presents together; processed as unit
- Relationships verified via documents
- All four issued provisional CLP
- Placement algorithm considers: schools with capacity, healthcare jobs, family housing
- Matched to Cleveland hub

**Tier A: Hub City (Months 1-10)**

- Family placed in 2-bedroom apartment (subsidized)
- Children enrolled immediately in school; Sofia in middle school, Diego in elementary
- Both children receive EL services
- Maria: enrolled in credential evaluation; works as home health aide while pursuing RN pathway
- Roberto: connects with auto shop; works while studying for ASE certification
- Parents attend Saturday ESL (family literacy program)
- Family achieves A1/A2 English; children advance faster

**Tier B: Corridor City (Months 10-28)**

- Family advances together (joint advancement rule)
- Transfers to Akron (Maria got hospital job; Roberto opens small mechanic shop)
- Sofia now fluent; mainstreamed out of EL services
- Diego progressing well; honor roll
- Parents achieve A2; working toward B1
- Maria completes nursing bridge program; becomes RN

**Tier C: National Mobility (Months 28-40)**

- Family achieves Tier C (parents at B1)
- Children are essentially American kids
- Family purchases home in Akron (CLP holders with history can qualify)
- Strong community ties; decide to stay

**Permanent Residence (Month 40+)**

- PR applications filed for all four
- Diego (now 10) is effectively growing up American
- Sofia (now 15) planning for college

### Key Takeaways

- Family kept together throughout
- Children integrated faster than adults
- Two-generation strategy worked (kids in school, parents working and learning)
- Joint advancement kept family on same timeline
- Community roots developed during corridor phase

## Case Study 3: Unaccompanied Minor

### Profile: Ana

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Age at entry** | 16 |
| **Country of origin** | El Salvador |
| **Reason for flight** | Gang threats |
| **Family in US** | Uncle (US citizen) in Chicago |

### ICS Journey

**Phase 0: Intake (Days 1-60)**

- Apprehended at border; TVPRA screening
- Transferred to ORR custody within 72 hours
- Placed in licensed shelter facility
- Legal orientation; assigned attorney (ICS-funded)
- Asylum application filed (credible fear found)
- Uncle located; background check initiated
- Best-interest determination completed
- Released to uncle at day 45

**Post-Release + Tier A (Months 2-14)**

- Living with uncle in Chicago (not hub city, but sponsor-based exception)
- Enrolled in high school immediately
- Receives post-release services: case management, mental health, legal
- Asylum interview scheduled
- Continues ESL at school; rapid progress
- Case manager checks in at 30, 60, 90 days

**Asylum Decision + Tier B (Month 14)**

- Asylum granted based on gang persecution
- CLP terminates; Ana is now asylee
- Better status: can apply for PR after 1 year
- Continues high school; college prep

**Asylee Status (Months 14-26)**

- Ana is asylee, not CLP
- Full federal benefits access
- Applies for PR at month 26 (1 year after asylum grant)

**Permanent Residence and Beyond**

- PR approved at month 30
- Graduates high school; attends community college
- Can petition for mother in El Salvador
- Becomes citizen at 24

### Key Takeaways

- TVPRA protections fully maintained
- Family reunification prioritized
- Legal representation made difference in asylum case
- Dual-track (CLP + asylum) worked smoothly
- Successful asylee pathway faster than CLP track

## Case Study 4: Legacy Track Applicant

### Profile: Miguel

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Age** | 45 |
| **Years in US** | 22 |
| **Occupation** | Restaurant owner |
| **Family** | US citizen wife; 2 US citizen children (adults) |
| **Current status** | Undocumented (overstayed visa) |

### ICS Journey

**Legacy Registration (Months 1-6)**

- ICS enacted; legacy track opens
- Miguel registers within 6-month window
- Provides: continuous presence documentation, tax records, business records, family ties
- Background check completed
- No disqualifying criminal history

**Legacy CLP Issuance (Month 6)**

- Granted CLP under legacy track
- Geographic waiver: can remain in current location (established business, family)
- Already meets B1 English (22 years in US)
- Already employed (owns business)

**Tier Placement (Month 6)**

- Placed directly in Tier C (legacy track accelerated)
- No hub/corridor requirement (waived for established residents)
- Full US mobility immediately

**Permanent Residence (Months 6-18)**

- Files PR application immediately
- US citizen wife could sponsor, but legacy track is faster
- PR approved at month 15

**Citizenship (Month 75)**

- Becomes citizen at month 75 (5 years after PR)
- Finally fully regularized after 27 years in US

### Key Takeaways

- Legacy track recognizes long-term integration
- Geographic waiver for established residents
- Accelerated timeline for those already meeting benchmarks
- Tax compliance and business ownership counted
- Family unity preserved (wife and children already citizens)

## Case Study 5: Asylum Seeker with Denial

### Profile: Jean

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Age** | 32 |
| **Country of origin** | Cameroon |
| **Claim basis** | Political persecution |
| **Entry** | Port of entry asylum request |

### ICS Journey

**Phase 0: Intake (Weeks 1-4)**

- Arrives at port of entry; requests asylum
- Credible fear screening: positive
- CLP issued; referred for asylum hearing
- Placed in Detroit hub

**Tier A: Hub City + Asylum Processing (Months 1-18)**

- Works at warehouse job
- Attends ESL; achieves A2
- Asylum hearing at month 12
- Decision: denied (adjudicator found claim not credible)

**Tier B + Appeals (Months 18-36)**

- CLP continues; advances to Tier B despite asylum denial
- Moves to Grand Rapids (corridor city)
- Files BIA appeal
- BIA denies at month 24
- Files federal court petition
- Federal court denies at month 30

**Post-Exhaustion Review (Month 36)**

- All appeals exhausted
- Removal proceedings initiated
- CLP status under review
- Jean can still seek withholding of removal or CAT
- If both denied: removal order issued

**Potential Outcomes**

*Outcome A: Withholding Granted*

- Cannot be removed to Cameroon
- CLP continues (provides integration structure)
- No path to PR through asylum, but CLP path remains

*Outcome B: All Relief Denied*

- Removal order issued
- Due process completed over 3 years
- Voluntary departure option offered
- Jean returns to Cameroon with savings from US work

### Key Takeaways

- CLP protected Jean during lengthy appeals (3+ years)
- Due process fully provided
- Work authorization allowed self-support throughout
- Even denied, Jean had stability during process
- System didn't warehouse Jean in detention

## Case Study 6: Elderly Family Member

### Profile: Rosa

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Age** | 68 |
| **Country of origin** | Mexico |
| **Entry** | Accompanying daughter's family |
| **Health** | Diabetic; limited mobility |
| **English** | Very limited |

### ICS Journey

**Phase 0: Intake (Weeks 1-4)**

- Enters with daughter's family
- Not derivative (adults cannot be derivatives)
- Must qualify independently
- Issued own CLP based on general eligibility

**Tier A: Hub City (Months 1-18)**

- Placed with daughter's family (same hub)
- Enrolled in senior-pace ESL program
- Healthcare at FQHC; diabetes management
- Limited mobility accommodated
- Language progress slow; achieves A1 at month 18

**Tier B: Corridor City (Months 18-42)**

- Advances with modified benchmarks (elderly accommodation)
- Language requirement: continued participation, not B1
- Remains with family; caregiver role for grandchildren
- Healthcare continues at FQHC

**Tier C Consideration (Month 42+)**

- B1 requirement waived (elderly exemption)
- Advances to Tier C based on time + good standing
- Continues healthcare access
- Limited work; focuses on family caregiving

**Permanent Residence (Month 48+)**

- Applies for PR through legacy/elderly pathway
- Daughter (now PR) could petition
- Rosa becomes PR; eligible for more benefits
- Medicare eligibility accrues with work history (limited)

### Key Takeaways

- Elderly accommodations prevent permanent exclusion
- Family kept together despite independent status
- Healthcare access throughout
- Language exemption for genuine hardship
- Longer timeline acceptable for elderly
