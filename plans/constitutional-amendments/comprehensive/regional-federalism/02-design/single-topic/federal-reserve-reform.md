# Article XV: Federal Reserve Reform Amendment

## Status: Draft

This standalone constitutional amendment establishes constitutional parameters for the Federal Reserve System, balancing monetary policy independence with democratic accountability. It works with either the existing federal/state system or with Regional Federalism.

### Section 1. Constitutional Status of the Federal Reserve

(a) There shall be a Federal Reserve System serving as the central bank of the United States.

(b) The Federal Reserve shall be an independent agency within the government of the United States, subject to this Constitution and to such laws as Congress may enact consistent with this Article.

(c) The independence of the Federal Reserve in conducting monetary policy shall not be construed to place it beyond the oversight authority of Congress or the accountability requirements of this Article.

(d) Congress shall have power to reform the structure and governance of the Federal Reserve System by law, subject to the limitations in this Article.

### Section 2. Monetary Policy Mandate

(a) The primary objective of monetary policy shall be price stability, defined as maintaining the purchasing power of the currency over time.

(b) Subject to achieving price stability, the Federal Reserve shall support:

(1) maximum sustainable employment;

(2) moderate long-term interest rates;

(3) the stability of the financial system.

(c) The Federal Reserve shall establish and publish a numerical target for price stability. Deviations from the target shall be explained in writing to Congress.

(d) Monetary policy decisions shall be based on economic data and analysis, not political considerations. No officer of the executive branch shall direct or attempt to direct specific monetary policy actions.

(e) The Federal Reserve shall not monetize the debt of the United States by purchasing Treasury securities directly from the Treasury except during declared emergencies under Section 7.

### Section 3. Board of Governors

(a) The Federal Reserve System shall be governed by a Board of Governors consisting of seven members.

(b) Governors shall be appointed by the President and confirmed by the Senate for staggered terms of fourteen years. No Governor may serve more than one full term.

(c) The Chair and Vice Chair shall be designated by the President from among the Governors for terms of four years. They may be redesignated but may not serve more than eight years in those positions.

(d) No more than four Governors may be members of the same political party.

(e) Governors shall be persons of demonstrated experience and competence in economics, finance, banking, or monetary policy.

(f) Governors may be removed only for cause, defined as:

(1) malfeasance in office;

(2) neglect of duty;

(3) incapacity to perform duties;

(4) conviction of a felony.

(g) Removal requires a two-thirds vote of the Senate upon recommendation by the President.

### Section 4. Federal Open Market Committee

(a) Monetary policy decisions shall be made by the Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC), consisting of:

(1) the seven members of the Board of Governors;

(2) five presidents of Federal Reserve Banks, on a rotating basis as provided by law.

(b) The Chair of the Board of Governors shall serve as Chair of the FOMC.

(c) FOMC decisions shall be made by majority vote. Dissenting views shall be recorded and published.

(d) The FOMC shall meet at least eight times per year and shall publish minutes of its meetings within three weeks.

### Section 5. Federal Reserve Banks

(a) There shall be not fewer than eight nor more than twelve Federal Reserve Banks, located throughout the United States as provided by law.

(b) Each Federal Reserve Bank shall be governed by a board of nine directors:

(1) three directors representing the public interest, appointed by the Board of Governors;

(2) three directors representing member banks, elected by member banks;

(3) three directors representing the diverse economic interests of the district, elected by member banks but who may not be officers, directors, or employees of any bank.

(c) The president of each Federal Reserve Bank shall be appointed by its board of directors, subject to approval by the Board of Governors, for a term of five years, renewable once.

(d) Federal Reserve Bank presidents shall be persons of demonstrated competence in economics, finance, or banking and shall not have been employed by any institution supervised by the Federal Reserve within the preceding five years.

### Section 6. Accountability and Transparency

(a) The Chair of the Board of Governors shall testify before Congress at least four times per year on monetary policy, economic conditions, and the activities of the Federal Reserve.

(b) The Federal Reserve shall publish:

(1) a quarterly monetary policy report explaining its assessment of economic conditions and policy decisions;

(2) transcripts of FOMC meetings after five years;

(3) annual financial statements audited by an independent auditor;

(4) the economic projections and models used in policy decisions.

(c) The Government Accountability Office shall conduct an annual audit of the Federal Reserve's administrative operations and financial statements, but shall not audit or second-guess monetary policy decisions.

(d) Congress may by law require additional disclosures consistent with this Article.

(e) The Federal Reserve shall operate a public digital ledger of its balance sheet, updated weekly.

### Section 7. Emergency Authority

(a) During a financial emergency declared by the President or by joint resolution of Congress, the Federal Reserve may:

(1) extend credit to any participant in financial markets to prevent systemic collapse;

(2) purchase a broader range of assets than normally permitted;

(3) provide liquidity to foreign central banks through swap arrangements;

(4) take other extraordinary measures to preserve financial stability.

(b) Emergency authority under this section:

(1) shall expire after one hundred eighty days unless extended by Congress;

(2) shall be reported to Congress within forty-eight hours;

(3) shall be subject to quarterly review by Congress during its exercise.

(c) The Federal Reserve shall establish in advance, and publish, the terms and conditions under which emergency credit will be extended, and shall not extend credit on terms more favorable than those published except with the approval of the Secretary of the Treasury.

(d) Emergency lending shall be at a penalty rate above normal market rates and shall be collateralized.

(e) Within one year after the termination of emergency authority, the Federal Reserve shall report to Congress on:

(1) all emergency actions taken;

(2) the recipients and terms of emergency credit;

(3) any losses incurred;

(4) lessons learned and recommendations for reform.

### Section 8. Limitations on Federal Reserve Activities

(a) The Federal Reserve shall not:

(1) allocate credit to specific sectors, industries, or firms except as necessary during emergencies under Section 7;

(2) purchase equity securities of private corporations;

(3) make unsecured loans except to depository institutions and except during emergencies under Section 7;

(4) engage in fiscal policy by distributing funds directly to individuals or businesses;

(5) discriminate among financial institutions on grounds unrelated to safety and soundness.

(b) The Federal Reserve may not implement a central bank digital currency without authorization by Act of Congress specifying:

(1) privacy protections for users;

(2) limits on surveillance of transactions;

(3) the relationship to physical currency;

(4) protections against financial exclusion.

### Section 9. Relationship to Fiscal Authority

(a) The Federal Reserve and the Treasury shall coordinate on matters of mutual concern while respecting the independence of monetary policy.

(b) The Treasury may not direct the Federal Reserve to purchase Treasury securities except during emergencies under Section 7 and with the approval of Congress.

(c) The Federal Reserve shall remit its net earnings to the Treasury annually, after providing for reserves as determined by the Board of Governors.

(d) The Federal Reserve shall not directly finance fiscal deficits except during emergencies under Section 7.

### Section 10. Protection of Currency

(a) The dollar shall remain the official currency of the United States.

(b) Congress shall have power to define the dollar and regulate its value, but shall not delegate this power to any international body.

(c) No treaty or international agreement shall commit the United States to adopt a foreign currency or cede monetary sovereignty without an amendment to this Constitution.

## Implementation Notes

This amendment requires implementing legislation including:

- Federal Reserve Reform Act (governance structure updates, FOMC composition)
- Monetary Policy Transparency Act (reporting requirements, digital ledger)
- Emergency Lending Procedures Act (terms, conditions, penalty rates)
- Central Bank Digital Currency Framework Act (if CBDC is authorized)

## Relationship to RF Core

If the Regional Federal Constitution is adopted:

- Article XV applies to the Federal Reserve as a national institution
- Monetary policy remains exclusively federal
- No Regional role in central banking

If adopted standalone:

- Article XV applies to the existing Federal Reserve System
- Supersedes inconsistent statutory provisions
- Establishes constitutional parameters for monetary policy

## Design Rationale

### Why Constitutional Level?

- Monetary policy affects every person and transaction
- Independence from short-term political pressure is essential
- Current statutory framework is vulnerable to interference
- Constitutional entrenchment provides needed stability

### Why Dual Mandate with Price Stability Primary?

- Inflation imposes regressive tax on savings and wages
- Employment goal is important but monetary policy has limits
- Clear hierarchy prevents conflicting objectives
- Numerical target provides accountability

### Why Limit Emergency Powers?

- 2008-2020 experience showed need for emergency authority
- But also showed risk of permanent expansion
- Time limits and congressional oversight prevent mission creep
- Penalty rates and collateral requirements protect taxpayers

### Why Protect Against CBDC Abuse?

- Digital currency could enable total surveillance
- Could enable direct government control of spending
- Congressional authorization with privacy requirements essential
- Preserves choice between digital and physical money
