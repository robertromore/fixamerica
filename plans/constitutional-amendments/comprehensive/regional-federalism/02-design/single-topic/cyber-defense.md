# Article XVI: Cyber Defense Amendment

## Status: Draft

This standalone constitutional amendment establishes a framework for national cyber defense, protecting critical infrastructure while preserving civil liberties in the digital domain. It works with either the existing federal/state system or with Regional Federalism.

### Section 1. Digital Rights

(a) The right of the people to be secure in their digital persons, papers, communications, and effects against unreasonable searches and seizures shall not be violated.

(b) No warrant shall issue for access to digital information but upon probable cause, supported by oath or affirmation, and particularly describing the accounts, devices, or data to be searched and the information to be seized.

(c) Every person shall have the right to:

(1) use encryption to secure their communications and data;

(2) maintain anonymity in online speech to the same extent as in offline speech;

(3) access and correct personal data held by government agencies;

(4) be informed when their data is accessed by government, except where a court finds notification would compromise an ongoing criminal investigation.

(d) No law shall require the creation of vulnerabilities in encryption systems or compel disclosure of encryption keys except pursuant to a warrant meeting the requirements of subsection (b).

### Section 2. Critical Infrastructure Protection

(a) Congress shall have power to provide for the protection of critical digital infrastructure from cyber attacks, including:

(1) systems supporting national defense and intelligence;

(2) financial systems and critical economic infrastructure;

(3) energy grids and utility control systems;

(4) telecommunications and internet backbone infrastructure;

(5) healthcare systems and public health data;

(6) election systems and voter registration databases;

(7) water, transportation, and other essential services.

(b) The federal government shall:

(1) establish and maintain minimum cybersecurity standards for critical infrastructure;

(2) provide threat intelligence and warnings to infrastructure operators;

(3) coordinate incident response across sectors;

(4) assist in recovery from significant cyber incidents.

(c) Private operators of critical infrastructure shall:

(1) implement reasonable security measures consistent with federal standards;

(2) report significant cyber incidents to the appropriate federal agency;

(3) cooperate with federal authorities in threat mitigation.

(d) Nothing in this section shall authorize the federal government to operate or control private critical infrastructure except during declared emergencies under Section 6.

### Section 3. Cyber Defense Authority

(a) The President, as Commander in Chief, shall have authority to defend the United States and its digital infrastructure against cyber attacks.

(b) Defensive cyber operations may include:

(1) monitoring federal networks for intrusions;

(2) blocking malicious traffic targeting federal systems;

(3) neutralizing imminent threats to critical infrastructure;

(4) coordinating defense of allied networks under treaty obligations.

(c) Offensive cyber operations that could cause significant effects outside United States networks shall require:

(1) authorization from the President;

(2) notification to the congressional intelligence committees within forty-eight hours;

(3) compliance with the laws of armed conflict if the operation could reasonably be expected to cause physical damage or casualties.

(d) Cyber operations that constitute acts of war require authorization from Congress under the War Powers provisions of this Constitution.

(e) No cyber operation shall target the domestic communications or data of United States persons except pursuant to lawful warrant or foreign intelligence authorization.

### Section 4. Cybersecurity Agency

(a) There shall be a Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA) as the lead federal agency for civilian cybersecurity.

(b) CISA shall:

(1) protect federal civilian networks;

(2) coordinate with private sector critical infrastructure operators;

(3) provide cybersecurity assistance to state and local governments;

(4) issue warnings about cyber threats and vulnerabilities;

(5) conduct research on emerging cyber threats.

(c) CISA shall be led by a Director appointed by the President and confirmed by the Senate for a term of five years. The Director may be removed only for cause.

(d) CISA shall have no law enforcement or intelligence collection authority. Its activities shall be limited to defensive cybersecurity.

(e) Military cyber operations shall be conducted by the Department of Defense and shall be coordinated with CISA for the protection of civilian infrastructure.

### Section 5. Election Security

(a) Congress shall provide for the security of federal elections against cyber threats.

(b) All voting systems used in federal elections shall:

(1) produce voter-verified paper records that are the official record for audit and recount purposes;

(2) not be connected to the internet during voting operations;

(3) be tested and certified by the Election Assistance Commission or its successor;

(4) be subject to post-election audits comparing electronic results to paper records.

(c) Voter registration databases shall:

(1) maintain backup systems that can restore accurate voter rolls within twenty-four hours of a cyber incident;

(2) be subject to regular security assessments;

(3) implement multi-factor authentication for administrative access.

(d) Federal authorities shall provide threat intelligence to state election officials and shall assist in securing election infrastructure upon request.

(e) Foreign interference in elections, including through cyber means, shall be prohibited and punishable as Congress provides by law.

### Section 6. Cyber Emergency Authority

(a) The President may declare a cyber emergency upon finding that:

(1) a significant cyber attack has occurred or is imminent;

(2) the attack threatens critical infrastructure, national security, or public safety;

(3) extraordinary measures are necessary to respond.

(b) During a cyber emergency, the President may:

(1) direct federal agencies to assist in incident response;

(2) authorize defensive measures to neutralize ongoing attacks;

(3) coordinate with private sector operators to protect critical infrastructure;

(4) request assistance from allied nations under treaty obligations.

(c) A cyber emergency shall expire after thirty days unless extended by Congress.

(d) Congress may terminate a cyber emergency at any time by joint resolution.

(e) During a cyber emergency, the government may temporarily operate or direct operations of critical infrastructure only if:

(1) the operator is unable to respond effectively;

(2) continued disruption poses imminent threat to life or critical services;

(3) control is returned to the operator within seventy-two hours of restoration.

### Section 7. Privacy and Surveillance Limitations

(a) Mass surveillance of domestic communications is prohibited. The government may not collect the contents of communications, or metadata sufficient to reveal the substance of communications, without individualized suspicion.

(b) Foreign intelligence surveillance of persons within the United States shall require a warrant from the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court except:

(1) for foreign powers or their agents who are not United States persons;

(2) in emergencies, where a warrant must be obtained within seventy-two hours.

(c) Any surveillance program shall:

(1) be authorized by statute;

(2) be subject to oversight by the courts and Congress;

(3) include minimization procedures to protect information about United States persons;

(4) be subject to regular review for compliance.

(d) No information obtained in violation of this section may be used in any legal proceeding, and any person whose rights are violated shall have a cause of action for damages.

### Section 8. Data Protection

(a) Every person shall have the right to control their personal data held by the government, including:

(1) the right to access their data;

(2) the right to correct inaccurate data;

(3) the right to know what data is collected and for what purpose;

(4) the right to be informed of data breaches affecting their information.

(b) Federal agencies shall:

(1) collect only personal data necessary for their statutory functions;

(2) retain data only as long as necessary;

(3) protect data with reasonable security measures;

(4) not share data between agencies except as authorized by law.

(c) Congress shall provide by law for the protection of personal data held by private entities, consistent with the principles of this section.

### Section 9. International Cyber Engagement

(a) The United States shall pursue international agreements establishing norms for responsible state behavior in cyberspace, including:

(1) prohibitions on attacks against critical civilian infrastructure;

(2) rules for attribution and response to cyber attacks;

(3) protections for the core infrastructure of the internet;

(4) frameworks for mutual legal assistance in cybercrime investigations.

(b) No treaty or agreement shall authorize foreign access to the communications or data of United States persons except pursuant to lawful process.

(c) The President shall report annually to Congress on international cyber threats and diplomatic efforts to establish cyber norms.

## Implementation Notes

This amendment requires implementing legislation including:

- Critical Infrastructure Cybersecurity Act (standards, reporting requirements)
- Cyber Emergency Response Act (procedures, authorities, limitations)
- Digital Privacy Act (data protection, breach notification)
- Election Security Act (voting system standards, audits)

---

## Relationship to RF Core

If the Regional Federal Constitution is adopted:

- Article XVI applies to federal cybersecurity as specified
- Regions may establish additional protections for Regional systems
- Election security applies to all federal elections regardless of Regional structure

If adopted standalone:

- Article XVI applies to federal government cybersecurity
- States retain authority over state systems with federal coordination
- Critical infrastructure standards apply nationwide

## Design Rationale

### Why Constitutional Level?

- Digital infrastructure is as critical as physical territory
- Civil liberties in digital realm need explicit protection
- Statutory protections have proven inadequate
- Technology changes faster than legislation; principles endure

### Why Separate Encryption Rights?

- Encryption is the foundation of digital security
- Government backdoors create vulnerabilities for everyone
- Forcing disclosure eliminates security benefits
- Explicit protection prevents erosion over time

### Why Paper Ballots Required?

- Software cannot be verified by voters
- Paper provides audit trail independent of technology
- Air-gapped systems prevent remote attacks
- Multiple layers of verification protect integrity

### Why Limit Mass Surveillance?

- Fourth Amendment principles apply to digital realm
- Mass collection chills free expression
- Targeted surveillance is more effective and proportionate
- Privacy is foundational to democratic participation
