## The Problem

### Gap 60: The "Para-Diplomacy" Conflict (Regional Foreign Engagement Vacuum)

Regions are designed to manage "cross-border realities" like climate, labor, and energy, but in a globalized economy, "Regional Coordination" often requires international cooperation. The Constitution grants the federal government exclusive foreign policy authority (Art II §1; Art VI §4), creating a "Multiple Voices" crisis when Regions engage with foreign entities on matters within Regional Exclusive Domains.

**The Core Tension:**

| Domain | Authority |
|--------|-----------|
| Foreign policy, treaties | Federal Exclusive (Art II §1) |
| Cross-border environmental coordination | Regional Exclusive (Art II §1(e)(2)) |
| Labor market management | Regional Primary |
| Infrastructure coordination | Regional Exclusive |

**The Collision Point:** When "Regional Exclusive Domains" require coordination with foreign governments, who speaks? Who negotiates? Who commits?

**Real-World Examples:**

- **Pacific Region**: Fisheries with Canada, tech standards with Japan/Korea, port operations with Asian partners
- **South Region**: Border management with Mexico, Gulf environmental coordination, Caribbean trade
- **Great Lakes Region**: Water management with Canada, cross-border infrastructure, binational pollution
- **Northeast/Atlantic**: Maritime coordination with Canada, North Atlantic fisheries, European trade

### Gaming Vectors

1. **The "Treaty Power" Backdoor** — President cannot achieve 60% House majority for domestic National Floor. Negotiates international treaty on same subject (carbon pricing, labor standards). Treaty overrides Regional Exclusive Domains under Supremacy Clause. The 60% House rule is bypassed entirely through foreign policy.

2. **The "Regulatory Arbitrage via Para-Diplomacy"** — Region negotiates favorable terms with foreign partners, creating de facto trade advantages not available to other Regions. "Coordination" becomes back-channel trade negotiation.

3. **The "Foreign Capture" Risk** — Foreign governments cultivate relationships with sympathetic Regions. Regions become vectors for foreign influence. Federal counterintelligence cannot cover all Regional contacts.

4. **The "Treaty Nullification via Divergence"** — Federal government signs treaty committing U.S. to standard X. Region adopts X+50% or X-50%. Treaty becomes unenforceable in that Region.

5. **The "Subnational Coordination Bypass"** — Regions coordinate with foreign provinces/states. Technically not "foreign policy" but achieves same effect. Federal government cannot object without appearing to restrict legitimate coordination.

---

## Why This Is Worse in This System

Under current U.S. federalism, states have limited foreign engagement capacity and the federal government has dominant preemptive authority. Under Regional Federalism:

1. **Regions have constitutionally protected Exclusive Domains** — The 60% House supermajority requirement to legislate National Floors in Regional Exclusive Domains creates enormous incentive to use the Treaty Power as a backdoor. There is no constitutional mechanism to prevent this.

2. **Regions are large enough to conduct meaningful para-diplomacy** — A Pacific Region with ~65M people and a GDP larger than most nations can negotiate as a significant partner. States cannot; Regions can.

3. **The One Voice Doctrine (Art VI §4) is prohibition-only** — It says what Regions *cannot* do but does not affirmatively authorize what they *can* do. Without a structured engagement framework, legitimate Regional coordination (Great Lakes water management with Canada, Pacific fisheries) exists in a legal gray zone.

4. **No treaty entry safeguard** — Art II §19 regulates treaty *withdrawal* but nothing regulates treaty *entry* vis-a-vis Regional Domains. The President can sign a treaty that preempts Regional Exclusive Domains with only traditional Senate supermajority approval — bypassing the House entirely.

## Existing Constitutional Coverage (Overlap Analysis)

Two major provisions already address portions of this problem:

### Article VI, Section 4 — The One Voice Doctrine (from Gap 266)

```text
(a) Exclusive Federal Authority. The conduct of foreign relations is the
    exclusive domain of the Federal Government. No Region, State, local
    government, or instrumentality thereof may:
    - (i) enter into any treaty, alliance, or confederation with a foreign power;
    - (ii) enter into any agreement, compact, MOU, framework, or arrangement
      with a foreign government... that contradicts Federal foreign policy,
      undermines Federal diplomatic objectives, interferes with sanctions,
      creates commitments within Federal foreign affairs authority, or provides
      foreign powers access that could compromise national security.

(b) Void Ab Initio. Any agreement in violation of (a) is void from the
    beginning.

(c) Permitted Activities. Does not prohibit: purely commercial transactions,
    academic exchanges, cultural activities, tourism promotion, technical
    cooperation on non-sensitive matters with federal notification.

(d) Federal Notification and Review. Before any Regional entity enters into
    any arrangement with a foreign government: notify State Department; 30-day
    review; objection goes to Constitutional Court; failure to notify =
    presumptively void.

(e) Designated Prohibited Partners. Sanctioned countries, foreign intelligence
    services, foreign SOEs in sensitive sectors, political parties of adversary
    nations.

(e-1) Regional Official Financial Interest Prohibition (from Gap 172).
      No financial interests in Prohibited Partner entities; no SOE compensation;
      5-year post-service bar.

(f) Enforcement. AG injunctive relief; personal liability; federal court
    jurisdiction to void.

(g) Existing Arrangements. 1-year review; conflicting arrangements terminated;
    failure to submit = void.
```

(f) Federal Exclusivity over Foreign Relations includes regulatory alignments,

(g) Prohibited Regional Actions (without federal approval):

### Genuinely Additive Elements (the other ~55%)

The following proposal elements have NO meaningful existing coverage and represent the core value of this resolution:

1. **Parallel Threshold Requirement (Part 2, §§e-i)** — Prevents the Treaty Power from becoming a backdoor to bypass the 60% House supermajority for Regional Domain legislation. This is the most critical additive element.

2. **Facilitative vs. Prohibitive Coordinator (Part 1, §d)** — Transforms State Department role from gatekeeper to facilitator. Currently, Art I §5(j) makes 90-day failure-to-respond = denial; the proposal inverts this to a facilitative model with explicit anti-gatekeeping safeguards.

3. **Procedural/Material Split for Conflict Resolution (Part 3, §§j-n)** — Creates explicit ARB (procedural: does it violate a specific treaty?) vs. NCC (material: does it impair foreign relations?) division. Currently, Art VI §4(d)(iii) just says "Constitutional Court" without specifying standard of review.

4. **Classified National Security Exception (Part 4, §§o-r)** — Emergency brake for genuine security threats with judicial review, personal certification, and anti-abuse safeguards. Current Art I §5(l) "Geopolitical Fragmentation Finding" is a different mechanism (presidential finding + congressional action, no classified proceedings).

5. **IFC In-Kind Valuation (Part 5, §t)** — Prevents "free AI system valued at $1" manipulation of foreign contribution thresholds. IFC currently has fiscal capacity measurement authority but not foreign contribution appraisal.

6. **National Common Market Bridge (Part 6, §aa)** — Ensures that foreign standards harmonization doesn't create internal trade barriers. The current Art I §5(h) functional test catches some cases but lacks the affirmative equivalency pathway mechanism.

## The Proposed Resolution

**Full draft constitutional text from the gap entry. All 6 parts, 29 subsections (a)-(bb).**

### Part 1: Regional Foreign Engagement Authorization (§§a-d)

> (a) **Authorized Regional Foreign Engagement.** Regions may engage directly with foreign governments and subnational entities for purposes of:
>
> - (i) Coordination on matters within Regional Exclusive Domains that have transboundary effects;
> - (ii) Implementation of existing federal treaties within the Region;
> - (iii) Information sharing and technical cooperation;
> - (iv) Emergency response coordination with adjacent foreign jurisdictions.
>
> (b) **Prohibited Regional Foreign Engagement.** Regions may not:
>
> - (i) Negotiate or enter agreements that conflict with federal treaties or executive agreements;
> - (ii) Make commitments binding the United States or other Regions;
> - (iii) Engage in activities that would constitute recognition of foreign governments or territorial claims;
> - (iv) Receive foreign government funding or in-kind support without federal disclosure and approval;
> - (v) Establish permanent diplomatic missions or representations abroad.
>
> (c) **Notification Requirement.** Regions shall notify the Department of State within thirty (30) days of:
>
> - (i) Initiating substantive discussions with any foreign government or subnational entity;
> - (ii) Reaching any understanding, memorandum, or coordination agreement;
> - (iii) Receiving any proposal from a foreign entity for cooperation or coordination.
>
> (d) **State Department Liaison (Facilitative Role).**
>
> - (i) The Department of State shall designate a Regional Foreign Engagement Coordinator for each Region;
> - (ii) The Coordinator's role shall be **facilitative**, not prohibitive:
>     - (A) Provide briefings on relevant federal foreign policy priorities;
>     - (B) Facilitate Regional participation in appropriate international forums;
>     - (C) Transmit Regional notifications to appropriate State Department offices;
>     - (D) Offer non-binding guidance on potential conflicts with federal policy.
> - (iii) **Limitations on Coordinator Authority.** The Coordinator may not:
>     - (A) Require prior approval for Regional foreign contacts;
>     - (B) Directly communicate with foreign officials to discourage engagement with a Region;
>     - (C) Withhold information from Regions based on policy disagreements;
>     - (D) Delay transmission of Regional notifications or requests.
> - (iv) **Exclusive Enforcement Authority.** Only the ARB or the National Constitutional Court may issue binding determinations or orders regarding Regional foreign engagement.
> - (v) **Coordinator Misconduct.** If the ARB finds that a Coordinator has exceeded their facilitative role, the finding shall be reported to Congress and the Secretary of State shall reassign the Coordinator within thirty (30) days.

### Part 2: Treaty Consultation with Parallel Threshold Requirement (§§e-i)

> (e) **Mandatory Regional Consultation.** Before finalizing any treaty or executive agreement that would affect Regional Exclusive Domains, impose implementation obligations on Regions, or create standards interacting with Regional regulatory authority, the President shall provide affected Regions with:
>
> - (A) Draft text at least ninety (90) days before signature;
> - (B) Opportunity for written comment;
> - (C) At least one consultation meeting with Regional representatives.
>
> (f) **Regional Impact Statement.** Treaties submitted to the Senate shall include a Regional Impact Statement identifying domains affected, summarizing consultation comments, and estimating implementation costs.
>
> (g) **Parallel Threshold Requirement.**
>
> - (i) **Pre-Signature Certification.** Before signing any treaty containing provisions that the ARB has certified as affecting Regional Exclusive Domains:
>     - (A) The President shall submit the treaty text to the House of Representatives;
>     - (B) The House shall vote on Provisional Approval of the Regional Domain provisions within sixty (60) days;
>     - (C) If the House approves by three-fifths (60%) majority, the President may proceed to signature;
>     - (D) If the House fails to approve, the President shall not sign unless the Regional Domain provisions are removed or modified.
> - (ii) **ARB Certification.** The ARB shall certify within thirty (30) days whether a treaty triggers the Parallel Threshold Requirement.
> - (iii) **Final Ratification.** After signature, Senate ratification proceeds under existing procedures; House Provisional Approval converts to Final Approval upon Senate ratification.
>
> (h) **Emergency Exception.** In cases of urgent national security:
>
> - (i) The President may sign without Pre-Signature Certification if the President certifies grave harm from delay;
> - (ii) The House shall vote within thirty (30) days of signature;
> - (iii) If House fails to approve, treaty provisions affecting Regional Domains have no domestic legal effect;
> - (iv) Emergency Exception may be invoked only once per Congress for any given subject matter.
>
> (i) **Anti-Circumvention.** The following constitute evidence of circumvention:
>
> - (i) Negotiating provisions similar to domestic legislation that failed 60% House majority within preceding five (5) years;
> - (ii) Characterizing domestic policy goals as "international commitments" without genuine foreign policy rationale;
> - (iii) Using executive agreements to avoid the Parallel Threshold Requirement (such provisions are void).

### Part 3: Conflict Resolution with Procedural/Material Split (§§j-n)

> (j) **Federal Preemption of Conflicting Regional Agreements.**
>
> - (i) **Secretary of State Determination.** If the Department of State determines a Regional foreign engagement conflicts with federal foreign policy, the Secretary shall provide written notice specifying the conflict;
> - (ii) **Regional Response.** The Region shall have sixty (60) days to modify, terminate, or dispute the finding by filing with the ARB.
>
> (k) **ARB Procedural Verification.** If the Region disputes the finding, the ARB shall determine within sixty (60) days:
>
> - (i) Whether the Regional engagement directly contradicts a specific, identified federal treaty obligation;
> - (ii) Whether the Regional engagement creates binding commitments purporting to bind the United States;
> - (iii) Whether the Secretary's notice complies with procedural requirements.
>
> The ARB shall NOT determine whether the engagement "materially impairs" foreign relations or the national interest.
>
> (l) **National Constitutional Court Determination.** If the ARB finds a procedural conflict exists, OR if the Secretary claims "material impairment" beyond specific treaty violations:
>
> - (i) The matter shall proceed to the National Constitutional Court;
> - (ii) The Court shall determine whether the Regional engagement materially impairs the federal government's ability to conduct foreign relations;
> - (iii) The Court may receive classified briefings and expert testimony;
> - (iv) The Court's determination shall be final.
>
> (m) **Standard of Review.**
>
> - (i) **ARB Standard (Procedural):** Find "procedural conflict" only if engagement directly contradicts specific treaty obligation OR creates binding U.S. commitments;
> - (ii) **Court Standard (Material Impairment):** Find "material impairment" only if engagement would substantially undermine an active federal diplomatic initiative, impairment is concrete and demonstrable (not speculative), and federal government has no reasonable alternative means.
>
> (n) **Protected Activities.** A Regional engagement does not constitute conflict or impairment merely because it addresses the same subject matter as federal foreign policy, is stricter than federal treaty obligations, involves communication with foreign entities on Regional matters, or expresses policy preferences different from the federal executive.

### Part 4: National Security Exception with Classified Negative-Key (§§o-r)

> (o) **Classified Negative-Key.** Notwithstanding Coordinator limitations, if the Secretary of State certifies in writing that a specific Regional foreign engagement poses a "Specific and Identifiable National Security Threat":
>
> - (i) The Secretary shall provide a classified briefing to the ARB within forty-eight (48) hours;
> - (ii) The ARB may issue an immediate temporary stay on the engagement for up to thirty (30) days;
> - (iii) During the stay, the Region shall suspend the engagement pending resolution.
>
> (p) **"Specific and Identifiable" Standard.** Requires:
>
> - (i) Intelligence indicating the foreign entity is controlled by or acting on behalf of a hostile foreign government or intelligence service;
> - (ii) Identified risk of transfer of sensitive technology, data, or infrastructure access;
> - (iii) The threat must be specific to this engagement, not a general characterization of the foreign nation.
>
> (q) **Closed-Door Judicial Review.** Within thirty (30) days of a temporary stay:
>
> - (i) The National Constitutional Court shall conduct a closed-door hearing with appropriate security clearances;
> - (ii) The Court may extend the stay for up to one hundred eighty (180) days if it finds the threat credible;
> - (iii) The Court may permanently enjoin the engagement if it finds the threat substantial and ongoing;
> - (iv) If the Court finds the certification unsupported, the stay is lifted.
>
> (r) **Anti-Abuse Safeguards.**
>
> - (i) Secretary of State must personally certify each invocation (no delegation);
> - (ii) All invocations reported to Intelligence Committees (classified);
> - (iii) If Court finds two certifications within three years unsupported, subsequent certifications require prior Court approval;
> - (iv) False or pretextual certification is an impeachable offense.

### Part 5: Foreign Influence Safeguards with IFC Appraisal (§§s-x)

> (s) **Foreign Government Funding Prohibition.** No Region shall accept from any foreign government or foreign-controlled entity:
>
> - (i) Direct funding for Regional government operations;
> - (ii) In-kind contributions exceeding one hundred thousand dollars ($100,000) in appraised market value per year;
> - (iii) Funding for infrastructure projects without federal review and approval;
> - (iv) Loans or credit facilities with terms more favorable than market rates.
>
> (t) **IFC In-Kind Valuation.** All in-kind contributions from foreign entities shall be appraised by the Independent Fiscal Council using:
>
> - (i) Market-rate benchmarks for comparable goods or services;
> - (ii) Replacement cost methodology where market comparables are unavailable;
> - (iii) For data, software, or intellectual property: licensing fees for comparable commercial products.
> - (iv) **Presumption of High Value:** If IFC cannot determine market value, the contribution is presumed to exceed $100,000 and requires federal approval.
> - (v) **Anti-Structuring:** Dividing contributions to avoid threshold shall be aggregated.
>
> (u) **Disclosure Requirements.** Annual reports to State Department and ARB disclosing all foreign government contacts, agreements, funding, and pending proposals.
>
> (v) **Counterintelligence Cooperation.** Regions shall cooperate with federal counterintelligence regarding foreign government contacts and report suspected foreign intelligence activities.
>
> (w) **Foreign Capture Finding.** If the ARB determines a Region has engaged in a pattern of accepting undisclosed foreign funding, coordinating against federal policy with foreign governments, refusing counterintelligence cooperation, or Systemic Symbolic Contradiction during a declared foreign crisis:
>
> - (i) The ARB may suspend the Region's direct foreign engagement authority for up to five (5) years;
> - (ii) All foreign contacts must be conducted through federal channels.
>
> (x) **Systemic Symbolic Contradiction.** Means a pattern of Regional acts that materially impair the federal government's unified diplomatic position during a declared foreign crisis (Presidential declaration confirmed by Congressional majority within 30 days). Requires three or more of: official resolutions, gubernatorial statements, flag-flying, "technical cooperation" with conflict parties, hosting foreign faction representatives. ARB may issue "Cease and Desist" for non-essential foreign engagement during the crisis.

### Part 6: Subnational Coordination with National Common Market Bridge (§§y-bb)

> (y) **Subnational Coordination Authorized.** Regions may coordinate directly with foreign subnational entities (provinces, states, prefectures, municipalities) without federal approval for:
>
> - (i) Technical standards harmonization within Regional Exclusive Domains, subject to subsection (aa);
> - (ii) Emergency response and disaster coordination;
> - (iii) Environmental monitoring and data sharing;
> - (iv) Cross-border infrastructure planning;
> - (v) Educational and cultural exchanges.
>
> (z) **Subnational Coordination Limits.** Subnational coordination shall not:
>
> - (i) Purport to bind the foreign national government;
> - (ii) Address matters reserved to the foreign national government;
> - (iii) Create trade preferences or tariff arrangements;
> - (iv) Include provisions that would violate federal law if applied domestically.
>
> (aa) **National Common Market Bridge.**
>
> - (i) **Compatibility Requirement.** Any technical standards harmonization agreement with a foreign entity must:
>     - (A) Be compatible with applicable Federal Floors;
>     - (B) Not create barriers to goods, services, or credentials from other Regions that meet Federal Floors;
>     - (C) Include a "National Common Market Bridge" demonstrating interoperability with national standards.
> - (ii) **ARB Review.** Before a technical standards agreement takes effect, the Region shall submit to the ARB. The ARB shall determine within sixty (60) days whether it creates internal trade barriers. If so, the Region must modify or provide an equivalency pathway.
> - (iii) **Equivalency Pathway.** Shall permit goods/services/credentials meeting Federal Floors to access the Regional market via a reasonable process not exceeding ninety (90) days and five thousand dollars ($5,000) in fees. The process must be ministerial, not substantive.
>
> (bb) **Model Subnational Coordination Agreement.** The Department of State shall publish Model Agreements for common coordination needs. Regions using Model Agreements are presumed compliant.

## Gaming Vectors to Verify Against

The proposed resolution itself could be exploited in these ways:

1. **Coordinator Capture / Sabotage** — The facilitative Coordinator is appointed by the Secretary of State. A hostile administration could appoint a Coordinator who technically complies (doesn't "require prior approval") but actively undermines Regional engagement through leaks to foreign governments, briefings designed to discourage engagement, or strategic delays in transmitting notifications. The §d(iii) limitations may be insufficiently enforceable.

2. **ARB Certification Gaming (Parallel Threshold)** — The ARB must certify within 30 days whether a treaty triggers the Parallel Threshold. A compliant ARB could narrowly interpret "affecting Regional Exclusive Domains" to avoid triggering the House vote, or a hostile ARB could trigger it for every treaty to paralyze foreign policy.

3. **"Material Impairment" Creep** — The NCC standard for "material impairment" (§m(ii)) requires the impairment be "concrete and demonstrable" and the federal government have "no reasonable alternative means." Over time, the Court could either expand this to block most Regional engagement or narrow it to meaninglessness.

4. **Foreign Capture Weaponization** — The ARB can suspend a Region's foreign engagement authority for up to 5 years via "Foreign Capture Finding." A politically aligned ARB could use this as a weapon against opposition Regions, especially the "Systemic Symbolic Contradiction" provision during declared foreign crises. The "Congressional majority within 30 days" confirmation of crisis declaration may be insufficient safeguard.

5. **5-Year Lookback Manipulation (Anti-Circumvention)** — The anti-circumvention provision (§i(i)) looks at whether similar domestic legislation "failed 60% House majority within preceding five (5) years." A President could delay introducing a treaty until the 5-year window expires from the failed domestic legislation, then proceed.

6. **Model Agreement Monopoly** — The State Department publishes Model Agreements (§bb) and Regions using them are "presumed compliant." The State Department could make Model Agreements so restrictive that they functionally limit Regional engagement, while non-Model agreements face heightened scrutiny.

7. **Classified Negative-Key Chilling Effect** — Even with anti-abuse safeguards, the mere existence of the 48-hour classified briefing + 30-day stay mechanism could chill legitimate Regional engagement. Foreign partners may be reluctant to coordinate if the engagement could be frozen at any time.

8. **National Common Market Bridge as Barrier** — The equivalency pathway (§aa(iii)) is capped at 90 days and $5,000 but is "ministerial, not substantive." A Region could design standards that technically offer a ministerial pathway but make compliance impractical in practice.

9. **Intelligence Committees Reference** — The proposal references "Intelligence Committees" (§r(ii)) for reporting, but these are statutory committees not constitutional bodies. If Congress restructures or eliminates them, the constitutional requirement becomes unenforceable.

10. **ARB "Foreign Capture" vs. Procedural-Only Design** — The proposal's own design rationale states "ARB limited to procedural questions" (Part 3 rationale) but Part 5 §(w) asks the ARB to make "Foreign Capture" determinations — which are political judgments about whether a Region is "coordinating against federal policy with foreign governments." This violates the stated procedural/material split.

## Placement Verification

### Original Proposal Target

**Article I, Section 9(a-cc)**

### Why It's Wrong

1. **Article I, Section 9 is occupied** — It is currently "Mutual Recognition" (full faith and credit, professional credentials, family status portability, etc.). The section has extensive subsections through (dd) covering domestic inter-Regional recognition.

2. **Article I is "Regional Structure"** — It governs domestic inter-Regional relations (boundaries, cooperation, transit, water rights, data sovereignty, law enforcement, compacts). Foreign affairs engagement is not a domestic inter-Regional structure question.

3. **Article VI already exists for foreign affairs** — Article VI is "Foreign Affairs and Regional Limitations." The One Voice Doctrine (§4, from Gap 266) is already there. Gap 60's structured para-diplomacy framework is the natural companion section.

### Corrected Placement

**Article VI, Section 5** in `03-regional-governance.md`

**Rationale:**

- Article VI is "Foreign Affairs and Regional Limitations" — exact topical match
- Section 4 (One Voice Doctrine) establishes the general rule (federal exclusivity)
- Section 5 would establish the structured exceptions (authorized engagement, safeguards, treaty consultation)
- Creates clear authority hierarchy: §4 = prohibition, §5 = permitted engagement within bounds
- Follows the pattern of other resolved gaps placing related provisions in adjacent sections

### Note on Article I §5(f-l) Interaction

Article I §5(f-l) (Foreign Regulatory Integration prohibition) has significant overlap with Gap 60's prohibition elements. The new Art VI §5 should cross-reference Art I §5(f-l) rather than duplicate its prohibitions. Art I §5(f-l) covers the *regulatory* dimension (standards alignment, infrastructure interconnections); Art VI §5 covers the *diplomatic* dimension (engagement authorization, treaty safeguards, conflict resolution).

## Design Questions

### D1: Placement Strategy

- **Option A**: Article VI, Section 5 as standalone — new section in the foreign affairs article. Art VI §4 = prohibition (One Voice), §5 = structured engagement framework.
- **Option B**: Expand Article VI, Section 4 — fold Gap 60 into §4 as subsections (h)-(kk), making §4 a comprehensive foreign affairs section.
- **Option C**: Split placement — Parts 1, 3, 4, 5 in Article VI §5 (foreign engagement); Part 2 (treaty consultation) in Article II alongside §19 (treaty withdrawal); Part 6 (subnational coordination) in Article I alongside §5 (inter-Regional cooperation).

**Coordinator recommendation**: Option A. Standalone section preserves readability. Art II §19 governs treaty *exit*; Art VI §5 governs treaty *entry* vis-a-vis Regions — different enough to warrant separate articles. Split placement creates maintenance risk.

### D2: Intelligence Committees Reference

The proposal references "Intelligence Committees" in §r(ii) for classified reporting, but these are statutory committees, not constitutional bodies.

- **Option A**: Replace with "the committees of Congress having primary jurisdiction over intelligence matters" — generic constitutional language that survives committee restructuring.
- **Option B**: Create a constitutional Intelligence Oversight Board.
- **Option C**: Defer to implementing legislation — "reported to Congress in accordance with applicable law."

**Coordinator recommendation**: Option A. Generic language is the established constitutional pattern (cf. "committees of jurisdiction"). Creating a new constitutional body for this single reporting function is disproportionate.

### D3: ARB "Foreign Capture" Scope

The proposal's design rationale says "ARB limited to procedural questions" but §(w) gives the ARB authority to make "Foreign Capture" determinations — a political judgment.

- **Option A**: Keep as-is — the ARB has broad investigative authority under Art II §7 and "Foreign Capture" determinations are factual (pattern of behavior), not policy judgments.
- **Option B**: Move Foreign Capture Finding to NCC — maintains procedural/material split. ARB investigates and presents evidence; NCC makes the finding.
- **Option C**: Split — ARB makes factual findings (undisclosed funding, counterintelligence refusal); NCC makes the political judgment (coordinating against federal policy, Systemic Symbolic Contradiction).

**Coordinator recommendation**: Option C. The first two criteria (undisclosed funding, counterintelligence refusal) are factual and fit ARB's procedural role. The latter two (coordinating against federal policy, Systemic Symbolic Contradiction) are inherently political and should require judicial determination.

### D4: NCC Classified Proceedings

The proposal asks the NCC (Supreme Court exercising constitutional review jurisdiction) to conduct "closed-door hearings with appropriate security clearances" and receive "classified briefings."

- **Option A**: Keep as-is — SCOTUS already handles national security cases with in camera review of classified materials (e.g., *United States v. Reynolds*).
- **Option B**: Create a specialized panel — designate a 3-judge panel from the NCC with permanent security clearances for foreign engagement disputes, rather than requiring the full Court to maintain clearances.
- **Option C**: Delegate to FISA-like mechanism — create a separate constitutional security review court. This would require additional constitutional infrastructure.

**Coordinator recommendation**: Option A with minor language adjustments. SCOTUS already reviews classified materials in camera. The phrase "closed-door hearing" should be changed to "in camera review" to match existing judicial practice. No new institutional infrastructure needed.

### D5: Overlap with Art I §5(f-l) and Art VI §4

The proposal duplicates prohibitions already in Art I §5(f-l) (Foreign Regulatory Integration) and Art VI §4 (One Voice Doctrine).

- **Option A**: Cross-reference only — Art VI §5 cross-references Art I §5(f-l) and Art VI §4 for prohibitions, adds only the genuinely additive elements (engagement authorization, treaty consultation, conflict resolution, national security exception, IFC valuation, Common Market Bridge).
- **Option B**: Replace and consolidate — move Art I §5(f-l) prohibitions into Art VI §5, replacing Art I §5(f-l) with a forward reference. Creates one comprehensive foreign engagement section.
- **Option C**: Leave both, add deconfliction clause — keep existing prohibitions, add new provisions, include explicit deconfliction clause stating that Art VI §5 controls where it conflicts with Art I §5(f-l).

**Coordinator recommendation**: Option A. Cross-referencing avoids duplication, respects existing architecture, and reduces risk of unintended changes. Art I §5(f-l) handles the regulatory alignment dimension; Art VI §5 handles the diplomatic engagement dimension.

### D6: Facilitative vs. Prohibitive Coordinator

The proposal creates a "facilitative" Coordinator with extensive anti-gatekeeping safeguards (§d). But Art I §5(j) currently allows the State Department to deny Regional foreign engagement (90-day failure = denial).

- **Option A**: Keep the Art I §5(j) prohibitive model but add the facilitative Coordinator as a separate channel. Regions can seek approval via Art I §5(j) OR notify via Art VI §5(c)/(d), depending on the nature of the engagement.
- **Option B**: Amend Art I §5(j) to make it facilitative — change "failure to respond constitutes denial" to "failure to respond constitutes approval." Art VI §5 provides the facilitative framework for all foreign engagement.
- **Option C**: Create a two-track system — Art I §5(j) remains prohibitive for regulatory integration (standards alignment, infrastructure interconnections); Art VI §5(d) is facilitative for diplomatic coordination (within Regional Exclusive Domains).

**Coordinator recommendation**: Option C. Regulatory integration (aligning Region's standards with a foreign government's) warrants a prohibitive approach — the risk of fragmenting the domestic market is high. Diplomatic coordination on Regional Exclusive Domain matters (fisheries, environment, infrastructure planning) warrants a facilitative approach — the risk of blocking legitimate engagement is high. Two tracks match two risk profiles.

### D7: IFC Foreign Contribution Valuation Authority

The IFC currently measures "fiscal capacity" and "non-human economic output" (Art X §5) but does not have explicit constitutional authority to appraise foreign government contributions.

- **Option A**: Add explicit IFC valuation authority in Art VI §5(t) — self-contained. The IFC already has valuation expertise; this extends it to a new domain.
- **Option B**: Amend Art X §5 to add foreign contribution valuation to IFC's enumerated duties — more architecturally correct since IFC duties are defined in Art X.
- **Option C**: Delegate to implementing legislation — "appraised at market value by an independent federal body designated by Congress."

**Coordinator recommendation**: Option A. Self-contained authority in Art VI §5 is cleaner than amending Art X §5 for a narrow extension. The IFC is referenced by name, and its valuation methodology is specified in the provision itself.

### D8: Scope of Part 2 (Treaty Consultation)

The Parallel Threshold requirement is the most significant new mechanism. How broad should it be?

- **Option A**: As proposed — applies to all treaties "affecting Regional Exclusive Domains." This is the broadest interpretation and maximizes protection of Regional authority.
- **Option B**: Narrower — applies only to treaties that would require the 60% House supermajority if enacted as domestic legislation. This precisely closes the "Treaty Power backdoor" without extending the House role to all treaties.
- **Option C**: Narrowest — applies only to self-executing treaty provisions that directly create domestic law in Regional Exclusive Domains. Non-self-executing treaties (requiring implementing legislation) would go through normal legislative process, which already requires 60% House if affecting Regional Domains.

**Coordinator recommendation**: Option B. The backdoor problem is specifically about circumventing the 60% House requirement. Option A is overbroad (a trade treaty touching Regional commerce doesn't need House pre-approval). Option C is too narrow (a treaty could be formally non-self-executing but practically preemptive). Option B precisely targets the gap.
