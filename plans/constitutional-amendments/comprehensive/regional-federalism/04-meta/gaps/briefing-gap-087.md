# Briefing: Gap 87 — "Fiscal Starvation" of the ARB and Independent Councils

## Context

You are reviewing a proposed resolution for the Regional Federal Constitution — a constitutional design that reorganizes the US into ~6-8 Regions between federal and state levels.

- Constitutional design files: `02-design/constitution/` (numbered article files)
- Gap tracking files: `04-meta/gaps/` (organized by theme)
- This gap is **Medium** severity, status **Proposal Available**
- Target file: `02-design/constitution/07-implementation.md` (Article XXI)

**Important:** This gap has **~75-80% overlap** with existing provisions. The original proposal was written before several major resolved gaps added extensive institutional protections. Your primary task is to evaluate whether the **genuinely additive elements** are correctly scoped and whether they create conflicts with existing provisions.

---

## The Problem

**Gap 87 (Fiscal Starvation of the ARB and Independent Councils):** The constitution creates several independent oversight bodies — most critically the Allocation Review Board (ARB), which arbitrates power allocation disputes between federal, Regional, and State governments. If Congress retaliates against unfavorable rulings by defunding oversight bodies, the constitutional system's enforcement mechanism collapses.

### Gaming Vectors (from gap entry)

1. **Ruling Retaliation:** ARB rules against federal overreach → House zeroes ARB budget → no arbiter for future disputes.
2. **Slow Starvation:** Congress reduces funding 20% annually → backlogs grow → effective nullification by delay.
3. **Rider Requirement:** Appropriation rider says "No funds for cases involving X" → Congress controls docket through budget.
4. **Selective Protection:** IFC budget is constitutionally protected, but ARB and other bodies are not → most important arbiter is most vulnerable.

### Why This Is Worse in This System

In the current US system, congressional defunding of oversight bodies is a political problem. In the Regional Federal system, it is a **structural crisis** because:

- The ARB is the primary arbiter of federal-Regional-State power disputes — without it, domain allocation freezes
- The IFC certifies fiscal capacity for equalization transfers — if sister bodies cannot function, IFC operates in isolation
- The Boundary Review Commission conducts mandatory 20-year reviews — defunding prevents constitutional boundary revision
- The National Election Court certifies Senator selections (Gap 71, Art IV §3(i)(3)) — defunding blocks the Senate continuity framework
- With only ~6-8 Regions, a single dysfunctional oversight body affects every governmental relationship

## Existing Coverage (~75-80% overlap)

**This is the most critical section.** The following provisions ALREADY exist and must NOT be duplicated:

### ARB Budget Protection (Art II §7-A(cc)-(ee))

- §7-A(cc): **Automatic Funding Formula** — 0.002% of federal revenue for ARB; automatically appropriated without annual congressional action; inflation-adjusted
- §7-A(dd): **Prohibition on Budgetary Manipulation** — Congress may not: reduce below formula, impose conditions, transfer funds, delay disbursement beyond 30 days
- §7-A(ee): **Enforcement** — Any ARB member may petition Supreme Court for mandamus to compel disbursement

### IFC Budget Protection (Art X §5(e))

- 0.01% of equalization transfers or $50 million (indexed), whichever is greater
- Full budgetary independence with automatic appropriations

### Boundary Review Commission Budget Protection (Art I §4-A(i)-(m))

- §4-A(i): **Dedicated Operational Budget** — 0.0002% of federal revenue (non-review years); 0.001% during review window (5 years around census)
- §4-A(j): **Automatic Appropriation** — without annual congressional action, disbursed within 30 days of fiscal year start
- §4-A(k): Congress may not reduce/condition/transfer/delay
- §4-A(l): Carryover of unspent funds
- §4-A(m): **Budget Mandamus** — any Commissioner may petition Supreme Court
- §4-A(v): **Savings Clause** — if Constitutional Oversight Fund established, Commission funding reduced by that amount but total may not fall below formula

### Implementation Institution Protections (Art XXI §2-A(g)-(h))

- §2-A(g)(1): Appropriations may not be conditioned on unrelated policy matters
- §2-A(g)(2): May not be reduced below IFC-certified adequate level
- §2-A(g)(3): If inadequately funded for 1 year → mandatory spending trigger at inflation-adjusted prior level; IFC quarterly certification can trigger immediately
- §2-A(g)(4): New mandated functions require commensurate appropriations
- §2-A(h)(1): Appointment diversity (no single appointing authority may select majority)
- §2-A(h)(2): Removal only for cause by 2/3 Senate
- §2-A(h)(3): Compensation protection during term
- §2-A(h)(4): **Budgetary floor** — mandatory appropriations at greater of: (A) institution-certified/GAO-confirmed adequate level, or (B) prior year + CPI
- §2-A(h)(5): Operational autonomy (own rules, staff, budget allocation)
- §2-A(h)(6): Anti-retaliation
- §2-A(h)(7): Savings clause (more protective provision controls)

### Material Weakening Protection (Art XXI §2-A(e)(2))

- "Eliminating, defunding, or reducing the operational independence of any institution established by the implementation act" constitutes material weakening
- Material weakening requires 3/5 supermajority; otherwise void ab initio

### Holdover Authority (Art IV §4-D)

- Members of Constitutional Oversight Bodies (ARB, IFC explicitly named) continue serving up to 1 year beyond term expiration until successor confirmed
- Full authority during holdover
- Chief Justice temporary appointment backstop if holdover expires

### Poison Pill Severability (Art XXI §2-A(c))

- Provisions in implementation acts addressing unrelated matters, requiring supermajority, or designed to make act unacceptable are severable
- ARB may sever without invalidating remainder

## What Is NOT Covered (Genuinely Additive ~20-25%)

### 1. NEC Budget Gap

The National Election Court (Art VII §7(b)) has **no specific constitutional budget floor**. The ARB has Art II §7-A(cc)-(ee). The IFC has Art X §5(e). The Commission has Art I §4-A(i)-(m). But the NEC — which now has critical jurisdiction over Senator selection certification (Gap 71, Art IV §3(i)(3)) — has no equivalent protection.

The NEC **may** inherit Art XXI §2-A protections if created by the Elections Implementation Act. But if that Act is never passed (or the NEC is established constitutionally rather than statutorily), the NEC falls through the cracks.

### 2. Case-Specific Rider Prohibition

Art XXI §2-A(g)(1) prohibits conditioning funding on "unrelated policy matters." But a rider saying "No ARB funds shall be used to process cases challenging federal authority under Domain Y" could be argued as **related** to the ARB's subject matter. No existing provision specifically prohibits:

- Conditioning funding on case outcomes
- Prohibiting use of funds for specific categories of cases
- Requiring dismissal of pending matters as a condition of funding
- Using reporting requirements to compromise independence (e.g., requiring pre-decisional disclosure of case analysis)

This is Gaming Vector #3 (Rider Requirement) — the most sophisticated attack and the one least addressed by existing protections.

### 3. Interference Reporting Mandate

No existing provision requires oversight bodies to publicly report interference attempts. Without affirmative reporting, soft interference (informal pressure, staffing threats, facility manipulation) remains invisible.

### 4. GAO Audit with Case-Decision Exclusion

No existing provision explicitly defines the boundary between legitimate fiscal audit of oversight bodies and illegitimate scrutiny of their substantive decisions. A GAO audit mandate with explicit case-decision exclusion would clarify this boundary.

### 5. Operational Independence Specifics

Art XXI §2-A(h)(5) provides "operational autonomy" but does not specifically address:

- Prohibition on required co-location with executive branch agencies
- Prohibition on required use of executive branch administrative services
- These are minor but close a specific capture vector (physical/administrative dependence)

## Proposed Resolution (Narrowed)

Based on the overlap analysis, the resolution should be **narrowed to 4-6 subsections** focusing on genuinely additive elements. Draft text below for review — all placement, scope, and specific mechanisms are open for reviewer input.

### Proposed Placement: Article XXI, Section 9

(Section 2(e) is occupied; Sections 2-A through 8 are occupied; Section 9 is the next available.)

### Draft Constitutional Text

> #### Section 9. Constitutional Oversight Body Docket Independence and Transparency
>
> (a) **Scope.** This section applies to any body established by this Constitution to exercise independent quasi-judicial, regulatory, or arbitral authority over disputes between levels of government, including but not limited to the Allocation Review Board, the Independent Fiscal Council, the Independent Boundary Review Commission, and the National Election Court. Where a body's enabling constitutional provision or Article XXI, Section 2-A establishes protections exceeding those of this section, the more protective provision controls.
>
> (b) **Prohibited Docket Conditions.** No appropriation, authorization, or other legislative enactment may:
>
> - (1) Condition funding of a body subject to this section on the outcome of any pending or future adjudication, certification, or determination;
> - (2) Prohibit the use of funds for any category of cases, disputes, or certifications within the body's constitutional jurisdiction;
> - (3) Require the dismissal, stay, or deprioritization of pending matters as a condition of funding;
> - (4) Impose reporting requirements that would require pre-decisional disclosure of case analysis, deliberative materials, or draft determinations.
>
> (c) **Automatic Severability.** Any legislative provision violating subsection (b) is severed by operation of law. The remainder of the enactment retains full force. The affected body may disregard the severed provision without judicial action; any party may petition the Supreme Court for declaratory relief confirming severance, which shall be heard on an expedited basis within thirty (30) days.
>
> (d) **NEC Operational Budget Floor.** The National Election Court shall receive a dedicated operational budget set as a fixed percentage of federal revenue, not less than 0.001% of federal revenue, automatically appropriated without annual congressional action and adjusted annually for inflation. Congress may not reduce funding below this floor, impose conditions on the use of these funds, transfer these funds to other purposes, or delay disbursement beyond thirty (30) days after the start of each fiscal year. Any member of the National Election Court may petition the Supreme Court for a writ of mandamus to compel disbursement at or above the floor amount.
>
> (e) **Interference Transparency.** Each body subject to this section shall include in its annual report:
>
> - (1) Any communication from a member of Congress, executive official, or their staff that directly or indirectly seeks to influence the outcome of a pending matter, with the date, source, and substance of the communication;
> - (2) Any legislative proposal, introduced or circulated in draft form, that would condition, reduce, or restrict the body's funding in connection with specific cases or categories of cases;
> - (3) Any attempt to require co-location with executive branch agencies, mandatory use of executive branch administrative services, or other arrangements that would compromise operational independence;
> - (4) Reports under this subsection are public records and shall be transmitted simultaneously to Congress, all Regional Governors, and the Supreme Court.
>
> (f) **Fiscal Accountability.** The Government Accountability Office shall audit each body subject to this section annually. Such audits:
>
> - (1) Shall examine financial management, procurement, personnel costs, and operational efficiency;
> - (2) Shall not examine, evaluate, or second-guess the substance of adjudications, certifications, determinations, or other exercises of the body's constitutional judgment;
> - (3) Shall be published and transmitted to Congress within one hundred eighty (180) days of each fiscal year's close.

## Gaming Vectors to Verify Against

Reviewers should check whether the proposed resolution adequately addresses:

1. **Rider relabeling:** Congress labels case-specific restriction as "jurisdictional clarification" rather than funding condition — does §9(b) catch this?
2. **Indirect docket control:** Congress doesn't restrict specific cases but zeroes funding for the office/division handling those cases — is this covered?
3. **Reporting weaponization:** §9(e) interference reporting could itself be weaponized — oversight body selectively reports to embarrass political opponents. Is there a false-reporting or bad-faith constraint?
4. **GAO audit scope creep:** §9(f)(2) prohibits examining "substance of adjudications" but auditor claims "we're examining process, not substance" — is the boundary clear enough?
5. **NEC budget gaming:** §9(d) establishes 0.001% floor, but what if NEC caseload grows dramatically (e.g., post-division under Gap 57)? Is percentage sufficient?
6. **Cross-body coordination attack:** Congress can't defund individual bodies but can defund shared infrastructure (IT systems, physical facilities) — does operational autonomy under Art XXI §2-A(h)(5) cover this?
7. **Sunset gaming:** Does §9 survive constitutional amendment or can it be amended by simple majority alongside an appropriation bill?
8. **"Related policy" argument:** §2-A(g)(1) prohibits conditions on "unrelated policy" — could Congress argue that docket restrictions are "related" to the body's mission? §9(b) must close this gap clearly.
9. **Interference reporting chilling effect:** §9(e) could deter legitimate oversight communications from Congress — is there a carve-out for routine administrative inquiries?
10. **NEC political capture via budget:** If NEC receives 0.001% automatically, Congress might argue it can condition *additional* above-floor funding on docket priorities — does §9(b) apply to all funding or only floor funding?

## Design Questions

**D1: Scope — Functional definition or closed list?**

- Option A: Functional definition only ("any body established by this Constitution to exercise independent quasi-judicial, regulatory, or arbitral authority over disputes between levels of government"). Pros: captures future bodies. Cons: boundary disputes about which bodies qualify.
- Option B: Closed enumerated list (ARB, IFC, Commission, NEC). Pros: certainty. Cons: requires amendment for new bodies.
- Option C: Functional definition with explicit named inclusions (draft text approach). Pros: certainty for named bodies + future-proofing. Cons: slightly more complex.

**D2: Placement — Where in Art XXI?**

- Option A: New Section 9 (self-contained, clean separation from §2-A). Pros: clear topical boundary. Cons: separated from related §2-A provisions.
- Option B: New Section 2-B (pairs with §2-A Implementation Act Integrity). Pros: keeps institutional protections together. Cons: §2-A is already large; adding §2-B may create confusion.
- Option C: Distribute across individual articles (NEC floor → Art VII; rider prohibition → Art XXI §2-A extension). Pros: collocates with relevant bodies. Cons: fragments the anti-gaming framework.

**D3: NEC budget floor — What percentage?**

- Option A: 0.001% of federal revenue (matches Commission's review-window rate; ~$45M at current revenue). Pros: proportionate for a certification tribunal. Cons: may be insufficient during heavy election cycles.
- Option B: 0.002% of federal revenue (matches ARB rate; ~$90M). Pros: ensures robust capacity. Cons: may be excessive for body with narrower jurisdiction than ARB.
- Option C: Variable rate tied to caseload (base rate + per-certification supplement). Pros: scales with need. Cons: more complex; IFC certification adds friction.

**D4: Interference reporting — Mandatory or discretionary?**

- Option A: Mandatory with enumerated categories (draft text approach). Pros: comprehensive transparency. Cons: could chill legitimate congressional oversight inquiries.
- Option B: Mandatory with safe harbor for routine administrative inquiries and legislative hearings on non-case matters. Pros: balances transparency with legitimate oversight.
- Option C: Discretionary with annual aggregate reporting (body reports number and general nature of contacts but not individual details). Pros: less confrontational. Cons: easier to suppress.

**D5: GAO audit — Include or defer?**

- Option A: Include with explicit case-decision exclusion (draft text §9(f)). Pros: clarifies accountability/independence boundary. Cons: adds complexity.
- Option B: Omit — existing accountability structures sufficient. Pros: leaner text. Cons: leaves boundary implicit.

**D6: Rider severability — Self-executing or judicial?**

- Option A: Self-executing with optional judicial confirmation (draft text §9(c)). Pros: body acts immediately; no delay. Cons: body makes own determination of what to sever.
- Option B: Judicial only — body petitions Supreme Court for severance. Pros: neutral arbiter. Cons: 30-day delay during which rider could suppress cases.
- Option C: Self-executing with mandatory judicial confirmation within 30 days (hybrid). Pros: immediate protection + legitimacy. Cons: routine Supreme Court docket burden.

**D7: Should the resolution address the "National Electoral Commission" naming inconsistency?**

- Option A: Use "National Election Court (NEC)" throughout (Art VII §7(b) is the judiciary article — authoritative source for court naming). Flag the "National Electoral Commission" references in Art XIII §5 (safeguards) as a pre-existing naming inconsistency to be corrected separately.
- Option B: Use both names with "also known as" clause.
- Option C: Defer — use whatever name appears in the target article (Art XXI uses neither currently).

## Verification Questions

1. **Art XXI §2-A(h) scope:** Does the phrase "Any institution established by an implementation act" cover the ARB (established by Art II §7-A) and the NEC (established by Art VII §7)? If not, these bodies may not inherit §2-A protections at all — which would make Gap 87's additive elements more critical than the overlap analysis suggests.

2. **Art I §4-A(v) savings clause:** The Commission's savings clause anticipates a "Constitutional Oversight Fund or similar mechanism." Does the proposed §9(d) NEC budget floor interact with this clause, or are they independent?

3. **Art II §7-A(dd) scope:** The ARB's anti-manipulation provision says Congress may not "impose conditions on the use of formula funds." Does this already cover case-specific riders for the ARB specifically? If so, §9(b) is additive primarily for bodies without equivalent anti-conditioning provisions.

4. **Existing NEC jurisdiction:** Art VII §7(b) grants the NEC jurisdiction over "election disputes that cross Regional boundaries or affect national elections." Gap 71 expanded this to Senator selection certification (Art IV §3(i)(3)). Is there any other provision granting the NEC budget protection that we may have missed?
