# Briefing: Gap 91 — "Emergency Nesting" Power Grab

## For External LLM Review

---

## 1. Context

This briefing supports multi-LLM review of Gap 91 in the Regional Federal Constitutional system. The constitution creates a comprehensive emergency powers framework at both federal (Art XVII) and Regional (Art XVII-RF) levels. Gap 91 concerns the scenario where overlapping federal and Regional emergency declarations create confusion about authority, enable perpetual emergencies through "nesting" (stacking, sequential re-declaration, scope creep), and allow pretextual emergencies to entrench executive power.

**Gap Severity:** High | **Status:** Proposal Available | **Theme:** Emergency/Military

---

## 2. The Problem

Art XVII-RF §3 currently contains only three lines: (a) federal authority supersedes when emergencies overlap, (b) Regions may request federal assistance, (c) Guard deployment coordination. Four gaming vectors were identified:

1. **Overlapping Emergency Confusion** — Federal and Regional governments declare emergencies over the same event with conflicting orders; citizens face compliance uncertainty; no procedural mechanism determines which level leads
2. **Perpetual Emergency via Duration Gaming** — Emergency renewed indefinitely; no absolute cap; graduated escalation absent
3. **Emergency Scope Creep** — Powers used for purposes unrelated to declared emergency; permanent structural changes made under emergency authority
4. **Pretextual Emergency** — Emergency declared for political rather than genuine reasons; no mechanism for finding and consequences

## 5. Key Interactions and Collisions

### 5.1 Art XVII-RF §3(a) Supersession vs. Primary Incident Command

Current §3(a): "When federal and Regional emergencies overlap, federal authority supersedes." Gap 91's Primary Incident Command uses COMPETENCY-BASED criteria — the level with enumerated authority, proximity, capacity, and expertise leads. These are CONTRADICTORY. If competency-based approach is adopted, §3(a) must be replaced, not supplemented.

**COLLISION**: Federal supremacy (§3(a)) vs. competency-based assignment (Gap 91 Part 1). Design decision required on which principle governs.

### 5.2 Art XVII-RF §2(a) 30-day vs. Gap 91 60-day Renewals

Art XVII-RF §2(a): "30-day periods." Gap 91 Part 2(e)-(f): "60-day" initial and renewal periods. These are CONTRADICTORY. The existing 30-day periods provide MORE FREQUENT legislative oversight.

**COLLISION**: Adopting Gap 91's 60-day periods would WEAKEN existing protection. Must not duplicate or contradict.

### 5.3 Art XVII §4(e) 2-year Absolute vs. Gap 91's 730-day Graduated

Art XVII §4(e): Simple 2-year absolute with 30-day wind-down. Gap 91 Part 2(g): Graduated escalation (180d→2/3, 365d→3/4, 730d→absolute). These serve the same purpose but with different mechanisms. The existing law applies the 2-year absolute at federal level; Art XVII-RF §1(b) cross-references federal time limits for Regional level. The graduated escalation might be additive IF it's seen as a Regional-specific layer, but may conflict with the cross-referenced federal framework.

### 5.4 Bio-Security Override (Art XVII §11 / Art XVII-RF §4)

Art XVII §11(b)(1): Bio-Security Emergency creates "direct federal requirements that supersede conflicting subnational laws." This is an ABSOLUTE federal override that would conflict with a competency-based Primary Incident Command. During Bio-Security Emergencies, federal supremacy is constitutionally mandated and competency-based assessment would be inappropriate.

**INTERACTION**: Primary Incident Command must explicitly exclude Bio-Security Emergencies under §4 / Art XVII §11.

### 5.5 Art XVII §8(c) vs. Gap 91 §3(j) Prohibited Actions

Federal prohibited actions (§8(c)): torture, collective punishment, punishment without individual determination, discrimination, speech suppression. Gap 91 prohibited actions (§3(j)): permanent structural changes, constitutional rights override, preemption of other levels, suspension of elections/sessions/proceedings. These are DIFFERENT LISTS addressing DIFFERENT RISKS. Not contradictory — potentially complementary.

### 5.6 Art XVII §6 Judicial Review vs. Gap 91 ARB Scope Review

Art XVII §6: Judicial review of emergency declarations, 30-day expedited, no deference, DC District Court or local. Gap 91 §3(k): ARB scope review, 14-day expedited. These create TWO parallel review mechanisms. Must clarify relationship: Is ARB review a prerequisite, an alternative, or concurrent? Which controls on conflict?

### 5.7 Art XVII-RF §2(e)-(h) Anti-Ratcheting Completeness

The existing anti-ratcheting framework at §2(e)-(h) is MORE COMPREHENSIVE than Gap 91's stacking prohibition: it includes cooling-off periods, cumulative duration tracking, and 5-year pattern triggers that Gap 91 lacks. Gap 91's stacking prohibition would be a step backward if it replaced §2(e)-(h).

## 6. Gaming Vectors (Post-Overlap Residual)

These are risks NOT addressed by existing provisions:

1. **"Competency Shopping"** — During overlapping emergency, both levels race to declare first and establish facts on the ground. First-to-declare level begins exercising authority before ARB determination. 48-hour ARB timeline creates window for fait accompli.

2. **"Federal Emergency Pre-emption"** — Federal government declares emergency over a genuinely Regional matter to preempt Regional response. Art XVII-RF §3(a) "federal supersedes" enables this. A competency-based approach would mitigate but creates its own gaming risk (see vector 1).

3. **"Cooperative Nesting"** — Federal and Regional executives of the same party coordinate overlapping emergencies that appear distinct but serve the same expansionist purpose. Anti-stacking provisions address single-level stacking but not coordinated multi-level "nesting."

4. **"Scope Migration"** — Emergency declared for genuine cause A; powers gradually used for unrelated purpose B. Each individual step is marginally related to A but the cumulative drift serves B. 14-day ARB review may not catch gradual migration.

5. **"Pretextual Defense via Genuine Origin"** — Emergency begins as genuine but is maintained long after threat resolves. Executive argues in good faith that conditions still warrant emergency. Pretextual finding requires ARB to assess subjective intent — difficult standard.

6. **"Shadow Emergency"** — Executive achieves emergency-like concentration of power through ordinary legislation, executive orders, or regulatory action without formally declaring an emergency. Framework only triggers on formal declarations.

7. **"Committee Emergency"** — Regional legislature delegates emergency-like powers to governor through ordinary statute (not emergency declaration), bypassing the entire emergency framework. Art XVII-RF §1(b) requires "category-specific powers only" but applies to emergency declarations, not legislative delegations.

8. **"ARB Capture During Emergency"** — During a high-intensity emergency, ARB members face political pressure on 48-hour Primary Incident Command determination. Independence of ARB determination is not specifically protected for emergency contexts.

## 7. Design Questions

**D1: Placement and relationship to existing §3**

- A: Expand §3 — replace 3-line provision with comprehensive section incorporating existing content
- B: Create §3-A — preserve §3 as default rule, add procedures in new section
- C: Expand §3 for de-confliction only; place accountability provisions elsewhere

**D2: Supersession principle — federal supremacy vs. competency-based**

- A: Replace §3(a) federal-always-supersedes with competency-based Primary Incident Command (Gap 91 approach)
- B: Keep federal-supersedes as DEFAULT; allow ARB to assign Regional lead when Regional enumerated powers are primary
- C: Federal supersedes for enumerated federal powers; competency-based only for concurrent/ambiguous areas
- D: Federal supersedes always (no change to principle); add only procedural coordination

**D3: Duration provisions — include or omit?**

- A: Include all (duplicates §2(a)-(h) and §4(e))
- B: Omit entirely — existing provisions are comprehensive and in some cases STRONGER
- C: Include only the graduated supermajority element (180d→2/3, 365d→3/4) if not already captured
- D: Cross-reference existing provisions; add only genuinely new elements

**D4: Prohibited emergency actions — placement and scope**

- A: Add Regional-specific prohibited actions list to expanded §3 (complements Art XVII §8(c))
- B: Amend Art XVII §8(c) to add the Regional-specific items (permanent structural changes, preemption of other levels, suspension of proceedings)
- C: Omit — existing §8(c) + §10 sufficient
- D: Add to expanded §3 but cross-reference §8(c) to show complementarity

**D5: ARB scope review — relationship to judicial review**

- A: ARB scope review as alternative to judicial review (petitioner chooses forum)
- B: ARB scope review as prerequisite (exhaust ARB before courts)
- C: ARB scope review concurrent with judicial review (no preclusion)
- D: Omit ARB scope review — existing judicial review under §6 is sufficient

**D6: Pretextual Emergency Finding**

- A: ARB pretextual finding with automatic termination (Gap 91 approach)
- B: Courts make pretextual finding under existing §6 framework (no new mechanism)
- C: ARB pretextual finding as expedited proceeding, subject to immediate judicial review
- D: Either ARB or courts may find pretextual (parallel authority)

**D7: Executive accountability consequences**

- A: Full Gap 91 consequences — impeachment, personal liability, loss of declaration authority
- B: Personal liability and loss of declaration authority only (impeachment already available)
- C: Loss of declaration authority only (most innovative element; liability already available under §8(d))
- D: No specific consequences — existing impeachment and §8(d) liability sufficient

**D8: Bio-Security Emergency carve-out**

- A: Explicit exclusion — Primary Incident Command does not apply during Bio-Security Emergencies under Art XVII §11 / Art XVII-RF §4 (federal absolute supremacy governs)
- B: Primary Incident Command applies to all emergencies including Bio-Security (competency-based even for pandemics)
- C: Primary Incident Command applies to Bio-Security but with rebuttable presumption of federal lead
