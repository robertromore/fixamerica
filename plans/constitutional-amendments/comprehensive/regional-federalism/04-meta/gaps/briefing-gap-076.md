# Briefing: Gap 76 — Strategic "Subregional" Gerrymandering of Regional Constitutions

## For External LLM Review

---

## 1. Context

This briefing supports multi-LLM review of Gap 76 in the Regional Federal Constitutional system. The constitution creates 7 Regions (Art I §4(y)(i)), each adopting its own constitution subject to federal requirements (Art I §3). The gap concerns Regional constitutions being designed to entrench minority rule through structural gerrymandering of legislatures, executive elections, and amendment processes.

**Gap Severity:** Medium | **Status:** Proposal Available | **Theme:** Intergovernmental

---

## 2. The Problem

Art I §3 requires Regional constitutions to protect against "domination by any single metropolitan area or rural bloc" but does not fully specify how. Four gaming vectors were identified:

1. **"County Senate" Minority Rule** — Regional Legislature creates chamber with one vote per county; 20% rural population controls 80% of chamber
2. **"Weighted Voting" Manipulation** — Facially complex weighting formula designed to ensure rural majority
3. **"Initiative Threshold" Barrier** — Requiring signatures from majority of counties prevents metro areas from using initiatives regardless of population support
4. **"Gubernatorial Electoral College"** — County-weighted system produces popular vote losers winning governorship

## 5. Key Interactions and Collisions

### 5.1 Art I §16 (RCV) Collision

Art I §16(e)(4) applies RCV to "all Regional elections for offices with Federal significance **as defined by Congress**." This:

- Requires Congressional action to apply to gubernatorial elections
- Until Congress acts, Regions may use FPTP for gubernatorial elections
- Gap 76 Part 2(i)(iii) says "No ranked-choice or alternative system may produce a winner who would not have won the runoff" — this is potentially ANTI-RCV since RCV can produce Condorcet paradox outcomes

**COLLISION**: Gap 76's runoff requirement may conflict with Art I §16's RCV framework. Need design decision on whether to mandate RCV, traditional runoff, or Region's choice.

### 5.2 Art V §4 Ambiguity

Art V §4: "Each Region shall have a Regional Governor elected by the people of the Region. The method of election and term of office shall be established by Regional constitution, provided terms do not exceed six years."

"Elected by the people" could be argued to permit:
- County-weighted electoral colleges (people vote, but votes are aggregated by county)
- Legislative appointment with popular ratification (people vote to confirm)
- Parliamentary-style systems where people elect legislature which selects Governor

The phrase "method of election... shall be established by Regional constitution" explicitly delegates this choice.

### 5.3 §3-A(c)(3) "Weighted Committee" Interaction

§3-A(c)(3) explicitly authorizes "weighted committee representation" as a structural mechanism. This is subject to §3-A(c)(4) population deviation limit for chambers, but committees are NOT chambers. Could be gamed to give geographic minorities effective veto power through committee control over legislation.

### 5.4 Gap 134 Deconfliction

Gap 134 (Metro-Balkanization Loophole) was resolved with §3-A(c)(4)-(5) and §3-A(i). Any new provisions must not:
- Weaken the 15% deviation limit (proposal's 25% bicameral compromise would do this)
- Create conflicting enforcement timelines
- Assign certification to a different body than ARB

## 6. Gaming Vectors (Post-Overlap Residual)

These are risks NOT addressed by existing §3-A provisions:

1. **"People's Electoral College"** — County-weighted gubernatorial election where people vote within counties but county-level results determine the winner. Art V §4 "elected by the people" arguably permits this. §3-A(c)(4) 15% deviation only applies to "legislative chambers."

2. **"Initiative Desert"** — Region simply doesn't provide for citizen initiatives. Constitution doesn't mandate initiative processes. All policy change must go through legislature that may be structurally biased (even within 15% deviation).

3. **"Non-Electoral Amendment Lock-In"** — §3-A(c)(5) protects electoral amendments but not others. Region locks favorable tax/environmental/land-use policy behind geographic supermajority that metro areas cannot achieve. Different type of minority rule, not representation-based.

4. **"Plurality Governor"** — Governor wins with 30% in a multi-candidate field. Art I §16 RCV requires Congressional action for Regional offices. Until then, plurality wins are possible.

5. **"Shadow Initiative" via County Convention** — Regional constitution gives amendment power to a "Regional Constitutional Convention" where delegates are chosen per-county. Technically not a "citizen initiative" so initiative access standards don't apply. §3-A(c)(5) requires population-majority ratification for electoral amendments but convention delegates control what reaches the ballot.

6. **"Judicial Circuit Gerrymandering"** — Regional court circuits drawn to ensure rural-dominated courts uphold minority-rule structures. No provision addresses Regional judicial districting.

7. **"Parliamentary Evasion"** — Regional constitution establishes parliamentary system where legislature selects executive. Art V §4 says "elected by the people" but "the people" could be argued to mean "through their elected representatives." This is a stretch but worth addressing.

8. **"Committee Veto"** — §3-A(c)(3) authorizes "weighted committee representation." A Region creates committees with geographic weighting that controls all legislation's path to the floor. 15% deviation applies to the chamber vote but the committee can prevent legislation from reaching the floor.

## 7. Design Questions

**D1: Gubernatorial election standard placement**
- A: Amend Art V §4 (where gubernatorial election is defined)
- B: Add to Art I §3-A(j)-(k) (keep all electoral standards in one section)
- C: New Art I §3-C (separate section)

**D2: Gubernatorial election method**
- A: Direct popular vote only (no electoral college variant; strict)
- B: Popular vote with ≤2% deviation tolerance (as proposed)
- C: "Elected by the people" with explicit prohibition on county-weighted/legislative selection (clarify Art V §4)

**D3: Gubernatorial majority mechanism**
- A: Require RCV consistent with Art I §16 (extends §16 to gubernatorial races without waiting for Congress)
- B: Require traditional runoff between top 2 candidates
- C: Region's choice of RCV or traditional runoff (either satisfies)

**D4: Initiative access provisions**
- A: Include — if Region provides for initiatives, require population-based signatures and cap geographic distribution
- B: Omit — Regions aren't required to have initiatives; existing law is sufficient
- C: Conditional with mandate — require all Regions to provide some form of citizen initiative, then apply access standards

**D5: Amendment access scope**
- A: Expand §3-A(c)(5) to cover ALL Regional constitutional amendments (population-majority ratification for everything)
- B: Keep existing scope (electoral/representational only) — other policy lock-in is acceptable Regional autonomy
- C: Expand to enumerated additional categories (fiscal policy, environmental regulation, civil rights)

**D6: Weighted voting / weighted committees**
- A: Explicit prohibition on weighted voting in all chambers (redundant with 15% deviation)
- B: Current 15% deviation sufficient (no change needed)
- C: Extend population deviation limit to committees with binding power (block, delay, or condition legislation)

**D7: Bicameral second chamber**
- A: Adopt proposal's 25% deviation for second chamber (WEAKENS existing law)
- B: Keep existing 15% for all chambers (maintain existing §3-A(c)(4))
- C: Allow up to 20% for geographic chamber only if it lacks appropriation authority

**D8: Enforcement mechanism**
- A: Cross-reference existing §3-A(d)-(i) for new standards (expand scope of compliance review)
- B: New enforcement mechanism tailored to gubernatorial/initiative standards
- C: Integrate into §3-A(i) by expanding "compliance" definition
