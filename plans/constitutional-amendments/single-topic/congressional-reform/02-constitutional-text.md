# Congressional Reform Constitutional Provisions

## Draft Amendment Framework

*Note: This document outlines constitutional provisions for congressional reform. Detailed amendment text to be developed.*

---

### Section 1. House Representation

(a) The House of Representatives shall be elected in a manner that ensures proportional representation of the electorate.

(b) States may use multi-member districts with proportional allocation, ranked-choice voting, or other systems that ensure minority representation.

(c) Single-member districts, if used, shall be drawn by independent commissions.

---

### Section 2. Senate Considerations

(a) *Note: Senate reform is constitutionally difficult due to Article V's protection of equal state suffrage.*

(b) Options for consideration:

- Proportional voting within Senate (weighted by state population)
- Expansion of Senate seats
- Constitutional convention to address representation

---

### Section 3. Procedural Requirements

(a) Each chamber shall establish rules that permit a majority to conduct business.

(b) Supermajority requirements may be established for specified matters but shall not apply to ordinary legislation.

(c) Committees shall report legislation to the floor within specified timeframes.

---

### Section 4. Ethics Enforcement

(a) Each chamber shall establish an independent ethics office with authority to investigate and sanction members.

(b) Ethics investigations shall be completed within ninety days.

(c) Serious violations shall result in public reporting regardless of member status.

---

### Section 5. Disclosure Requirements

(a) Members shall file financial disclosures within thirty days of any significant transaction.

(b) All meetings with lobbyists shall be disclosed.

(c) Campaign finance records shall be publicly available in real-time.

---

## Implementation Considerations

- House reforms can be implemented by statute
- Senate representation requires constitutional amendment
- Procedural reforms can be implemented by rule change
- Ethics reforms require constitutional backing for independence
