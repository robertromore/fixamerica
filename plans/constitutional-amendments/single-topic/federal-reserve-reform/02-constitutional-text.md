# Federal Reserve Reform Constitutional Amendment

## Proposed Amendment Text

---

### Section 1. Independence of Monetary Authority

(a) The Federal Reserve System, or its statutory successor, shall be the monetary authority of the United States and shall conduct monetary policy independently of political direction.

(b) The primary objectives of monetary policy shall be:

- price stability,
- maximum sustainable employment,
- moderate long-term interest rates,
- stability of the financial system.

(c) Congress may by statute modify these objectives, but may not direct specific monetary policy decisions.

---

### Section 2. Governance Structure

(a) The Board of Governors of the Federal Reserve System shall consist of seven members appointed by the President and confirmed by the Senate.

(b) Governors shall serve single fourteen-year staggered terms. No Governor may be reappointed after serving a full term.

(c) Terms shall be staggered such that one term expires every two years.

(d) The Chair and Vice Chair shall be designated by the President from among the Governors for four-year terms, subject to Senate confirmation.

(e) Governors may be removed only for cause, defined as:

- malfeasance in office,
- neglect of duty,
- incapacity,
- conviction of a felony.

(f) Policy disagreement, unpopular decisions, or failure to achieve economic outcomes desired by the President or Congress shall not constitute cause for removal.

---

### Section 3. Prohibitions

(a) The President may not:

- direct, threaten, or coerce the Federal Reserve regarding monetary policy decisions,
- publicly demand specific interest rate actions,
- condition appointments on commitments regarding future policy decisions.

(b) Congress may not:

- enact legislation requiring specific monetary policy actions,
- reduce the Federal Reserve's operational budget as retaliation for policy decisions,
- subject monetary policy decisions to legislative veto or approval.

(c) The Federal Reserve may not:

- purchase debt instruments directly from the Treasury except in declared national emergencies,
- extend credit to political campaigns, parties, or candidates,
- make lending decisions based on the political affiliation of borrowers.

---

### Section 4. Transparency and Accountability

(a) The Federal Reserve shall:

- publish the minutes of policy meetings within three weeks,
- provide quarterly reports to Congress on monetary conditions and policy rationale,
- submit to annual audit by the Government Accountability Office regarding administrative functions (but not monetary policy deliberations).

(b) The Chair shall testify before Congress at least twice annually regarding monetary policy.

(c) Monetary policy deliberations, including internal debates, forecasts, and dissenting views, shall be protected from disclosure for five years to preserve deliberative independence.

---

### Section 5. Emergency Monetary Powers

(a) In declared national emergencies, the Federal Reserve may exercise expanded lending authority as provided by statute.

(b) Emergency lending programs shall:

- be disclosed to Congress within seven days of initiation,
- sunset automatically when the emergency declaration expires,
- be subject to retrospective audit by the Government Accountability Office.

(c) No emergency lending authority may be used to benefit specific private parties selected on a basis other than neutral criteria established in advance.

---

### Section 6. Regional Reserve Banks

(a) The Federal Reserve System shall include regional Reserve Banks distributed geographically across the United States.

(b) The boundaries and governance of regional Reserve Banks are matters of statute.

(c) Presidents of regional Reserve Banks shall be appointed through a process that includes both public and private sector representation, as provided by statute.

---

### Section 7. Enforcement

(a) Any Governor who is removed other than for cause as defined in Section 2(e) may seek reinstatement through the federal courts.

(b) Any person aggrieved by a violation of Section 3 may seek declaratory and injunctive relief in federal court.

(c) The Federal Reserve shall have standing to challenge in federal court any law or executive action that violates this article.

---

### Section 8. Congressional Enforcement

Congress shall have the power to enforce this article by appropriate legislation consistent with its provisions.

---

### Section 9. Effective Date

This article shall take effect upon ratification. Governors serving at ratification shall continue for the remainder of their terms.

---

## Commentary

### Why Constitutional Protection?

Central bank independence is a key predictor of macroeconomic stability. Countries with independent central banks have:

- Lower average inflation
- More stable inflation expectations
- Lower interest rate volatility
- More credible monetary policy

The Federal Reserve's statutory independence has been eroded by:

- Presidential tweets demanding rate cuts
- Legislative proposals to audit monetary policy decisions
- Threats to fire Fed chairs for policy disagreements
- Appointment of governors based on political loyalty rather than expertise

Constitutional protection raises the cost of political interference and signals permanent commitment to sound money.

### Section-by-Section Analysis

**Section 1 (Independence)**: Codifies what has been Fed practice since Volcker: monetary policy is made by the Fed, not the White House. The dual mandate is constitutionalized but Congress retains authority to modify objectives (e.g., adding financial stability as done after 2008).

**Section 2 (Governance)**: The fourteen-year term predates the Constitution but is elevated to constitutional status. Single terms prevent appointees from currying favor for reappointment. Staggered terms ensure continuity and limit any single president's influence.

**Section 3 (Prohibitions)**: Makes explicit what norms previously implied:

- Presidents cannot publicly berate the Fed for rate decisions
- Congress cannot legislate specific interest rates
- The Fed cannot monetize debt except in true emergencies
- No political lending (preventing use of Fed for patronage)

**Section 4 (Transparency)**: Balances accountability with deliberative independence. The five-year delay on deliberations prevents real-time political pressure on individual governors while ensuring eventual transparency.

**Section 5 (Emergencies)**: Permits crisis response (as in 2008-2009 and 2020) but with:

- Disclosure requirements (Congress must know what's happening)
- Automatic sunset (no permanent emergency powers)
- Retrospective audit (accountability after the fact)
- Neutrality requirement (no picking winners)

**Section 6 (Regional Banks)**: Preserves the System's geographic diversity while clarifying that regional bank boundaries are statutory, not constitutional.

**Section 7 (Enforcement)**: Creates justiciable rights:

- Removed governors can sue for reinstatement
- Private parties can challenge improper political interference
- The Fed itself can defend its independence in court

### Comparison to Other Central Banks

| Central Bank | Independence Source | Removal Standard |
|--------------|---------------------|------------------|
| Federal Reserve (current) | Statute | Unclear |
| Federal Reserve (proposed) | Constitution | For cause only |
| European Central Bank | Treaty | Treaty violation |
| Bank of England | Statute | Various |
| Reserve Bank of Australia | Convention | Political discretion |
| Bundesbank (historical) | Basic Law | For cause only |

The proposed amendment places the Fed among the most protected central banks globally.

### Potential Objections

**"This is undemocratic"**: Monetary policy requires insulation from short-term political pressures. Elected officials set objectives; the Fed implements them. This is no different from judicial independence.

**"The Fed needs political accountability"**: The amendment preserves accountability through:

- Congressional testimony
- GAO audits of administration
- Transparent policy statements
- Eventually public deliberations

What it prevents is real-time political direction of interest rates.

**"This prevents necessary reforms"**: Congress retains authority to:

- Modify the Fed's objectives
- Change its governance structure
- Adjust regional bank boundaries
- Expand or contract its regulatory role

What Congress cannot do is direct specific interest rate decisions.

**"Emergency powers could be abused"**: The sunset and disclosure requirements prevent permanent emergency regimes. The neutrality requirement prevents bailouts of political allies.

### Relationship to Regional Federalism

This amendment originated as Article XI of the Regional Federal Constitution. It has been adapted to work standalone by:

- Removing cross-references to the Regional emergency powers article
- Simplifying "declared emergencies" to "declared national emergencies"
- Adding enforcement and effective date provisions

If adopted as part of Regional Federalism, use the Article XI version which integrates with the broader emergency powers framework.
