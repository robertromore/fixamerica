# Emergency Powers Reform Constitutional Amendment

## Proposed Amendment Text

---

### Section 1. Declaration Authority

(a) Emergencies may be declared by:

- the President, for matters within federal constitutional authority,
- State Governors, for matters within State authority.

(b) No emergency may be declared for matters outside the declaring authority's constitutional jurisdiction.

(c) Declaration must be in writing, must specify:

- the nature of the emergency,
- the geographic scope,
- the powers to be exercised,
- the duration (not to exceed thirty days without reauthorization),
- the conditions for termination.

(d) Declarations must be published immediately and transmitted to the relevant legislature within twenty-four hours.

---

### Section 2. Categories of Emergency

(a) Natural Disaster Emergency: Declared in response to hurricanes, earthquakes, floods, wildfires, pandemics, or other natural events causing widespread harm.

(b) Public Health Emergency: Declared in response to epidemic disease, contamination, or other threats to population health.

(c) Economic Emergency: Declared in response to financial system collapse, critical infrastructure failure, or severe economic disruption.

(d) Security Emergency: Declared in response to armed attack, invasion, insurrection, or imminent threat thereof.

(e) Constitutional Crisis Emergency: Declared in response to breakdown of constitutional order, including refusal to transfer power, institutional collapse, or widespread constitutional violations.

(f) The category of emergency determines the powers available under Section 3.

---

### Section 3. Emergency Powers by Category

(a) Natural Disaster Emergency powers include:

- evacuation orders,
- requisition of private property for public use (with compensation),
- suspension of regulatory requirements impeding response,
- deployment of emergency personnel,
- allocation of emergency supplies.

(b) Public Health Emergency powers include:

- quarantine and isolation orders (subject to due process),
- mandatory testing and reporting,
- emergency medical resource allocation,
- temporary closure of public gatherings,
- emergency authorization of medical countermeasures.

(c) Economic Emergency powers include:

- temporary price controls on essential goods,
- emergency lending authority,
- suspension of debt collection proceedings,
- temporary nationalization of failing critical infrastructure (with compensation).

(d) Security Emergency powers include:

- mobilization of armed forces,
- enhanced border controls,
- temporary curfews in affected areas,
- protection of critical infrastructure.

(e) Constitutional Crisis Emergency powers include:

- deployment of National Guard or federal forces for constitutional enforcement,
- federal assumption of election administration,
- emergency judicial appointments,
- temporary suspension of legislative or executive functions of a defiant jurisdiction.

(f) No emergency power includes authority to:

- suspend habeas corpus except as provided in Section 7,
- suspend the right to vote in scheduled elections,
- prohibit peaceful assembly or petition,
- impose prior restraint on the press,
- detain citizens without charge beyond seventy-two hours,
- conduct warrantless searches of homes.

---

### Section 4. Time Limits and Reauthorization

(a) Initial Declaration. No emergency declaration may exceed thirty days.

(b) Reauthorization. Emergencies may be extended by:

- legislative reauthorization for thirty-day periods,
- for federal emergencies: majority vote of the House of Representatives,
- for State emergencies: as provided by State constitution.

(c) Maximum Duration. No emergency may remain in effect for more than:

- one hundred eighty days for Natural Disaster, Public Health, or Economic emergencies,
- ninety days for Security emergencies (unless Congress declares war),
- sixty days for Constitutional Crisis emergencies.

(d) Renewal After Maximum. After reaching maximum duration, an emergency may only be renewed by:

- a two-thirds vote of the relevant legislature, and
- approval by the relevant judiciary (Supreme Court for federal).

(e) Automatic Termination. Any emergency not reauthorized before expiration terminates automatically. All powers exercised under the emergency cease immediately.

---

### Section 5. Judicial Review

(a) All emergency declarations are subject to judicial review.

(b) Expedited Review. Courts shall rule on emergency challenges within seven days at trial level, seven days on appeal, and seven days at supreme court level.

(c) Standards of Review:

- Whether the declared emergency exists in fact (de novo review),
- Whether the powers exercised are authorized for the emergency category,
- Whether the geographic scope is justified,
- Whether less restrictive alternatives were reasonably available.

(d) Burden of Proof. The declaring authority bears the burden of demonstrating the necessity and proportionality of emergency measures.

(e) Injunctive Relief. Courts may enjoin emergency measures pending review. The government may seek expedited appeal of injunctions within forty-eight hours.

---

### Section 6. Restoration Mandate

(a) Every emergency action must include explicit restoration conditions specifying when and how normal governance resumes.

(b) Restoration conditions must be:

- objective and measurable,
- achievable within the emergency's maximum duration,
- publicly stated at the time of declaration.

(c) Upon termination of emergency:

- all emergency measures cease,
- all suspended laws resume full effect,
- all property requisitioned must be returned or compensated within sixty days,
- all emergency personnel return to normal status,
- all emergency regulations are void.

(d) Post-Emergency Review. Within ninety days of emergency termination, the declaring authority shall submit a report to the relevant legislature detailing:

- actions taken under emergency authority,
- resources expended,
- rights limited and duration of limitations,
- assessment of necessity and effectiveness.

---

### Section 7. Suspension of Habeas Corpus

(a) The privilege of the writ of habeas corpus may be suspended only when:

- rebellion or invasion occurs, and
- the public safety requires suspension.

(b) Suspension Authority:

- Only Congress may suspend habeas corpus, by two-thirds vote of both chambers.
- No executive officer may suspend habeas corpus.
- No State authority may suspend habeas corpus.

(c) Scope Limitations:

- Suspension must specify the geographic area affected.
- Suspension must specify categories of persons subject to detention.
- Suspension may not exceed sixty days without reauthorization.

(d) Procedural Protections During Suspension:

- Detained persons must be informed of the basis for detention within twenty-four hours.
- Detained persons must have access to counsel within forty-eight hours.
- Detained persons must be brought before a military tribunal or emergency court within seven days.
- No person may be detained under suspension for more than ninety days without charge.

(e) Restoration. Upon expiration or termination of suspension:

- All persons detained solely under suspension authority must be released or charged within forty-eight hours.
- Persons charged must be tried in regular federal courts.

---

### Section 8. Prohibition on Emergency Expansion of Power

(a) Emergency powers may not be used to:

- expand the enumerated powers of any level of government,
- transfer power between levels of government except as expressly authorized,
- create new permanent programs or institutions,
- modify constitutional structures,
- extend the term of any elected official.

(b) Any action taken under emergency authority that is later found to exceed authorized powers is void ab initio.

(c) Officials who knowingly exceed emergency authority are personally liable and may be removed from office.

---

### Section 9. Definition of Constitutional Crisis

For purposes of this Article, "constitutional crisis" means:

(a) refusal by an incumbent to transfer power following a certified election,

(b) refusal by a jurisdiction to comply with final court orders regarding constitutional violations,

(c) armed resistance to lawful federal authority,

(d) systematic violation of constitutional rights affecting a substantial portion of a jurisdiction's population,

(e) collapse of functioning government in a jurisdiction such that constitutional protections cannot be enforced.

This definition is exclusive. Mere policy disagreement, unpopular court decisions, or political gridlock do not constitute constitutional crisis.

---

### Section 10. Termination of Existing Emergencies

(a) All national emergencies in effect at the time of ratification shall terminate one year after ratification unless Congress reauthorizes them under this Article's requirements.

(b) No emergency predating ratification may be reauthorized for more than sixty additional days total.

---

### Section 11. Congressional Enforcement

Congress shall have the power to enforce this article by appropriate legislation.

---

## Commentary

### Why Emergency Powers Reform?

Emergency powers are necessary for government to respond to crises. But unchecked emergency powers are dangerous:

- **Weimar precedent**: Hitler used emergency powers to establish dictatorship
- **American creep**: National emergencies now last indefinitely
- **Rights erosion**: Emergency becomes excuse for permanent surveillance
- **Accountability gap**: No one reviews whether emergency still exists

The existing National Emergencies Act (1976) failed because:

- Congress rarely votes to terminate
- Courts defer to executive claims
- No automatic termination mechanism
- No category-specific limitations

### Section-by-Section Analysis

**Section 1 (Declaration)**: Requires written, specific declarations. Prevents vague claims of emergency. Twenty-four hour legislative notification ensures oversight.

**Section 2 (Categories)**: Five distinct emergency types. Each has different powers and time limits. Prevents using disaster authority for political purposes.

**Section 3 (Powers)**: Enumerated powers for each category. What you can do depends on what emergency you declared. Absolute prohibitions protect core rights regardless of emergency type.

**Section 4 (Time Limits)**: Thirty-day initial limit forces regular reauthorization. Maximum durations (60-180 days) prevent indefinite emergencies. Two-thirds + judicial approval required after maximum.

**Section 5 (Judicial Review)**: De novo review of whether emergency exists. Government bears burden of proof. Expedited but real review (21 days total through Supreme Court).

**Section 6 (Restoration)**: Every emergency must have exit criteria. Automatic restoration when emergency ends. Post-emergency reporting creates accountability.

**Section 7 (Habeas)**: Codifies existing constitutional text with procedural protections. Two-thirds congressional vote required. Sixty-day maximum. No executive suspension.

**Section 8 (Anti-Expansion)**: Emergencies cannot create permanent new powers. Cannot modify constitutional structure. Personal liability for exceeding authority.

**Section 9 (Crisis Definition)**: Narrow, exclusive definition of "constitutional crisis." Policy disagreement is not crisis. Prevents abuse of most powerful emergency category.

**Section 10 (Existing)**: Terminates all current national emergencies within one year. Maximum sixty-day reauthorization for any predating amendment.

### Comparison to Current Law

| Feature | Current Law | Proposed Amendment |
|---------|-------------|-------------------|
| Declaration | Presidential discretion | Written, specific requirements |
| Categories | None | Five distinct types |
| Powers | Unlimited | Enumerated by category |
| Time limit | None (annual renewal) | 30-180 days maximum |
| Judicial review | Deferential | De novo, expedited |
| Termination | Congressional vote | Automatic if not renewed |
| Habeas | Unclear | Two-thirds Congress only |
| Existing emergencies | Continue indefinitely | Terminate within one year |

### Potential Objections

**"This hampers emergency response"**: The amendment permits robust response within categories. Thirty days is sufficient for most emergencies; reauthorization is available for longer crises.

**"Courts shouldn't second-guess emergencies"**: Courts already review emergency actions under arbitrary and capricious standards. De novo review simply requires government to prove emergency exists.

**"Automatic termination is dangerous"**: The alternative-indefinite emergencies-is more dangerous. Congress can always reauthorize if emergency continues.

**"This limits presidential authority"**: Emergency powers were never intended to be unlimited presidential authority. The amendment restores constitutional balance.

### Relationship to Regional Federalism

This amendment originated as Article XVII of the Regional Federal Constitution. It has been adapted for standalone use by:

- Removing "Regional Governors" from declaration authority
- Simplifying references to other articles
- Adding Section 10 (Termination of Existing Emergencies)
- Adding Section 11 (Congressional Enforcement)

If adopted as part of Regional Federalism, use the Article XVII version which integrates with Regional governance structures.
